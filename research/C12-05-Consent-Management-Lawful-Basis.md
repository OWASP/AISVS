# C12.5: Consent Management & Lawful-Basis Tracking

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-16

## Purpose

Record, enforce, and revoke consent across AI processing pipelines. GDPR Articles 6-7 require valid legal basis for all personal data processing. Without centralized consent tracking, organizations cannot demonstrate lawful basis for AI training data, leading to regulatory exposure. The Italian Garante fined OpenAI EUR 15M in November 2024 for lack of lawful basis (violating GDPR Articles 5(1)(a), 5(2), 6, 12, 13, 24, 25) and imposed a six-month mandatory information campaign — but the Court of Rome annulled Decision No. 755 on 18 March 2026, scrapping both the fine and the media-campaign order. The court has not yet published its reasoning, but the reversal is a significant setback for aggressive GDPR enforcement against generative AI and underscores how unsettled the legal-basis analysis remains for large-scale training data; the Garante has indicated it may appeal. To date this remains the only final GDPR enforcement action in Europe targeting the launch period of generative AI, and its annulment leaves regulators without a clear precedent going into the EU AI Act's full enforcement window. The EDPB's 2024 opinion clarified that legitimate interest requires controllers to articulate sufficiently clear purposes and ensure processing remains within reasonable data subject expectations — the opinion applies a two-pronged anonymity test (unlikely to identify individuals and unlikely to extract personal data through queries) and warns that unlawful processing during model development can taint downstream deployment legality unless the model is duly anonymized. Most CMPs were designed for web cookie consent and are poorly integrated with ML pipelines — no standard exists for consent tokens in inference APIs. The European Commission's Q4 2025 Digital Package proposal marks the first significant GDPR reform initiative, expanding the Records of Processing Activities exemption while enforcement intensifies around AI processing and consent manipulation. As of April 2026, cumulative GDPR penalties have surpassed EUR 7.1 billion, with EUR 1.15 billion issued in 2025 per the EDPB's annual report — AI processing, consent UX, and vendor management are the three fastest-growing fine triggers heading into H2 2026. The EDPB and EDPS issued Joint Opinion 1/2026 on the proposed AI Omnibus Regulation, and a public consultation on joint GDPR-AI Act interplay guidelines (opened October 2025, closed December 2025) drew over 100 responses published in March 2026, with final guidelines expected throughout 2026. The EU AI Act reaches full enforcement for high-risk systems on 2 August 2026, layering a second penalty regime of up to EUR 35 million or 7% of global turnover — exceeding GDPR's EUR 20 million/4% cap. Across the Atlantic, 20 US states now have comprehensive privacy laws in effect, many requiring explicit consent for sensitive-data processing (including AI-derived inferences); the Texas Responsible AI Governance Act (January 2026) applies existing privacy requirements to data collected for AI systems and creates limited exceptions for biometric data used in AI training. Brazil's ANPD continues extending GDPR-style enforcement to Latin America, targeting AI training data collection without proper consent. Under EU AI Act Article 53 (applicable to GPAI model providers from August 2025, with full AI Office enforcement powers from August 2026), providers must document and publish training data summaries including identification of opt-outs submitted by rights holders. The Commission's mandatory training data summary template (published July 2025) requires structured disclosure of data sources, collection methods, and content types, with six-month update cadence for post-market training changes. GPAI models placed on the market before August 2025 have until August 2027 to publish their summaries. Article 53 also requires providers to identify and comply with copyright opt-outs using "state-of-the-art technologies" — a deliberately open-ended standard that regulators can ratchet as detection capabilities improve. Non-compliance with GPAI transparency obligations carries penalties of up to 3% of global turnover or EUR 15 million (whichever is higher), separate from the broader high-risk AI system penalties. Beyond the EU, consent enforcement for AI training is globalizing rapidly. South Korea's PIPC issued its Generative AI Guidelines (August 2025) requiring documented legal basis under PIPA for all AI training data, and the March 2026 PIPA amendments raise the maximum administrative penalty from 3% to 10% of total revenue for repeated violations involving willful misconduct or gross negligence, violations affecting 10 million+ data subjects, or failure to comply with corrective orders — the PIPC also demonstrated willingness to order algorithm deletion, not merely data deletion, in the January 2025 Kakao Pay/Alipay enforcement (KRW 8.3 billion fine) where Alipay built credit scores from 40 million users' data without consent. The PIPC separately fined Meta KRW 21.6 billion (November 2024) for bundled consent practices, confirming that pre-ticked or bundled permissions do not satisfy Korean standards for sensitive data. Brazil's ANPD set enforcement priorities for 2026-2027 covering AI systems, facial recognition, and recommendation systems; the ANPD previously blocked Meta from using legitimate interest as a legal basis for AI training under the LGPD, holding that the processing could involve sensitive data. GDPR Article 22 adds a further consent dimension: European DPAs increasingly interpret it as a default prohibition on solely automated decisions producing legal effects — not merely a right individuals must invoke — which elevates the consent bar for AI-powered profiling, credit scoring, and hiring systems. On the opt-out side, noyb sent Meta a formal cease-and-desist in May 2025 over its plan to train Meta AI on EU Facebook and Instagram data under claimed legitimate interest from 27 May 2025, and has urged 11 supervisory authorities to take emergency measures; under the EU Collective Redress Directive, noyb and other qualified entities can pursue EU-wide class actions that could produce multi-billion-euro damages claims if the processing proceeds unchallenged. IAPP's 2026 Global Privacy Summit surfaced a consistent message from enforcers: operationalized privacy beats documented policy, and dark-pattern enforcement is expanding into consent flows, GPC signal handling, and opt-out friction — with US states already levying seven-figure penalties for GPC treated as device-specific rather than account-wide or for tracking that continued after opt-out. Agentic AI is forcing a rethink of consent itself; traditional one-shot consent models cannot meaningfully authorize autonomous agents that issue thousands of downstream actions per hour, and standards bodies (OpenID Foundation, IETF OAuth WG) are working on scoped, short-lived, actor-bound delegation tokens rather than extending legacy CMP patterns.

| **12.5.1** | **Verify that** a Consent-Management Platform (CMP) records opt-in status, purpose, and retention period per data-subject. | 1 | Processing personal data without valid legal basis, violating GDPR Articles 6-7. Without centralized consent tracking, organizations cannot demonstrate lawful basis for AI training data, leading to regulatory exposure. The Italian Garante fined OpenAI EUR 15M (2024) for lack of lawful basis. The EDPB's 2024 opinion applies a three-step legitimate interest test and warns that unlawful processing during development taints downstream deployment unless the model is anonymized. MITRE ATLAS AML.T0037 (Data from Information Repositories) — adversaries exploit poorly governed data stores lacking consent provenance. | Inspect CMP implementation (OneTrust, TrustArc, Cookiebot, Ethyca Fides, BigID, or custom). Verify it records: consent timestamp, specific purposes consented to, retention period, withdrawal mechanism, and lawful basis type (consent vs. legitimate interest). Sample consent records and cross-reference with data processing activities. Test consent withdrawal flow end-to-end. For AI-specific pipelines, verify the CMP integrates with data loading stages — Ethyca's Fides platform (open-source) can translate regulatory obligations into machine-readable definitions enforced at the pipeline level, while BigID's unified privacy management (March 2026) connects personal data discovery with consent enforcement across AI environments. Ketch's data permissioning engine embeds consent controls directly into LLM and AI workflows, with its Opt-Out Sync framework (March 2026) enforcing "Do Not Sell or Share" choices across every device and system. OneTrust's March 2026 expansion shifts AI governance from static compliance to a continuous control plane with cross-platform monitoring and programmatic guardrail enforcement. Check for Global Privacy Control (GPC) signal recognition per emerging US state law requirements — several state DPAs now treat GPC as an account-wide rather than device-specific signal and have assessed seven-figure penalties where companies honored opt-outs narrowly or re-enabled tracking after an opt-out. For record structure itself, verify consent records conform to ISO/IEC TS 27560:2023 (Privacy technologies — Consent record information structure), which defines a machine-readable schema covering the processing purpose, privacy notice, collection method, and consent event lifecycle. The W3C Data Privacy Vocabulary (DPV v2.3, February 2026) provides an RDF ontology that implements ISO/IEC TS 27560:2023 and ISO/IEC 29184:2020 Privacy Notices — usable as a controlled vocabulary so consent records from different CMPs can be mapped, compared, and audited across AI training pipelines without bespoke parsers. Newer entrants include DataGrail (Vera AI agent, March 2026 — automates PIA/DPIA population, consent banner monitoring, and orchestrates privacy tasks via a privacy MCP server across 2,500+ integrations), Privado AI (code-scanning platform that auto-discovers personal data flows and consent gaps across first- and third-party software), and Collibra AI Governance (stitches end-to-end data lineage from source datasets through model training and inference, connecting consent policy enforcement to AI pipelines). For jurisdictions beyond the EU, verify compliance with South Korea's PIPA (PIPC Generative AI Guidelines require documented legal basis for all AI training data; bundled/pre-ticked consent is explicitly insufficient) and Brazil's LGPD (ANPD blocked Meta from using legitimate interest for AI training where sensitive data was involved). | Most CMPs were designed for web cookie consent, not ML training pipelines. Integrating consent status checks into data loading pipelines requires custom development or newer AI-aware platforms like Ethyca Astralis or BigID's unified privacy management (which now connects data discovery, consent enforcement, and AI governance across the enterprise, including deletion from vector databases). The EDPB opinion emphasized that supervisory authority sanctions should factor into deployment assessments, potentially undermining legitimate interest justifications for controllers deploying models trained with unlawfully processed data. Regulatory focus is shifting from "did you collect consent?" to "can you prove how it was applied?" — downstream enforcement across analytics, CRMs, CDPs, and training pipelines is the new audit target. Clearview AI's cumulative fines across France, Italy, Austria, Greece, and the Netherlands now exceed EUR 95 million for scraping publicly available images without consent — a cautionary case for any AI system ingesting public data without lawful basis. EU AI Act Article 53 requires GPAI providers to publish training data summaries using the Commission's mandatory template (published July 2025), with six-month update cadence for post-market training changes — the AI Office gains full enforcement powers in August 2026, layering a second penalty regime (EUR 35M / 7% of turnover) on top of GDPR. The template's structured disclosure of data sources and collection methods creates an auditable artifact that DPAs can cross-reference against consent records. |
| **12.5.2** | **Verify that** APIs expose consent tokens that encode the data subject's opt-in status, purpose, and retention period. | 2 | Inference on data where consent has been withdrawn or was never granted for the specific processing purpose. Without runtime consent validation, models continue to process data even after subjects revoke consent. AI shifts privacy harm from "what was shared" to "what can be inferred" — even consented data can yield sensitive inferences that exceed the original consent scope. | Review API specifications for consent token endpoints. Test that inference requests without valid consent tokens are rejected. Verify token scope validation checks both data subject consent status and specific processing purpose. Test with expired, revoked, and scope-mismatched tokens. Evaluate consent signal standards: IAB Transparency & Consent Framework (TCF), IAB GPP (Global Privacy Platform), and Global Privacy Control (GPC). Ethyca Fides provides a Consent API (v3) with propagation policies that distribute consent decisions to downstream systems. Verify tokens encode purpose limitation — a consent grant for "service improvement" must not authorize "profiling" without separate consent. | Real-time consent token validation adds latency to inference. Token architecture must handle scale (millions of subjects) without becoming a bottleneck. No universal standard exists equivalent to IAB TCF for AI-specific consent tokens — existing standards (TCF, GPP, GPC) were designed for web/ad contexts and require adaptation. Emerging protocols like Grantex (Apache 2.0) provide signed JWT grant tokens with scoped claims, per-agent DIDs, user consent flows with FIDO2/WebAuthn passkeys, granular revocation with cascade, and delegation chains — but these target agent authorization rather than data-subject consent propagation. The IETF OAuth WG Internet-Draft `draft-oauth-ai-agents-on-behalf-of-user-01` introduces two new parameters — `requested_actor` in the authorization request (identifying the specific AI agent seeking delegation) and `actor_token` in the token exchange (authenticating that agent to the authorization server) — so the issued access token binds user consent to a named actor identity, the consent screen must disclose both the client and the actor, and resource servers can use HTTP 401/403 with `WWW-Authenticate` to force re-consent if scopes drift. While drafted for agent authorization, the same mechanism fits data-subject consent propagation: the `sub` claim pins the data subject, the actor binding pins which processor/agent is authorized, and scope claims pin the purpose — giving inference endpoints a single JWT to validate. Consent token revocation propagation across distributed systems requires careful cache invalidation design. Fides supports multiple consent signal standards (IAB TCF, USPrivacy, GPP, GPC) but extending these to ML inference pipelines is still emerging. Modern platforms now support bidirectional preference synchronization — consent flows from user interfaces to systems and back into a unified record — but the consent decision from a settings page must still follow data through ingestion, indexing, retrieval, inference, storage, and deletion, and few platforms achieve this end-to-end today. In the US, 20 state privacy laws now require opt-in consent for sensitive data processing, adding jurisdictional complexity for inference endpoints serving multi-state users. |
| **12.5.3** | **Verify that** denied or withdrawn consent halts processing pipelines within 24 hours. | 2 | Continued processing after consent withdrawal, violating GDPR Article 7(3) which requires withdrawal to be as easy as giving consent. Delayed processing halt means data continues to be used without lawful basis. Once content enters model training weights, deletion becomes practically unenforceable — prevention is the only viable strategy, making fast consent withdrawal propagation critical. | Withdraw consent via the CMP and measure time until all processing pipelines halt for that data subject. Verify across training pipelines, inference endpoints, batch jobs, and analytics. Check that 24-hour SLA is monitored with alerting configured for breaches. Test with BigID's automated consent withdrawal handling, which detects withdrawn consent and surfaces opt-outs across AI environments. Ethyca Fides provides propagation policies that distribute consent revocations to downstream systems via its Consent API. Verify that long-running training jobs check consent status at checkpoint intervals, not just at job start. For AI crawlers (GPTBot, Google-Extended, ClaudeBot, CCBot, Bytespider), verify a layered opt-out approach: robots.txt blocks, HTML meta tags (noai, noimageai), HTTP X-Robots-Tag headers, WAF/CDN blocking rules, and behavioral bot detection. | The 24-hour window is tighter than many batch processing schedules. Long-running training jobs may need to be interrupted and restarted at checkpoints. Streaming pipelines can halt quickly, but batch ETL jobs and scheduled retraining may not check consent status frequently enough. Coordination across microservices and data platforms adds complexity. For trained models, machine unlearning remains an active research area with meaningful progress and sobering limitations. The EUPG framework (Universitat Rovira i Virgili, July 2025) prepares models for deletion from the start by training on k-anonymized or differentially private data, then fine-tuning on originals — upon withdrawal, the model reverts to the privacy-protected version and retrains without the subject's data. Source-free unlearning methods (UC Riverside, September 2025) can operate without access to the original training data, addressing a major obstacle for commercial LLMs. However, a widely cited NeurIPS 2025 paper ("Machine Unlearning Doesn't Do What You Think," Cooper et al., Google DeepMind) identifies fundamental mismatches between unlearning goals and feasible implementations for generative models, concluding that unlearning is not a general-purpose solution — generative AI blurs the line between memory and inference, so even deleted data may be indirectly reconstructable. Prevention (blocking data ingestion upon consent withdrawal) remains the primary enforcement mechanism. The ICML 2025 MUGen workshop is driving toward standardized evaluation frameworks for unlearning verification. Agentic unlearning is an emerging subfield addressing LLM agents with persistent memory: Synchronized Backflow Unlearning (SBU, arXiv:2602.17692) jointly unlearns across model parameters and memory stores to prevent parameter-memory backflow where retrieval reactivates parametric remnants. The ALU framework (arXiv:2502.00406) takes a multi-agent, retrain-free approach — multiple LLM agents each handle a step in the unlearning process without weight updates, making it model-agnostic. Both approaches matter for consent withdrawal in RAG-based systems where deletion must cover both vector stores and model-level traces. Audit trail requirements add overhead: version-controlled robots.txt, access logs queryable by user-agent, and crawler activity records should be retained for 3+ years minimum. |
| **12.5.4** | **Verify that** models validate consent token scope before inference and refuse processing when the token is absent, invalid, or does not cover the requested operation. | 2 | Unauthorized inference — processing data without valid consent for the specific operation, violating GDPR's purpose limitation principle (Article 5(1)(b)). A consent grant for "customer support" does not authorize "behavioral profiling" or "model fine-tuning." Without scope validation at inference time, models silently process data beyond the originally consented purpose, creating regulatory exposure under both GDPR and EU AI Act. MITRE ATLAS AML.T0024 (Infer Training Data Membership) — attackers may exploit inference endpoints to determine if specific data subjects' records were used in training, compounding consent violations. | Inject inference requests with absent, expired, scope-mismatched, and revoked consent tokens — verify all are rejected with appropriate error codes. Verify the model serving layer (e.g., API gateway or middleware) checks token validity before forwarding to the model. Test that purpose-scoped tokens are validated against a purpose registry: a token consented for "recommendation" must not authorize "credit scoring." Evaluate integration with Ethyca Fides Consent API v3 or equivalent for runtime scope resolution. Verify that rejected requests are logged with reason codes for audit trails. Test at scale — consent validation must not degrade p99 latency beyond acceptable thresholds (measure with load testing tools like Locust or k6). | Runtime consent scope validation at inference time is an emerging pattern with limited production tooling. Most current implementations use API gateway middleware with custom consent-checking logic rather than standardized frameworks. Consent scope taxonomies are organization-specific — no universal purpose ontology exists for AI operations, though ISO/IEC 42001 (AI management systems) provides a governance structure and NIST AI RMF addresses data governance in its MAP function. The Grantex protocol (Apache 2.0, 2026) offers a promising model for scoped authorization tokens with signed JWT claims, per-agent DIDs, and granular revocation cascades — though designed for agent authorization, its architecture could inform data-subject consent scope validation. Agentic JWT (A-JWT, arXiv:2509.13597; also published as IETF `draft-goswami-agentic-jwt-00`, "Secure Intent Protocol: JWT Compatible Agentic Identity and Workflow Management") tackles what the authors call the "intent-execution separation problem" — stochastic reasoning, prompt injection, and multi-agent orchestration can silently expand an agent's privileges even when the original consent token is valid. A-JWT introduces three mechanisms relevant to consent scope: (1) a cryptographic agent identity via a checksum hash over the agent's system prompt, tools, and configuration — so a prompt-injected variant cannot reuse a legitimately issued token; (2) workflow-aware token binding that links a specific user intent to a specific execution step; and (3) a new OAuth 2.0 grant type `agent_checksum`. The reference Python proof-of-concept demonstrates sub-millisecond overhead and blocks scope-violating requests, replay, in-process impersonation, and prompt-injection pathways — making it a credible defensive layer when consent scope must survive agentic pipelines rather than single-shot inference calls. Caching consented-scope lookups helps with latency but introduces a consistency window during which revoked consent may still be honored. The EDPB and European Commission are co-developing joint GDPR-AI Act interplay guidelines (public consultation closed December 2025, 100+ responses published March 2026) expected to formalize requirements for inference-time consent validation throughout 2026 — these follow the precedent set by the DMA-GDPR joint guidelines, the first such cross-regulation guidance. Questions around AI-driven profiling and automated decision-making will be central to these guidelines. Under the EU AI Act's August 2026 enforcement, high-risk AI systems that process personal data without proper scope validation face penalties of up to EUR 35 million or 7% of global turnover — compounding GDPR exposure. GDPR Article 22's prohibition on solely automated decisions with legal effects adds a further consent dimension: DPAs increasingly treat it as a default prohibition rather than an opt-out right, meaning AI-driven credit scoring, hiring, or profiling systems must implement human oversight or obtain explicit consent as a precondition, not just as a fallback. South Korea's PIPC has demonstrated that regulators may order deletion of the algorithm itself (not just the training data) when consent was absent — the January 2025 Kakao Pay/Alipay enforcement ordered Alipay to erase its credit-scoring model built from 40 million users' data transferred without consent. |
