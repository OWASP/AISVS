# C14.7: User-Facing Transparency Reports

> [Back to C14 Index](C14-Human-Oversight.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-16 (5th pass)

## Purpose

Provide periodic disclosures on incidents, drift, and data usage. Transparency reports are the primary mechanism for public accountability — giving stakeholders, regulators, and affected populations visibility into how AI systems perform, fail, and handle their data.

The International AI Safety Report 2026 — produced by 100+ experts from 30+ countries — explicitly identifies limited publicly available information as a major challenge for AI safety oversight. Since the 2025 report, the number of companies publishing Frontier AI Safety Frameworks has more than doubled, an encouraging trend but the real-world effectiveness of these frameworks remains uncertain.

A regulatory cliff is forcing convergence: the EU AI Act high-risk provisions (August 2026), Colorado AI Act SB 205 (June 30, 2026), California's Frontier AI Transparency Act (SB 53, effective January 2026), and New York's RAISE Act amendments (signed March 2026) are creating simultaneous compliance deadlines across jurisdictions. Colorado SB 205 requires consumer disclosure when interacting with AI systems and annual impact assessments — the first US state-level mandate with enforcement teeth ($20,000 per violation). California SB 53 mandates critical safety incident reporting to the Office of Emergency Services within 15 days (or 24 hours for imminent risk of death/injury). New York's RAISE Act requires frontier developers to file disclosure statements with NYDFS, report critical safety incidents within 72 hours, and submit quarterly catastrophic risk assessment summaries. Organizations operating across jurisdictions face converging compliance deadlines that demand a unified reporting framework satisfying multiple regimes simultaneously.

Meanwhile, the Stanford Foundation Model Transparency Index (December 2025) found that industry transparency is actually declining — average scores dropped from 58/100 in 2024 to 40.69/100 in 2025 across 13 major developers. Companies remain systemically opaque about training data, training compute, post-deployment usage, and societal impact. Only a handful of companies (IBM, Writer, AI21 Labs) scored above 70, while major labs like Anthropic, Google, and OpenAI averaged around 36. Meta's transparency score dropped particularly sharply (60 to 31), and Meta did not release a technical report for Llama 4. Companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google no longer disclose training code, parameter counts, or dataset sizes — indicating a broad industry retreat from the openness norms of earlier years.

Two concrete infrastructure developments are worth tracking. The OECD published its Common Reporting Framework for AI Incidents (OECD AI Papers No. 34, February 2025) — a 29-criteria taxonomy organized across eight dimensions (economic context, environmental impact, societal impact, technical system details, inputs, outputs, tasks, and stakeholders), with 7 mandatory and 22 optional reporting fields. The framework is designed for cross-jurisdictional interoperability and is already being tested through open submissions to the OECD AI Incidents Monitor (AIM), which tracks over 14,000 incidents and hazards from public sources as of early 2026. The AI Incident Database (AIID, maintained by the Partnership on AI) recorded 362 incidents in 2025 — up 55% from 233 in 2024 — with impersonation/fraud and synthetic media misinformation as the fastest-growing categories. These databases provide the empirical baseline that transparency reports should reference.

The regulatory picture is now genuinely global. Beyond the EU and US, South Korea's AI Basic Act (effective January 2026) mandates transparency, risk assessment, and human oversight for high-impact AI systems across 11 domains, with extraterritorial reach to systems affecting Korean users. Japan's AI Promotion Act (effective June 2025) establishes non-binding transparency goals across government agencies. Singapore updated its AI Verify open-source governance testing toolkit in May 2025 with generative AI risk testing capabilities — the AI Verify Foundation now has 90+ member organizations including Dell, Hitachi, and IBM, and maintains a Global Model Evaluation Toolkit for LLMs and multimodal models. Brazil's Bill No. 2338 (passed Senate December 2024, awaiting final approval) would establish EU-aligned impact assessments and incident reporting for high-risk systems. Vietnam's Law on Digital Technology introduces AI transparency and labeling provisions effective 2026. The FTC issued its Policy Statement on AI and Section 5 of the FTC Act in March 2026, signaling proactive enforcement against algorithmic discrimination, deceptive AI-generated content, and privacy violations — building on a dozen AI-washing enforcement cases in 2025 (including settlements against DoNotPay, IntelliVision, and Evolv for misleading AI capability claims).

A significant shift is underway in how transparency disclosures are structured. Model cards and system cards — once voluntary technical documents — are functioning as proto-regulatory documents that regulators consult first. OpenAI's GPT-5 system card (August 2025) disclosed 5,000+ hours of red teaming by 400+ external testers across violent attack planning, jailbreaks, and prompt injections, plus quantified hallucination reductions (8x improvement over o3) and mental health safety evaluations. Red Hat introduced open AI system cards (December 2025) with a machine-readable schema under CC BY 4.0 licensing (github.com/RedHatProductSecurity/ai-system-card), enabling automated policy checks and side-by-side comparisons across vendors — a practical step toward standardized, machine-readable transparency. The EU AI Act Article 50 Code of Practice (first draft published December 2025, final expected June 2026 before August 2026 enforcement) establishes a formal distinction between provider obligations (technical marking and detectability of AI-generated content) and deployer obligations (public labeling of deepfakes and AI-generated text on matters of public interest). Third-party AI assurance is also emerging: PwC launched its "Assurance for AI" service in June 2025 — the first Big Four offering to provide independent assurance over AI systems under AICPA attestation standards, covering design and operating effectiveness of AI controls in a format resembling SOC Type II reports. ISO 42001 certifications are now being achieved in practice — Microsoft 365 Copilot, Miro, and Cornerstone Galaxy all earned ISO 42001 certification through 2025, and ISO/IEC 42006:2025 establishes requirements for certification bodies conducting AI management system audits.

On the governance tooling side, the market has matured considerably. Credo AI (Forrester Wave Leader, Q3 2025) provides end-to-end AI governance with policy management, regulatory compliance audit, and evidence-based transparency reporting. IBM OpenPages 9.1 now integrates agentic AI capabilities with an AI Model Register that tracks all models across business units for audit-ready documentation. Holistic AI offers specialized algorithmic auditing and compliance tracking (notably for NYC Local Law 144 and similar accountability laws). Arthur AI open-sourced its Arthur Engine in March 2025 — the first real-time AI evaluation engine designed for monitoring, debugging, and improving generative AI and traditional ML models, with local execution for data sovereignty. As of early 2026, Arthur has safeguarded over 1 billion tokens across real-world deployments. The Harvard Shorenstein Center's CLeAR Documentation Framework (May 2024) provides a principled approach to AI documentation — Comparable, Legible, Actionable, and Robust — with contributions from IBM Research, Microsoft Research, Hugging Face, and NYU, and is increasingly referenced in governance discussions as a practical complement to regulatory requirements. These platforms and frameworks can automate much of the impact assessment and metric collection that transparency reports require — though organizations still need to make editorial decisions about what and how much to disclose.

Three more developments reshape the 2026 transparency picture. First, the European Commission announced the Digital Omnibus on AI (November 19, 2025) as part of a broader simplification package — the proposal would extend Annex III high-risk enforcement to December 2, 2027 for stand-alone systems and August 2, 2028 for embedded systems, and defer mandatory machine-readable marking of AI-generated content to February 2, 2027 pending the Code of Practice on marking (draft expected May/June 2026). Organizations should not interpret the delay as a compliance reprieve — the obligations themselves are unchanged, and national market surveillance authorities can still act under GPAI provisions that took effect August 2025. Second, California's Executive Order N-5-26 (March 30, 2026) established a first-of-its-kind procurement-based transparency framework: within 120 days the Department of General Services and Department of Technology must recommend AI vendor certification requirements covering illegal content exploitation, harmful bias, and civil rights violations, plus best-practice guidance for watermarking AI-generated images and video. Because California is the largest state AI market, these procurement standards will function as de facto national benchmarks — vendors selling to any large enterprise should expect parallel attestations. Third, state-level AI provenance laws are multiplying: Utah HB 276 (Digital Content Provenance Standards Act, signed by Governor Cox) and Washington HB 1170 (signed by Governor Ferguson, effective for providers with over one million monthly users) both mandate latent disclosures and provenance detection tools, modeled on California's AI Transparency Act (operative August 2, 2026). Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy v3 (February 2026) illustrates the countervailing trend: it introduces quarterly-to-semiannual Risk Reports describing capability thresholds and mitigation progress, but removes the prior hard commitment to pause training when capabilities outstrip safeguards — replacing bright-line commitments with graded public goals. Transparency reports should therefore distinguish between binding commitments and aspirational goals when referencing frontier safety frameworks.
