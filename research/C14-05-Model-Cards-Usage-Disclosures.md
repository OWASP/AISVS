# C14.5: Model Cards & Usage Disclosures

> [Back to C14 Index](C14-Human-Oversight.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-03-29

## Purpose

Maintain model cards for intended use, performance metrics, and ethical considerations. Model cards are the primary mechanism for closing the transparency gap between AI system capabilities and stakeholder understanding — documenting what a model was designed for, where it falls short, and what ethical risks it carries.

The International AI Safety Report 2026 highlights major evidence gaps from limited publicly available information about how GPAI systems are developed. The AI Transparency Atlas study (December 2025, arXiv:2512.12443) provides sobering evidence of the current state: analyzing documentation from 50 models, the researchers found 947 unique section names across model cards with extreme naming inconsistency (usage information alone appeared under 97 different labels). Frontier labs achieved ~80% transparency compliance while most providers fell below 60%, with safety-critical categories showing the largest deficits.

Since the 2025 report, the number of companies publishing Frontier AI Safety Frameworks has more than doubled, indicating growing adoption of structured model documentation. NVIDIA's Model Card++ initiative extends the standard template with ethical considerations and enhanced performance documentation. For agentic AI systems, model cards need to document tool permissions, action scopes, and autonomy boundaries — no standard template for this exists yet.

| **14.5.1** | **Verify that** model cards document intended use cases, limitations, and known failure modes. | 1 | Misuse of models outside their intended scope; users deploying models in high-risk contexts the model was never designed or evaluated for; inability to assess fitness-for-purpose. | **14.5.2** | **Verify that** performance metrics across different applicable use cases are disclosed. | 1 | Deploying a model that performs well on average but fails badly for specific subgroups or use cases; hidden performance disparities across demographics or data distributions. The UnitedHealth nH Predict case (2025) showed a system optimized for aggregate denial rates rather than accuracy across patient populations, with a 90% error rate on appeals. | Verify that metrics are reported per relevant subgroup or use case, not just aggregate. Check that metrics include both accuracy-type and fairness-type measures where applicable. Confirm metrics are computed on held-out evaluation sets, not training data. For agentic systems, include task completion rates, error rates per tool, and human override frequency as disclosed metrics. | Disaggregated metrics are essential. A model with 95% overall accuracy that has 60% accuracy for a minority subgroup is problematic. Standard disaggregation dimensions include demographics, geographic regions, language variants, and edge-case categories relevant to the domain. Conformal prediction (2025-2026 advances) can provide per-subgroup coverage guarantees as a disclosed metric, ensuring that uncertainty bounds are valid across demographic groups — see ConformaSight and conformal fairness research (arxiv:2503.23819). |
| **14.5.3** | **Verify that** ethical considerations, bias assessments, fairness evaluations, training data characteristics, and known training data limitations are documented and updated regularly. | 2 | Undisclosed biases that lead to discriminatory outcomes; training data that is unrepresentative or contains toxic content; stakeholders unable to assess ethical risks before deployment. | Review documentation for bias assessments, fairness metrics (demographic parity, equalized odds, etc.), training data composition, and data collection methodology. Verify that documents are dated and updated on a defined schedule (e.g., quarterly or per model update). Cross-reference with EU AI Act Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment requirements for high-risk systems. | This is a compound requirement covering multiple documentation areas. Bias assessment tooling includes Fairlearn, AI Fairness 360, and What-If Tool. Training data documentation can follow the Datasheets for Datasets framework (Gebru et al. 2021). Regular updates are critical because model behavior and data distributions drift over time. EU AI Act high-risk obligations taking effect August 2026 will require documented bias and fairness assessments — organizations should be building this documentation now. ISO 42001 establishes continuous improvement as a board-level expectation, formalizing the "updated regularly" aspect. |
| **14.5.4** | **Verify that** model cards are version-controlled and maintained throughout the model lifecycle with change tracking. | 2 | Stale documentation that does not reflect the current model version; inability to compare how model characteristics changed across versions; no audit trail for documentation changes. | Verify that model cards are stored in version control (e.g., Git) alongside model artifacts. Check that each model version has a corresponding model card version. Confirm that a changelog or diff is available showing what changed between versions. For agentic AI, model cards should also version the agent's tool permissions, system prompts, and approval workflow configurations. | Integration with ML experiment tracking tools (MLflow, Weights & Biases, Neptune) can automate parts of model card maintenance. The challenge is keeping free-text sections (limitations, ethical considerations) up to date, which requires human effort beyond what automation provides. The growing mismatch between AI capability advances and governance pace (International AI Safety Report 2026) means model cards can become outdated within weeks of publication — automated pipelines that flag model card staleness against deployment versions are increasingly necessary. The AI Transparency Atlas pipeline (arXiv:2512.12443) demonstrates that automated model card evaluation is now practical and cheap — their multi-agent LLM pipeline evaluated 50 models for under $3 total, using web search to collect evidence from dispersed public sources and multi-agent consensus for scoring. This approach could be adapted for internal model card freshness monitoring. |
