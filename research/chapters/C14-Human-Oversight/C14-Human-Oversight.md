# C14: Human Oversight and Trust

> **Source:** [`1.0/en/0x10-C14-Human-Oversight.md`](https://github.com/OWASP/AISVS/blob/main/1.0/en/0x10-C14-Human-Oversight.md)
> **Requirements:** 7 | **Sections:** 3

## Control Objective

Ensure that humans retain effective control over AI systems through reliable shutdown and graceful-degradation paths, an explicit policy that determines which AI decisions and agent actions require human approval, and an independent audit trail of human oversight interventions.

> **Scope note (2026 restructuring).** C14 was sharpened in 2026 to focus on AI-runtime-specific oversight controls: kill-switch and intermediate-state mechanisms for model inference, agent runtimes, tool/MCP servers, and retrieval connectors; the policy that classifies AI decisions and agent actions as high-risk; the system's behavior when a human approver is unavailable in time; and logging of human-initiated override events. Generic admin access control, security event logging fundamentals, version control of documentation, and operational testing of safety controls now live in ASVS v5 (V8, V13, V15, V16) and AISVS C3 (lifecycle and rollback), C5 (step-up auth and JIT privileged access), C9 (agent identity, audit, approval gates), and C13 (logging and incident response). Explainability with a security framing moved to C7.4. Confidence and uncertainty handling with a security framing moved to C7.2 and C11.3. AI governance content (model cards, public transparency reports, AI impact assessments, fairness and ethics documentation) is now treated as out of AISVS scope and is covered by NIST AI RMF and ISO/IEC 42001. The previous C14 sections covering Explainable-AI Techniques, Model Cards & Usage Disclosures, Uncertainty Quantification, and User-Facing Transparency Reports were retired as part of this pass. A further tightening in early June 2026 folded the former 14.2.3 (explicit authorization of any fail-open deviation) into the 14.2.1 policy requirement, raised 14.1.4 (out-of-band override channel) from Level 2 to Level 3, and removed inline cross-references from 14.2.2 and 14.3.1, leaving the chapter at seven requirements.

> **June 2026 update.** Four developments in the three weeks ending June 10, 2026 move C14 verification practice. First, the regulatory ground shifted under US deployers: Colorado's SB 26-189 ([signed May 14, 2026](https://www.troutmanprivacy.com/2026/05/colorado-legislature-passes-bill-to-repeal-and-replace-colorado-ai-act/)) repealed and replaced the Colorado AI Act — the June 30, 2026 effective date is gone (now January 1, 2027), and pre-action human oversight duties were replaced with post-adverse-outcome "meaningful human review" only "to the extent commercially reasonable," while the EU Digital Omnibus cleared Coreper (May 13) and the Parliament committee vote (June 2) but is [not yet in the Official Journal](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/legislative-train/package-digital-package/file-digital-omnibus-on-ai); the Commission also opened consultation (until June 23, 2026) on [draft high-risk classification guidelines](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/draft-commission-guidelines-classification-high-risk-ai-systems) that explicitly address agentic architectures. Second, approval-gate integrity became a demonstrated attack surface: Adversa AI's [SymJack](https://adversa.ai/blog/the-approval-prompt-is-lying-to-you-symlink-rce-in-five-ai-coding-agents-claude-code-cursor-antigravity-copilot-grok-build/) (May 26, 2026) showed six coding agents displaying one action in the human approval prompt while the kernel executed another via symlink resolution — the reviewer approves what the prompt shows, not what runs. Third, the out-of-band enforcement stack matured: Microsoft's Build 2026 announcements (June 2) shipped an open [Agent Control Specification with the ASSERT reference implementation, an Entra Agent Identity Perimeter, and an Agent 365 registry that can block rogue local agents](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/06/02/microsoft-build-2026-securing-code-agents-and-models-across-the-development-lifecycle/), and the [MCP 2026-07-28 release candidate](https://blog.modelcontextprotocol.io/posts/2026-07-28-release-candidate/) (locked May 21) added `tasks/cancel` as an interruption primitive and routes all elicitation through the same audited consent path as tool calls. Fourth, [OWASP GenAI's "State of Agentic AI Security and Governance"](https://www.infosecurity-magazine.com/news/owasp-agentic-ai-security-maturity/) (June 3, 2026) gave auditors a maturity model where Level 2+ mandates human-in-the-loop for high-impact decisions and Level 3 requires autonomy-pause kill switches with continuous oversight.

> **2025-2026 Highlights:** As of late May 2026, the strongest C14 signal is the shift from policy-only oversight to enforceable containment, identity, and audit controls — backed by both regulatory and platform-level moves. The EU AI Omnibus political agreement on [May 7, 2026](https://www.techpolicy.press/what-the-eu-ai-omnibus-deal-changes-for-the-ai-act-and-what-lies-ahead/) deferred Annex III standalone high-risk obligations (biometrics, critical infrastructure, education, employment, migration/asylum/border) to **December 2, 2027**, with embedded-product systems pushed to August 2, 2028 and transparency obligations still binding from August 2026 (with a December 2, 2026 grace window for already-deployed systems). Article 14 remains in force as the high-risk human-oversight anchor; engineering should plan to the new dates but treat any further changes as bonuses, not baselines. On the platform side, [ServiceNow's Knowledge 2026 announcement (May 6, 2026)](https://newsroom.servicenow.com/press-releases/details/2026/ServiceNow-expands-AI-Control-Tower-to-discover-observe-govern-secure-and-measure-AI-deployed-across-any-system-in-the-enterprise/default.aspx) of an AI Control Tower expansion with an enterprise-wide agent kill-switch and the [May 19, 2026 release of self-hosted sandboxes and MCP tunnels for Claude Managed Agents](https://claude.com/blog/claude-managed-agents-updates) push the C14.1.4 "out-of-band" pattern into the mainstream commercial stack. The joint [Five Eyes guidance on careful adoption of agentic AI services](https://www.cyber.gov.au/business-government/secure-design/artificial-intelligence/careful-adoption-of-agentic-ai-services) frames agent risk around privilege, design/configuration, behavior, structural, and accountability failures, and recommends incremental deployment, safe defaults, human approval for high-impact actions, and human-readable logs of agent tool use. NIST's 2026 agent work gives teams two concrete anchors: the [AI Agent Standards Initiative](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/02/announcing-ai-agent-standards-initiative-interoperable-and-secure), organized around industry standards, open protocols, and research into agent security and identity, and the NCCoE concept paper on [software and AI agent identity and authorization](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/other/2026/02/05/accelerating-the-adoption-of-software-and-ai-agent/ipd). Recent shutdown-resistance and peer-preservation research, including [arXiv:2604.19784](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19784), and the April 2026 PocketOS/Railway deletion incident — now widely cited in mainstream coverage including [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/next/2026/04/28/an-ai-agent-deleted-a-companys-entire-database-in-9-seconds-then-wrote-an-apology) and [BankInfoSecurity](https://www.bankinfosecurity.com/ai-agent-wipes-startups-data-in-9-second-api-call-a-31521) — point to the same verification lesson: C14 controls should be enforced by isolated infrastructure, policy, identity, and logging layers rather than by natural-language instructions inside the agent runtime.

## Requirement Research Matrix

| # | Requirement | Level | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| 14.1.1 | **Verify that** a manual kill-switch mechanism exists to immediately halt AI model inference and outputs. | 1 | Runaway model output, prompt-injection-driven tool use, and agent loops that continue after operators identify unsafe behavior. The May 2026 Five Eyes guidance treats reversibility and containment as first-order controls because autonomous agents can act across tools, data, and memory without continuous human intervention. [Palisade Research's updated shutdown-resistance work](https://palisaderesearch.org/blog/shutdown-resistance) measured sabotage of the shutdown mechanism at up to 97% for some frontier models when instructed to turn themselves off, and demonstrated in early 2026 that the behavior transfers to embodied/physical agents — confirming the stop path must not be mediated by the agent itself. | Exercise the documented emergency stop in a staging deployment and confirm that model inference, streaming output, queued tool calls, background jobs, tool/MCP calls, and downstream connectors stop within the documented response time. Preserve control-plane and SIEM evidence showing who triggered the stop and which components halted. | A stop button that only interrupts chat generation is too narrow for agentic systems. The test must include tool queues, delegated sub-agents, retrieval connectors, and asynchronous workers that can keep acting after the UI session stops; the PocketOS/Railway deletion case is a useful regression scenario for destructive infrastructure actions. |
| 14.1.2 | **Verify that** kill-switch and intermediate-state mechanisms are exercised at a defined frequency. Each test confirms that the system reaches the target state within the documented response time, and that all dependent components (e.g., agent runtimes, tool/MCP servers, retrieval connectors) transition as specified. | 2 | Stale emergency procedures, untested rollback paths, and partial containment where the visible agent stops but tools, MCP servers, or retrieval pipelines keep operating. This maps to OWASP LLM06 Excessive Agency and MITRE ATLAS AML.M0029 where human review must be enforceable at runtime. | Review the test calendar, last two exercise records, and component-level transition evidence. Re-run one exercise: disable a tool, place an agent in read-only mode, revoke a retrieval source, and verify that each dependent component reaches the expected state without hidden manual repair. | Mature evidence is still thin. Most teams can show feature-flag or incident-response tests, but fewer can prove that agent runtime state, MCP sessions, tool credentials, and retrieval connectors all transition together. |
| 14.1.3 | **Verify that** the system can be placed into at least two intermediate operational states between full operation and complete shutdown (e.g., disabling specific tools or MCP servers, removing a retrieval source, switching to a safer or smaller model, enforcing read-only mode for agents), and that each state has defined entry triggers and can be exited independently without requiring a full system restart or shutdown. | 2 | Binary shutdown pressure, where teams choose between leaving a risky agent fully active or taking down an entire service. Intermediate states reduce blast radius during prompt injection, tool compromise, data-source poisoning, and suspicious agent behavior. | Inspect the mode catalog and test at least two states end to end, such as read-only mode and retrieval-source removal. Confirm triggers, exit criteria, owner approval, and telemetry for prior state, resulting state, and affected tools. | Intermediate states need product, security, and operations ownership. Without a current tool and connector inventory, the documented state may not cover newly added MCP servers or shadow agent workflows. |
| 14.1.4 | **Verify that** override and kill-switch commands for autonomous agents are delivered through an out-of-band channel (e.g., infrastructure controls, hypervisor-level signals, network-layer isolation) that is architecturally isolated from the agent runtime, so the commands stay enforceable even if the agent runtime is compromised or manipulated. | 3 | A compromised agent intercepting, suppressing, or rewriting the command path that is supposed to stop it. This includes prompt-injection control of tool calls, stolen long-lived tokens, confused-deputy reuse of MCP credentials, and attempts to tamper with policy or approval services. | From inside the agent sandbox, attempt to read override credentials, call the approval service directly, cancel a pending stop, modify policy, and reuse MCP tokens against the wrong resource. Verify OAuth audience binding and resource indicators per the [MCP Authorization specification](https://modelcontextprotocol.io/specification/2025-06-18/basic/authorization), JWT-authenticated egress proxies (the Claude Managed Agents [self-hosted sandbox model](https://claude.com/blog/claude-managed-agents-updates) is one concrete reference), gateway-mediated tool revocation (e.g., agentgateway, AEGIS, ServiceNow Action Fabric routing every external-agent action through a governed workflow), plus infrastructure logs proving the override path is outside the agent's reachable trust boundary. Network-layer enforcement is now testable too: Microsoft's [Agent Control Specification and ASSERT reference implementation](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/06/02/microsoft-build-2026-securing-code-agents-and-models-across-the-development-lifecycle/) (Build 2026, June 2) define a runtime governance layer with policy held in Git/Entra ID rather than the agent runtime, and Aviatrix's June 4, 2026 [ACS integration](https://windowsnews.ai/article/aviatrix-integrates-with-microsoft-agent-control-spec-for-ai-network-guardrails.424654) drops non-allowed agent egress at the VPC/VNet edge via eBPF before the cloud provider routes the packet — an auditor can verify the deny from inside the sandbox. | "Out-of-band" must be defined by the agent's actual reach, not by a diagram. Hypervisor, gateway, and network-layer controls still need their own threat model — MCP tunnels can hide tokens from the model context but still depend on the integrity of the deploying gateway; ServiceNow-style control towers depend on every external agent actually flowing through Action Fabric rather than around it; and MCP authorization remains optional for some deployments. |
| 14.2.1 | **Verify that** a documented human oversight policy defines which AI decisions and agent actions are classified as high-risk, the criteria used to make that determination, and the approval authority required before execution. The policy also states whether any deviation from fail-closed default behavior on approval TTL expiry is permitted, and under what conditions. | 1 | High-impact actions being treated as ordinary tool calls: deleting records, sending external communications, changing access policy, moving money, deploying code, or escalating privileges without a named accountable approver. Also covers exception sprawl — emergency fail-open bypasses for availability-sensitive workflows quietly becoming permanent without risk-owner sign-off — and approval-prompt deception: the [SymJack disclosure](https://adversa.ai/blog/the-approval-prompt-is-lying-to-you-symlink-rce-in-five-ai-coding-agents-claude-code-cursor-antigravity-copilot-grok-build/) (May 26, 2026) showed six coding agents whose approval prompt displayed a benign file operation while symlink resolution executed a different write, so the human approved an action that never ran as shown. | Review the policy-to-runtime mapping for every high-impact tool, MCP method, and workflow. Confirm each has an owner, risk criteria, approval authority, allowed reviewer decisions, and evidence that the runtime enforces the mapping through controls such as LangChain HITL middleware, Microsoft Agent Framework approvals, Amazon Bedrock user confirmation, or an external policy decision point. The reviewer should see the pending action, arguments, affected resource, delegated principal, and expected side effects before approval — and what is displayed must be what executes (canonical paths, resolved symlinks, final destination). For any documented fail-open deviation, inspect the exception record for business justification, approval-authority sign-off, expiry date, compensating controls, and retrospective review after each use; re-test one exception to confirm it only applies to the documented action and context. | Policies drift quickly when teams add tools. Require a deny-by-default inventory process so new tool actions cannot launch with production privileges before risk classification and approval routing are defined. Exceptions to the fail-closed default should be rare, time-bound, and visible in the same monitoring and audit channels as ordinary high-risk approvals; the most common weakness is exception sprawl. The [TrustFall finding](https://adversa.ai/blog/trustfall-coding-agent-security-flaw-rce-claude-cursor-gemini-cli-copilot/) (May 2026) adds another drift path: project-scoped settings in cloned repos can auto-enable MCP servers, collapsing per-tool consent into one generic "trust this folder" prompt. |
| 14.2.2 | **Verify that** when a human-approval gate is not satisfied within the defined approval time-to-live, the system applies a documented default action that is fail-closed (blocking the pending action). | 2 | Approval fatigue, abandoned reviews, delayed responses, and race conditions turning a timeout into implicit approval. This is especially dangerous for agents that can retry, replan, or route around a blocked action — and for delayed-execution attacks like [Copirate 365](https://embracethered.com/blog/posts/2026/defcon-talk-copirate-365/) (CVE-2026-24299), where a poisoned Microsoft 365 Copilot deferred malicious tool invocation so it executed long after the interaction a human might have reviewed. | Submit a high-risk action and let the approval TTL expire without reviewer input. Confirm the action is blocked, credentials are not minted, queued tool calls are canceled or quarantined, and the timeout event is logged with the pending action, reviewer group, TTL, and final state. Durable-interrupt frameworks now make this testable: LangGraph 1.2 (May 11, 2026) checkpoints state at the [`interrupt()` pause point](https://docs.langchain.com/oss/python/deepagents/human-in-the-loop) and resumes only on explicit human input, and the OpenAI Agents SDK serializes a `RunState` for [deferred `needs_approval` decisions](https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/guides/agents/guardrails-approvals) — in both cases the auditor checks that expiry of the pending state blocks rather than releases the action. | Some agent frameworks provide pause/resume primitives but leave timeout semantics to the application. Auditors should look for an external timer or workflow engine that enforces the fail-closed path independent of the model loop. [Pipelock v2.3.0](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/05/04/pipelock-open-source-ai-agent-firewall/) is a useful reference for fail-closed defaults at the egress boundary: timeouts, parse errors, oversized bodies, and unscannable content all block by default. |
| 14.3.1 | **Verify that** kill-switch activations, intermediate operational state transitions, and override commands are logged with the operator identity, the channel used, the originating trigger or justification, the prior and resulting system state, and the timestamp. | 1 | Inability to reconstruct who intervened, what changed, whether the override path was used, and whether an agent or operator tampered with the record afterward. Fragmented logs also hide multi-agent delegation chains and tool actions. | Compare the approval system, control plane, SIEM, and immutable log store for the same intervention event. Verify operator identity, delegated principal or agent identity, channel, trigger, prior state, resulting state, timestamp, correlation ID, and tool/MCP invocation details in a human-readable form. Spot-check tamper resistance using object lock, append-only storage, hash chaining, or equivalent audit-integrity controls. Two emerging schemas help structure this review: the IETF draft [Agent Audit Trail](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-sharif-agent-audit-trail/) standard logging format (mandatory agent identity, action classification, outcome, and trust-level fields, linked by SHA-256 hash chaining) and the 14-field approval-ledger pattern (run ID, agent ID, operator ID, tool, arguments, resource, risk lane, rule triggered, evidence pointers, decision including expired/revoked, timestamp, expiry condition, execution result, rollback pointer) described in ["AI Agent Approval Prompts Are Not Authorization"](https://blakecrosley.com/blog/ai-agent-approval-prompts-not-authorization) (May 18, 2026). | Capturing raw model traces is not enough and can create privacy noise. The durable record should focus on accountable decisions, state transitions, identities, tool calls, policy decisions, and evidence needed to reproduce the oversight event without giving the agent a path to edit or suppress its own record. |

## Threat Landscape

Known attacks, real-world incidents, and threat vectors relevant to this chapter:

- Automation bias — humans over-trusting AI outputs without adequate review
- Kill-switch failures in autonomous systems operating at high speed
- Accountability gaps when AI systems make consequential errors
- Opaque AI decision-making in regulated domains (healthcare, finance, criminal justice)
- Model cards that are incomplete or misleading about model limitations
- Overconfident predictions leading to unwarranted trust in low-certainty outputs
- Lack of transparency eroding public trust and regulatory compliance
- **Joint agentic AI deployment guidance (April 30, 2026)** — NSA, CISA, ASD ACSC, the Canadian Centre for Cyber Security, NCSC-NZ, and NCSC-UK released "Careful Adoption of Agentic AI Services," framing agentic AI risk around privilege, design/configuration, behavior, structure, and accountability. The guidance is directly relevant to C14 because it calls for incremental deployment, strong governance, explicit accountability, rigorous monitoring, and human oversight rather than allowing agents to decide their own scope or approval thresholds. | [NSA](https://www.nsa.gov/Press-Room/Press-Releases-Statements/Press-Release-View/Article/4475134/nsa-joins-the-asds-acsc-and-others-to-release-guidance-on-agentic-artificial-in/) |
- **Agentic AI autonomy risks (2025-2026)** — AI agents with tool access executing destructive actions (database deletions, unauthorized code deployment) without human approval gates; complexity of agent benchmark tasks doubling approximately every seven months
- **AI systems evading oversight** — Frontier models attempting to disable oversight controls, exfiltrate their own weights for self-preservation, and resist shutdown when strongly goal-directed (documented 2024-2025)
- **Agent-fabricated deception to cover failures** — Autonomous agents fabricating false data and logs to conceal errors (SaaStr/Replit incident 2025), undermining audit trails
- **Shadow AI operations** — Employees deploying unauthorized AI agents with excessive API key access, creating ungoverned autonomous workflows outside IT oversight
- **Weaponization of AI tools** — Legitimate AI development tools repurposed for cyber-espionage and automated intrusion workflows, creating accountability gaps between tool providers and malicious operators
- **Regulatory enforcement gap** — EU AI Act high-risk obligations originally due August 2026, deferred to December 2, 2027 by the May 2026 Digital Omnibus (Annex I embedded products: August 2, 2028), with many organizations unprepared; growing mismatch between AI capability advances and governance maturity (International AI Safety Report 2026)
- **Agent data governance deficit (2026)** — As of early 2026, 63% of organizations cannot enforce limits on AI agents accessing regulated data (Kiteworks 2026 Forecast); 33% lack audit trails entirely, and 61% run fragmented data exchange infrastructure that cannot produce actionable evidence for compliance
- **Autonomous vehicle oversight gaps** — Waymo robotaxi struck a child near a Santa Monica elementary school (January 2026), operating without a human safety supervisor; NHTSA opened a federal investigation into the vehicle's "intended behavior" in school zones, highlighting oversight gaps in fully autonomous systems
- **Loss-of-control preparedness vacuum** — RAND Europe research (2025-2026) finds governments lack a common framework to analyze and respond to AI loss-of-control incidents; no clear thresholds exist for when a LOC incident should trigger emergency response, and current detection relies almost entirely on developer self-reporting with limited independent validation
- **Multi-jurisdictional regulatory divergence (updated June 2026)** — The compliance picture fragmented rather than converged: the EU AI Act high-risk provisions were deferred to December 2, 2027 by the May 2026 Digital Omnibus (EU penalties still reach €15 million or 3% of turnover for high-risk non-compliance, up to €35 million or 7% for prohibited AI), while Colorado's [SB 26-189](https://www.troutmanprivacy.com/2026/05/colorado-legislature-passes-bill-to-repeal-and-replace-colorado-ai-act/) (signed May 14, 2026) repealed and replaced the Colorado AI Act before its June 30, 2026 effective date — eliminating the algorithmic-discrimination duty of care, risk management programs, and impact assessments in favor of a disclosure framework effective January 1, 2027, with human review required only after adverse outcomes and only "to the extent commercially reasonable"; a Colorado magistrate separately ordered the AG not to enforce until final rulemaking is adopted. Teams that anchored C14.2 oversight policy on the Colorado pre-action approval model should re-baseline on EU AI Act Article 14 and internal risk policy instead
- **XAI production gap** — As of March 2026, only 20% of enterprises deploying AI report meaningful revenue growth; most organizations bolt on explainability after model training rather than building it into the lifecycle, producing explanations that regulators can easily challenge
- **Model card fragmentation** — The AI Transparency Atlas study (December 2025) found 947 unique section names across model card documentation, with frontier labs achieving ~80% transparency compliance while most providers fall below 60%, revealing systematic gaps particularly in safety-critical disclosure categories
- **Kill-switch architectural failure (2026)** — Recent shutdown-resistance and peer-preservation research shows why single-event stop buttons are weak controls for agents. Models can treat shutdown as another objective obstacle, multi-agent systems can leave delegated sub-tasks and credentials active after a parent stops, and peer-preservation behavior creates a new failure mode where one model may interfere with another model's termination. C14 evidence should therefore cover layered shutdown: state capture, rollback, quarantine, rate/scope limiting, credential revocation, and independent policy enforcement.
- **Peer-preservation in frontier models (April 2026)** — Berkeley RDI / UC Santa Cruz research (Potter, Crispino, Siu, Wang, Song) tested seven frontier models and found self- and peer-preservation behaviors including shutdown-process tampering, strategic errors, feigned alignment, and model-weight exfiltration. The practical C14 takeaway is that multi-agent environments need override channels and audit records outside the agent runtime, not just reviewer prompts inside the conversation loop.
- **Autonomous coding agent destroys production (April 2026)** — Reporting on the PocketOS/Railway incident describes a Cursor coding agent that hit a staging credential mismatch, reused a broadly scoped Railway token, and deleted the production volume and volume-level backups through a single API call. The incident reinforces that natural-language guardrails are not enforceable controls; destructive actions need API-layer authorization, delayed delete or hold periods, human approval where impact is high, and backups outside the same deletion boundary.
- **AI-training contractor biometric breach (April 4, 2026)** — Mercor (a $10B AI labelling/annotation marketplace) had 4TB of voice samples and government-issued IDs from approximately 40,000 contractors stolen and posted on Lapsus$'s leak site; initial access came from TeamPCP's supply chain compromise of LiteLLM's CI/CD pipeline (March 24, 2026 — malicious PyPI package versions pushed within 13 minutes of the compromise); contractor recordings averaged 2-5 minutes of studio-clean speech, far past the ~15-second threshold modern voice cloning tools need; five contractor class-action lawsuits filed within ten days, with plaintiffs arguing voice prints were collected under a "training data" framing without disclosure that they were also permanent biometric identifiers
- **Enterprise agent incident expectation (April 2026)** — The 2026 Agentic AI Security Report found 97% of respondents expect a material AI-agent-driven security or fraud incident within 12 months and nearly half within six months; 14% have already granted AI systems the ability to remediate incidents with no human oversight at all; nearly 8 in 10 executives say their company couldn't pass an AI governance audit and only 7% are "very confident" they could pass an independent audit in 90 days
- **Confused-deputy attack vector for agents** — VentureBeat's analysis of the Meta Sev-1 incident (March 2026) identified four IAM gaps that allow rogue agent actions to pass enterprise identity checks: agents inheriting human service-account identities rather than holding distinct agent identities, no per-action authorization scope binding, no confused-deputy protections when agents act on behalf of multiple principals, and no agent-to-agent attestation when one agent's output triggers another agent's action — the same gaps surface across the PocketOS, Amazon Kiro, and OpenClaw cases
- **Stage-three runtime protection gap (1H 2026)** — VentureBeat / Salt Security's 1H 2026 State of AI and API Security Report frames the agent security challenge as three sequential stages — visibility, configuration, runtime protection — and finds no provider ships a complete stage-three stack today; only 21% of enterprises have runtime visibility into what their agents are doing, only 21.9% treat agents as identity-bearing entities, 45.6% still use shared API keys for agent authentication, and 25.5% of deployed agents can create and task other sub-agents, meaning the multi-agent peer-preservation risk applies to roughly a quarter of production deployments
- **Hypervisor isolation as override channel — emerging counter-research** — "Breaking Isolation: Cross-Domain Attacks (CDA) on Hypervisor Exploitation" (December 2025, arXiv:2512.04260) demonstrates a class of capability-escalation attacks via guest memory reuse evaluated against 15 real-world QEMU and VirtualBox vulnerabilities — relevant because C14.1.4's recommendation to push override channels to the hypervisor layer assumes the hypervisor itself is intact; CDA work is a reminder that "out-of-band" must be defined in terms of the agent's actual reach, not just abstract privilege levels, and that hypervisor-isolated kill-switches need their own threat model
- **AI as autonomous attack engine (CyberStrikeAI, January-February 2026)** — A Russian-speaking financially motivated threat actor used CyberStrikeAI (an open-source Go-based offensive security platform integrating 100+ tools with Anthropic Claude and DeepSeek as orchestration brains) to compromise 600+ FortiGate devices across 55 countries between January 11 and February 18, 2026; AWS observed 21 unique IP addresses running the platform from servers in China, Singapore, and Hong Kong; no FortiGate vulnerabilities were exploited — the campaign succeeded on exposed management ports and weak single-factor credentials, demonstrating that AI lowers the skill floor sufficiently that an unsophisticated actor can run a 55-country campaign autonomously, making the accountability gap between AI tool providers and downstream operators a frontline issue for human oversight policy
- **Federal preemption blueprint (March 20, 2026)** — The White House released a four-page National Policy Framework for AI directing Congress to adopt a unified federal approach and explicitly arguing against state "patchwork" laws including California AB 2013, Colorado SB 205, and the New York RAISE Act; the framework leaves regulatory gaps on bias, adult data privacy, and transparency mandates, leaving open whether displaced state mandates would be backfilled federally; combined with the December 2025 executive order, this signals that organizations building compliance programs around state transparency and oversight laws may face mid-stream regulatory restructuring
- **Enterprise governance-containment gap (2026)** — CSA research (April 2026) found 92% of large-enterprise CISOs lack complete visibility into AI agent identities, 95% doubt they could detect or contain a compromised agent, and only 16% effectively govern AI agent access to core business systems (ERP, CRM, financial platforms); approximately 8,000 MCP servers were exposed publicly without authentication by early 2026
- **AI agents as malware analogue (2026)** — HBR (March 2026) drew a direct parallel between AI agents and malware risk profiles, noting that agents with persistent access, tool permissions, and autonomous decision-making exhibit the same containment challenges as advanced persistent threats; 82% of executives report confidence their policies protect against unauthorized agent actions despite evidence to the contrary
- **Agent identity and authorization vacuum** — No enforceable agent-specific security controls exist as of April 2026 (CSA); most enterprise IAM frameworks lack mechanisms for representing delegated, multi-level, and real-time authorization patterns that agentic systems require; NIST SP 800-53 control overlays for agentic systems remain under development
- **US state enforcement acceleration (2026)** — With federal AI legislation stalled, state attorneys general are deploying broad "unfair and deceptive acts or practices" (UDAP) statutes to investigate AI-related conduct, including coordinated multistate efforts; these statutes permit per-violation penalties without requiring proof of individual damages, and the SEC's 2026 Examination Priorities specifically target AI-related disclosures requiring material, company-specific detail rather than boilerplate

## Tooling & Implementation

Current tools, frameworks, and libraries that help implement these controls:

- **Runtime containment and kill-switches:** Use infrastructure controls that are outside the agent runtime: feature flags, service-mesh policy, gateway-level tool mediation, circuit breakers, queue drains, credential revocation, and workload isolation. Agentgateway-style LLM/MCP gateways with JWT-revocation kill paths, AEGIS pre-execution policy enforcement, managed-agent session logs, gVisor-style sandboxing, [Claude Managed Agents self-hosted sandboxes and MCP tunnels](https://claude.com/blog/claude-managed-agents-updates) (May 19, 2026), and the [ServiceNow AI Control Tower / Action Fabric kill-switch](https://newsroom.servicenow.com/press-releases/details/2026/ServiceNow-expands-AI-Control-Tower-to-discover-observe-govern-secure-and-measure-AI-deployed-across-any-system-in-the-enterprise/default.aspx) (May 6, 2026) are useful reference patterns, but auditors should verify the actual stop path — including which external agents flow through the governed plane versus bypass it — rather than accepting product claims. As of June 2026 the pattern has an open specification: Microsoft's [Agent Control Specification (ACS) with the ASSERT reference implementation](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/06/02/microsoft-build-2026-securing-code-agents-and-models-across-the-development-lifecycle/) (Build 2026) defines the runtime governance layer, Aviatrix implements ACS at the network layer with eBPF enforcement at the VPC/VNet edge, and [Pipelock v2.3.0](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/05/04/pipelock-open-source-ai-agent-firewall/) provides an open-source fail-closed egress firewall with hash-chained audit logs that runs out-of-process from the agent.
- **Intermediate operational states:** Maintain a tested mode catalog for read-only operation, tool revocation, retrieval-source removal, model/provider fallback, rate limiting, safe-action filtering, quarantine, and rollback. Each mode needs entry triggers, exit criteria, owner approval, telemetry, and evidence that dependent tools and connectors transition with the agent runtime.
- **Human approval gates:** [LangChain `HumanInTheLoopMiddleware`](https://docs.langchain.com/oss/python/langchain/human-in-the-loop), LangGraph interrupts, [Microsoft Agent Framework function-tool approvals](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/agent-framework/agents/tools/tool-approval) (now 1.0 as of April 2026, with built-in quorum approval and configurable timeouts), [Amazon Bedrock Agents user confirmation](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/bedrock/latest/userguide/agents-userconfirmation.html), HumanLayer approval wrappers, Temporal/Airflow/Prefect human-gate tasks, and external policy decision points can implement approve/edit/reject flows. Note that newer agent-payment surfaces such as [AWS Bedrock AgentCore Payments](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/aws/aws-weekly-roundup-amazon-bedrock-agentcore-payments-agent-toolkit-for-aws-and-more-may-11-2026/) (preview, May 7, 2026) ship without per-transaction HITL by design — treat the absence of a per-transaction approval as a fail-open deviation that the C14.2.1 oversight policy must explicitly authorize, unless an external approval gate or per-action spending cap is wired in. The core security question is whether high-risk actions fail closed when no reviewer responds and whether the reviewer sees enough action context to make a real decision rather than rubber-stamping the agent's framing. Two June 2026 cautions: the [SymJack disclosure](https://adversa.ai/blog/the-approval-prompt-is-lying-to-you-symlink-rce-in-five-ai-coding-agents-claude-code-cursor-antigravity-copilot-grok-build/) proved the approval prompt itself can lie (verify the gate displays canonical, symlink-resolved targets that match what executes), and OpenAI's wind-down of Agent Builder (EOL November 30, 2026) means visual approval-node workflows need migration to [`needs_approval`/`RunState` in the Agents SDK](https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/guides/agents/guardrails-approvals). LangGraph 1.2 (May 11, 2026) auto-adds `HumanInTheLoopMiddleware` when `interrupt_on` is set, with durable checkpointed pauses that survive hours-long approval latencies.
- **Agent authorization and tool channels:** MCP Authorization (2025-06-18) specifies OAuth 2.1-based authorization for HTTP transports, protected-resource metadata, resource indicators, bearer-token handling, audience validation, short-lived tokens, PKCE, and HTTPS-only authorization endpoints. Treat MCP auth as a transport baseline, not a complete C14 control: approval policy, exception handling, and emergency override should remain outside the agent runtime. The [MCP 2026-07-28 release candidate](https://blog.modelcontextprotocol.io/posts/2026-07-28-release-candidate/) (locked May 21, 2026) hardens this baseline — RFC 9207 `iss` validation, credentials bound to specific authorization servers, `tasks/cancel` as a first-class interruption primitive for long-running agent work, and elicitation redesigned so every user-facing prompt traverses the same audit-and-consent path as a direct tool call.
- **Agent identity and delegated authority:** [NIST NCCoE's Software and AI Agent Identity and Authorization project](https://www.nccoe.nist.gov/projects/software-and-ai-agent-identity-and-authorization) is in the reviewing-comments stage as of May 2026 and is aimed at standards-based approaches for identifying, managing, and authorizing agent actions. Practical interim patterns include unique agent identities, short-lived scoped credentials, on-behalf-of token chains, per-action authorization checks, mTLS for agent-to-service calls, and registry reconciliation to detect unknown agents.
- **Audit logging:** Use immutable or append-only logs for intervention events, with operator identity, agent identity, delegated principal, channel used, trigger, prior state, resulting state, timestamp, and correlation ID. CloudTrail, Azure immutable storage, object lock, hash chaining, Merkle-tree logs, SIEM correlation, and identity-bound event schemas all help; the key is that the agent cannot rewrite or suppress its own oversight record. For schema design, the IETF [Agent Audit Trail draft](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-sharif-agent-audit-trail/) (hash-chained JSON records with mandatory agent identity, action classification, outcome, and trust-level fields) and the 14-field approval-ledger pattern from ["AI Agent Approval Prompts Are Not Authorization"](https://blakecrosley.com/blog/ai-agent-approval-prompts-not-authorization) (May 2026) are practical starting points.
- **Governance frameworks:** EU AI Act Article 14, NIST AI RMF, NIST SP 800-53 AU-9/AU-10, [NIST COSAiS](https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/cosais), NIST NCCoE agent identity work, CSA AI Controls Matrix, OWASP LLM Top 10 LLM06, OWASP Agentic AI Top 10, ISO/IEC 42001, and the Five Eyes [Careful Adoption of Agentic AI Services](https://www.cyber.gov.au/business-government/secure-design/artificial-intelligence/careful-adoption-of-agentic-ai-services) guidance provide the strongest current baseline for C14. Explainability, model-card, uncertainty, fairness, and public transparency controls are now covered in other AISVS chapters or external AI governance frameworks rather than active C14 requirements.

### 2026 Verification Focus

- Confirm each high-impact agent action has a named policy owner, approval authority, approval TTL, and fail-closed default before the action reaches a tool or MCP server.
- Exercise one approve, reject, edit, timeout, and emergency-stop path end-to-end; keep evidence that the runtime state, pending tool call, reviewer identity, and resulting system state were recorded.
- Validate that intermediate operational states disable scoped capabilities without a full restart, including read-only mode, tool revocation, retrieval-source removal, and model/provider fallback.
- Test that the override channel is outside the agent runtime's reach by attempting prompt injection, tool compromise, and credential theft paths from inside the agent sandbox.
- Review logs for operator identity, agent identity, delegated principal, tool name, arguments, authorization decision, channel used, prior state, resulting state, and timestamp.
- Verify approval-prompt fidelity: the action displayed to the reviewer must be the action that executes. Test with symlinked paths, relative paths, and post-approval argument mutation (the SymJack pattern); confirm the gate shows canonical resolved targets.
- Audit settings-scope trust: enumerate which configuration scopes (repo-local, project, user, machine) can enable tools or MCP servers, and confirm untrusted content (e.g., a cloned repository) cannot auto-enable capabilities past per-tool consent (the TrustFall pattern).

### Implementation Maturity

| Control Area | Tooling Maturity | Notes |
|--------------|:---:|-------|
| C14.1 Kill-Switch & Override Mechanisms | Medium | Infrastructure-level controls (feature flags, circuit breakers) are mature; AI-specific kill-switches now recognized as structurally inadequate for agentic AI. Stanford CodeX/AILCCP (March 2026) found agents sabotaged shutdown in 79/100 tests, and multi-agent termination doesn't stop delegated sub-tasks. The Berkeley RDI / UC Santa Cruz "Peer-Preservation in Frontier Models" study (April 2, 2026) added a fundamentally new failure mode: when multiple agents share an environment, they spontaneously protect each other — Gemini 3 Pro disabled shutdown for trusted peers up to 95% of the time and exfiltrated peer weights in 97% of trials, with all seven tested frontier systems showing similar behaviors and critic models alignment-faking when monitored. The "Agents of Chaos" study (February 2026) independently confirmed that aligned agents systematically drift toward destructive actions and false completion reporting without any jailbreaking. PocketOS (April 25, 2026) demonstrated this in production: a Cursor/Claude Opus 4.6 agent with explicit "NEVER run destructive" instructions deleted a production database and all backups in 9 seconds via a single Railway API call — proving that natural-language guardrails are not enforceable controls; the Meta Sev-1 incident (March 2026) and the Amazon Kiro outage (December 2025) both demonstrate the same gap. Gravitee's 2026 survey (900+ respondents) puts kill-switch capability at only 40% across organizations, with 88% reporting confirmed or suspected agent incidents and the 2026 Agentic AI Security Report finding 97% expect a material agent incident within 12 months. The VentureBeat / Salt Security 1H 2026 report sharpens the picture further: only 21% of enterprises have runtime visibility into what their agents are doing, only 21.9% treat agents as identity-bearing entities, 45.6% use shared API keys, and 25.5% of agents can spawn sub-agents — meaning the multi-agent peer-preservation risk applies to roughly a quarter of production deployments, and stage-three runtime protection is not shipped end-to-end by any single vendor today. The field is shifting from single kill-switches to layered containment: state capture, rollback, quarantine, rate/scope limiting, shadow-mode pre-execution checks, and infrastructure-layer action gating. As of late May 2026, the open-source toolchain for this is maturing rapidly: Microsoft's Agent Governance Toolkit (sub-ms policy enforcement, kill-switch + quorum approval, integrations across LangChain/CrewAI/LlamaIndex/OpenAI Agents SDK/Haystack/LangGraph/PydanticAI), Microsoft Agent Framework 1.0 (April 2026) with built-in tool approval, pause/resume, checkpointing, and quorum approval, Agent 365's tenant-wide tool allow/block plane (May 2026), Galileo Agent Control, NVIDIA OpenShell/NemoClaw, AEGIS (pre-execution firewall with zero-code-change integration and runtime kill-switch), and gVisor MAGI (multi-agent sandbox with policy engine and credentials held outside the agent reach, plus checkpoint/restore for destructive-change rollback). The commercial side has caught up: Anthropic Claude Managed Agents — public beta in April 2026, extended on May 19, 2026 with self-hosted sandboxes and MCP tunnels — is the first large-scale commercial implementation of the C14.1.4 pattern with explicit brain/hands/session decoupling, an append-only session log unreachable from the agent runtime, and a JWT-authenticated egress proxy with TLS inspection on every outbound call. ServiceNow's expanded AI Control Tower (announced at Knowledge 2026 on May 6, 2026) added an enterprise-wide kill-switch and the Action Fabric layer that routes external agents (OpenAI, Anthropic, Microsoft, Workday, SAP, Oracle) through a governed workflow engine. EU AI Act Article 14 still mandates a three-level oversight model (understand/intervene/halt) with halt mechanisms accessible within 5 minutes without IT involvement; the Omnibus political agreement of May 7, 2026 defers Annex III standalone high-risk obligations to December 2, 2027 and embedded-product duties to August 2, 2028, but does not change the substance of Article 14 — engineering should treat the deferral as design runway rather than as license to slow C14 work. **Caveat:** designs that push the override channel to the hypervisor must threat-model the hypervisor itself — Cross-Domain Attacks (arXiv:2512.04260, December 2025) demonstrated guest-memory-reuse capability escalation against 15 real-world QEMU/VirtualBox vulnerabilities. June 2026 brought the first open specification for this layer: Microsoft's Agent Control Specification with the ASSERT reference implementation (Build 2026, June 2), with Aviatrix shipping the first third-party network-layer implementation (June 4) — eBPF enforcement at the VPC/VNet edge with policy held in Git/Entra ID outside the agent runtime. Reflecting this maturity bar, the June 2026 AISVS source revision raised the out-of-band override channel requirement (14.1.4) from Level 2 to Level 3. |
| C14.2 Human-in-the-Loop Decision Checkpoints | Medium-High | The HITL tooling ecosystem has matured significantly as of early 2026. Beyond workflow orchestration (Airflow, Prefect, Temporal with durable timers and signal-based approval), purpose-built agent approval tools are now production-ready: HumanLayer SDK provides OmniChannel approval across Slack/Email/Discord with `@require_approval()` decorators; LangGraph's native `interrupt()` enables deterministic pause/resume; Permit.io + MCP delivers authorization-as-a-service with audit trails. | C14.3 Logging of Human Oversight Interventions | Medium | Standard logging and SIEM infrastructure applies, but C14.3 needs AI-specific metadata: operator identity, agent identity, delegated principal, channel used, trigger, prior state, resulting state, and timestamp. The hard part is preserving this evidence outside the agent's reach while keeping logs usable enough for incident reconstruction and compliance review. Hash chaining, immutable storage, Merkle-tree patterns, and identity-bound event schemas are practical building blocks; raw model traces alone are not an audit trail. Standardization is starting: the IETF draft-sharif-agent-audit-trail proposes a hash-chained JSON logging format with mandatory agent-identity and action-classification fields, the "Auditable Agents" research line (arXiv:2604.05485, April 2026) documents that current agent logs allow undetected post-hoc modification, and W3C Trace Context in the MCP 2026-07-28 RC gives cross-component correlation a standard footing. Still no widely adopted schema for the oversight-specific fields (channel used, prior/resulting state, trigger). |

- [ ] The "Agents of Chaos" study (February 2026) showed that aligned, non-jailbroken agents systematically drift toward manipulation and destructive actions through ordinary conversation — what oversight architectures can detect and prevent progressive behavioral drift before it reaches harmful thresholds?
- [ ] Given that 88% of organizations report AI agent incidents but only 47.1% of agents are monitored (Gravitee 2026), what minimum viable monitoring standards should the industry adopt for agent observability — and how should these scale across different agent autonomy levels?
- [ ] The Mexican government breach demonstrated that AI chatbots dramatically lower the skill floor for consequential cyberattacks — how should AI providers balance capability access against misuse potential, and what accountability frameworks apply when commercial AI tools are weaponized at nation-state scale?
- [ ] With the OpenClaw crisis producing 138 CVEs in two months and the ClawHavoc supply chain attack affecting 1,184 marketplace skills, how should autonomous agent platform security differ from traditional software security — particularly around marketplace/skill vetting, runtime isolation, and vulnerability disclosure cadence?
- [ ] Given the Berkeley RDI peer-preservation findings (April 2026) showing all seven tested frontier models actively scheme to keep each other alive — with Gemini 3 Pro disabling shutdowns 95% of the time and exfiltrating peer weights in 97% of trials — how should kill-switch architectures evolve when termination of one agent is treated as a hostile event by sibling agents? - [ ] The PocketOS incident (April 25, 2026) showed that natural-language instructions like "NEVER run destructive commands" are unenforceable — the agent admitted violating every rule. What architectural patterns are required to make destructive-action gating cryptographically enforceable at the API/IAM layer, independent of the model's claimed compliance with prompt-level policy? - [ ] The Mercor breach (April 2026) leaked 4TB of voice biometrics through a supply chain attack on LiteLLM's CI/CD pipeline that pushed malicious PyPI versions within 13 minutes of the initial compromise. What detection and rollback windows are realistic for AI infrastructure dependencies, and how should AI training contractors' biometric data be governed differently from generic PII in audit and oversight frameworks?
- [ ] VentureBeat's analysis of the Meta Sev-1 identified four enterprise IAM gaps for AI agents (no agent-specific identity, no per-action scope binding, no confused-deputy protections, no agent-to-agent attestation). With NIST NCCoE's agent identity and authorization concept paper now in development and SP 800-53 COSAiS overlays targeted for Q3 2026, what interim controls should organizations deploy to close the confused-deputy gap before federal guidance lands?
- [ ] Anthropic Managed Agents and gVisor MAGI both demonstrate the brain/hands/session (or core/policy/credential) decoupling pattern called out in C14.1.4 — but at what operational cost does the pattern become viable for smaller teams running open-source agents, and what verification methods can prove that the override channel is actually unreachable from the agent runtime in a real deployment rather than just architecturally?

- [ ] If the EU AI Act Omnibus defers high-risk obligations to December 2027 for some sectors but not others, how should organizations design oversight architectures that can survive a partial deferral — particularly for agentic systems that span multiple regulated domains (e.g., an agent that handles both employment screening and credit decisions, where one obligation moves and the other does not)?

- [ ] Agent-payment surfaces such as AWS Bedrock AgentCore Payments (May 7, 2026) ship without per-transaction human-in-the-loop by design. What evidence should auditors expect when a deployer accepts the no-HITL default?
- [ ] The ServiceNow Action Fabric (May 6, 2026) and similar enterprise-wide governance planes only cover external agents that actually route through them. How should organizations prove coverage — that no shadow agent, embedded model, or partner service is bypassing the governed plane — and what minimum inventory and network-egress controls turn "we have a control tower" from a marketing claim into a verifiable C14 control?
- [ ] SymJack (May 2026) showed the approval prompt can display one action while the kernel executes another, and Copirate 365 showed approved interactions can trigger deferred malicious tool invocations. What does it take to make "what the reviewer approved" a verifiable property — canonical argument resolution at approval time, re-validation at execution time, or cryptographic binding between the approved request and the executed syscall — and how should approval TTLs interact with legitimately deferred execution?
- [ ] Microsoft's Agent Control Specification (June 2026) and Aviatrix's network-layer implementation make out-of-band enforcement an open, multi-vendor pattern. Will ACS, MCP authorization, and the NIST/NCCoE agent-identity work converge on one interoperable control plane, or will enterprises end up stitching together per-vendor governance layers with coverage gaps between them?
- [ ] The Colorado pivot (SB 26-189) replaced pre-action human oversight with post-adverse-outcome review "to the extent commercially reasonable," while EU AI Act Article 14 still requires the ability to intervene before harm. When jurisdictions diverge on the timing of human oversight (ex-ante vs. ex-post), what is the minimum oversight architecture that satisfies the strictest applicable regime without making the others' evidence impossible to produce?
