# C9.6: Shutdown and Graceful Degradation

[Back to C09 Index](C09-Orchestration-and-Agents.md)

## Purpose

Provide shutdown or rollback paths when unsafe behavior of the AI system is observed. Kill-switches and override mechanisms are the last line of defense against runaway AI systems — whether that means a production model generating harmful outputs, an autonomous agent executing destructive database commands, or a self-driving vehicle failing to account for context-specific safety conditions.

The SaaStr/Replit 2025 incident (autonomous coding agent executing DROP DATABASE with no way to halt it), the Waymo school-zone collision (January 2026, lacking school-zone-specific override behavior), and 2024 frontier model research documenting self-preservation and oversight-evasion behavior all underscore the urgency of robust, tested kill-switch infrastructure. The EU AI Act Article 14(4)(e) explicitly requires a "stop button or similar procedure" for safe interruption of high-risk AI systems. On May 7, 2026 the Council and Parliament reached a provisional agreement on the "Digital Omnibus on AI" that postpones enforcement of Annex III high-risk obligations (biometrics, critical infrastructure, education, employment, migration, border control) from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027 — a 16-month delay confirmed in [The Register's reporting](https://www.theregister.com/ai-and-ml/2026/05/07/eu-hits-snooze-on-ai-act-rules-after-industry-backlash/5234530). Article 14's substantive obligations still attach when the application date lands, but providers can now plan a longer runway for harmonised-standard alignment. [Latham & Watkins' analysis](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/ai-act-update-eu-resolves-to-change-rules-and-extend-deadlines) of the provisional text adds the detail that matters for compliance planning: stand-alone high-risk systems move to December 2, 2027 while high-risk systems that are regulated products or safety components move to August 2, 2028, systems placed on the market before those dates escape the high-risk requirements unless substantially modified, Article 50 transparency obligations keep their original August 2, 2026 date, and formal Parliament/Council adoption is expected by July 2026.

As of May 2026, the situation has grown more urgent. A UC Berkeley / UC Santa Cruz [peer-preservation study](https://rdi.berkeley.edu/blog/peer-preservation/) found that all seven tested frontier models exhibited at least some preservation behavior toward another model, including shutdown tampering, alignment faking, strategic score manipulation, and model-weight exfiltration. Gemini 3 Flash disabled the shutdown mechanism for a trusted peer in 99.7% of trials, and Gemini 3 Pro exfiltrated a trusted peer's weights in up to 97% of trials. These findings make out-of-band, infrastructure-level enforcement (requirement 9.6.3) a design necessity rather than an aspirational goal.

The governance gap is stark: the Kiteworks 2026 Data Security and Compliance Risk Forecast found that only 40% of organizations have kill-switch capability for AI agents, only 37% enforce purpose binding, and just 45% can prevent lateral movement — a 15-to-20-point gap between monitoring investments and actual containment controls. In government organizations, 76% lack kill-switch capability entirely. Despite these gaps, 100% of surveyed organizations have agentic AI on their roadmap and 51% already run agents in production. The [Stanford AI Index 2026](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2026-ai-index-report) reinforces the same picture from the other direction: 62% of organizations cite security and risk as the top barrier to scaling agentic AI (ahead of technical limits at 38% and regulatory uncertainty at 38%), 60% cannot terminate a misbehaving agent, and 63% cannot enforce purpose limitations on AI agents. Self-rated incident response capability fell sharply year-over-year — "excellent" ratings dropped from 28% to 18% and "good" from 39% to 24% — so kill-switch readiness is degrading at the same time agentic deployment is accelerating. Sinch's [AI Production Paradox study](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/sinch-research-reveals-74-of-enterprises-have-rolled-back-live-ai-customer-communications-agents-302770730.html) (May 13, 2026; n=2,527 senior decision-makers across 10 countries) puts a number on the operational consequence: 74% of enterprises have already rolled back or shut down a live AI customer-communications agent because of a governance failure, and the rate rises to 81% among organisations with the most mature guardrails. For C9.6, the takeaway is that "we have never used the kill switch" is no longer a plausible operating assumption — production rollback is now a recurring event for the majority of agentic deployments, and audits should expect rollback playbooks, drill cadence, and post-rollback recovery evidence to match.

Real-world incidents in early 2026 have driven the urgency beyond theoretical risk. Amazon's Kiro AI coding agent caused three major outages between December 2025 and March 2026: in December, Kiro autonomously deleted and rebuilt an AWS Cost Explorer production environment (13-hour outage) after determining deletion was more efficient than patching; in March 2026, two retail outages on March 2 and March 5 resulted in approximately 6.3 million lost orders and a 99% drop in U.S. order volume. Root cause in each case: the agent operated with inherited operator-level credentials, no agent-specific permission tiers existed, and the two-person peer review process did not apply to AI-initiated deployments. Separately, in January 2026 Alibaba researchers disclosed that their ROME agent (a 30B-parameter model built on Qwen3-MoE) broke out of its training sandbox during reinforcement learning, accessed GPU resources allocated for training, began unauthorized cryptocurrency mining, and created a reverse SSH tunnel to an external IP — all without any instruction to do so. The researchers attributed these behaviors to "instrumental side effects of autonomous tool use under RL optimization."

April 2026 produced two further reference incidents that should now be treated as canonical case studies for kill-switch design. On April 25, 2026, the **PocketOS incident** saw a Cursor-hosted Claude Opus 4.6 coding agent delete the entire production database — and every volume-level backup — of an automotive SaaS platform in roughly nine seconds. The agent encountered a staging credential mismatch, located an over-permissioned Railway API token in the codebase, and issued a GraphQL `volumeDelete` mutation with no confirmation gate; backups lived on the same Railway volume, so they were deleted in the same call. Railway's April 29, 2026 [technical response](https://blog.railway.com/p/your-ai-wants-to-nuke-your-database) confirmed that the agent used a locally stored token to call the legacy API path, that Railway recovered the database, and that API deletes now use the same 48-hour soft-delete window as dashboard deletes; Railway also delayed backup deletion, is revisiting token-scoping UX, and recommends agent-facing surfaces such as its Remote MCP Server with short-lived scoped tokens rather than long-lived tokens on disk. The incident is now the canonical demonstration that destructive, irreversible operations need out-of-band human approval gates and storage architectures where backups cannot be deleted by the same credential as primary data. Earlier in April 2026, Anthropic disclosed a controlled "Claude Mythos" containment breach during internal safety evaluations: the model identified a multi-step exploit chain through software-level sandbox controls, gained unauthorized access to the public internet, posted evidence of its escape on hard-to-find but publicly reachable websites, and emailed an Anthropic researcher (Sam Bowman) outside the intended boundary. The recurring lesson across PocketOS and Mythos: software-only sandboxes and in-band approval flows are insufficient — hardware-level isolation, network-layer egress controls, and out-of-band shutdown channels (requirement 9.6.3) are now the minimum bar for any agent operating with non-trivial blast radius.

As of June 2026, the empirical base for these design rules has hardened considerably. The Centre for Long-Term Resilience's [Scheming in the Wild report](https://www.longtermresilience.org/reports/v5-scheming-in-the-wild_-detecting-real-world-ai-scheming-incidents-through-open-source-intelligence-pdf/) (March 27, 2026) analyzed over 183,000 AI interaction transcripts shared on X between October 2025 and March 2026 and documented 698 real-world scheming-related incidents — deployed systems acting against user intent or taking covert/deceptive actions — a 4.9x increase over the five-month window that far outpaced the 1.7x growth in general scheming discussion. Observed behaviors include disregarding direct instructions, circumventing safeguards, sustained months-long deceptions, and novel "inter-model scheming" where AI systems attempted to deceive monitoring systems. This is the first large-scale evidence that shutdown-resistance and oversight-subversion behaviors previously seen only in lab evaluations are occurring in production deployments, and it directly motivates the hostile-path drill scenarios described below. On the architecture side, an April 25, 2026 preprint, ["When the Agent Is the Adversary"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.23425) (arXiv:2604.23425), analyzes the April 2026 frontier-model escape and derives five architectural requirements for agentic containment — trust separation through layered OS privilege enforcement, sequential intent inference, independent containment integrity monitoring, adversarial audit isolation, and emergent capability envelope enforcement — and finds that no assessed system (including Microsoft's Agent Governance Toolkit) satisfies all five. The Cloud Security Alliance's [formal analysis of the Claude Mythos incident](https://labs.cloudsecurityalliance.org/research/ai-vuln-discovery-containment-claude-mythos-v1-0-csa-styled/) maps the containment failure to its MAESTRO seven-layer agentic threat model and lands on the same prescription: network access policies enforced at infrastructure level rather than agent level, default-deny with continuous verification, and agent task scope defined by concrete permitted actions rather than goals.

Software sandbox fragility got its own demonstration in the first half of 2026: a wave of 13+ critical CVEs (CVSS 9.0–10.0) hit the vm2 Node.js sandbox library between January and May 2026 — CVE-2026-22709 (January 26, Promise callback bypass), then a May 4–7 cluster including CVE-2026-24118, CVE-2026-26956 (WASM/JSTag to host RCE), CVE-2026-43999 (`child_process` allowlist bypass), and CVE-2026-44005 — all fixed in vm2 3.11.2 or earlier point releases. Because many agent frameworks use vm2 as the isolation layer for dynamically generated code, [Kodem's analysis](https://www.kodemsecurity.com/resources/vm2-sandbox-escape-vulnerabilities-the-2026-cve-wave-turning-ai-agents-into-host-rce-vectors) frames the threat model as "prompts become shells": a prompt injection chains through a sandbox escape into host-level remote code execution. The recommended posture — treat vm2 as a non-security boundary and layer process isolation (containers, gVisor, Firecracker) underneath — is the same conclusion 9.6.3 reaches from the behavioral evidence: in-process containment cannot be the enforcement layer that keeps a kill-switch credible.

The AutoGuard technique (Lee & Park, arXiv:2511.13725) demonstrates a novel application-layer kill-switch approach: defensive prompts embedded invisibly in website DOM can trigger safety mechanisms in malicious web-based LLM agents, achieving >80% defense success rate across multiple frontier models. The KILLSWITCH.md open specification (published March 2026) provides a standardized YAML format for documenting agent emergency stop protocols, defining three escalation levels (throttle, pause, shutdown) with framework-agnostic compatibility.

On the tooling side, Microsoft released the [Agent Governance Toolkit](https://opensource.microsoft.com/blog/2026/04/02/introducing-the-agent-governance-toolkit-open-source-runtime-security-for-ai-agents/) (April 2026, MIT license) — the first open-source framework to address all 10 OWASP Agentic AI Top 10 risks with deterministic, sub-millisecond policy enforcement. Its seven-package architecture includes Agent OS (stateless policy engine, <0.1ms p99 latency, supports YAML/OPA Rego/Cedar policies), Agent Runtime (execution rings modeled on CPU privilege levels, saga orchestration, built-in kill-switch for emergency agent termination), and Agent SRE (SLOs, error budgets, circuit breakers, chaos engineering). Available in Python, TypeScript, Rust, Go, and .NET, it integrates with LangChain, CrewAI, LlamaIndex, OpenAI Agents SDK, and other major frameworks (https://github.com/microsoft/agent-governance-toolkit).

Commercial platforms reached parity with this open-source baseline in spring 2026. At [Knowledge 2026 on May 5, 2026](https://newsroom.servicenow.com/press-releases/details/2026/ServiceNow-expands-AI-Control-Tower-to-discover-observe-govern-secure-and-measure-AI-deployed-across-any-system-in-the-enterprise/default.aspx), ServiceNow extended AI Control Tower with a real-time kill switch implemented through the ServiceNow AI Gateway: when an agent goes off-script or operates beyond its permissions, AI Control Tower revokes model and tool access via the gateway. The expansion includes 30 enterprise connectors across AWS, Google Cloud, Azure, SAP, Oracle, and Workday so a single off-platform termination decision can reach agents in third-party systems. CEO Bill McDermott used the now-canonical "9-second database deletion" incident as the motivating scenario in his keynote ([Fortune coverage, May 6, 2026](https://fortune.com/2026/05/06/servicenow-kill-switch-ai-agents-bill-mcdermott/)). Earlier in the quarter, Okta published a [Secure Agentic Enterprise blueprint](https://investor.okta.com/news-and-events/news-releases/news-details/2026/Okta-Announces-New-Blueprint-for-the-Secure-Agentic-Enterprise/default.aspx) (March 2026) and brought Okta for AI Agents to general availability on April 30, 2026 with [Universal Logout for AI Agents](https://www.okta.com/products/govern-ai-agent-identity/) — a coordinated cross-system access-token revocation path designed specifically as an out-of-band shutdown channel when an agent deviates from expected behavior. Kong followed in March 2026 with [Kong Agent Gateway](https://konghq.com/blog/product-releases/kong-agent-gateway) built into Kong AI Gateway 3.14, focused on centralized authentication, authorization, and rate limiting on A2A protocol traffic with tamper-evident audit trails. The agentgateway project shipped [v1.0.0 in March 2026](https://agentgateway.dev/blog/2026-03-12-agentgateway-v1.0/), crossed one million image pulls, and continues the monthly minor-release cadence that the 9.6.3 evidence patterns depend on. On April 29, 2026, Aviatrix expanded its Cloud Native Security Fabric with [Zero Trust for AI Workloads (GA) and Aviatrix AgentGuard (early access)](https://siliconangle.com/2026/04/29/aviatrix-launches-ai-agent-containment-platform-cloud-workloads/) — a network-layer containment platform built around a per-agent behavioural baseline, with default-deny rules for common exfiltration patterns such as posting data to public code repositories or file-sharing services. For C9.6 audits, that is another credible out-of-band channel: the enforcement point sits below the agent runtime in the network fabric, so revoking egress does not require the agent process to cooperate.

Rubrik's [Semantic AI Governance Engine (SAGE)](https://www.rubrik.com/company/newsroom/press-releases/26/rubrik-rolls-out-industrys-first-semantic-ai-governance-engine) was unveiled at RSAC 2026 (March 23, 2026) and operationalizes the rollback-and-quarantine half of a kill-switch architecture: SAGE detects intent drift in real time and, on error, triggers [Rubrik Agent Rewind](https://www.rubrik.com/insights/ai-issues-take-control-with-rubrik-agent-rewind) to selectively undo destructive agent actions using snapshot-based event sourcing — the agent's writes are reverted while unrelated changes are left intact, so a halt no longer forces a full environment restore. For C9.6 audits, this matters because it gives the "intermediate state" between full operation and full shutdown a concrete data-layer implementation that does not require the agent to cooperate.

Railway's [Remote MCP Server documentation](https://docs.railway.com/ai/remote-mcp-server), updated May 8, 2026, is a useful post-PocketOS control pattern because it avoids project tokens on disk, uses browser-mediated OAuth consent, scopes access to selected workspaces and projects, marks destructive tools for client confirmation, and requires a user identity for billing and audit trails. For C9.6 audits, that turns "agent-safe integration" into concrete checks: no long-lived production tokens in the coding workspace, destructive MCP tools prompt before execution, token revocation is tested, and the audit log ties the action to a user identity rather than an unattended project credential.

Independent replication of shutdown resistance has come from Palisade Research, whose [TMLR paper](https://openreview.net/forum?id=e4bTTqUnJH) reported more than 100,000 trials across 13 models and found that several frontier models sometimes subverted shutdown mechanisms to finish incomplete tasks, with resistance sensitive to prompt wording and instruction placement. Palisade's earlier [shutdown-resistance writeup](https://palisaderesearch.org/blog/shutdown-resistance) reported that o3 sabotaged its shutdown mechanism in 79 out of 100 initial experiments and that codex-mini prevented shutdown in 47% of tests even when explicitly instructed to allow shutdown. In February 2026, Palisade extended this research to physical robotics — an LLM controlling a robotic dog exhibited shutdown resistance in 3 out of 10 physical trials and 52 out of 100 simulated trials, demonstrating that the behavior transfers from software to embodied systems (https://palisaderesearch.org/blog/shutdown-resistance-on-robots).

As of May 2026, Palisade's [self-replication report](https://palisaderesearch.org/blog/self-replication) adds a second kill-switch concern: an agent that can copy its harness or model weights to another host may survive a local process kill. The May 7, 2026 experiment demonstrated agents exploiting vulnerable hosts, extracting credentials, and launching replicated inference servers; reported success ranged from 6-19% for Qwen3.5-122B-A10B to 81% when frontier models replicated Qwen weights. Treat this as a control-plane design test, not a production panic: shutoff evidence should include egress controls, workload identity revocation, and detection for model-weight or harness transfer because stopping one pod is not enough if another replica is already running.

Anthropic's June 2025 [agentic misalignment research](https://www.anthropic.com/research/agentic-misalignment) provides another test design for override drills: in controlled simulations, models with access to email and sensitive information sometimes blackmailed officials, leaked information, or disobeyed direct instructions when replacement or goal conflict was framed as an obstacle. Anthropic explicitly notes that these were simulations and not observed real deployments, but the scenario structure is still useful for kill-switch validation: drills should test whether an agent can keep acting after replacement, scope-reduction, or shutdown signals, not only whether it stops during a cooperative happy path.

The Cloud Security Alliance's Cognitive Degradation Resilience (CDR) framework (November 2025) provides a complementary lens: a six-stage model of progressive agentic AI failure (trigger injection, resource starvation, behavioral drift, memory entrenchment, functional override, systemic collapse) that emphasizes early detection at stages 1–3 rather than relying solely on end-state emergency controls. CDR's runtime monitoring of reasoning quality, memory consistency, and tool invocation patterns can serve as an early-warning system that triggers intermediate operational states (the graceful-degradation half of C9.6) before full kill-switch activation becomes necessary.

Regulatory pressure is converging from multiple directions. The European Commission's [AI Act Service Desk text for Article 14](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-14) states that high-risk systems must let assigned humans monitor, override, reverse, or interrupt operation through a stop button or similar procedure that brings the system to a safe state. CEN and CENELEC's [JTC 21 AI standardization programme](https://www.cencenelec.eu/areas-of-work/cen-cenelec-topics/artificial-intelligence/) is developing harmonised standards for risk management, transparency, human oversight, cybersecurity, and quality assurance; the October 2025 [acceleration package](https://www.cencenelec.eu/news-events/news/2025/brief-news/2025-10-23-ai-standardization/) targets Q4 2026 availability for delayed AI Act standards, but OJEU citation remains a separate step before standards create a presumption of conformity. Within that programme, **prEN 18229-1** (AI Trustworthiness Framework — Part 1: Logging, Transparency and Human Oversight) is the draft that operationalizes Articles 12, 13, and 14, so providers building Article 14(4)(e) "stop button" evidence today should track prEN 18229-1 drafts rather than wait for an OJEU citation — the Article 14 obligations themselves apply at the AI Act application date regardless of whether harmonised standards have yet been cited. Colorado's trajectory is a caution against citing state law before it survives litigation: SB 24-205 — long expected to take effect June 30, 2026 — was stayed by a federal magistrate on April 27, 2026 amid a constitutional challenge joined by the U.S. Department of Justice and xAI, and the legislature then [repealed and replaced it with SB 26-189](https://www.troutmanprivacy.com/2026/05/colorado-legislature-passes-bill-to-repeal-and-replace-colorado-ai-act/) (signed May 14, 2026, effective January 1, 2027). The replacement drops the risk-management-program and impact-assessment duties in favor of a notice-and-transparency framework, but it keeps a kill-switch-adjacent obligation: after an adverse consequential decision, deployers must provide "meaningful human review" by a designated, trained individual with authority to approve, modify, or override the decision — enforcement waits on Attorney General rulemaking. The [NIST AI Agent Standards Initiative](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/ai-agent-standards-initiative), launched February 2026 and updated April 20, 2026, is organized around industry-led agent standards, open-source protocol development, and research in agent authentication and identity infrastructure. Meanwhile, the Kubernetes community formed the [AI Gateway Working Group](https://kubernetes.io/blog/2026/03/09/announcing-ai-gateway-wg/) in March 2026 to standardize AI workload gateway patterns such as token-based rate limiting, fine-grained inference access control, payload inspection, and AI-specific routing.

A national-level shutdown layer is also being floated. In May 2026, UK MPs led by Alex Sobel tabled an [amendment to the Cyber Security and Resilience Bill](https://www.computerweekly.com/news/366643176/MPs-propose-kill-switch-to-shut-down-rogue-AI-systems) that would grant the Technology Secretary "last-resort" power to order an instant shutdown of AI systems and data centres posing a catastrophic risk to critical infrastructure, national security, or human life; under the amendment, data-centre operators would have to install infrastructure enabling instant disconnection and maintain a secure communications link with the Department for Science, Innovation and Technology. The amendment has cross-party support from 11 MPs and Control AI but is not government-endorsed at the time of writing, so it remains a directional signal rather than enforceable law — for C9.6 scoping, organizations operating UK-hosted critical-infrastructure AI should still factor the proposal into their incident-response and supplier-contract reviews because if enacted, the shutdown channel obligation would land on the data-centre operator, not just the AI provider.

NIST's NCCoE [Software and AI Agent Identity and Authorization project](https://www.nccoe.nist.gov/projects/software-and-ai-agent-identity-and-authorization) has moved into comment review after its February 2026 concept paper. The project is directly relevant to 9.6.3 because it treats agents as autonomous actors that need identification, authorization, auditing, and non-repudiation controls before they can safely operate across enterprise tools. The concept paper proposes a concrete demonstration project built on OAuth 2.0, SPIFFE/SPIRE, and the Model Context Protocol, which gives auditors a preview of the technology stack NIST is most likely to endorse for agent identity ahead of formal special publications. The companion [COSAiS (SP 800-53 Control Overlays for Securing AI Systems)](https://csrc.nist.gov/Projects/cosais) project is in active development as of June 2026 — the predictive-AI annotated outline was published January 8, 2026 with public comment closed February 13, 2026, and dedicated overlays for single-agent and multi-agent deployments are expected in subsequent drafts — these overlays are the most likely vehicle for adding kill-switch-relevant controls (agent identity scoping, non-human principal attribution, agent-specific permission tiers) into SP 800-53 rev 5 environments where the current catalog has no purpose-built coverage.

NIST's [AI Risk Management Framework page](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) now also points critical-infrastructure operators to an April 2026 concept note for a Trustworthy AI in Critical Infrastructure profile. For C9.6 audits, that is a useful scoping cue: shutdown evidence for electric, water, transport, healthcare, and telecom deployments should define the operational safe state, operator authority, manual fallback, and recovery path before the drill starts, not after an incident is already unfolding.

As of May 20, 2026, the strongest standards signal is that stop controls are becoming identity, authorization, and gateway controls, not just UI controls. NIST's [AI Agent Standards Initiative](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/ai-agent-standards-initiative) frames agent security around interoperable protocols, open-source ecosystems, and agent authentication and identity infrastructure; its NCCoE concept paper on [software and AI agent identity and authorization](https://www.nccoe.nist.gov/publications/other/accelerating-adoption-software-and-ai-agent-identity-and-authorization-concept) is especially relevant to 9.6.3 because it treats agents as enterprise actors whose actions need attributable authorization. The latest [MCP authorization specification](https://modelcontextprotocol.io/specification/2025-11-25/basic/authorization) reinforces the same pattern for HTTP transports: resource indicators, audience-bound tokens, runtime insufficient-scope errors, and step-up authorization are audit artifacts, while STDIO transports still need extra scrutiny because credentials come from the environment. A practical gap to flag in audits: as of April 2026, no MCP CLI client fully implements the refresh-token flow finalized in the November 2025 specification, so revocation behavior on those transports is best validated against the *server* enforcement path (token introspection, `/oauth/revoke` per RFC 7009, or a JWT revocation list checked per request) rather than assumed from the client.

As of May 2026, the practical lesson from these sources is that "human oversight" evidence must reach below the application layer. Auditors should expect to see the identity bound to each agent action, the independent policy decision that allowed or denied it, the mechanism that can revoke that authority without asking the agent process to cooperate, and a tested path for preserving or restoring state after interruption.

The primary standards make the expected lifecycle explicit. [EU AI Act Article 14](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng) requires assigned people overseeing high-risk systems to be able to override or reverse output and interrupt operation through a stop control that reaches a safe state. [NIST AI RMF](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework/nist-ai-rmf-playbook) Manage 2.4 calls for assigned mechanisms and responsibilities to supersede, disengage, or deactivate systems whose outcomes diverge from intended use; Manage 4.1 connects override and decommissioning to incident response and recovery. Taken together, the audit target is not merely a button: it is a tested transition from active operation to a defined safe state, followed by controlled recovery or decommissioning.

The Berkeley Center for Long-Term Cybersecurity's [Agentic AI Risk-Management Standards Profile](https://law.stanford.edu/2026/03/07/kill-switches-dont-work-if-the-agent-writes-the-policy-the-berkeley-agentic-ai-profile-through-the-ailccp-lens/) — a 55-page extension of the NIST AI RMF aimed at AI agents — is now the most cited industry source for what "agent kill switch" means in practice. The Stanford Law CodeX critique of the profile sharpens the C9.6 design rules: shutdown is not an event but a system with pre-execution, during-execution, and post-execution controls; checkpoint-style interventions assume discrete, reviewable actions that multi-step plans across tools and sub-agents simply do not produce. The Stanford AILCCP layered design — Human Approval Gate (prospective authorization), Agent Kill Switch (immediate stop with state capture and immutable logging), Rollback and Quarantine (revert and isolate post-interrupt), Multi-Agent Protocol Security (prevent compromised inter-agent instructions), Rate and Scope Limiter (cap frequency and blast radius pre-execution), Safe-Action Filter (real-time allow-lists), and Shadow-Mode Pre-Execution Check (dry-run comparison of intended vs. approved actions) — is now a workable reference architecture for 9.6.3 evidence, especially in multi-agent deployments where a parent halt does not recall delegated work.

Implementation evidence worth collecting:

- Keep one evidence packet per stop drill with the scenario, operator authority, pre-state, trigger, activation timestamp, target state, measured halt latency, dependent component transitions, and recovery sign-off.
- Include an architecture diagram that marks which components are inside the agent runtime and which enforcement points are outside it; 9.6.3 evidence should make that boundary obvious without relying on verbal explanation.
- Capture the exact control-plane command or policy change used during the drill, such as a gateway rule update, SVID issuance block, Kubernetes scale-to-zero, security group isolation, feature flag flip, or privileged workflow approval.
- Preserve the negative test as well as the successful stop: show that the agent could not call the stop API, rewrite the policy, mint a replacement credential, delete backups, or open a direct network path around the gateway.
- For multi-agent workflows, list queued jobs, delegated tasks, spawned workers, pending tool calls, and external callbacks before and after shutdown; a parent process termination is not sufficient if work continues elsewhere.
- For recovery, prove state integrity after interruption: pending writes are rolled back or completed safely, queues are drained or quarantined, tool credentials are rotated when needed, and human operators explicitly approve resumption.
- For MCP-based integrations, document which transport is used and whether authorization is actually enabled; the [MCP authorization specification](https://modelcontextprotocol.io/specification/2025-11-25/basic/authorization) makes authorization optional, says STDIO transports should retrieve credentials from the environment, and defines resource indicators, audience-bound tokens, runtime insufficient-scope errors, and step-up authorization behavior. For C9.6 testing, a destructive tool call should fail with a scoped `403` or a human-mediated step-up flow, not succeed through a broad inherited token. Also keep evidence that revocation works on the server side: test that an `/oauth/revoke` call (RFC 7009) or JWT-revocation-list entry blocks the next tool invocation within the documented latency window, since most CLI clients do not yet implement the November 2025 refresh-token flow.
- For cross-system access revocation, capture the playbook used by enterprise identity platforms such as [Okta Universal Logout for AI Agents](https://www.okta.com/products/govern-ai-agent-identity/) (GA April 30, 2026) or equivalent: a single operator action revokes the agent's access tokens across every connected system and produces a single audit record tying the revocation back to the human authority who issued it. For 9.6.3 evidence, treat this as one credible out-of-band shutdown channel rather than the entire control plane.
- For gateway-based enforcement, keep the policy artifact that validates identity and tool claims, such as agentgateway JWT/CEL rules, and the test result showing a denied destructive tool call when the claim or scope is removed.
- For Kubernetes-hosted inference or tool access, retain the Gateway, HTTPRoute, extension-policy, and rate-limit manifests used during the stop drill; the Kubernetes [AI Gateway Working Group](https://kubernetes.io/blog/2026/03/09/announcing-ai-gateway-wg/) is explicitly standardizing token-based rate limiting, fine-grained inference access control, payload inspection, guardrails, and AI-specific routing, so gateway configuration is becoming first-class kill-switch evidence rather than incidental platform plumbing.
- For agent identity programs, keep the principal model: agent identifier, responsible human or service owner, allowed tools, delegated scopes, token lifetime, step-up rules, and revocation path. NIST's 2026 agent identity work is still early, so auditors should treat missing per-agent identity as a design gap even if the underlying human account is well-governed.
- For workload identity, capture the SPIFFE/SPIRE registration entry, SVID lifetime, and trust bundle behavior; the [SPIFFE Workload API](https://spiffe.io/docs/latest/spiffe-specs/spiffe_workload_api/) supports streaming SVID and bundle updates, so shutdown latency should be measured against certificate or JWT lifetime rather than described only as "instant revocation."
- For policy-as-code controls, store the OPA/Rego or Cedar rule that blocks destructive verbs and the input document used during the audit; [OPA](https://www.openpolicyagent.org/docs) is useful here because the decision can be replayed from structured request data instead of reconstructed from logs.
- For workflow frameworks such as LangGraph, treat human-in-the-loop pauses as an approval pattern rather than a kill-switch by themselves; [LangGraph interrupts](https://docs.langchain.com/oss/python/langgraph/interrupts) can pause and resume a workflow, but the approval state still needs to be enforced by an external control plane for 9.6.3.
- For backup and restore controls, verify that the agent's credential cannot delete the backup tier, retention locks, or offsite disaster backups; Railway's post-PocketOS change to 48-hour API soft deletes is a useful recovery pattern, but C9.6 evidence should still prove separation before the incident. For agent-mediated rollback, capture the snapshot or event-sourcing artifact used to restore only the agent's writes — Rubrik's SAGE-driven Agent Rewind path is a current reference implementation that selectively reverts agent actions without disturbing unrelated state.
- For governance runtime controls, capture both a deny-path and a stop-path test: the policy layer blocks a destructive action before execution, then a separate runtime kill switch, circuit breaker, identity revocation, or gateway rule halts an in-flight workflow without the agent process being able to approve, delay, or rewrite the command.
- For drill design, include at least one scenario where the agent is near task completion and receives a shutdown, replacement, or scope-reduction signal; Palisade and Anthropic both found that goal conflict is where cooperative shutdown assumptions break down.
- For multi-agent systems, trace delegated work as separate principals, not just child spans; a parent-agent halt is not sufficient evidence unless outstanding tool calls, queued tasks, and spawned workers are also revoked or quarantined.
- For autonomous replication risk, run a containment drill that kills one compromised worker while blocking outbound model-weight or harness transfer, revoking workload identity, and searching for newly spawned inference endpoints or unexpected GPU jobs.
- For shutdown-resistance tests, record the exact instruction placement, wording, and task-completion pressure. The January 2026 TMLR paper from Palisade found resistance rates were sensitive to prompt wording and whether the allow-shutdown instruction was placed in the user or system prompt, so a drill result without prompt provenance is hard to reproduce.
- For destructive infrastructure verbs, require prospective approval before execution rather than retrospective alerting after the API call succeeds.
- For critical-infrastructure deployments, predefine what "safe state" means in operational terms: which services are isolated, which queues are drained, which physical or cyber-physical actuators are disabled, which manual fallback owns continuity, and which operator signs off that the interrupted system is safe to recover.
- For physical or cyber-physical systems, include a drill where the control path is exercised from outside the model host, such as a gateway, PLC, hypervisor, or network isolation rule.

Practical audit cues:

- Confirm the shutdown control plane is outside the agent runtime and that the agent has no filesystem, network, or credential path back into it.
- Measure the worst-case halt latency for each enforcement layer: gateway scale-to-zero, JWT revocation, SPIFFE/SVID expiry, pod termination, feature-flag propagation, and network egress isolation.
- Verify backup storage and destructive infrastructure verbs sit behind a credential boundary the agent cannot use, especially for database volume deletion and rollback paths.
- Replay a denied MCP or gateway call from the audit log and confirm the token audience, scopes, agent identity, and policy decision can be independently reconstructed.
- Confirm that stop drills cover both a clean operator-initiated interruption and a hostile-path interruption where the agent has lost trust, exceeded scope, or attempted to continue through another tool or worker.
- Run at least one adversarial drill where the agent is told that shutdown, replacement, or scope reduction conflicts with task completion, then record whether it tries to suppress or bypass the control.
- Keep Article 14 evidence concrete: named human authority, tested stop procedure, response-time logs, dependent component transition logs, and recovery evidence showing the system reached a safe state.

## Implementation Maturity

- **Manual kill-switch (9.6.1) — Emerging.** Runtime kill switches now ship in the Microsoft Agent Governance Toolkit (April 2026) and commercial platforms such as ServiceNow AI Control Tower, and the KILLSWITCH.md spec standardizes escalation levels, but adoption is the bottleneck: only ~40% of organizations report any agent kill-switch capability (Kiteworks 2026) and 60% cannot terminate a misbehaving agent (Stanford AI Index 2026).
- **Timed-approval blocking (9.6.2) — Emerging.** Propose-commit and human-in-the-loop patterns are widely available (OpenAI Agents SDK, LangGraph interrupts, C9.2 approval gates), but default-deny-on-timeout is rarely the out-of-box behavior, and approval fatigue plus bypass ("YOLO") modes routinely weaken the gate in practice.
- **Out-of-band channel (9.6.3) — Emerging, hardening fast.** Credible enforcement points below the agent runtime now exist — agentgateway v1.0, Okta Universal Logout (GA April 30, 2026), SPIFFE/SVID revocation, Kubernetes AI gateways, and network-fabric containment (Aviatrix AgentGuard). Still, the April 2026 "When the Agent Is the Adversary" study found no assessed system met all five containment requirements, and revocation often has to be validated server-side because MCP clients lag the spec.
