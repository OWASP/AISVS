# C12.3: Differential-Privacy Safeguards

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-15 (3rd pass)

## Purpose

Track and enforce privacy budgets to provide formal guarantees against individual data leakage. The Fundamental Law of Information Recovery (Dwork & Roth, 2014) shows that too many adaptive queries destroy privacy — without explicit budget tracking, the cumulative epsilon can grow without limit, degrading privacy guarantees to meaninglessness. As of 2025-2026, the DP tooling ecosystem has matured significantly: Google's VaultGemma (September 2025) demonstrated production-grade DP-SGD for LLMs at 1B parameters with epsilon <= 2.0, proving formal DP guarantees are achievable at LLM scale. DynamoEnhance extends DP-SGD to >7B-parameter models via multi-GPU DeepSpeed integration. Google's user-level DP fine-tuning research (ELS/ULS) shows orders-of-magnitude noise reduction versus prior approaches. PrivCode (NDSS 2026) is the first DP synthesizer for code datasets. However, DP reversal via LLM feedback (2025-2026 research) shows that confidence score analysis can defeat DP protections at the inference layer — API-level mitigations (hiding logits) are needed alongside training-time DP. PETAL (USENIX Security 2025) demonstrates that even label-only access (no logits) suffices for membership inference: per-token semantic similarity approximates output probabilities well enough (Pearson r = 0.765) to achieve AUC 0.64 on Pythia-6.9B, and common defenses like paraphrasing and deduplication provide negligible protection. Separately, Khalil et al. (November 2025) showed that membership inference vulnerability extends beyond overfitting — outlier samples (noisy, hard-to-classify records near class boundaries) leak membership even in well-generalizing models, which means DP must protect per-sample privacy regardless of aggregate model accuracy. A separate privacy-hallucination tradeoff has been quantified: DP fine-tuning at ε = 8 reduces factual text generation by 17-24% compared to non-private models, and DP pre-training causes a 43% factuality drop — a critical consideration for high-stakes domains like healthcare. On the efficiency front, FlashDP (NeurIPS 2025) introduced a cache-friendly per-layer DP-SGD that reaches 90% of non-private throughput on Llama-13B pre-training on four A100s, while TTLoRA-DP (January 2026) applies tensor train decomposition to shrink the trainable parameter space by 7.6x versus standard LoRA with lower membership leakage even without DP noise. For inference-time privacy, Yang & Zhu (March 2026) formalized token-level and message-level differential privacy for AI agents accessing enterprise databases, deriving privacy bounds as a function of generation temperature and message length — a complementary approach when training-time DP is unavailable. NIST finalized SP 800-226 on March 6, 2025, providing the "DP pyramid" evaluation framework — privacy parameters (ε, δ) at the top, trust models and security at the middle, data collection practices at the base — requiring evaluation of every layer for meaningful guarantees. The OpenDP Differential Privacy Deployments Registry (launched November 2025) now catalogs 21 real-world DP deployments as structured "deployment cards," helping practitioners benchmark epsilon choices against comparable use cases — accompanying research accepted at IEEE S&P 2026 shows practitioners use the registry to compare deployments by dataset size and domain. Full enforcement of the EU AI Act begins August 2026, recognizing privacy-enhancing technologies including DP as a compliance mechanism for high-risk AI systems.

| **12.3.2** | **Verify that** black-box privacy audits estimate ε̂ within 10% of declared value. | 2 | Discrepancy between claimed and actual privacy guarantees. Implementation bugs in DP mechanisms (e.g., floating-point vulnerabilities in Gaussian noise, incorrect sensitivity calibration) can silently void privacy guarantees. Mironov (2012) showed that floating-point arithmetic can break DP. DP reversal via LLM feedback has emerged as a practical attack: adversaries feed target records into an LLM and analyze completion confidence to perform membership inference. PETAL (USENIX Security 2025) takes this further — a label-only MIA that achieves AUC 0.64 on Pythia-6.9B using per-token semantic similarity to approximate output probabilities, matching logit-based attacks without needing probability access. Khalil et al. (November 2025) demonstrated that MIA vulnerability extends beyond overfitting: outlier samples — noisy or hard-to-classify records sitting on class boundaries — leak membership even in well-generalizing models, meaning audits must specifically test per-sample vulnerability on atypical records, not just aggregate metrics. Additionally, DPO-aligned models are theoretically more vulnerable to MIA than PPO-aligned models (Amazon Science, 2024), meaning alignment method choice affects privacy exposure. | Run black-box auditing tools (e.g., DP-Sniper, privacy auditing via membership inference as in Jagielski et al., 2020). Ensure |epsilon-hat - epsilon| / epsilon < 0.10. Tight auditing requires many queries and is expensive. The 10% tolerance is ambitious for complex pipelines. Composition across multiple DP mechanisms compounds estimation difficulty. Inference-time attacks (LLM feedback-based reversal and label-only PETAL) are not caught by training-time DP audits alone — API-level mitigations such as hiding logits and controlling maximum prompt length are needed as complementary controls. As of 2025, no standard defense reliably stops label-only MIA against pre-trained LLMs; even nucleus sampling and contrastive search offer no meaningful protection. |
| **12.3.3** | **Verify that** formal proofs cover all post-training fine-tunes and embeddings. | 3 | Privacy budget exhaustion through fine-tuning. A Carnegie Mellon study (2025) showed that approximate unlearning methods merely suppress model outputs rather than robustly forgetting target knowledge — relearning on public articles can restore the supposedly-unlearned information, meaning unlearning cannot substitute for proper DP budgeting. Verify that user-level DP guarantees use Example-Level Sampling (ELS) or User-Level Sampling (ULS) where applicable — Google's 2025-2026 research demonstrates orders-of-magnitude noise reduction versus naive conversions from example-level to user-level DP. The privacy-hallucination tradeoff (quantified September 2025) means that formal proofs must balance epsilon budgets against factuality requirements — in healthcare and legal domains, a 17-24% factuality drop may be unacceptable, forcing practitioners toward less aggressive DP budgets or inference-time DP approaches (Private Evolution, DP prediction) that avoid training-time noise entirely. NIST SP 800-226 emphasizes evaluating the full composition stack but does not prescribe how to handle post-training budget accounting specifically. |

## Tooling

- **Differential privacy:** Opacus (PyTorch), TensorFlow Privacy (TF), Google DP Library (C++/Java/Go with Python bindings), OpenDP/SmartNoise (Rust core with Python API, Harvard/Microsoft), PipelineDP4j (JVM — Google's newest release for parallelizable DP computations), Diffprivlib (IBM), DynamoEnhance (multi-GPU DP training for >7B-parameter LLMs built on DeepSpeed, supports LoRA + BitsAndBytes + flash attention). FlashDP (NeurIPS 2025, open-sourced at github.com/kaustpradalab/flashdp) provides cache-friendly per-layer DP-SGD achieving 90% non-private throughput on Llama-13B with 50% less memory movement than GhostClip. TTLoRA-DP (January 2026) applies tensor train decomposition to LoRA for 7.6x parameter reduction with improved privacy-utility tradeoffs. Google's VaultGemma (September 2025) demonstrated production-grade DP-SGD for LLMs at 1B parameters with epsilon <= 2.0 — weights available on Hugging Face/Kaggle. Google's user-level DP fine-tuning research (ELS/ULS) shows orders-of-magnitude noise reduction versus prior approaches. PrivCode (NDSS 2026) is the first DP synthesizer specifically for code datasets, using a two-stage DP-SGD + utility-boosting pipeline. AWS fast-differential-privacy library provides mixed ghost clipping with 1-10% memory overhead and <2x slowdown versus non-private training.
- **Budget tracking & dashboards:** Tumult Analytics (PureDPBudget/RhoZCDPBudget session tracking with `remaining_privacy_budget` API and hard-stop enforcement), AWS Clean Rooms Differential Privacy (managed "remaining queries" gauge with epsilon budget policy and CloudWatch integration), Google dp-accounting library (PLD/RDP/GDP accountants with `DpEvent` composition — Connect-the-Dots PLD is 5x faster than PRV Accountant), OpenDP/SmartNoise SQL (odometer-based cumulative epsilon tracking across query workloads)
- **Inference-only DP approaches:** Private Evolution (PE) — generates DP synthetic data by querying inference APIs without fine-tuning, compatible with foundation models accessible only through APIs; DP prediction — releases only model outputs with DP guarantees, avoiding training-time noise entirely. These approaches sidestep the privacy-hallucination tradeoff inherent in DP-SGD training.
- **Privacy auditing:** DP-Sniper (black-box DP auditing), membership inference attack toolkits (ML-Doctor, privacy meter), canary-based memorization testing (ICLR 2025 methodology for LLM-specific auditing), PETAL label-only MIA (USENIX Security 2025 — per-token semantic similarity, no logit access needed), ensemble privacy defense (arXiv 2512.03100, December 2025)
- **Inference-time agent DP:** Yang & Zhu (March 2026, arXiv 2603.17902) — token-level and message-level DP framework for AI agents accessing enterprise databases, with privacy bounds tied to generation temperature and message length
- **Standards & guidance:** NIST SP 800-226 (March 2025, "DP pyramid" evaluation framework with supplemental Python Jupyter notebooks and interactive assessment tools), EU AI Act (full enforcement August 2026 — recognizes PETs including DP as compliance mechanism for high-risk AI systems), ISO/IEC 42001 (AI management system standard, can be used alongside DP for risk-tiered governance), OpenDP Differential Privacy Deployments Registry (November 2025 — 21 structured deployment cards cataloging real-world DP implementations with epsilon choices and rationale; accompanying IEEE S&P 2026 study validates practitioner utility)
