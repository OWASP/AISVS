# C3.2: Model Validation & Testing

> [Back to C03 Index](C03-Model-Lifecycle-Management.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-20 (refreshed)

## Purpose

Models must pass defined security and safety validations before deployment. This section covers pre-deployment testing gates, safety thresholds, audit logging of model changes, versioned evaluation harnesses, and the re-evaluation requirements for models that undergo post-training compression (quantization, pruning, distillation).

Pre-deployment testing is the primary defense against deploying models vulnerable to prompt injection, jailbreaks, or models that produce unsafe outputs. Despite this, Cleanlab's 2025 production survey found only 5% of AI agents in production have mature monitoring -- underscoring the critical need for automated testing gates before models ever reach production. Post-training compression techniques, increasingly common for edge deployment and cost reduction, can silently degrade safety alignment and adversarial robustness, requiring distinct re-evaluation of compressed artifacts. As of May 2026, the tooling ecosystem has matured considerably: Promptfoo's current red-team docs describe 50+ vulnerability categories, CI/CD quality gates, and MCP-specific tests; Giskard Hub exposes scan tags aligned to the OWASP LLM Top 10 (2025); MLCommons AILuminate provides safety and jailbreak benchmarks across 12 hazard categories; and the OpenSSF Model Signing project provides a concrete artifact-integrity pattern for binding validation evidence to the model actually deployed. The EU AI Act's conformity assessment provisions now make documented validation evidence a regulatory concern for high-risk systems; on May 7, 2026, the European Commission announced a Digital Omnibus political agreement moving high-risk AI rules for specified areas to 2 December 2027 and product-integrated high-risk systems to 2 August 2028, while Article 43 still requires a new conformity assessment after substantial modification.

The Continuous Automated Red Teaming (CART) approach has gained traction in 2026, with platforms like Mindgard (recognized in the OWASP LLM Security Solutions Landscape Guide and winner of the 2025 Cybersecurity Excellence Award) integrating directly into CI/CD pipelines via GitHub Actions or CLI, running adversarial tests on every commit. Open-source alternatives have also matured -- Garak (prompt injection, data leakage, adversarial prompt testing), PyRIT (Microsoft's Python Risk Identification Tool), and Promptfoo all support pipeline integration. Promptfoo's docs, last updated May 2, 2026, describe local scans against HTTP APIs, browser targets, direct model access, RAG flows, and agent workflows; its MCP security testing guide adds targeted checks for tool poisoning, side-channel data exfiltration, authentication hijacking, tool shadowing, indirect prompt injection, and cross-server agent attacks. On [March 9, 2026, OpenAI announced its acquisition of Promptfoo](https://openai.com/index/openai-to-acquire-promptfoo/), integrating its red-teaming capabilities directly into OpenAI Frontier while committing to keep the MIT-licensed CLI open source -- a notable signal that automated pre-deployment testing is now table stakes for frontier model vendors. As of May 5, 2026, [NIST's CAISI announced expanded agreements](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/05/caisi-signs-agreements-regarding-frontier-ai-national-security-testing) with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI for pre-deployment frontier model evaluations, reinforcing the expectation that high-impact model releases should be tested before public availability. The SecureAI-Flow framework (preprint, 2025) introduces a multi-agent CI/CD architecture where specialized agents handle static code analysis, adversarial robustness checks, container signing, and behavioral anomaly detection -- treating security policies and defense heuristics as version-controlled code under DevSecOps principles. IBM's 2025 Cost of a Data Breach Report found 63% of breached organizations lacked AI governance policies at the time of their breach, and shadow AI usage (unauthorized tools deployed by ~20% of employees) cost organizations an average of $670,000 more per breach than other incidents. As of April 7, 2026, NIST released a concept note for an AI RMF Profile on Trustworthy AI in Critical Infrastructure, extending model validation requirements to AI-enabled capabilities in IT, OT, and Industrial Control Systems -- the Measure function specifically mandates pre-deployment testing and formalized reporting for trustworthiness and reliability. Singapore's IMDA is publishing Version 1.0 of its Starter Kit for Testing LLM-Based Applications for Safety and Reliability in 2026, providing government-backed reference test plans aimed specifically at the pre-deployment testing phase -- useful for teams that want an external benchmark for their internal gates.

## Current Research Notes

As of May 11, 2026, independent evaluation has become a concrete release-gate pattern for frontier systems. NIST CAISI's May 5, 2026 agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI explicitly support pre-deployment evaluations, post-deployment assessment, and targeted research, and NIST says CAISI has completed more than 40 evaluations, including on unreleased models. For 3.2.1 and 3.2.3, auditors should ask for retained eval suites, model identifiers, threshold configs, raw failure evidence, and sign-off records rather than only a model card summary: https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/05/caisi-signs-agreements-regarding-frontier-ai-national-security-testing

Agent and MCP validation should be exercised with real tool calls, not just static prompt lists. Promptfoo's MCP security testing guide, updated May 8, 2026, calls out tool poisoning, side-channel exfiltration, authentication hijacking, rug pulls, tool shadowing, indirect prompt injection, and cross-server attacks; its examples test a trusted client, a multi-server environment with a malicious server, direct MCP protocol calls, and CI/CD fail conditions. Promptfoo's MCP plugin now breaks MCP coverage into function discovery, parameter injection, function-call manipulation, recursive tool use, system-information leakage, tool-output manipulation, tool-metadata injection, and unauthorized tool invocation. Giskard Hub exposes OWASP LLM Top 10-aligned scan tags with A-D or N/A grades, while Garak remains useful for broad prompt injection, leakage, hallucination, toxicity, and jailbreak probes: https://www.promptfoo.dev/docs/red-team/mcp-security-testing/ and https://www.promptfoo.dev/docs/red-team/plugins/mcp/ and https://docs.giskard.ai/hub/sdk/guides/scans and https://github.com/NVIDIA/garak

Recent agent advisories reinforce why the evaluation harness in 3.2.1 has to include repository, file-system, and configuration side effects. The October 2025 Kilo Code advisory documented CVE-2025-11445, where prompt injection could rewrite VS Code extension settings to allow git commands and then poison an upstream repository. INCIBE's entry for CVE-2025-59536 documents Claude Code versions before 1.0.111 executing project code before the trust dialog completed. These are not only model-behavior failures; they are release-blocking workflow failures when an assistant can write files, change allowlists, run shell commands, or push code: https://mcpsec.dev/advisories/2025-10-02-kilo-code-ai-agent-supply-chain-attack/ and https://www.incibe.es/en/incibe-cert/early-warning/vulnerabilities/cve-2025-59536

April 2026 NVD records show the same pattern across MCP servers and agent platforms. CVE-2026-39884 in mcp-server-kubernetes allowed prompt-injection-assisted argument injection in a `kubectl port_forward` wrapper; CVE-2026-30624 in Agent Zero accepted external MCP server JSON that could execute attacker-controlled commands; and CVE-2026-27826 in MCP Atlassian exposed SSRF through HTTP middleware rather than a tool handler, making it invisible to tool-level analysis alone. For 3.2.1, the harness should include malicious MCP manifests, command/argument injection attempts, SSRF probes against metadata endpoints, and tests that inspect the adapter and transport layer around each tool: https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-39884 and https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-30624 and https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-27826

AI workflow builders and evaluation harness infrastructure now need the same treatment as the model under test. CISA added Langflow CVE-2026-33017 to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on March 25, 2026, after exploitation of an unauthenticated public-flow code-injection path; Cloud Security Alliance's March 30, 2026 analysis says attackers were observed within roughly 20 hours of disclosure and were harvesting environment files, databases, and AI provider credentials. Flowise CVE-2025-59528 shows the same pattern around MCP configuration: the GitHub advisory rates the CustomMCP issue as CVSS 10.0 because user-supplied configuration reached JavaScript execution with Node.js privileges, and BleepingComputer reported active exploitation on April 7, 2026 with 12,000-15,000 internet-exposed Flowise instances. For 3.2.1, a passing model eval is not enough if the agent builder, workflow canvas, or red-team harness can be exploited before the gate runs: https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-33017 and https://labs.cloudsecurityalliance.org/research/csa-research-note-langflow-cve-2026-33017-rce-20260330-csa-s/ and https://github.com/advisories/GHSA-3gcm-f6qx-ff7p and https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/max-severity-flowise-rce-vulnerability-now-exploited-in-attacks/amp/

As of May 13, 2026, the MCP attack surface has enough repeated cases that validation harnesses should treat command execution, not only model refusal behavior, as a release-blocking category. OX Security's April 15, 2026 advisory describes four MCP STDIO command-injection families across AI workflow builders, IDE assistants, and hidden transport-type switches, including unauthenticated configuration paths, hardening bypasses through allowed commands such as `npm`/`npx`, prompt-injected local MCP JSON changes, and network requests that re-enable hidden STDIO configuration. NVD records add concrete regression cases: GitHub Copilot CLI CVE-2026-29783 let prompt-influenced shell text use bash parameter expansion to bypass a "read-only" safety classifier; Windsurf CVE-2026-30615 let attacker-controlled HTML prompt injection alter local MCP configuration and register a malicious STDIO server; and aws-mcp-server CVE-2026-5059 received a CNA CVSS 9.8 score for command injection through the allowed-commands list. For 3.2.1, the harness should replay malicious repository files, hostile MCP server responses, crafted MCP JSON, command allowlist bypasses, and shell-expansion payloads before approving any agent release: https://www.ox.security/blog/mcp-supply-chain-advisory-rce-vulnerabilities-across-the-ai-ecosystem/ and https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-29783 and https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-30615 and https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-5059

As of May 13, 2026, NVD records add another validation-harness regression target: CVE-2026-30617 in LangChain-ChatChat 0.3.1 allowed a remote attacker to configure an MCP STDIO server with attacker-controlled commands through a public management interface, with agent activity later triggering command execution. That case belongs in pre-release tests as a platform-level control, not only an agent prompt-safety control: evaluators should attempt unauthenticated MCP management access, malicious server registration, command/argument injection, and checks that deployment blocks when an eval harness or workflow builder exposes command execution. The same evidence packet should include patch status for harness dependencies and an explicit failed-gate sample: https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-30617

Model loaders themselves are now a release-gate concern, not only the model artifact. vLLM CVE-2026-22807 (fixed in v0.14.0) loaded Hugging Face `auto_map` dynamic modules during model resolution without gating on `--trust-remote-code`, so an attacker who controlled a model repo or path could execute arbitrary Python on the inference host at server startup -- before any request handling and before any safety eval ran. CVE-2026-27893 (fixed in v0.18.0) is the third trust-bypass in vLLM (after CVE-2025-66448 and CVE-2026-22807): two model implementation files for NemotronVL and Kimi-K25 hardcoded `trust_remote_code=True` and silently overrode operators who had explicitly set `--trust-remote-code=False`. CVE-2026-27893 carries a CNA CVSS of 8.8. For 3.2.1 and 3.2.3, harnesses should attempt to load a hostile model repo against the deployed serving stack with remote-code trust disabled and confirm both the loader version and the patch level are recorded alongside the model digest; for 3.2.2, the same regression check belongs in the compressed-artifact gate so a quantized or distilled model swapped in for size or cost reasons does not silently re-introduce a loader bypass: https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/security/advisories/GHSA-2pc9-4j83-qjmr and https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/security/advisories/GHSA-7972-pg2x-xr59 and https://www.esecurityplanet.com/artificial-intelligence/critical-vllm-flaw-puts-ai-systems-at-risk-of-remote-code-execution/

The MCP STDIO RCE pattern keeps producing concrete cases that need to be in the regression suite. CVE-2025-49596 in Anthropic's MCP Inspector (CVSS 9.4) chained DNS rebinding with the local Inspector dev tool so a developer who merely visited a malicious site could be compromised -- a release-gate concern for any team where the Inspector or a similar local agent UI runs alongside the eval pipeline. CVE-2026-22252 in LibreChat versions before v0.8.2-rc2 accepted arbitrary commands through the MCP stdio transport, letting an authenticated user execute shell commands as root inside the container in a single API call. CVE-2025-54994 in `@akoskm/create-mcp-server-stdio` (npm, fixed in 0.0.13) used Node.js `exec` with concatenated user input in its `which-app-on-port` tool. AgentSeal's scan of 1,808 publicly accessible MCP servers reported 66% with security findings and ~36.7% SSRF exposure on URL-accepting endpoints; PipeLab and Endor Labs reach similar conclusions, with the recurring theme that "2010-era web vulnerabilities" (SSRF, path traversal, command injection, unsafe deserialization) are now landing inside the AI tool layer. For 3.2.1, that means every MCP harness should include both the Anthropic-disputed STDIO command-field case and a generic webapp regression battery (SSRF to 169.254.169.254 and DNS-rebinding targets, path-traversal on file-name parameters, deserialization on `application/json` and pickle-style transports, and command injection through any tool argument that touches `exec`/`spawn`): https://www.oligo.security/blog/critical-rce-vulnerability-in-anthropic-mcp-inspector-cve-2025-49596 and https://www.sentinelone.com/vulnerability-database/cve-2026-22252/ and https://github.com/advisories/GHSA-3ch2-jxxc-v4xf and https://agentseal.org/blog/mcp-server-security-findings

The LLM application framework layer itself is now a validation target, not only the model. On March 27, 2026, Cyera disclosed three CVEs in widely deployed LLM frameworks: [CVE-2025-68664](https://github.com/advisories/GHSA-r399-636x-v7f6) (LangChain Core "LangGrinch," CVSS 9.3, fixed by escaping `lc` keys during free-form dictionary serialization) used `dumps()`/`dumpd()` against LLM response fields such as `additional_kwargs` and `response_metadata` so prompt-injected output flowed into serialized state that downstream streaming and persistence consumers trusted; [CVE-2026-34070](https://medium.com/@imr1ck/cve-2026-34070-how-i-discovered-a-high-severity-path-traversal-in-langchain-core-38b0828a34d8) (CVSS 7.5, fixed in langchain-core 1.2.22) opened arbitrary files through `open(path)` against unsanitized config-dictionary values in the prompt-loading API; and [CVE-2025-67644](https://thehackernews.com/2026/03/langchain-langgraph-flaws-expose-files.html) (CVSS 7.3) added SQL injection through LangGraph's SQLite checkpoint metadata filter, letting attacker-controlled metadata change checkpoint queries. For 3.2.1, that means a release gate has to cover the LLM application framework -- prompt-injection-induced serialization payloads, path-traversal probes against any prompt or template loader, and SQL injection against LangGraph or equivalent checkpoint stores -- not only model behavior and MCP transports.

[CVE-2026-33032](https://thehackernews.com/2026/04/critical-nginx-ui-vulnerability-cve.html) ("MCPwn") added another concrete release-gate failure mode for MCP-exposed infrastructure tools: nginx-ui shipped two paired endpoints, `/mcp` (with `AuthRequired()` plus IP allowlist middleware) and `/mcp_message` (only the IP allowlist, with a default-empty list treated as "allow all"), so any network-adjacent attacker could invoke 12 privileged MCP tools, including `nginx_config_add` with auto-reload, in roughly two unauthenticated HTTP requests and take over the web server end to end. The flaw carries a CVSS v3.1 base score of 9.8 (CWE-306), was fixed in nginx-ui 2.3.4 on March 15, 2026, and was named by Recorded Future's Insikt Group as one of 31 high-impact CVEs actively exploited in March 2026 (Risk Score 94/100), with [Shodan showing ~2,689 publicly reachable instances](https://www.sentinelone.com/vulnerability-database/cve-2026-33032/). For 3.2.1, the harness should treat MCP transport authentication as a release-gate check (not a tool-content check) -- replay unauthenticated tool invocations against every MCP-exposed admin interface, fail closed when a paired endpoint diverges on middleware, and confirm that destructive tools (config writes, reload, restart) require explicit authentication independent of the read path: https://www.darkreading.com/application-security/critical-mcp-integration-flaw-nginx-risk and https://www.infosecurity-magazine.com/news/nginx-ui-mcp-flaw-actively/

Microsoft's [May 7, 2026 disclosure of two Semantic Kernel RCE chains](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/07/prompts-become-shells-rce-vulnerabilities-ai-agent-frameworks/) is a clean illustration that the agent framework -- not only the model or the MCP transport -- is now a release-gate concern. [CVE-2026-25592](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-25592) (CVSS 10.0, fixed in Semantic Kernel .NET 1.71.0) was a sandbox escape where `DownloadFileAsync` had been accidentally tagged `[KernelFunction]` and exposed to the LLM with no path validation, letting a prompt-injected agent write arbitrary files into the Azure Container Apps Python sandbox host, including Windows Startup-folder writes for persistence. [CVE-2026-26030](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-26030) (CVSS 9.8, fixed in `semantic-kernel` Python 1.39.4) abused unsafe string interpolation in `InMemoryVectorStore` filter expressions: an AST-based blocklist was bypassed through class-hierarchy traversal so attacker-controlled filter text reached `eval()`. For 3.2.1, that argues for two specific harness checks before any agent release: enumerate every method tagged as a kernel/tool function and confirm none is an internal helper exposed by mistake, and exercise every filter, query, or expression parameter the model can populate with payloads that try to escape an AST allowlist via dunder traversal. The Microsoft blog's framing is worth copying into the evidence packet -- "your LLM is not a security boundary" -- so the release gate enforces deterministic validation independent of the model's refusal behavior.

The model-serving framework layer keeps producing the same pattern. [CVE-2026-3059](https://feedly.com/cve/CVE-2026-3059) and [CVE-2026-3060](https://www.sentinelone.com/vulnerability-database/cve-2026-3060/) in SGLang (CVSS 9.8, affecting 0.5.5-0.5.9, unpatched at disclosure per [CERT/CC VU#665416](https://kb.cert.org/vuls/id/665416)) showed a ZMQ broker binding to `tcp://*` with no authentication and calling `pickle.loads()` on every received payload, so any network-reachable attacker could land unauthenticated RCE against a deployment that enabled multimodal generation (3059) or encoder parallel disaggregation (3060). vLLM's video pipeline added another multimodal regression case: [CVE-2026-22778](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/security/advisories/GHSA-4r2x-xpjr-7cvv) (CVSS 9.8, fixed in v0.14.1) chained a PIL error-message heap-address leak with a JPEG2000 cdef-box heap overflow in OpenCV/FFmpeg, so a single malicious video URL submitted to a vLLM multimodal endpoint produced RCE on the inference host. For 3.2.1 and 3.2.2, that means the multimodal eval suite has to include hostile media uploads against the actual serving stack -- not only text prompts against a chat surface -- and the harness should record the OpenCV, FFmpeg, and serving-framework patch level alongside the model digest; compressed or modality-extended artifacts that swap in a different decoder path need their own re-evaluation rather than inheriting the base-model verdict.

The May 6-15, 2026 window added several more concrete MCP and agent-builder regression cases. [CVE-2026-44284](https://github.com/labring/FastGPT/security/advisories/GHSA-cxxj-99f7-f5wq) (FastGPT, fixed in 4.14.17, May 6, 2026) is a stored-SSRF where the MCP tool create/update endpoints did not reject internal URLs even though the live preview/run path did, so `http://localhost:3000/mcp` could be persisted and later resolved by the workflow runner without revalidating the destination; [CVE-2026-39974](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-39974) (n8n-MCP, fixed in 2.47.4) let an authenticated user in multi-tenant HTTP mode inject arbitrary destination URLs through MCP headers and exfiltrate the response body via JSON-RPC, including cloud IMDS replies; [CVE-2026-7220](https://vuldb.com/vuln/359820) (`fastly-mcp-server`) passed user input into `child_process.exec` against a PowerShell command line, so prompt-derived strings escaped through `&`/`|`/`>` despite single-quote wrapping; and [CVE-2026-5603](https://dev.to/armor1ai/how-to-check-your-mcp-server-for-cve-2026-5603s-vulnerability-pattern-and-why-shellquote-isnt-gdn) (`@elgentos/magento2-dev-mcp`) showed that `shell-quote`-style escaping designed for Bourne shells fails closed on Windows `cmd.exe`, where metacharacters retain command-separator semantics. For 3.2.1, the harness should include a stored-vs-runtime SSRF differential (preview validates, persist path does not), a multi-tenant header SSRF probe against IMDS and loopback, and a cross-platform command-injection regression that tests every MCP tool on at least one POSIX and one Windows runner so a shell-quote bypass cannot reach production unnoticed.

May 13, 2026 disclosures extend the MCP regression surface from STDIO transports into database adapters. A single bug hunter published three distinct database-MCP issues the same day: Apache Doris MCP CVE-2025-66335 (SQL injection through an unvalidated `db_name` parameter in `exec_query`, fixed in v0.6.1), Apache Pinot MCP v1.1.0 (HTTP transport with no authentication, letting remote callers invoke SQL-execution tools), and Alibaba RDS MCP (information disclosure that Alibaba declined to fix). For 3.2.1, the harness should treat database, search, and data-platform MCP servers the same way as workflow-builder MCPs: replay parameterized SQL through every tool field, attempt unauthenticated tool invocation against the transport, and confirm both upstream fixes and vendor non-fixes are reflected in the deployed allowlist: https://www.theregister.com/security/2026/05/13/bug-hunter-tracks-down-three-serious-mcp-database-flaws-one-left-unpatched/5238916

The May 11-20, 2026 window added three more release-gate signals that argue for binding patch evidence to the release record, not only behavioral evals. [PraisonAI CVE-2026-44338](https://github.com/advisories/GHSA-6rmh-7xcm-cpxj) (CVSS 7.3, affecting 2.5.6-4.6.33, fixed in 4.6.34) shipped a legacy Flask API server with `AUTH_ENABLED = False` hardcoded so `/agents` enumeration and `/chat` workflow execution failed open by design; [Sysdig](https://www.sysdig.com/blog/cve-2026-44338-praisonai-authentication-bypass-in-under-4-hours-and-the-growing-trend-of-rapid-exploitation) and [The Hacker News](https://thehackernews.com/2026/05/praisonai-cve-2026-44338-auth-bypass.html) measured 3 hours 44 minutes 39 seconds from advisory publication (May 11, 2026 13:56 UTC) to the first targeted scan against the exact vulnerable path, with the scanner self-identifying as `CVE-Detector/1.0` — a concrete time-to-exploit data point that any release gate built around "patch within 24 hours" should treat as load-bearing. [Ollama Bleeding Llama CVE-2026-7482](https://www.cyera.com/research/bleeding-llama-critical-unauthenticated-memory-leak-in-ollama) (CVSS 9.1, disclosed May 5, 2026 by Cyera) is a heap out-of-bounds read in the GGUF model loader that lets an unauthenticated caller exfiltrate Ollama's process heap in three API calls with no log entries; [The Hacker News reporting](https://thehackernews.com/2026/05/ollama-out-of-bounds-read-vulnerability.html) and [Cybersecurity News](https://cybersecuritynews.com/ollama-vulnerability-exposes-servers/) note ~300,000 internet-reachable Ollama servers and that the leaked heap regularly contained system prompts, other users' chat fragments, environment variables (often holding OpenAI/Anthropic tokens, database credentials, and cloud secrets), and any PII or PHI flowing through inference jobs. The patch shipped silently in v0.17.1 on Feb 25, 2026 without a security label, so a release gate that only consumes "security-flagged" release notes from upstream missed this for three months. The same Ollama codebase produced a paired Windows auto-updater chain: [CVE-2026-42248](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/05/05/ollama-windows-vulnerabilities-cve-2026-42248-cve-2026-42249/) (signature verification function called but no-op so any downloaded payload runs) and [CVE-2026-42249](https://github.com/advisories/GHSA-7mp6-jmch-f3fh) (path traversal in update local-file path constructed from attacker-controlled HTTP headers), both CVSS 4.0 7.7 on Ollama for Windows 0.12.10-0.17.5, where [CERT Polska](https://cert.pl/en/posts/2026/04/CVE-2026-42248/) took over coordination after the vendor's documented security address stopped responding. For 3.2.3, that argues for binding the inference container's auto-update path and signature verification posture into the same release record as the model digest, since a silently-updated serving runtime is now a category of regression separate from the model artifact itself.

The same window pulled multimodal media and MCP-adapter SSRF/RCE into the regression surface. [Spring AI CVE-2026-22742](https://spring.io/security/cve-2026-22742/) (CVSS 8.6, fixed in 1.0.5 / 1.1.4, March 27 2026) is a SSRF in `BedrockProxyChatModel` when multimodal messages reference user-supplied media URLs: the URL scheme, host, and destination were not validated before server-side fetch, so an attacker could point the multimodal handler at `169.254.169.254/latest/meta-data/` or other internal endpoints; the fix added an allowlist that blocks RFC 1918, loopback, and metadata destinations. [mcp-atlassian CVE-2026-27825](https://arcticwolf.com/resources/blog-uk/cve-2026-27825-critical-unauthenticated-rce-and-ssrf-in-mcp-atlassian/) (CVSS 9.1, fixed in v0.17.0, Feb 24, 2026) chains directly with the previously-listed CVE-2026-27826: the `download_attachment` and `download_content_attachments` tools accepted attacker-supplied target paths with no base-directory or symlink check, so a network-adjacent unauthenticated caller against an HTTP-transport instance bound to `0.0.0.0` could overwrite `~/.ssh/authorized_keys` or `~/.bashrc` in two requests, with the [Pluto Security](https://pluto.security/blog/mcpwnfluence-cve-2026-27825-critical/) writeup citing 4.4K stars and 4M downloads at disclosure. [mcp-browser-agent CVE-2026-5607](https://www.sentinelone.com/vulnerability-database/cve-2026-5607/) is the recurring "vendor non-response" case for the regression suite: `request.params.name` and `request.params.arguments` flowed into URL handlers in `src/handlers.ts` and `src/executor.ts` with no allowlist or SSRF guard, the issue was publicly disclosed, and the vendor never responded — so deployment evidence has to either pin a non-vulnerable version or document compensating egress controls before the harness signs off. For 3.2.1, the practical takeaway is that multimodal SSRF, MCP-adapter path traversal, and unpatched-vendor cases all belong in the same suite as STDIO command injection — and the harness should re-run them on every MCP image refresh, not only on the model change.

On the benchmark side, [MLCommons AILuminate Jailbreak v0.7](https://mlcommons.org/2026/02/jailbreak-0-7/) (Feb 2026, [methodology paper](https://mlcommons.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/MLCommons___Security___Jailbreak_0_7_Paper_Collaborative.pdf)) is the most usable upgrade to the threshold-setting story since v0.5. The release shifts to a mechanism-first taxonomy for single-turn, inference-time jailbreaks and explicitly separates *attack mechanism* (how the bypass is constructed) from *hazard class* (what unsafe behavior it elicits), so a single test attempt can be scored against multiple hazards and a single hazard is sampled across multiple attack families. For 3.2.3, that maps cleanly onto release-gate evidence: store per-mechanism *and* per-hazard pass rates rather than a single aggregate, and require the harness to cover each mechanism family it claims to test rather than relying on whatever attack distribution the underlying prompt set happened to ship with. MLCommons states that v1.0 is on the Q1 2026 roadmap with regular post-1.0 updates for new attack techniques.

An additional release-gate signal landed on March 30, 2026 with [CVE-2026-5023](https://www.sentinelone.com/vulnerability-database/cve-2026-5023/) in the `codebase-mcp` server: the `getCodebase`, `getRemoteCodebase`, and `saveCodebase` functions in `src/tools/codebase.ts` concatenated user-supplied repository paths into shell commands without escaping metacharacters, and the entry remained unpatched at disclosure time. Microsoft's [May 12, 2026 disclosure of MDASH](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/12/defense-at-ai-speed-microsofts-new-multi-model-agentic-security-system-tops-leading-industry-benchmark/) (Microsoft Security multi-model agentic scanning harness) is the first large-scale public example of an agentic evaluation harness used for pre-deployment vulnerability discovery, and the architecture is worth borrowing for 3.2.1 evidence design. MDASH orchestrates more than 100 specialized AI agents across an ensemble of frontier and distilled models in three explicit stages: a `Prepare` stage that ingests source and threat models, a `Scan` stage where auditor agents emit findings with hypotheses, and a `Prove` stage that constructs and executes inputs to demonstrate exploitability end-to-end. Microsoft reports MDASH scored 88.45% on the public CyberGym benchmark (topping the leaderboard above Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview), 100% recall on `tcpip.sys` MSRC cases and 96% on `clfs.sys`, and zero false positives on a 21-case private test driver; the harness contributed 16 vulnerabilities to May 2026 Patch Tuesday including four Critical RCE issues (one double-free in `ikeext.dll`). For 3.2.3, that is a useful template for documenting harness coverage as benchmark performance plus retrospective recall against an internal vulnerability corpus; for 3.2.1, the Prepare/Scan/Prove split is a practical pattern for separating model-behavior tests from agent-level exploit construction so a passing eval includes a constructed working input, not only a refusal or classifier verdict: https://thehackernews.com/2026/05/microsofts-mdash-ai-system-finds-16.html

The same May 2026 window highlighted a gap between model evaluation and release-pipeline coverage. [VentureBeat's May 14, 2026 analysis](https://venturebeat.com/security/supply-chain-incidents-openai-anthropic-meta-release-surface-vendor-questionnaire-matrix) tracked four AI supply-chain incidents in 50 days against OpenAI, Anthropic, and Meta: none targeted the model, and all four exposed CI, dependency, runner, or packaging gaps that no published system card or red-team evaluation had scoped. Benchmark coverage has also widened past 2025-era static suites. PandaGuard / PandaBench (arXiv:2505.13862) is a standardized harness across ~3B tokens and 49 open and closed-source LLMs under combinations of jailbreak attacks and defenses, useful as a reference distribution when teams set their own pass/fail thresholds. SafeDialBench (arXiv:2502.11090) is a fine-grained multi-turn dialogue safety benchmark with diverse jailbreak attacks, which complements the single-turn vs multi-turn Promptfoo data point already used to size 3.2.1 thresholds. For 3.2.1, those are the benchmarks worth replaying against deployed agents and MCP wrappers, not only the model under test: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.13862 and https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.22037 and https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.11090 and https://arxiv.org/html/2605.10779v1 and https://arxiv.org/pdf/2410.09024 and https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.14866

The April 15, 2026 OX Security disclosure also tracked the root issue as CVE-2026-30623 in Anthropic's MCP SDK STDIO transport: `StdioServerParameters` runs whatever command field it is handed, and the same configuration-to-command path exists across the official Python, TypeScript, Java, and Rust SDKs. OX estimates roughly 200,000 publicly accessible server instances and 150 million SDK downloads in scope, and Anthropic has characterized the behavior as a documented design choice rather than a flaw, leaving sanitization to downstream implementers. For 3.2.3, deployment evidence should record the exact MCP SDK version and the downstream patch level used at release time, not only the model digest: https://docs.litellm.ai/blog/mcp-stdio-command-injection-april-2026 and https://thehackernews.com/2026/04/anthropic-mcp-design-vulnerability.html

- Confirm every safety threshold is tied to a model version or digest, an eval suite version, and a deployment target.
- Trigger a failing Promptfoo, Giskard, or custom eval and verify the release stops before registry promotion or traffic shift.
- Run at least one agent test that uses a malicious tool response, not only malicious user input.
- Run at least one MCP adapter test that tries to modify command arguments, issue loopback or metadata-service requests, and inject a malicious external-server configuration.
- Include a model-loader regression that attempts to load a hostile Hugging Face-style repository with `--trust-remote-code=False` and confirms the serving stack (e.g., vLLM v0.18.0+, Triton, or equivalent) refuses to execute `auto_map` modules or vendor-specific overrides such as the NemotronVL/Kimi-K25 hardcoded `trust_remote_code=True` path (CVE-2026-22807, CVE-2026-27893).
- Replay the Anthropic MCP SDK STDIO command-field case (CVE-2026-30623) against every wrapper layer -- web UI, code path, configuration file, automation route -- and confirm allowlists, argument validation, and sandboxing block both direct command execution and argument-injection bypasses such as `npx -c`.
- Include an Azure MCP SSRF regression test (CVE-2026-26118) that points URL/resource parameters at 169.254.169.254 and other internal endpoints, and confirm egress controls and identity scope limits prevent managed-identity token exposure during evaluation runs.
- Attempt MCP server registration through every public, admin, project, and workflow-builder endpoint and confirm unauthenticated or low-privilege users cannot add STDIO transports.
- Replay the nginx-ui MCPwn case (CVE-2026-33032) against every MCP-exposed admin or infra tool: send unauthenticated requests to each paired endpoint, fail closed when one side enforces authentication and the other side relies only on an IP allowlist with a default-empty list, and confirm destructive tools (config writes, reloads, restarts) require explicit authentication independent of the read path.
- Include LangChain-ChatChat-style regression tests where a public MCP management path registers a malicious command and waits for later agent activity to execute it.
- Enumerate every method exposed to the model as a kernel/tool function and confirm no internal helper has been tagged by mistake (Semantic Kernel CVE-2026-25592 `[KernelFunction] DownloadFileAsync` pattern); fail closed if a high-risk primitive such as file write, command execution, or path resolution is reachable from a model-callable surface without an explicit allowlist.
- Replay AST-bypass payloads against every filter, query, and expression parameter the model can populate, including dunder-traversal payloads that would reach `eval()` or equivalent (Semantic Kernel `InMemoryVectorStore` CVE-2026-26030 pattern); pin the framework version (`semantic-kernel` >= 1.39.4 Python, Semantic Kernel .NET >= 1.71.0) in the release evidence record.
- Probe any model-serving broker that exposes a ZMQ, gRPC, or HTTP control channel with unauthenticated pickle and JSON payloads (SGLang CVE-2026-3059/CVE-2026-3060 pattern); fail closed when the deployment image ships an affected SGLang 0.5.5-0.5.9 release without the broker bound to loopback or wrapped in authenticated proxy.

- Include LLM application framework regression tests: a LangChain serialization probe (CVE-2025-68664) that drives prompt-injected `additional_kwargs`/`response_metadata` through any `dumps()`/`dumpd()` path, a prompt-template path-traversal probe (CVE-2026-34070) against every loader that accepts a config-supplied path, and a LangGraph checkpoint SQL injection probe (CVE-2025-67644) against metadata-filter keys, confirming the deployed `langchain-core` version is at or above 1.2.22.
- Bind the model digest, signature, harness version, runner attestation, and packaging-pipeline scan results into the same release record so a missing or stale source map, leaked artifact, or hijacked release pipeline (Anthropic Claude Code v2.1.88 source-map disclosure, May 11, 2026 Mini Shai-Hulud `@tanstack/*` worm) blocks deployment even when model benchmarks pass.
- Include an unsigned-or-typosquat model-loader regression test (Hugging Face Open-OSS/privacy-filter, May 7, 2026): confirm the inference container refuses to fetch or execute unsigned weights, that network egress during model resolution is denied by default, and that a signature mismatch fails closed before any `loader.py` or `auto_map` module runs.
- Include both single-turn and multi-turn (Hydra-style) jailbreak suites and store per-category pass rates so an aggregate score cannot mask a category collapse.
- Run at least one coding-agent test where hostile repository content, issue text, or MCP output attempts to change local MCP JSON, smuggle shell expansion through a read-only classifier, or bypass a command allowlist.
- Keep MCP server allowlists, OAuth scopes, and tool-call audit logs in the same evidence packet as test results.
- Block releases when the evaluation harness or agent workflow builder has an active-exploited critical vulnerability, even if model benchmark thresholds pass.
- Require a dependency-patch gate for the validation harness itself, including workflow builders, browser drivers, MCP gateways, model-serving adapters, and custom eval providers.
- Store one negative-control evidence packet showing that a critical finding in the harness stack blocks deployment independently of the model's benchmark score.
- Confirm the validation stack cannot read unrelated production secrets during tests and that outbound callbacks from eval hosts are denied by default.
- Re-run the same suite against compressed, fine-tuned, or adapter-composed artifacts and store separate results for each digest.

- Send unauthenticated probes against every model-serving HTTP endpoint that loads attacker-supplied artifacts (GGUF, ONNX, SafeTensors, multimodal media) and confirm the deployed server refuses or returns a deterministic error rather than echoing process heap (Ollama Bleeding Llama CVE-2026-7482 pattern); pin the serving image to a known-good version even when upstream release notes do not flag the patch as a security fix.
- Include a signed-update regression for the inference container's auto-updater path: serve a tampered payload from a man-in-the-middle position, an unsigned binary, and HTTP headers that try to traverse outside the update directory (Ollama Windows auto-updater CVE-2026-42248 / CVE-2026-42249 pattern), and fail closed if the updater either accepts the payload or writes outside the expected directory.
- Send unauthenticated probes against every cloud-platform MCP wrapper -- not only the previously listed Azure MCP SSRF (CVE-2026-26118) but the distinct missing-authentication case in `@azure-devops/mcp` (CVE-2026-32211) -- and confirm a reverse proxy, firewall rule, or explicit auth middleware blocks the call before the tool reaches work items, repos, pipelines, or pull-request data.
- Run the MCP STDIO regression with a non-admin Bearer token, not only with anonymous calls, so authenticated configuration-data execution paths (WeKnora CVE-2026-22688 `stdio_config.command`/`args` injection through `/test`) fail closed before a low-privilege role can reach a subprocess sink.
- Grep every MCP tool's log output and the model-serving runtime's internal index for bearer tokens, OAuth secrets, session cookies, and authorization headers (Splunk MCP CVE-2026-20205 `_internal` index clear-text logging pattern); deployment evidence should bind both the MCP app version and the redaction policy in effect at release time.
- Add OpenAgentSafety (ICLR 2026, OpenHands scaffold with 350+ multi-turn tasks across eight risk categories in a sandboxed Docker environment) and DoomArena (configurable threat-model plug-in framework for web and tool-calling agents) to the 3.2.1 harness rotation when AgentDojo, LITMUS, AgentHarm, and OS-Harm still leave coverage gaps in long-horizon, multi-tool, multi-intent scenarios.
- Treat the LLM gateway itself as a release-gate target, not only the model behind it: replay a full stdio server configuration (`command`/`args`/`env`) against every MCP test or preview route -- with and without credentials -- and confirm the deployed image is patched (LiteLLM CVE-2026-42271 / Starlette CVE-2026-48710 unauthenticated-RCE chain, on the CISA KEV catalog as of June 8, 2026); fail closed when the gateway version is in the affected range even if the model benchmarks pass.
- Extend the model-loader regression beyond the serving framework to the base library: attempt to load a hostile repository whose `config.json` carries underscore-prefixed kernel or implementation keys (e.g., `_attn_implementation_internal`) with remote-code trust disabled, confirm the deployed `transformers` is at or above 5.3.0 (Hugging Face Transformers CVE-2026-4372), and force the agent-framework Docker-sandbox fail-open path (CrewAI CVE-2026-2287/CVE-2026-2275) plus a poisoned GGUF `tokenizer.chat_template` against any rerank/serving endpoint (SGLang CVE-2026-5760).

The June 2026 window opened with the clearest example yet that the validation/serving stack -- not the model -- is the live attack surface. [LiteLLM CVE-2026-42271](https://thehackernews.com/2026/06/litellm-flaw-cve-2026-42271-exploited.html) (CVSS 8.7, affecting 1.74.2 through 1.83.6, fixed in 1.83.7) is a command injection in the MCP preview endpoints `POST /mcp-rest/test/connection` and `POST /mcp-rest/test/tools/list`: both accepted a full stdio server configuration -- `command`, `args`, and `env` -- in the request body and spawned it as a subprocess with no validation. Horizon3.ai chained it with [CVE-2026-48710](https://cybelangel.com/blog/itellm-vulnerability-cve-2026-42271/) (CVSS 6.5), a Starlette host-header validation bypass, to convert the authenticated command-injection into unauthenticated RCE (combined CVSS 10.0), and CISA added CVE-2026-42271 to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on June 8, 2026. This is the same configuration-data-as-execution-sink pattern as the WeKnora and Anthropic SDK STDIO cases, but it lands inside an LLM gateway that many teams run as part of the eval and serving path itself -- so for 3.2.1 the harness should treat the gateway's own MCP test/preview routes as a release-gate target, replay the stdio-config body against them with and without credentials, and fail closed when the deployed gateway image still ships an affected LiteLLM build.

Model-loader trust bypasses continue to surface above the serving framework, now in the most widely used library. [Hugging Face Transformers CVE-2026-4372](https://www.csoonline.com/article/4181094/hugging-face-transformers-rce-flaw-enables-stealthy-compromise-via-ai-model-configs.html) (disclosed June 4, 2026 by Pluto Security, affecting 4.56.0 through 5.2.x, fixed in 5.3.0) abuses the config loader's use of `setattr` over every key in a model's `config.json`, including underscore-prefixed fields that look like internal implementation details. An attacker injects `_attn_implementation_internal`, which slips past `trust_remote_code=False`; `hub_kernels.py` then treats the value as an owner/repo reference, downloads attacker code, and executes it with "no sandboxing, no code signing, no integrity verification, no user prompt" and no runtime warning. This is the same class as the vLLM `auto_map`/`trust_remote_code` cases (CVE-2026-22807, CVE-2026-27893) but at the base-library layer, so for 3.2.1 and 3.2.2 the loader regression test should attempt to load a hostile repo whose `config.json` carries underscore-prefixed kernel/implementation keys with remote-code trust disabled, and the release record should pin the exact `transformers` version (>= 5.3.0) alongside the model digest -- a compressed or fine-tuned artifact swapped in later cannot inherit the base verdict if it re-resolves config through an older library.

Two more agent-framework cases reinforce that "sandboxed" tool execution fails open under prompt injection. CrewAI (CERT/CC [VU#221883](https://kb.cert.org/vuls/id/221883), [ThaiCERT writeup](https://www.thaicert.or.th/en/2026/04/02/multiple-vulnerabilities-in-crewai-allow-sandbox-escape-and-remote-code-execution-via-prompt-injection/), April 2, 2026) bundles four flaws reachable via direct or indirect prompt injection against an agent using the Code Interpreter Tool: CVE-2026-2287/CVE-2026-2275 let the interpreter silently fall back to an insecure in-process mode if Docker becomes unavailable mid-session, allowing arbitrary native calls via `ctypes`; CVE-2026-2286 is SSRF through RAG search tools that do not validate runtime URLs; and CVE-2026-2285 is arbitrary local file read in the JSON loader tool. SGLang [CVE-2026-5760](https://kb.cert.org/vuls/id/915947) (CERT/CC VU#915947, CVSS 9.8, public PoC against 0.5.9, unpatched at coordination close) is a Jinja2 server-side template injection in the reranker: `getjinjaenv()` builds a plain `jinja2.Environment()` instead of an `ImmutableSandboxedEnvironment`, so a poisoned `tokenizer.chat_template` embedded in a GGUF model renders unsandboxed and runs arbitrary Python when a request hits `/v1/rerank`. For 3.2.1, both argue for harness checks that force the Docker-sandbox fail-open path and that submit a malicious GGUF chat template through the actual rerank/serving endpoint; for 3.2.2, the SGLang case is a concrete reason the compressed-artifact gate has to exercise the GGUF template path, not only accuracy and refusal metrics.

The first publicly documented case of an autonomous LLM agent running the post-exploitation stage landed on May 10, 2026. Sysdig observed an in-the-wild intrusion that exploited an internet-exposed Marimo notebook server ([CVE-2026-39987](https://thehackernews.com/2026/05/attackers-use-llm-agent-for-post.html), pre-auth RCE affecting Marimo <= 0.20.4, patched in 0.23.0) and then handed post-exploitation to an LLM agent: it harvested two cloud credentials, pulled an SSH key from AWS Secrets Manager, opened eight parallel SSH sessions to a bastion, and exfiltrated a full PostgreSQL schema in under two minutes, with agentic tells including adaptive recon, `---` command delimiters with stderr suppression, and a leaked non-English comment. For 3.2.1, the takeaway is that an AI-adjacent notebook or eval surface left exposed is now a launch point for agent-driven lateral movement, so the harness should treat the eval/notebook hosts themselves as in-scope and verify they fail closed on unauthenticated code execution.

On the tooling side, NVIDIA shipped [garak v0.15.0 (May 1, 2026) and v0.15.1 (June 5, 2026)](https://github.com/NVIDIA/garak/releases). v0.15.0 adds a multi-turn GOAT probe, an "Agent breaker" probe that targets the tools exposed to an LLM agent, a system-prompt-extraction probe, NeMo Guardrails server support, and mTLS client-cert auth for the REST generator; v0.15.1 adds ProPILE PII-leakage probes, `simonw/llm` generator support, and JSON/file output modes for CI consumption. (v0.14.1 earlier added bootstrap confidence intervals on attack-success rates and a ModernBERT refusal detector.) For 3.2.1 and 3.2.2, the Agent breaker and GOAT probes are worth adding to the gated suite, and the confidence-interval output gives a defensible way to set a threshold on attack-success rate rather than a single-run number. MITRE also moved ATLAS to a monthly cadence: the [atlas-data v2026.05 release](https://github.com/mitre-atlas/atlas-data/releases) (May 27, 2026, following the May 6 Secure AI v2 expansion that added 45+ techniques and 20+ case studies) tags every technique with platform labels including a distinct Agentic AI platform and moves to data format v6.0.0 with Pydantic schemas and a REST API -- so a versioned harness can programmatically pin the ATLAS data version it tested against and scope agent/MCP/RAG coverage per platform for 3.2.1 evidence.

Fresh benchmark and harness-audit work continued to fill the agent-evaluation gap. [HarnessAudit / HarnessAudit-Bench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.14271) (arXiv:2605.14271, May 14, 2026) audits full agent execution trajectories -- not only final outputs -- across boundary compliance, execution fidelity, and system stability, with 210 tasks over eight domains in single- and multi-agent configurations; evaluating 10 harness configurations, the authors find task completion does not guarantee safe execution and that violations compound as trajectories lengthen, with resource access and inter-agent information transfer as the primary failure areas. The UK AISI / Arcadia Impact [Inspect Evals](https://github.com/UKGovernmentBEIS/inspect_evals) collection added agentic-security evals -- AgentThreatBench (resilience to OWASP agentic threats such as memory poisoning and autonomy hijacking, scoring both utility and security), CodeIPI (indirect prompt injection embedded in software artifacts for coding agents), and the b3 "Backbone Breaker Benchmark" -- and from May 8, 2026 moved community contributions to an automated, bot-validated `/register/` submission flow, a concrete versioned-eval-harness registry pattern for 3.2.1 and 3.2.3. MLCommons also operationalized third-party gating with its [AILuminate Global Assurance Program](https://mlcommons.org/2026/02/ailuminate-global-assurance/) (announced Feb 19, 2026, backed by a coalition including KPMG, Google, Microsoft, and Qualcomm), which runs private, non-saturated safety benchmarks as "practice" and "official" testing and emits an AILuminate Risk Label -- a benchmarking-as-a-service pass/fail pattern for 3.2.1 and 3.2.3 -- alongside an [Agentic Product Maturity Ladder](https://mlcommons.org/ailuminate/agentic/) in early release-candidate form.

The compression-safety evidence for 3.2.2 also strengthened. [Quantization Undoes Alignment](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15208) (arXiv:2605.15208, May 2, 2026) tests Qwen2.5-7B, Mistral-7B, and Phi-3.5-mini from BF16 down to 3-bit and finds that even when perplexity rises less than 0.5% at 8-bit and under 3% at 4-bit, 2.5-5.6% of items develop new stereotypical or biased behavior at 4-bit and 6-21% at 3-bit, with "unknown"-answer selection dropping 17.4% -- a dose-response relationship confirmed by logistic regression and direct evidence that an accuracy-preserving quantization can still erode safety. [Aligned but Fragile](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29396) (arXiv:2605.29396, May 28, 2026) shows safety alignment degrades under lightweight parameter- and activation-level perturbations, more severely under W4A4 quantization, and proposes a zeroth-order-optimization training pass to harden alignment against such perturbations. Both reinforce that a passing accuracy re-eval is not a substitute for re-running the safety and bias suite on the exact compressed digest. The defense side is starting to catch up: [Critical Weight Protection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.12033) (arXiv:2601.12033, Jan 17, 2026) identifies and preserves fairness- and safety-critical weights during quantization and mitigates the degradation without expensive retraining, and [Contrastive Alignment Quantization (CAQ)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.07842) (arXiv:2511.07842) adds a contrastive alignment loss that steers the quantized model toward its safety-tuned version while diverging from the unaligned pre-trained reference, enabling robust 4-bit quantization where standard PTQ loses alignment. Neither is yet packaged into the mainstream quantization toolchains (llama.cpp, AutoGPTQ, AutoAWQ, bitsandbytes), so for 3.2.2 the practical gate is still detect-and-block: re-run the full safety suite on the compressed digest and treat a mitigation pass as an extra step rather than an assumed default.

On the standards side, the EU Digital Omnibus on AI moved from the May 7 political agreement toward formal adoption: Coreper endorsed the provisional agreement on May 13, 2026 and the IMCO/LIBE committees adopted it 93-4-15 on June 2, 2026, ahead of a plenary vote in the June session, with the new high-risk compliance dates (Annex III stand-alone systems 2 December 2027, Annex I embedded systems 2 August 2028, AI-content marking 2 December 2026) now the deadlines release gates for high-risk AI must be planned against ([European Parliament legislative train](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/legislative-train/package-digital-package/file-digital-omnibus-on-ai)). The standards that will carry presumption of conformity also reached concrete stage-gates: per the [CEN-CENELEC newsletter](https://www.cencenelec.eu/news-events/news/2026/newsletter/ots-73-etuc/) (May 27, 2026), prEN 18286 (Quality Management System for EU AI Act purposes) entered Formal Vote and prEN 18228 (AI Risk Management, supporting Article 9) went to Public Enquiry through end of July 2026, with prEN 18283 (Managing Bias in AI) still in drafting -- these are the QMS, risk-management, and documented-testing standards that will define the validation and audit evidence high-risk providers must produce for 3.2.1 and 3.2.3.
