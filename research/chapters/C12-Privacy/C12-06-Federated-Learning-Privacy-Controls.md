# C12.6: Federated Learning with Privacy Controls

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-05-14 (updated with verifiable gradient-inversion attack evidence, NDSS 2026 MIA defenses, adaptive clipping research, and NVIDIA FLARE 2.7.2 security notes)

## Purpose

Apply differential privacy and poisoning-resistant aggregation to federated learning (FL) to protect individual participant data. FL avoids centralizing raw data, but gradient updates still leak sensitive information. Zhu et al. (2019) demonstrated pixel-accurate reconstruction of training images from shared gradients; Deng et al. (2021) showed text data can be recovered from BERT-style gradients. Research has accelerated since: the 2025 COLING paper "Exposing Text Data through Discrete Optimization" pushed text reconstruction exact-match rates up by 39% on TinyBERT-6, 20% on BERT-base, and 15% on BERT-large over prior baselines, and the ICCV 2025 paper "Geminio: Language-Guided Gradient Inversion Attacks" introduced attacks that use natural-language prompts to target specific content in gradients. MMGIA (IJCAI 2025) exploits intermodal correlations to invert gradients in multimodal FL, widening the attack surface beyond single-modality classifiers. Parameter-efficient FL fine-tuning is no longer a safe privacy shortcut: CVPR 2025 PEFTLeak reconstructed local samples from adapter gradients, and MineGrad (AISTATS 2026, last modified May 2, 2026) extends gradient inversion to LoRA fine-tuning across language and vision tasks when a malicious server controls the pretrained model or fine-tuning parameters. A 2025 medical-imaging study on retinal federated learning reconstructed images well enough to re-identify 92% of participants — a useful reality check for "FL is private by design" claims. The IEEE TPAMI 2026 survey "Exploring the Vulnerabilities of Federated Learning: A Deep Dive into Gradient Inversion Attacks" now organizes the field into optimization-based (OP-GIA), generation-based (GEN-GIA), and analytics-based (ANA-GIA) families and is the most current taxonomy reference. Two 2026 attacks reset the bar: ARES (Gong, Zhang et al., IEEE S&P 2026) recovers training samples from large batches by formulating activation recovery as a noisy sparse-recovery (generalized Lasso) problem, working without architectural modifications and significantly outperforming prior attacks at realistic FL batch sizes; and a verifiable gradient-inversion construction (arXiv 2604.15063, 2026) lets an attacker prove a successful reconstruction to a third party. USENIX Security 2026's CoFedMID paper shifts the membership-inference review question toward full training trajectories: it reports that client-by-client defenses can fail against trajectory-based MIAs and proposes coalition-level perturbation that cancels at aggregation time while limiting local memorization.

As of May 2026, tabular and operational FL deployments deserve the same inversion scrutiny as vision and language systems. The April 2026 VGIA paper shows exact tabular record recovery can be accompanied by a correctness certificate, so auditors should not treat "hard to visually inspect" reconstructions as failed attacks. NDSS 2026's distillation and contribution-aware aggregation defense is also a useful signal: current MIA reviews should test passive and active attacks, not only final-model membership probes.

Google's Gboard remains the canonical production reference: as of 2024-2025, all production Gboard neural language models trained on user data ship with formal DP guarantees via DP-FTRL and its successor BLT-DP-FTRL, with more than twenty deployed models achieving ρ-zCDP in the range ρ ∈ (0.2, 2) and two additionally using secure aggregation. Apple's Private Cloud Compute (PCC) takes a different path: end-to-end encrypted on-device-to-enclave inference on Apple Silicon with verifiable transparency, no persistent storage, and stateless computation — sidestepping gradient sharing entirely rather than hardening it. NIST SP 800-226 "Guidelines for Evaluating Differential Privacy Guarantees" was finalized in March 2025 and is now the baseline reference for evaluating ε claims, including for privacy-preserving machine learning; it introduces the "DP pyramid" and a catalog of privacy hazards that practitioners commonly miss. The February 2026 JAX-Privacy paper and current 1.1 documentation make this more operational by packaging batch selection, clipping, noise addition, accounting, and auditing primitives in one JAX/Keras-oriented stack. Federated Unlearning (FUL) has matured into a recognized subfield (see the 2024-2025 ACM Computing Surveys review and the PoPETs 2025 paper on unlearning clients, features, and samples in vertical FL) but production tooling for certified client removal remains thin.

| **12.6.2** | **Verify that** training metrics shared with the aggregator or with clients are differentially private and never reveal single-client loss or single-client gradients. | 2 | Per-client loss and metric leakage enabling membership inference and property inference. Melis et al. (2019) showed unprotected FL metrics reveal property-level information about participants' data; aggregated-but-unnoised metrics still leak information about outlier clients. USENIX Security 2026 CoFedMID is a useful current signal: trajectory-based MIAs can exploit information across training rounds, so final-round metric aggregation alone is not enough when checkpoints, per-round losses, or rich telemetry are visible. Operations dashboards, A/B logs, and model-quality telemetry are common leak points overlooked by teams that only protected model parameters. | Review the metric-aggregation pipeline and confirm DP noise is applied before reporting. Audit metric dashboards and logging systems for per-client loss values, per-client confusion matrices, per-client sample counts, per-round checkpoint metadata, or client-level update trajectories. For stronger protection, verify a secure-aggregation protocol (Bonawitz et al. 2017 SecAgg or a 2024-2025 successor such as the single-setup SecAgg with forward/backward secrecy in IACR ePrint 2024/1655 and arXiv 2502.08989) hides per-client contributions even from the server; for malicious-participant settings, include newer robust SA designs such as Aion (USENIX Security 2025) in the design review. | Noisy metrics make debugging harder — teams need to balance observability with privacy, typically by running a trusted eval set in the clear on the server. Metric privacy is still widely overlooked in FL deployments. |
| **12.6.3** | **Verify that** federated learning systems implement canary-based privacy auditing to empirically bound privacy leakage, with audit results logged and reviewed per training cycle. | 3 | Vacuous or over-stated theoretical ε guarantees that hide real leakage in production FL. Theoretical DP analysis is conservative and frequently overestimates the privacy that an actual adversary can break — without empirical audit, teams ship "ε = 1" claims that come from loose composition, miss-applied sub-sampling amplification, or accountant bugs. CANIFE (Maddock, Sablayrolles, Stock, ICLR 2023) showed empirical per-round ε in real FL pipelines can be 4-5× lower than the theoretical bound, while related work (Steinke, Nasr, Jagielski — "Privacy Auditing with One (1) Training Run," NeurIPS 2023 outstanding paper) and "Auditing f-Differential Privacy in One Run" (NeurIPS 2024) show the inverse failure mode: cases where empirical ε̂ exceeds claimed ε, exposing real bugs. The 2025 "How Well Can DP Be Audited in One Run?" analysis and "Tight Privacy Audit in One Run" (arXiv 2509.08704, Sep 2025) further demonstrate that earlier one-run audits can fail to give meaningful results under specific (ε, δ) configurations — meaning even a "passing" audit may be uninformative if the test setup is weak. Bhuekar's February 2026 Scientific Reports paper tightens DP-SGD auditing in the hidden-state threat model and shows why auditors need to record whether intermediate checkpoints, metrics, and optimizer state are observable. Maps to MITRE ATLAS membership-inference and exfiltration techniques (AML.T0024 family). | Require an audit report per training cycle that estimates ε̂ as an empirical lower bound at a stated confidence level (typically 95% via Clopper-Pearson on the binomial test in canary-detection auditing, or f-DP confidence intervals as in arXiv 2410.22235). Confirm the methodology — random gradient canaries on the unit sphere with cosine-similarity test statistic (Andrew, Kairouz et al., "One-shot Empirical Privacy Estimation for Federated Learning," ICLR 2024 / arXiv 2302.03098), CANIFE-style crafted canaries (https://github.com/facebookresearch/canife), or metagradient-optimized canaries (arXiv 2507.15836, NeurIPS 2025) — and that the number of canaries scales appropriately (k ≈ √d for the one-shot cosine method on d-dimensional models, ~1000 typical). Verify ε̂ is consistent with the declared theoretical ε within documented tolerance and that any ε̂ > ε triggers a privacy incident. Cross-check against NIST SP 800-226 §6 ("How Should I Evaluate the Privacy of a Particular Use of DP?") which now treats empirical audits as a recommended complement to formal accounting. Reproduce the audit run from source if possible and confirm canaries are inserted with realistic per-round participation patterns (not all canaries every round — that inflates leakage). Also confirm the audit threat model matches production telemetry: releasing per-round checkpoints or rich training metrics can invalidate a hidden-state privacy claim even when only the final model is intended for users. | Empirical ε̂ is a lower bound, not an upper bound — passing an audit does not prove ε ≤ claimed value, only that the specific attack used could not extract more leakage. Audit strength depends heavily on canary quality: random canaries underestimate leakage on low-dimensional models; CANIFE and metagradient canaries (Boglioni-Liu-Ilyas-Wu 2025, >2× improvement over random in some regimes) tighten bounds at the cost of compute. Practical auditing libraries: Meta's open-source canife (https://github.com/facebookresearch/canife) targets DP-FL specifically, JAX-Privacy 1.1 (Google DeepMind documentation; arXiv 2602.17861) ships clipping/noise/accounting/auditing primitives in one library, TensorFlow Privacy 0.12.3 splits into `tensorflow-privacy` and `tensorflow-empirical-privacy` for the audit side, and Trail of Bits' PrivacyRaven covers membership inference and model extraction (centralized but reusable patterns). Flower and NVIDIA FLARE provide DP training but no built-in canary auditing as of 2026 — teams currently bolt on Opacus + canife or one-shot canary clients themselves. Sequential auditing (arXiv 2509.07055, 2025) can detect violations within a few hundred gradient steps, useful for fast feedback during development. Honest reporting: many production FL deployments do not audit at all; even "we use DP" announcements rarely include ε̂ numbers. EU AI Act Article 27 FRIA + GDPR Article 35 DPIA increasingly expect documented privacy testing, not just paper proofs, for high-risk deployments that process personal data. |
| **12.6.4** | **Verify that** formal proofs document the overall ε budget and the corresponding utility loss against a declared baseline. | 3 | Excessive utility loss from privacy mechanisms incentivizing teams to weaken or disable them. Without a formal analysis that ties ε to measured utility, DP settings tend to drift toward looser values over time. This is also the requirement that surfaces epsilon-inflation bugs — naive composition, missed sub-sampling amplification, hidden checkpoint release, and double-counted queries all produce falsely small reported ε. | Request a formal privacy analysis with a utility-impact assessment. Verify ε is computed using a tight accountant — Rényi DP (RDP), zero-concentrated DP (zCDP), Gaussian DP, or Privacy Loss Distributions (PLD) — not naive composition. Reproduce the accountant run from source if possible. Compare model accuracy (task-specific metrics: F1, AUC, perplexity, BLEU, NDCG) between DP and non-DP runs and confirm utility loss is within the threshold. Walk through NIST SP 800-226 (finalized March 2025) and its accompanying Jupyter notebooks as the structured evaluation baseline. Cross-check against Google's Gboard production numbers (ρ-zCDP with ρ ∈ (0.2, 2), dozens of models in production as of 2024-2025) for expected privacy-utility frontiers on language tasks. For DP-SGD claims, require the proof to state whether intermediate models, optimizer state, server-side metrics, and per-round client updates are released; if the proof relies on a hidden-state threat model, verify production logging actually preserves that assumption. | The 5% threshold is application-dependent: safety-critical models may not tolerate 1%, recommender systems can tolerate 10%+. Tight accountants (RDP, Gaussian DP, PLD) can reduce reported ε by an order of magnitude vs. naive composition. DP-FTRL, BLT-DP-FTRL, and DP-Adam optimizers improve the frontier; large-batch training and synthetic-data pretraining (as in Google's Gboard 2024-2025 work) push it further. The February 2026 JAX-Privacy paper is useful reviewer evidence because it documents modular primitives for batch selection, clipping, noise addition, accounting, and auditing; its current docs still warn that users must compose the right pieces for their mechanism, and the higher-level Keras API currently covers DP-SGD with Poisson-sampled batches. For vertical FL and feature/sample-level unlearning, PoPETs 2025 (popets-2025-0048) gives evaluation methodology. Honest reporting: many public "ε = 1" claims come from loose composition — require auditability of the accounting, not just the final number. EU AI Act high-risk systems (Annex III, Article 27) and GDPR Article 35 DPIAs make the ε/utility trade-off part of governance evidence, not just a modeling artifact. |

## Notable Incidents and Research Signals

- **March 2026:** ARES showed that large-batch FL remains vulnerable when activation recovery can be framed as sparse recovery; auditors should treat "large batch" as a utility choice, not a privacy control by itself.
- **April 2026:** VGIA showed that gradient inversion against tabular FL can produce verifiable record-level reconstructions, including exact recovery in large-batch regimes where older attacks either failed or could not certify success.
- **NDSS 2026:** A distillation and contribution-aware aggregation defense targeted both passive and active membership inference, reinforcing that FL privacy reviews need adversarial-client and malicious-aggregator test cases.
- **April 2026 NVIDIA FLARE SDK:** NVIDIA disclosed authorization-bypass, deserialization, and path-traversal issues in FLARE and recommended v2.7.2 or later; FL privacy controls depend on the orchestration plane being patched, not just the DP math being correct.
- **2025 medical imaging FL:** retinal-image reconstruction work reported participant re-identification from inverted gradients, reinforcing that regulated healthcare FL needs empirical inversion tests, not only architectural claims about data staying local.
- **February 2026:** hidden-state DP-SGD auditing work tightened the review question around checkpoint and telemetry release: the same ε claim can mean different things depending on whether intermediate states are observable.
- **USENIX Security 2026:** CoFedMID showed that trajectory-based membership inference remains a live FL risk even when final model exposure is controlled; reviews should treat per-round telemetry as part of the privacy boundary.

## Implementation Maturity

- **DP training and accounting:** Mature for PyTorch/JAX/TensorFlow stacks when clipping, noise, sampling, and accountant configuration are reviewable and reproducible.
- **Federated DP integration:** Mature enough for Flower, TensorFlow Federated, and NVIDIA FLARE pilots, but still configuration-sensitive; verify whether DP is local, central server-side, or central client-side before accepting the ε claim.
- **Adaptive clipping:** Moving quickly but not standardized. PAC-DP (March 2026) and DP-FedPUAC (April 2026) improve the privacy-utility trade-off with personalized or per-update clipping, but auditors should require a reproducible accountant run and ablation against fixed clipping before accepting the tuning.
- **Canary auditing:** Emerging but practical for high-risk systems; CANIFE, one-shot canaries, JAX-Privacy auditing primitives, and TensorFlow empirical privacy tooling provide starting points, but auditors still need to tune the threat model and canary design.
- **Certified federated unlearning:** Research-to-early implementation; useful for deletion evidence, but not yet a turnkey control across mainstream FL platforms.

## Tooling

- **FL frameworks:** Flower (v1.29.0, Apr 11, 2026; fixed/adaptive central DP wrappers, `LocalDpMod`, and SecAgg/SecAgg+ support), TensorFlow Federated, NVIDIA FLARE (2.7.x docs, Apr 24, 2026 client API pattern, and Apr 28, 2026 security bulletin requiring v2.7.2 or later; DP + HE + PSI, with 2025 biopharma deployments documented in J. Biopharm Stat.), PySyft (OpenMined), FedML.
- **DP libraries:** Opacus (Meta-PyTorch, Fast Gradient Clipping and Ghost Clipping reduce DP-SGD memory; LoRA+peft+DP-SGD tutorial Dec 2024), TensorFlow Privacy 0.12.3 (per-example gradient clipping in Keras; the library is now split into `tensorflow-privacy` for DP training and `tensorflow-empirical-privacy` for empirical analysis), JAX-Privacy 1.1 documentation plus the February 2026 arXiv paper (Google DeepMind/Google Research — one stack for clipping, noise addition, batch selection, accounting, and empirical auditing, with Keras DP-SGD/LoRA examples), Google's DP library, OpenMined PyDP.
- **Privacy auditing libraries:** Meta `canife` (https://github.com/facebookresearch/canife — crafted-canary DP-FL auditing, modified FLSim base), Trail of Bits PrivacyRaven (membership inference, label-only black-box model extraction), JAX-Privacy auditing module (one-shot empirical ε̂), `tensorflow-empirical-privacy` (membership-inference attack reports), and reference implementations of one-shot cosine-canary auditing (Andrew, Kairouz et al. 2024) and metagradient canary optimization (NeurIPS 2025).
- **Secure aggregation:** Original Bonawitz SecAgg (2017), single-setup SecAgg with forward/backward secrecy (arXiv 2502.08989, 2025), Aion (USENIX Security 2025; robust multi-round single-mask SA for malicious participants), verifiable SecAgg variants (ScienceDirect 2025). FastLloyd (USENIX Security 2025) is narrower because it targets federated k-means, but it is a useful example of combining secure aggregation with central DP without falling back to local-DP utility loss.
- **Robust aggregation:** Multi-Krum, Trimmed-Mean, coordinate-wise median, Bulyan, FLTrust, FedGreed (arXiv 2508.18060), GAN-based defenses (arXiv 2503.20884), and DP-BREM+ (USENIX Security 2025) for cross-silo settings that need differential privacy, Byzantine robustness, and secure aggregation together.
- **Privacy-preserving inference alternatives:** Apple Private Cloud Compute (Apple Silicon + Secure Enclave + verifiable transparency + stateless computation), Azure Confidential Computing, AWS Nitro Enclaves, GCP Confidential Space, GPU-accelerated FHE for LLM inference (OpenFHE — as of 2026, practical for medium-sized models or short sequences, still far slower than cleartext).
- **Federated unlearning:** Still mostly research-stage but maturing fast. See ACM Computing Surveys (2024) and PoPETs 2025 for vertical-FL client/feature/sample removal. Certified federated-recommendation unlearning landed in ACM TOIS 2025 (CFRU). 2026 brings two notable additions: certified unlearning in decentralized FL via Newton-style updates with Fisher-information curvature approximations (arXiv 2601.06436), and the 2F2L / FedRemoval PyTorch implementation (https://github.com/Nanboy-Ronan/2F2L-Federated-Unlearning) which linearizes a pretrained network and runs server-only, communication-free certified deletion via a Newton-style correction over server-held linearized statistics. Still no single widely-adopted library — production deployments typically bolt this on themselves.
