# C1.5: Data Lineage and Traceability

> [Back to C01 Index](C01-Training-Data.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-05-02

## Purpose

When a model produces unexpected or harmful outputs, the first question in incident response is "what data trained this model, and where did it come from?" Without complete lineage records — tracking every transformation, augmentation, merge, and synthetic data generation step — that question is unanswerable. Lineage is also a regulatory survival requirement: the FTC's algorithmic disgorgement pattern (enforcement actions against Everalbum in 2021, Weight Watchers in 2022, Ring and Edmodo in 2023, and Rite Aid and Avast in 2024 — all requiring deletion of models trained on improperly collected data) means organizations that cannot reconstruct a dataset's full journey from source to model input face existential regulatory risk. As the FTC has made clear, if you cannot trace where everything came from and what transformations were applied, the enforcement agency is unlikely to be charitable.

This section requires recording the full provenance chain of each dataset, storing lineage records immutably for audit, and extending lineage tracking to cover synthetic data so that it remains clearly distinguishable from real data throughout the pipeline. As organizations increasingly generate synthetic training data from other models, the risk of inheriting and amplifying upstream poisoning (demonstrated by the Virus Infection Attack and the January 2026 "Basilisk Venom" incident where hidden prompts in GitHub code comments poisoned a fine-tuned model) makes synthetic data lineage tracking especially urgent. The March 2026 LiteLLM/Mercor supply chain breach — where TeamPCP hijacked LiteLLM's PyPI token on March 24, 2026 and published poisoned versions (1.82.7 and 1.82.8) containing auto-executing `.pth` files — cascaded into a 4TB exfiltration from Mercor AI affecting clients including OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google DeepMind. The stolen data included training strategies, labeling protocols, and RLHF methodologies, illustrating how a single dependency without provenance attestation can compromise an entire AI supply chain. Mitiga's April 2026 ["Brainjacked" disclosure](https://www.mitiga.io/blog/brainjacked-planting-a-false-reality-in-the-ai-training-pipeline) demonstrated an adjacent vector: a path traversal vulnerability in a file upload application allowed an attacker to write poisoned training data directly into a production S3 bucket feeding an automated SageMaker retraining pipeline, embedding a backdoor that produced attacker-controlled predictions on trigger inputs — all because the web application's IAM role shared permissions with the training bucket and no lineage integrity validation existed at the retraining trigger point.

The [CISA/NSA/FBI Joint Cybersecurity Information Sheet](https://www.cisa.gov/resources-tools/resources/ai-data-security-best-practices-securing-data-used-train-operate-ai-systems) (May 2025) — co-signed by cybersecurity agencies from the UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, South Korea, and Japan — identifies data provenance tracking and immutable logging as foundational controls for AI data security, specifically calling out data supply chain risks, poisoned data, and data drift as the three primary threat categories. The guidance recommends automated metadata tagging and digital signatures throughout the AI data lifecycle, targeting defense industrial bases, federal agencies, and critical infrastructure operators. A [Kiteworks 2026 Forecast Report](https://www.kiteworks.com/regulatory-compliance/state-ai-legislation-2026-compliance-data-governance/) found that 78% of organizations lack pre-training data validation and 77% lack provenance and lineage capabilities — underscoring how far the industry has to go.

Carnegie Mellon SEI's April 2026 [chain-of-custody analysis](https://www.sei.cmu.edu/blog/data-poisoning-in-ai-models-the-case-for-chain-of-custody-controls/) sharpens the operational point: for a 50,000-image training set, changing 50 images (0.1%) can be enough to create a poisoning effect. The recommended control pattern is not just "log the pipeline," but record who or what handled each data item, hash the data and metadata at each lifecycle step, sign dataset manifests, and require the training job to verify the chain of custody before loading the data. That makes lineage a preventive control, not merely a forensic convenience after a model has already been contaminated.

EU AI Act Article 10 (Data and Data Governance), entering enforcement on 2 August 2026, will make data lineage a hard compliance requirement for all high-risk AI systems. Critically, Article 10 demands *operational evidence* — timestamped, automated records proving that quality and lineage controls were active for a specific dataset at a specific pipeline stage at a specific point in time — not merely governance policies describing intended practices. With the deadline now roughly four months away, organizations deploying high-risk AI face fines up to €35 million or 7% of global revenue for non-compliance — making lineage infrastructure an urgent operational priority, not a future aspiration. ISO/IEC 42001 Annex A.7.5 (Data Provenance) independently requires continuous, automated provenance capture — including origin, consent, transformation logs with script versions and operator IDs, and tamper-resistant storage — making it a useful operational checklist even for organizations not yet subject to the EU AI Act. NIST AI 600-1 (GenAI Profile) similarly identifies provenance as a core risk management action mapped across the Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage functions of the AI RMF.

The OpenTelemetry-based pattern (Feb 2026) provides a practical reference architecture: OTel SDK instrumentation → OTel Collector with SHA-256 hashing → dual export to S3 Object Lock (immutable) + Elasticsearch (queryable), satisfying SOC 2, HIPAA, and EU AI Act audit requirements. Delta Lake and Apache Iceberg provide table-level time-travel and snapshot lineage as a complementary layer — Iceberg's snapshot history is queryable and supports point-in-time audits without redundant data copies. No major open-source lineage tool ships with built-in immutability as of April 2026, so the architectural layering approach remains necessary. |
| 1.5.3 | **Verify that** lineage tracking covers synthetic data generated via augmentation, synthesis, or privacy-preserving techniques. | 2 | Synthetic data contamination obscuring real data quality issues; inability to trace model behaviors to real vs. synthetic training examples; privacy violations if synthetic data retains real-data characteristics without proper tracking. The VIA (Virus Infection Attack) demonstrated how poisoned content propagates through synthetic data pipelines, making lineage of synthetic generation steps critical for containment. Model Autophagy Disorder (MAD) — where models trained recursively on their own synthetic outputs degrade — is another risk that lineage tracking helps detect. LoRA adapter poisoning (emerging 2025-2026) adds a new vector: malicious fine-tuning adapters are small, easy to distribute, and can introduce backdoors indistinguishable from legitimate fine-tuning — lineage must capture adapter provenance alongside data provenance. | Review lineage records for synthetic data generation steps. Verify each synthetic dataset records: the generator model/tool identity and version, the seed/source data used, generation parameters and prompts, and a timestamp. Verify synthetic data carries metadata tags distinguishing it from real data. Check that downstream consumers can filter by data origin type. Adoption in ML training pipelines for tabular and text data is still nascent. OpenDataDiscovery (ODD) is one of the few platforms that gives ML workflows (models, experiments, feature stores) first-class lineage support, though its community is smaller than OpenLineage's. |

For media assets, verify C2PA Content Credentials and the IPTC Photo Metadata Standard 2025.1 fields (`AI System Used`, `AI System Version Used`, `AI Prompt Information`, `AI Prompt Writer Name`) are preserved through resize, compression, and packaging steps. Most synthetic data tools can label their own outputs, but labels are easily lost in notebook exports, ad hoc dataframe joins, feature engineering, and model fine-tuning datasets unless enforced by data contracts. Media provenance is ahead of tabular/text provenance: C2PA and IPTC provide useful fields for images and some AI/ML artifacts, while structured datasets still depend on project-specific columns, sidecar manifests, or catalog tags. Privacy-preserving synthetic data also needs separate risk evidence (membership inference, nearest-neighbor similarity, and attribute disclosure checks); a "synthetic" label is not proof that real personal data cannot be recovered. |

### Immutable Storage Architecture

For organizations that need to satisfy both EU AI Act Article 10 documentation requirements and FTC algorithmic disgorgement defense, the recommended layered approach is:

1. **Event capture**: Instrument pipeline steps with OpenLineage or OpenTelemetry SDK to emit structured lineage events with SHA-256 content hashes
2. **Immutable persistence**: Write events to S3 Object Lock (compliance mode), Azure Immutable Blobs, or GCS with retention policies — pipeline operators must not have write/delete access
3. **Queryable projection**: Dual-export to Elasticsearch or a graph database for search and visualization
4. **Table-level snapshots**: Enable Delta Lake or Iceberg time-travel on all training data tables for point-in-time reconstruction
5. **Attestation**: Generate SLSA v1.1+ provenance attestations for model artifacts and training data snapshots

### Chain-of-Custody Control Pattern

For high-impact models, lineage should be enforced as a chain-of-custody gate before training data is loaded:

- **At creation**: Record source system, collector identity, timestamp, license/consent status, content hash, and metadata hash
- **At transfer**: Sign the transfer record with source, destination, tool identity, and observed checksum
- **At transformation**: Record script or notebook version, operator/service principal, input hashes, output hashes, and validation result
- **At dataset assembly**: Verify every constituent record or file has an unbroken custody chain before signing the dataset manifest
- **At training**: Require the loader to reject any dataset whose manifest, signatures, or hashes fail verification

### Synthetic Data Labeling Strategies

Until a universal standard emerges for non-media synthetic data labeling, practical approaches include:

- **Column-level tagging**: Add a mandatory `data_origin` field (`synthetic | real | augmented`) to every record at creation time, enforced by pipeline validation
- **Metadata sidecars**: JSON/YAML files that travel with datasets through storage and compute
- **Table properties**: Databricks Unity Catalog tags or Delta Lake table properties for dataset-level markers
- **Media assets**: Apply C2PA v2.2 Content Credentials + IPTC 2025.1 XMP fields at generation time; verify preservation through pipeline
- **Batch-level tracking**: Log synthetic-to-real ratios per training batch in experiment tracking systems (MLflow, W&B)

### Federated and Distributed Training Lineage

As organizations adopt federated learning (FL) and edge training, lineage tracking faces new challenges — data never leaves participant nodes, so traditional centralized lineage systems cannot observe the full pipeline. Emerging approaches include:

- **Model snapshot provenance**: Store snapshots at each FL aggregation round with client-level metadata (data volume, distribution statistics, training hyperparameters) to reconstruct how the global model evolved
- **Cryptographic attestation per node**: Each participant signs a lineage attestation covering their local data characteristics and training parameters before contributing gradients — enables post-hoc audit without centralizing raw data
- **Blockchain-anchored FL lineage**: Research prototypes (as of 2026) use blockchain to record per-round aggregation events immutably, though governance capabilities remain limited and real-time auditability is not yet production-grade
- **EDPS guidance**: The European Data Protection Supervisor's [June 2025 TechDispatch on Federated Learning](https://www.edps.europa.eu/data-protection/our-work/publications/techdispatch/2025-06-10-techdispatch-12025-federated-learning_en) highlights that FL does not eliminate provenance obligations — organizations must still demonstrate data quality and representativeness per Article 10, even when data remains decentralized

Gap: No production-ready open-source tool provides end-to-end lineage for federated learning pipelines as of April 2026. This is an active research area with significant regulatory implications as FL adoption grows.

### Poisoning Detection Techniques Enabled by Lineage

When lineage is comprehensive, incident response teams can apply targeted detection at specific pipeline stages:

- **Pre-training**: Per-class feature drift monitoring, label entropy checks, cosine similarity baselines against known-good data distributions
- **During training**: Gradient norm outlier detection (flags samples exerting outsized influence on weights), per-example influence function estimation, suspicious performance gains on narrow data slices
- **Post-training**: Backdoor activation probes with targeted trigger patterns, red-team prompt variants testing semantic preservation across formatting changes
- **Supply chain**: Transparency log monitoring via sigstore for unauthorized model signing events, signed dataset manifests for immutable dataset versioning
