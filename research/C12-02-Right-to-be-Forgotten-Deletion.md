# C12.2: Right-to-be-Forgotten & Deletion Enforcement

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-03-29

## Purpose

Ensure data-subject deletion requests propagate across all AI artifacts and that model unlearning is verifiable. The EDPB selected right to erasure (Art. 17 GDPR) as its 2025 coordinated enforcement priority — it is the most frequently exercised GDPR right and a top complaint category. The EDPB's 2025 CEF report (published February 2026) examined 764 controllers across 32 DPAs and found seven critical compliance gaps: absent documented procedures, inadequate staff training, insufficient data-subject information, misapplied Art. 17(3) exceptions, unclear retention periods, backup deletion failures (50% of DPAs report no backup-specific erasure procedures), and weak anonymization techniques used as substitutes for actual deletion. Nine DPAs launched formal investigations. Machine unlearning — the technical ability to make a model "forget" specific training data — faces a credibility crisis after CMU researchers (Hu et al., ICLR 2025) demonstrated that approximate unlearning methods merely suppress outputs rather than truly forgetting, and that "benign relearning attacks" using small amounts of public data can reverse the process.

## Requirements

| **12.2.1** | **Verify that** data-subject deletion requests propagate to raw datasets, checkpoints, embeddings, logs, and backups within service level agreements of less than 30 days. | 1 | GDPR Article 17 and CCPA deletion right violations. Regulators have issued significant fines for incomplete deletion (e.g., Italian DPA fined Clearview AI EUR 20M for failure to delete biometric data). The EDPB's 2025 coordinated enforcement on right to erasure examined 764 controllers across 32 DPAs and identified seven critical compliance gaps (published February 2026): absent documented procedures (17 DPAs reported this), inadequate staff training (~20% provide no refresher training), insufficient data-subject information, misapplied Art. 17(3) exceptions, unclear retention periods, backup deletion failures (50% of DPAs report no backup-specific erasure procedures), and weak anonymization techniques used as substitutes for actual deletion. Nine DPAs launched formal investigations with ongoing proceedings in Ireland, France, Portugal, Slovenia, and Germany. Erasure complaint volumes are surging: Netherlands 580 in 2024 (18.6% of total), Slovenia Art. 17 complaints rose from 4% (2020) to 19% (2024). | Audit deletion workflow end-to-end: submit test deletion requests, then verify removal from all storage tiers (raw data, feature stores, model checkpoints, vector databases, embeddings caches, logs, backups). Measure time-to-completion against the 30-day SLA. Given the EDPB's 2025 CEF findings, pay special attention to backup deletion procedures and verify that anonymization is not being used as a substitute for actual erasure. Test that deleted data cannot be restored from backups. | Model checkpoints and embeddings are often overlooked in deletion workflows. Backup retention policies may conflict with deletion SLAs — the EDPB's 2025 report found that 50% of DPAs identified controllers lacking specific erasure procedures for backups, with no processes to prevent restoration of deleted data. Vector databases (Pinecone, Weaviate, Milvus) require special handling to remove individual embeddings. Distributed systems with replication add propagation complexity. The EU Digital Omnibus (proposed late 2025) may delay high-risk AI system obligations from August 2026 to December 2027, but the EDPB/EDPS joint opinion (January 2026) warns this delay may harm fundamental rights. |
| **12.2.2** | **Verify that** "machine-unlearning" routines physically re-train or approximate removal using certified unlearning algorithms. | 2 | Residual memorization of deleted data in model weights. Even after data deletion, a model may still generate outputs influenced by the removed data, violating the spirit of erasure rights. A critical ICLR 2025 paper from Carnegie Mellon (Hu et al.) demonstrated that current approximate unlearning methods merely suppress model outputs rather than truly forgetting — "benign relearning attacks" using small amounts of loosely related public data can jog the model's memory and reverse unlearning effects (e.g., relearning on public medical articles caused an unlearned LLM to output harmful bioweapon knowledge). | Review implementation of unlearning algorithms: SISA training (Bourtoule et al., 2021), certified removal mechanisms (Guo et al., 2020), or fine-tuning-based approximate unlearning. As of 2025, the EUPG framework (Efficient Unlearning with Privacy Guarantees, Universitat Rovira i Virgili) offers a promising hybrid approach: train initially on k-anonymity/DP-transformed data, fine-tune on full data for utility, then revert to the privacy-protected checkpoint upon deletion — matching retraining quality at lower cost. Use evaluation frameworks such as Open Unlearning to assess effectiveness. Critically, test resistance to relearning attacks (Hu et al., ICLR 2025) by attempting to restore unlearned knowledge through benign fine-tuning. | Machine unlearning has three crystallized categories (as of 2025): exact unlearning (full retraining/SISA — reliable but costly), approximate unlearning (gradient ascent, Fisher forgetting — fast but lacking formal guarantees), and certified unlearning (provable guarantees — emerging but not scalable). The ICLR 2025 relearning attack results are a serious blow to approximate methods — they show that gradient-ascent-based unlearning is fundamentally obfuscation, not deletion. The EUPG framework and ICML 2025 MUGen workshop represent progress, and the WIPE-OUT 2026 workshop (ECML-PKDD, September 2026 in Naples) continues advancing the field. MMUnlearner (2025) extends unlearning to multimodal LLMs. FedRecovery offers DP-based federated unlearning by removing weighted sums of gradient residuals with calibrated Gaussian noise. Full retraining remains the gold standard but is prohibitively expensive for foundation models. The MUV Workshop at CVPR 2026 (Denver, June 2026) is the first major computer vision venue to dedicate a full workshop to unlearning — covering data-centric, parameter-centric, and training-free approaches — signaling that the field is maturing beyond NLP into multimodal domains. |
| **12.2.3** | **Verify that** shadow-model evaluation proves forgotten records influence less than 1% of outputs after unlearning. | 2 | Incomplete unlearning leaving residual influence from deleted records. Membership inference attacks (Shokri et al., 2017) can detect whether a specific record was in the training set. | Train shadow models on datasets with and without the forgotten records. Compare membership inference attack accuracy, output distributions, and per-sample loss values. The forgotten records should be statistically indistinguishable from never-seen records (influence < 1%). | Shadow-model evaluation is compute-intensive, requiring training multiple reference models. The 1% threshold needs careful statistical testing (e.g., hypothesis tests with sufficient power). For LLMs, measuring "influence on outputs" is harder than for classifiers since output spaces are vast. |
| **12.2.4** | **Verify that** deletion events are immutably logged and auditable for regulators. | 3 | Inability to demonstrate compliance with deletion requests to regulators during audits. Without immutable logs, organizations cannot prove they processed requests within required timeframes. | Inspect audit trail infrastructure: verify logs are append-only (e.g., using write-once storage, blockchain-anchored hashes, or tamper-evident logging like AWS CloudTrail with integrity validation). Confirm logs capture request receipt timestamp, processing steps, and completion confirmation. Attempt log tampering to verify immutability. | Logging deletion events creates a tension: the log itself contains information about the data subject (at minimum, that they existed and requested deletion). Logs must be designed to record compliance actions without creating a new privacy exposure. Retention periods for audit logs must also be defined. |

## Tooling

- **Machine unlearning:** SISA training (Bourtoule et al.), Open Unlearning (unified LLM unlearning evaluation framework), Vision Unlearning (CV-focused), MMUnlearner (multimodal LLMs, 2025), EUPG (Efficient Unlearning with Privacy Guarantees — hybrid k-anonymity/DP pre-training + fine-tuning approach, 2025), FedRecovery (DP-based federated unlearning via gradient residual removal). Approximate unlearning via gradient ascent/influence functions is now considered unreliable after ICLR 2025 relearning attack results. Certified unlearning with provable guarantees is emerging but not yet production-ready for foundation models.
- **Privacy auditing:** DP-Sniper (black-box DP auditing), membership inference attack toolkits (ML-Doctor, privacy meter), canary-based memorization testing
