# C1.2: Training Data Security & Integrity

> [Back to C01 Index](C01-Training-Data.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-16

## Purpose

Restricting access to training data, encrypting it at rest and in transit, and validating its integrity are fundamental controls against tampering, theft, and data poisoning. A compromised training dataset can silently degrade model behavior — and unlike traditional data breaches, the damage persists in model weights long after the poisoned data is removed from storage.

This section covers the full lifecycle of training data security: access controls and audit logging on storage and pipelines, encryption standards, cryptographic integrity verification, automated integrity monitoring, secure purging of obsolete data, and immutable versioning for rollback and forensics. The machine unlearning problem (data embedded in model weights cannot simply be "deleted") makes prevention and detection controls here especially critical — once poisoned data trains a model, remediation options are limited and expensive.

As of April 2026, the threat picture has sharpened considerably. MITRE ATLAS v5.4.0 (February 2026) formalizes `AML.T0020 Poison Training Data` and expanded supply-chain coverage following the October 2025 Zenity Labs collaboration that added 14 agent/GenAI techniques. Check Point's 2026 Tech Tsunami report labels prompt injection and data poisoning the "new zero-day" threats. The Joint Cybersecurity Information Sheet from NSA/CISA/FBI and allies (May 2025) names data supply chain tampering, maliciously modified data, and data drift as the three primary AI data risks, and its 10 recommended practices — quantum-resistant signatures, zero-trust enclaves, provenance checks on third-party data — are increasingly showing up in procurement and audit checklists. EU AI Act Article 10 enforcement against high-risk providers begins **2 August 2026**, forcing timestamped, pipeline-level evidence that data governance controls were actually applied.

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| 1.2.1 | **Verify that** access controls protect training data storage and pipelines. | 1 | Unauthorized access leading to data theft, exfiltration of proprietary datasets, or injection of poisoned samples (MITRE ATLAS `AML.T0020 Poison Training Data`, `AML.T0010 ML Supply Chain Compromise`). The LiteLLM supply-chain compromise (March 2026) is a documented example of `AML.T0048` initial access via an ML dependency. | Review IAM policies on data storage (S3 buckets, GCS, Azure Blob). Verify least-privilege access on pipeline orchestrators (Airflow, Kubeflow, Metaflow). Check for service-account scoping on training nodes. Confirm MFA and conditional access on admin roles touching the dataset bucket. For labelled and RLHF preference stores, verify separate ACLs from raw collection stores. | Standard cloud IAM tooling is mature. The gap is pipeline-level access control: ML orchestrators typically have coarser-grained permissions than storage layers, and most teams don't scope Kubeflow Pipeline ServiceAccounts per-dataset. LoRA/adapter stores (increasingly common in 2025–2026) are a new access-control blind spot — adapters can modify full model behavior but are often treated as low-sensitivity artifacts. |
| 1.2.2 | **Verify that** all access to training data is logged, including user, time, and action. | 1 | Undetected insider threats; inability to perform forensic analysis after a data poisoning incident; compliance violations. The NSA/CISA/FBI Joint CSI (May 2025) flags opaque data access as a core weakness enabling slow-burn poisoning. | Verify cloud audit logs (AWS CloudTrail data events, GCP Cloud Audit Logs, Azure Monitor diagnostic settings) are enabled for data storage. Check that pipeline access (reads/writes) is captured at the orchestrator layer, not just storage. Confirm retention matches the longest plausible forensic window — NIST AI RMF and EU AI Act Article 12 effectively require records long enough to reconstruct a training run years later. Test a sample forensic query: "who read shard N between date X and Y?" | Cloud-native logging is mature for storage access. Gap: pipeline-internal data access (e.g., a training script reading a shard from a local cache) is rarely logged at the application layer. Few teams instrument their data loaders. Distributed training across many GPU nodes multiplies the logging volume and blind spots — sampling strategies often miss the one read that matters. |
| 1.2.3 | **Verify that** training datasets are encrypted in transit and at rest, using current recommended cryptographic algorithms and key management practices. | 1 | Data theft via storage compromise or network interception; regulatory non-compliance for datasets containing PII or sensitive content. The NSA/CISA/FBI Joint CSI (May 2025) recommends AES-256 with FIPS 140-3 modules, TLS 1.3 for transit, zero-trust hardware-backed enclaves for sensitive operations, and quantum-resistant cryptographic standards to future-proof AI data authentication. | Verify encryption-at-rest on all storage (SSE-S3 / SSE-KMS, GCS default encryption, Azure Storage encryption). Check TLS 1.3 enforcement on data transfers. Review KMS key rotation policies (annual minimum). For regulated workloads, verify FIPS 140-3 modules and customer-managed keys (CMK) rather than platform-managed keys. Confirm ephemeral training-node caches inherit encryption (e.g., encrypted instance-store volumes on EC2 / LSSD on GCE). | Cloud provider default encryption handles most at-rest cases. Gap: datasets cached locally on training nodes (GPU instances) may land on unencrypted scratch disks. Checkpoint files and gradient shards staged between distributed trainers are a frequent oversight. Post-quantum adoption remains nascent — NIST finalized ML-DSA (formerly CRYSTALS-Dilithium) as FIPS 204 in August 2024, but production ML signing pipelines still almost universally use ECDSA/RSA. |
| 1.2.4 | **Verify that** cryptographic hashes or digital signatures are used to ensure data integrity during training data storage and transfer. | 2 | Silent data corruption during transfer; man-in-the-middle modification of datasets; undetected tampering by compromised storage systems; supply-chain injection (JFrog disclosed ~100 malicious models with embedded payloads on Hugging Face in 2024, several with thousands of downloads before detection). | Check for SHA-256 (or stronger) hash manifests accompanying datasets; verify hash validation is integrated into data loading pipelines (not just at download time). For signed datasets, verify signature validation against a pinned trust root. Inspect whether the OpenSSF Model Signing v1.0 specification (released April 2025, led by Google / NVIDIA / Hugging Face) or sigstore/model-transparency is used for the datasets the model consumes. Confirm Rekor transparency-log entries exist for signed artifacts. | Hashing individual files is straightforward. Gap: OpenSSF Model Signing (sigstore-based) is optimized for static **model** artifacts, and its maintainers openly acknowledge that **large, dynamic training datasets remain a hard problem** — signatures don't naturally compose across streaming ingest. Merkle-tree approaches help (Delta Lake, LakeFS, Iceberg snapshots all compute content-addressed commits) but there is no dominant standard for dataset-level signatures. Multi-TB datasets make full-corpus re-hashing expensive; most teams fall back to per-shard hashing with gaps between shards. |
| 1.2.5 | **Verify that** automated integrity monitoring is applied to guard against unauthorized modifications or corruption of training data. | 2 | Slow-burn data poisoning where small modifications accumulate over time; bit-rot or storage corruption going undetected until model retraining. The NYU/Washington University/Columbia medical-LLM poisoning study (Jan 2025) and the Pliny/Qwen-2.5 search-corpus seeding incidents show how low-volume injection — often well under 1% of the corpus — can push a model's behavior in an attacker-chosen direction without tripping naive integrity checks. | Verify automated hash-check jobs run on a schedule or on each ingest trigger. Check for file integrity monitoring on data lake paths (OSSEC, Wazuh 4.x, Tripwire Enterprise). Where the dataset lives in Delta Lake / LakeFS / Iceberg, verify the commit-chain is monitored and that alerts fire on unexpected force-pushes or branch deletes. For distributed object stores, confirm versioning is enabled and delete-markers are alerted on. Review alerting thresholds and response runbooks. | Classical FIM tools were designed for system files, not multi-TB datasets — they will either miss large dataset writes or flood on every legitimate ingest. Purpose-built ML-data integrity monitoring remains immature as of 2026; most teams implement custom scripts. Detecting poisoning (as opposed to random corruption) requires statistical/behavioral analysis that is more naturally covered by C1.4 poisoning-detection controls. |
| 1.2.6 | **Verify that** obsolete training data is securely purged or anonymized. | 1 | Stale data containing PII or deprecated patterns persisting in storage; regulatory risk from GDPR/CCPA right-to-deletion requests (EDPB made right-to-erasure its 2025 coordinated enforcement priority with 32 DPAs participating); poisoned data surviving past remediation; FTC algorithmic disgorgement risk (Cambridge Analytica 2019, Everalbum 2021, Weight Watchers 2022, Ring 2023, Edmodo 2023, Rite Aid 2024, Avast 2024 — commentators expect enforcement to intensify through 2026 as data-origin cases accumulate). | Review data retention policies and verify deletion procedures are actually executed (not just documented). Check for cryptographic erasure (key shredding) or secure deletion on storage. Confirm anonymization techniques (k-anonymity, differential privacy) where data is retained past its original purpose. Note California AB 1008 (effective January 2025) classifies AI-generated personal data as subject to CCPA deletion rights — your purge workflow must reach generated/synthetic derivatives, not just raw data. Verify tombstone records in lineage systems so downstream auditors can tell "deleted" from "never ingested." | Secure deletion on cloud object storage is generally reliable. Critical gap: data embedded in model weights cannot be "deleted" — machine unlearning remains research-grade as of April 2026 (ICML 2025 MUGen workshop, CVPR 2026 MUV workshop). Leading legal analyses (TechPolicy.Press, IAPP, CSA) call the right-to-be-forgotten in LLMs a governance problem as much as a technical one. Italian DPA fined OpenAI EUR 15M (Dec 2024) partly for training-data processing violations. Current workarounds: full retraining without the data, targeted fine-tuning, approximate unlearning with unverified guarantees (see Grimmelmann, "Machine Unlearning Doesn't Do What You Think"). |
| 1.2.7 | **Verify that** all training dataset versions are uniquely identified, stored immutably, and auditable to support rollback and forensic analysis. | 3 | Inability to reproduce training runs for audit (EU AI Act Article 10/12 requires this for high-risk systems — enforcement begins 2 August 2026); inability to roll back to a pre-poisoning dataset state; loss of forensic evidence after a security incident. | Verify a dataset versioning system is in use: **LakeFS** (which acquired DVC in November 2025 and now provides immutable commit-ID snapshots with zero-copy branching), **DVC** with a remote, **Delta Lake** (ACID transactions, automatic immutable snapshot per write), or Apache Iceberg. Check that version IDs are cryptographically linked to content (content-addressed storage or signed manifests). For true immutability, verify S3 Object Lock in **Compliance Mode** with `CloudTrail` data events enabled — in Compliance Mode not even the root account can remove protection before expiry. Test a real rollback end-to-end: restore dataset-v(N-1), re-run a training job, confirm reproducibility within the model's determinism budget. | LakeFS, DVC, Delta Lake, and Iceberg support versioning well and are the de facto standards in 2026. Gap: true immutability requires append-only storage (S3 Object Lock, Azure Blob Immutability Policies, GCS Bucket Lock), which adds cost and complexity — many teams use soft-delete and versioning without enforcing WORM. Object Lock **can only be enabled at bucket creation**, which is a frequent retrofit obstacle. Level 3 is appropriate given the tooling maturity but operational friction. The EU AI Act timeline puts real pressure on high-risk providers to close this gap before August 2026. |
