# C1.4: Training Data Quality and Security Assurance

> [Back to C01 Index](C01-Training-Data.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-16

## Purpose

Data quality validation is the last defensive line before poisoned, corrupted, or malformed data enters the training pipeline. This section treats quality checks as a security control, not a hygiene concern: the same statistical and schema checks that catch upstream pipeline bugs are also the cheapest broad-spectrum detector for adversarial contamination.

Two lines of research have reset the threat model since 2024:

- **Absolute-count poisoning.** Anthropic, the UK AI Security Institute, and the Alan Turing Institute published [*Poisoning Attacks on LLMs Require a Near-constant Number of Poison Samples*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07192) in October 2025. Across 72 models from 600M to 13B parameters trained on 6B–260B tokens, **250 crafted documents consistently produced a working backdoor** regardless of clean-data volume. In the 13B regime that is about 0.00016% of the corpus — well below the sensitivity of most percentage-based contamination audits. [Anthropic's research summary](https://www.anthropic.com/research/small-samples-poison) frames the asymmetry bluntly: defenders must detect fixed-count contamination, not dilute it.
- **Clinical and code-gen blast radius.** The [*Nature Medicine* study on medical LLMs (Dec 2024)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-024-03445-1) showed that replacing just **0.001% of training tokens** with targeted misinformation produced measurably more harmful completions while passing standard medical QA benchmarks. [CodeScan (Mar 2026, arXiv:2603.17174)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.17174) then demonstrated that spectral signatures, activation clustering, and static analysis all failed against triggerless code-generation poisoning — AST-normalization pushed detection back to ~97%, but only for that one attack class.

The practical implication for Section 1.4 is layered detection. Format validation catches obviously broken records, statistical/embedding analysis catches clumpy or out-of-distribution poisoning, confidence gates stop auto-labels from laundering upstream model errors into fresh training data, adversarial training raises the bar for evasion at inference, and security-exploitable-bias probing closes the evaluation loop for abuse-detection and fraud models. No single tool covers all of this; a research-grade check list means naming specific libraries, versions, and what each one does **not** catch.

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| 1.4.1 | **Verify that** automated tests catch format errors and nulls on every ingest or significant data transformation. | 1 | Malformed records causing silent training failures or model degradation; adversarial samples disguised as "just noisy" records sneaking past manual review; pipeline reliability issues that mask more serious tampering (for example, a poisoned batch arriving inside an otherwise broken day of ingests). | Review data validation code in ingest DAGs. Confirm schema validation runs via [Great Expectations](https://github.com/great-expectations/great_expectations) (0.18+ with Data Docs + Checkpoints for Spark/SQL/Pandas), [Pandera](https://github.com/unionai-oss/pandera) (0.19+ for typed Pandas/Polars contracts), or [TFX Data Validation](https://www.tensorflow.org/tfx/data_validation/get_started). Verify these tests run in CI/CD on every data change, not just at release. Google's published TFX deployment across 700+ pipelines found real data errors in 6% of training runs over a 30-day window — use that as a baseline for "expected" issue rate. | Tabular coverage is mature. Gap: schema validation for unstructured modalities (images, audio, long-form text, multi-modal) is still ad hoc. Format validation does not catch semantically poisoned-but-syntactically-valid records — the dominant LLM poisoning pattern. Also does not catch adversarial Unicode, zero-width, or homoglyph tricks used to evade lexical filters. |
| 1.4.2 | **Verify that** training and fine-tuning pipelines implement data integrity validation and poisoning detection techniques (e.g., statistical analysis, outlier detection, embedding analysis) to identify potential data poisoning or unintentional corruption in training data. | 2 | Data poisoning (MITRE ATLAS [AML.T0020](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0020)). Headline results: Anthropic/UK AISI/Turing (Oct 2025) — 250 documents backdoor any-size LLM ([arXiv:2510.07192](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07192)); Carlini et al. — web-scale poisoning for ~$60; CyLab/CMU ICLR 2025 — 0.1% poisoning survives safety alignment; *Nature Medicine* (Dec 2024) — 0.001% medical-token poisoning evades benchmarks while raising harmful-output rate 7–11%; [PoisonBench (ICML 2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.08038) — scaling model size does not reliably improve poisoning resilience; Virus Infection Attack ([arXiv:2509.23041](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041), NeurIPS 2025) — poisoning propagates through synthetic-data pipelines. | Inventory the detection stack and confirm something covers each attack family. Label/outlier/spurious-correlation screening: [Cleanlab 2.9](https://github.com/cleanlab/cleanlab) (Jan 2025, dropped TF support, strong for label errors and out-of-distribution samples). Classic backdoor defenses: [IBM ART 1.20+](https://github.com/Trusted-AI/adversarial-robustness-toolbox) for Neural Cleanse, Activation Clustering, Spectral Signatures, and STRIP. Dataset-level provenance: [Datasig](https://github.com/trailofbits/datasig) (Trail of Bits, [May 2025 release](https://blog.trailofbits.com/2025/05/02/datasig-fingerprinting-ai/ml-datasets-to-stop-data-borne-attacks/)) — MinHash fingerprints that let AIBOMs compare datasets without redistributing raw content. Code generation: [CodeScan (Mar 2026)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.17174) for AST-normalized triggerless-backdoor detection. Audit procedure: feed a known-poisoned sample set through the pipeline and confirm alerts fire; review outlier thresholds and who approves overrides. | Critical gap: the 250-document threshold means defenses must be sensitive to vanishingly small proportions. Clean-label and triggerless attacks remain hardest to catch — a Dec 2025 study ([arXiv:2512.06243](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.06243)) found that INT8 quantization alone drops Activation Clustering, STRIP, Spectral Signatures, and Fine-Pruning to 0% detection while the backdoor stays fully functional; only Neural Cleanse survived, and only at full precision. Triggerless code-gen poisoning evades spectral signatures, activation clustering, and static analysis (Aug 2025). Datasig is still prototype-stage and MinHash by design misses semantic manipulation. Virus Infection Attack amplifies through synthetic-data generation, so organizations generating training data from other models inherit upstream contamination. No single tool wins; a layered check with documented coverage gaps is the minimum defensible posture. |
| 1.4.3 | **Verify that** automatically generated labels (e.g., via models or weak supervision) are subject to confidence thresholds and consistency checks to detect misleading or low-confidence labels. | 2 | Propagation of model errors into training data (feedback loops); adversarial manipulation of auto-labeling models to inject systematic mislabels; quality degradation from overreliance on weak supervision; *Virus Infection Attack* (VIA, NeurIPS 2025) showing poisoning propagates through synthetic data generation and amplifies across generations ([arXiv:2509.23041](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041)). | Review the auto-labeling pipeline for explicit confidence gates. Confirm the pipeline uses Confident Learning (Prune-by-Class or Prune-by-Noise-Rate variants — see the [noisy-label-detection benchmark, arXiv:2510.16211](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.16211)) or equivalent statistical screening. For weak-supervision stacks, check [Snorkel](https://github.com/snorkel-team/snorkel) or [Snuba](https://www.vldb.org/pvldb/vol12/p223-varma.pdf)-style labeling-function aggregation and that conflicting votes are flagged, not silently averaged. Cross-model agreement (ensemble disagreement > X% = quarantine), temporal-stability checks against a held-out canary set, and documented human-review sampling rates for auto-generated labels should all be evidenced. Confirm synthetic data produced from potentially poisoned upstream models is screened against the dataset-level detection stack in 1.4.2 before re-ingest. | [Snorkel](https://github.com/snorkel-team/snorkel) and [Cleanlab](https://github.com/cleanlab/cleanlab) support confidence thresholds and CL-style joint-matrix screening. Gap: there is no standard methodology for *choosing* the threshold — teams often pick it empirically per project, which makes the control easy to satisfy on paper and weak in practice. Model collapse from recursive auto-labeling is a known failure mode ([Shumailov et al., *Nature* 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07566-y)). VIA means synthetic pipelines can silently inherit upstream poisoning — screening the *output* of synthetic generation against the 1.4.2 stack is load-bearing. |
| 1.4.4 | **Verify that** appropriate defenses, such as adversarial training, data augmentation with perturbed inputs, or robust optimization techniques, are implemented and tuned for relevant models based on risk assessment. | 3 | Adversarial evasion at inference time (MITRE ATLAS [AML.T0015](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0015) / [AML.T0042](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0042)); model brittleness under distribution shift; targeted attacks exploiting known model weaknesses. | Review training code for adversarial components: [PGD-AT (Madry et al.)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.06083), [TRADES](https://github.com/yaodongyu/TRADES), or newer dummy-class variants that claim to reduce the robustness-accuracy trade-off ([arXiv:2410.12671](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.12671v1)). Verify adversarial augmentation in the data pipeline and that evaluation uses the AutoAttack battery (APGD-CE, APGD-DLR, FAB, Square) rather than PGD alone — AutoAttack remains the accepted "defense-audit" benchmark. [IBM ART](https://github.com/Trusted-AI/adversarial-robustness-toolbox) and [Foolbox](https://github.com/bethgelab/foolbox) provide reference implementations. Confirm there is a documented risk assessment justifying the chosen defense (matching attack model to deployment surface) — a Level 3 control is expected to cite which threats it does and does not mitigate. | Adversarial training raises compute cost 2–10× and typically reduces clean accuracy 2–6 points. The robustness-accuracy trade-off is well characterized but poorly tooled for automated tuning. Appropriately Level 3 because of cost, skill required, and because many deployed systems do not face meaningful adversarial input. For LLMs specifically, adversarial-training analogues (safety RLHF, constitutional AI, red-team fine-tuning) are the practical substitute but are not covered by classical AT/PGD benchmarks. |
| 1.4.5 | **Verify that** automated tests catch label skews on every ingest or significant data transformation. | 2 | Class imbalance introduced by poisoning (flooding one class to shift decision boundaries); drift in label distributions indicating a compromised or tampered pipeline; adversarial manipulation of class proportions to weaken minority-class detection (e.g., injecting "benign" labels to dilute abuse-detection training). | Confirm label-distribution monitoring is wired into the ingest DAG and not just run ad hoc. [Great Expectations](https://github.com/great-expectations/great_expectations) and [Deepchecks](https://github.com/deepchecks/deepchecks) both support value-distribution checks; confirm a statistical test (chi-squared, KL divergence, population stability index) fires on each ingest with alerting thresholds set above expected legitimate drift. Verify that alerts require human sign-off before downstream training proceeds and that the baseline distribution is versioned in source control (so "the baseline moved" is itself a reviewable change). | Distinguishing malicious skew from legitimate distribution shift requires domain context; no tool does this automatically. Threshold tuning is manual. The control is also bypassable if the adversary poisons across classes proportionally — skew detection only fires when class counts move, not when class *semantics* move. Pair with 1.4.2 embedding/outlier analysis for coverage. |
| 1.4.6 | **Verify that** models used in security-relevant decisions (e.g., abuse detection, fraud scoring, automated trust decisions) are evaluated for systematic bias patterns that an adversary could exploit to evade controls (e.g., mimicking a trusted language style or demographic pattern to bypass detection). | 2 | Adversarial exploitation of demographic or stylistic biases in security models; evasion of abuse detection by mimicking under-policed styles; systematic blind spots in fraud detection that attackers can probe and weaponize; cross-model consistency attacks where the same input routes through a biased classifier. The [DHS 2023 adversarial AI risk memo](https://www.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/2023-12/23_1222_st_risks_mitigation_strategies.pdf) flags this as a defined threat vector, and 2026 security-research surveys increasingly treat bias as a security liability ([PurpleSec 2026](https://purplesec.us/learn/ai-security-risks/), [SentinelOne 2026](https://www.sentinelone.com/cybersecurity-101/data-and-ai/ai-security-risks/)). | Review bias-evaluation reports for each security-critical model. Confirm disaggregated performance metrics across relevant subgroups (style, dialect, geography, device class — whatever matters for the deployment). [Fairlearn](https://github.com/fairlearn/fairlearn) and [AI Fairness 360](https://github.com/Trusted-AI/AIF360) provide the measurement plumbing. Confirm an adversarial-probing step goes beyond measurement: red-team exercises that attempt to bypass abuse detection or fraud scoring using under-performing subgroup patterns, with findings logged and remediated. Cross-reference with [OWASP AI Testing Guide](https://www.getastra.com/blog/ai-security/owasp-ai-testing-guide/) for structured test procedures. | No dedicated "security-exploitable-bias" tooling exists — methodology is ad hoc across the industry. Fairness tools measure the gap; they do not tell you which gaps are weaponizable. This still requires human red-team design. Also in scope: ensuring that remediation does not itself create new exploitable classes (a fairness patch that over-indexes on one subgroup is a fresh bias). |

## Recent Research and Incident Context (2025–2026)

- **Oct 2025 — Anthropic/UK AISI/Turing.** [*Poisoning Attacks on LLMs Require a Near-constant Number of Poison Samples*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07192). 250-document threshold across 600M–13B parameter models. Resets the defender's math from percentages to absolute counts.
- **Dec 2024 — *Nature Medicine*.** [Medical LLMs vulnerable to data poisoning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-024-03445-1). 0.001% token replacement produces measurably harmful completions while evading standard medical QA benchmarks. A [PMC case study in breast-cancer clinical LLMs](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10984073/) extended the finding to specialty models.
- **May 2025 — Trail of Bits.** [Datasig release](https://blog.trailofbits.com/2025/05/02/datasig-fingerprinting-ai/ml-datasets-to-stop-data-borne-attacks/). MinHash-based dataset fingerprinting specifically designed for AIBOM / supply-chain consumption. [Source repo](https://github.com/trailofbits/datasig).
- **Aug 2025.** Triggerless code-generation poisoning study — spectral signatures, activation clustering, and static analysis all failed on AST-normalized attacks; [CodeScan (Mar 2026, arXiv:2603.17174)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.17174) reported ~97% detection for this one attack class and is currently the only defense with published numbers.
- **ICML 2025 — PoisonBench.** Scaling model size does not reliably improve poisoning resilience; large models can be *more* exposed on some attack types ([arXiv:2406.08038](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.08038)).
- **NeurIPS 2025 — Virus Infection Attack.** [arXiv:2509.23041](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041). Poisoning propagates through synthetic-data generation pipelines; organizations that generate training data from other models inherit and amplify upstream contamination.
- **Dec 2025 — Quantization Blindspots.** [arXiv:2512.06243](https://www.arxiv.org/pdf/2512.06243). INT8 quantization drops classic backdoor defenses (Activation Clustering, STRIP, Spectral Signatures, Fine-Pruning) to 0% detection while the backdoor remains fully functional. Only Neural Cleanse survived, and only at full precision. Changes the risk picture for edge deployments that quantize after training.
- **Jan 2025 — Cleanlab 2.9.** Dropped TensorFlow, PyTorch-focused refactor. Expanded multi-annotator consensus and label-quality scoring ([changelog via project page](https://cleanlab.ai/blog/learn/cleanlab-2.6)).
- **Aug 2026 EU AI Act enforcement milestone.** High-risk AI systems must demonstrate documented data governance, bias measurement, traceability, and human oversight. Training-data quality controls are now audit targets, not internal hygiene. See the [Cloud Security Alliance mapping of ISO/IEC 42001 and NIST AI RMF onto the EU AI Act](https://cloudsecurityalliance.org/blog/2025/01/29/how-can-iso-iec-42001-nist-ai-rmf-help-comply-with-the-eu-ai-act).

## Defensive Tooling Cheat Sheet

| Layer | Primary tools | Covers |
|---|---|---|
| Schema / format | Great Expectations 0.18+, Pandera 0.19+, TFX Data Validation, Deepchecks | Structural corruption, nulls, type drift, value ranges. Not semantic poisoning. |
| Label / outlier | Cleanlab 2.9, Confident Learning, SplitNet, Snorkel/Snuba | Label errors, low-confidence auto-labels, OOD samples. Not coordinated clean-label attacks. |
| Backdoor forensics | IBM ART 1.20+ (Neural Cleanse, Activation Clustering, Spectral Signatures, STRIP), CodeScan (code-gen specific) | Trigger-based backdoors at full precision. Degrades sharply under quantization. |
| Dataset provenance | Datasig (MinHash fingerprints), AIBOM tooling, dataset-versioning hashes | Detecting silent dataset swaps and tampering. Not content-level poisoning within a known dataset. |
| Adversarial robustness | PGD-AT, TRADES, IBM ART, Foolbox, AutoAttack for evaluation | Evasion attacks at inference. Expensive; orthogonal to training-data poisoning. |
| Security-exploitable bias | Fairlearn, AI Fairness 360, adversarial red-team probing | Measurement + exploit probing. No automated tool yet; human-led exercise. |
