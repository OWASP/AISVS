# C11.4: Model-Inversion Resistance

> **Chapter:** [C11 Adversarial Robustness & Attack Resistance](C11-Adversarial-Robustness.md)
> **Requirements:** 2 | **IDs:** 11.4.1--11.4.2

## Purpose

Prevent reconstruction of private training data or sensitive attributes from model outputs. Model inversion attacks exploit the information a model reveals through its predictions to reconstruct inputs or infer sensitive features of the training data. First demonstrated by Fredrikson et al. (2015) on facial recognition models, these attacks have since been extended to reconstruct text, tabular data, and other modalities. The risk is highest for models trained on sensitive data (medical records, biometric data, PII) where even partial reconstruction can violate privacy.

> **Scope note:** Rate limiting in C11.4 is scoped specifically to inversion attack resistance: throttling repeated adaptive queries from the same principal to raise the cost of reconstructing training data or sensitive attributes. It is not a substitute for generic API rate limiting (covered by OWASP ASVS) or orchestration execution budgets (C9.1). Both generic rate limiting and C11.4 may be satisfied simultaneously, but require distinct evidence — general abuse prevention cannot double-count as inversion resistance.

## Recent Research (2024--2026)

### Comprehensive Survey and Taxonomy (2025)

A comprehensive survey on deep learning model inversion attacks and defenses (January 2025) provides a systematic taxonomy covering optimization-based, training-based, and GAN-based attack methods, along with defense categories including differential privacy, output perturbation, model architecture modifications, and regularization-based approaches. The survey catalogs 16 state-of-the-art attack and defense methods, establishing that combined defense strategies can mitigate up to 50% of model inversion attacks while maintaining model utility.

### LLM-Specific Inversion Attacks

Recent work has demonstrated that LLMs face novel inversion attack surfaces distinct from traditional discriminative models:

- **Activation Inversion Attacks (AIA)** target decentralized and split-learning LLM training setups, using public datasets to construct shadow datasets of text labels and corresponding activations, then training attack models to reconstruct training data from intermediate activations. Dai et al. (ACL 2025) provided the first systematic evaluation, recovering names, phone numbers, and email addresses from intermediate activations transmitted between nodes. This is particularly relevant for federated or distributed LLM training architectures.
- **Cross-lingual Model Inversion** research shows that multilingual language models are more vulnerable to inversion attacks than monolingual models, with black-box multilingual and cross-lingual inversion attacks achieving higher reconstruction fidelity. Organizations deploying multilingual models should factor this into their inversion risk assessments.
- **PII Extraction from Llama 3** (Sivashanmugam, 2025) demonstrated that even smaller-scale LLMs like Llama 3.2 are susceptible to model inversion attacks that extract memorized PII -- passwords, email addresses, and account numbers were recovered through carefully crafted prompts. The attack leverages temperature tuning to surface rare memorized phrases and adaptive querying based on previous outputs, highlighting that inversion vulnerability grows as models scale.
- **Split-Learning Inversion** (Shu et al., 2025) introduced mutual information entropy analysis for transformer-based LLMs in split learning frameworks, with a two-stage attack that first projects intermediate representations into the embedding space, then uses a generative model to reconstruct the original text from those embeddings.

### Advanced Attack Techniques

**Stepwise Gradient Inversion (SGI)** (2024) significantly improves image reconstruction quality through a two-stage approach: first modeling the coefficient of variation of features and applying an evolutionary algorithm for label recovery, then performing stepwise gradient inversion. SGI demonstrates that inversion attacks continue to improve in fidelity, reinforcing the need for proactive defenses (requirements 11.4.1 and 11.4.2) rather than relying on attack difficulty as a defense.

### Embedding Protection

**Embedding Guard (EGuard)** (2024) employs transformer-based projection networks and textual mutual information optimization to protect embeddings while retaining LLM utility. This addresses a gap complementary to requirements 11.4.1 and 11.4.2 where traditional DP approaches are too costly -- EGuard provides embedding-level protection without full DP-SGD overhead, applicable to models where embeddings are exposed through APIs or shared architectures.

### MIBench Benchmark

**MIBench** (2025) is the first standardized benchmark for model inversion attacks and defenses. The March 2025 arXiv revision reports 19 state-of-the-art attack and defense methods and 9 standardized evaluation protocols, while the earlier OpenReview submission described 16 methods. This enables reproducible comparison of defense effectiveness and directly supports verification approaches for requirements 11.4.1 and 11.4.2 by providing standardized evaluation methodology.

### Vision-Language Model Inversion (2025)

As of early 2025, model inversion attacks have been extended to vision-language models (VLMs). Research demonstrated four novel attack strategies -- Token-based MI (TMI), Convergent Token-based MI (TMI-C), Sequence-based MI (SMI), and SMI with Adaptive Token Weighting (SMI-AW) -- successfully attacking LLaVA-v1.6, Qwen2.5-VL, and MiniGPT-v2. Human evaluators rated the reconstructed images at 75.31% attack accuracy on FaceScrub, and automated evaluation reached 77.40% on Stanford Dogs. Sequence-level gradient aggregation proved significantly more effective than independent token-level optimization, and logit-maximization loss outperformed cross-entropy. Notably, the paper proposed no new defenses, underscoring the gap between attack sophistication and available countermeasures for multimodal models.

### VaultGemma: Production-Scale DP (2025)

Google released VaultGemma (September 2025), the largest (1B parameters) open model fully pre-trained with differential privacy. Trained on the Gemma 2 architecture with DP-SGD at a privacy budget of epsilon less than or equal to 2.0 and delta less than or equal to 1.1e-10, VaultGemma demonstrates that DP can scale to production-grade language models. Key innovations include Poisson sampling (replacing uniform batches to reduce noise requirements) and new scaling laws for DP models that define optimal training configurations for a given privacy-compute budget. Open-sourced on Hugging Face and Kaggle, VaultGemma directly demonstrates that formal privacy guarantees no longer require sacrificing most model utility, particularly for regulated industries (healthcare, finance, legal) -- and serves as a reference implementation for DP training complementing the output-level controls in requirement 11.4.1.

### Stand-In Model Protection (2025)

Stand-in model protection introduces a synthetic defense layer against both membership inference and model inversion attacks. Rather than exposing the trained model directly, a stand-in model is distilled from the original and serves as the public-facing endpoint. This limits the information an adversary can extract, though the approach cannot fully control how closely the stand-in's unlabeled dataset resembles the original training data. The trade-off between fidelity and privacy remains an active area of work -- high-fidelity stand-ins may still leak sensitive patterns, while low-fidelity versions degrade downstream utility.

### Combined Defense Strategies

Research from 2024-2025 has demonstrated that no single defense is sufficient. The most effective approaches combine multiple layers: differential privacy during training, adaptive noise injection at inference, output generalization (requirement 11.4.1), and rate limiting (requirement 11.4.2). Combined systems integrating DP, federated learning, adaptive noise, and ensemble-based obfuscation show substantially better protection than any individual technique.

### FedGuard-CI (January 2026)

FedGuard-CI is a novel privacy-preserving framework published in January 2026 that integrates dual-stage differential privacy, trust-aware secure aggregation, and a Model Inversion Risk Estimator (MIRE). MIRE provides a quantitative risk score for model inversion vulnerability, enabling organizations to assess their exposure before deployment rather than relying solely on post-hoc defenses. This approach is particularly relevant for federated learning deployments where model updates are shared across participants.

### Data-Free Selective Unlearning via Model Inversion (January 2026)

An interesting reversal of the model inversion threat: DFSU (Data-Free Selective Unlearning) repurposes model inversion as a *defensive* mechanism. The approach trains a sequence-to-sequence transformer to reconstruct memorized PII from the target model's output logits, then uses Privacy-Selective Contrastive Unlearning (PSCU) within a LoRA adapter to selectively erase those memorized tokens. Evaluated on Pythia models (160M--1.4B parameters), DFSU achieved zero exact reconstruction rate across all scales -- matching oracle unlearning performance that has access to the original training data -- while maintaining near-identical utility (WikiText perplexity increased only from 8.69 to 8.83 on Pythia-410M). Entity-hit rates dropped from 28.78% to 0.13%. The practical significance is that organizations can remediate memorized PII without access to the original training corpus, addressing a real deployment constraint. The primary limitation is the requirement for white-box access to model logits.

### TTLoRA-DP: Parameter-Efficient DP Fine-Tuning (2026)

TTLoRA-DP extends differentially private training to Tensor Train Low-Rank Adaptation, a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method that uses 7.6x fewer parameters than standard LoRA (65.6K vs 497.7K) while achieving better privacy-utility tradeoffs. On the Enron dataset at a strict privacy budget of epsilon=0.5, TTLoRA achieved perplexity of 27.52 versus LoRA's 29.29. More notably, under membership inference attacks, TTLoRA remained nearly stable across privacy budgets (51.36--52.04% attack AUC) while LoRA vulnerability increased substantially (52.49--58.43%). The key insight is that structural constraints in the adapter architecture itself -- the compositional factorization of tensor train cores -- suppress membership signals that loss-based attacks exploit, offering privacy benefits beyond what the noise mechanism alone provides.

### VERIDP: Verifiable Differentially Private Training (March 2026)

VERIDP introduces cryptographic verification for DP-SGD training, addressing a trust gap in privacy-preserving ML: existing frameworks assume semi-honest participants, which breaks down in adversarial or federated environments. Using zero-knowledge proofs with polynomial commitments, sumcheck proofs, and incrementally verifiable computation, VERIDP generates compact proofs (3--4 KB) that verify correct gradient computation, clipping, averaging, and Gaussian noise generation per training iteration -- without revealing sensitive data or randomness. Verification takes just 2--5 milliseconds per proof. This is directly relevant to auditing the DP training foundation underlying requirement 11.4.1: rather than trusting that DP-SGD was correctly applied to constrain what sensitive attributes can be reconstructed from outputs, verifiers can cryptographically confirm it.

### GeoClip: Geometry-Aware Gradient Clipping (2025)

GeoClip improves upon standard per-sample gradient clipping in DP-SGD by clipping and perturbing gradients in a transformed basis aligned with the gradient distribution's geometry. The transformation is estimated adaptively using only previously released noisy gradients, incurring no additional privacy cost. This matters because standard isotropic clipping in DP-SGD discards directional gradient information that could improve convergence; GeoClip preserves this structure while maintaining the same formal privacy guarantees, offering better model utility at equivalent epsilon budgets.

### Regulatory Pressure: Right to Erasure and Model Inversion (2025--2026)

As of March 2025, the European Data Protection Board launched a coordinated enforcement action focused specifically on the right to erasure, with 30 data protection authorities participating. The regulatory implication for model inversion resistance is increasingly direct: if a model can reproduce training data through inversion, regulators may treat the model itself as a copy of that data subject to deletion obligations. Machine unlearning has emerged as the practical response -- source-free unlearning methods (September 2025) demonstrated certified unlearning without access to original training data, using surrogate datasets and calibrated noise to achieve performance comparable to full retraining at a fraction of the compute cost. This regulatory trajectory makes requirements 11.4.1 and 11.4.2 not just security controls but potential compliance obligations under GDPR and the EU AI Act.

### KV-Cache Inversion Attacks on LLMs (NDSS 2026)

As of March 2026, a new inference-time inversion surface has been demonstrated: the Key-Value (KV) cache used to accelerate LLM inference stores intermediate attention computations that can be exploited to reconstruct user inputs. Luo et al. (NDSS 2026) designed three attack vectors -- inversion (directly reversing cached key/value matrices using known model weights), collision (iteratively matching candidate inputs against intercepted cache states), and injection (semantic manipulation via cache poisoning). All three achieved practical reconstruction of sensitive user prompts from cached data. The proposed defense, **KV-Cloak**, applies reversible matrix-based obfuscation combined with operator fusion to render cached states unintelligible to attackers while preserving model accuracy with minimal performance overhead. Code is available in the [KV-Cloak repository](https://github.com/SiO-2/kvcloak). This attack class is distinct from training-time attribute inversion (requirement 11.4.1) because it targets inference infrastructure rather than learned parameters -- organizations deploying shared KV-cache systems (multi-tenant inference, prefix caching) should treat this as a separate threat vector requiring its own controls alongside the output-level and rate-limiting controls in 11.4.1 and 11.4.2.

### Data Poisoning as Model Inversion Defense (2024--2025)

Zhou et al. (December 2024, published in IEEE TIFS 2025) introduced a counterintuitive defense paradigm: using data poisoning against the *attacker's* inversion model rather than defending the victim classifier. The Label-Preserving Poisoning Attack (LPA) introduces subtle perturbations to all output vectors while preserving their correct labels, contaminating any dataset an attacker would collect by querying the model. This makes it significantly harder for ML-based inversion models to learn the mapping from outputs back to inputs. The key advantage is that LPA is retraining-free -- it does not require modifying or retraining the deployed classifier, making it practical for protecting large established models where retraining with DP would be prohibitively expensive. The approach outperformed existing defenses while maintaining full classifier utility, offering a complementary layer to rate limiting (requirement 11.4.2) and output generalization controls (requirement 11.4.1).

### Shadow Defense for Gradient Inversion in Federated Learning (2025)

Jiang et al. (Medical Image Analysis, October 2025) proposed a shadow-model-based defense against gradient inversion attacks in federated learning. The approach uses a GAN as a shadow model to simulate adversarial reconstruction, then applies targeted, sample-specific noise injection to the gradient components most vulnerable to inversion -- rather than adding uniform noise across all gradients. On medical imaging datasets (ChestXRay, EyePACS), the defense degraded attack reconstruction quality by 3.73 PSNR on ChestXRay and 2.78 PSNR on EyePACS while maintaining model F1 scores within 1% of undefended baselines. This precision-targeting approach addresses a longstanding limitation of uniform DP noise, which often degrades model accuracy more than necessary by protecting already-safe gradient components equally.

### LLM Internal States Inversion (USENIX Security 2025)

Dong et al. (USENIX Security 2025) demonstrated that depth in LLM architectures gives a false sense of privacy. Contrary to the assumption that deep internal representations are too abstract to invert, the paper presented four attack methods -- two white-box optimization attacks (for low-depth and high-depth internal states), a black-box transferability attack leveraging derived models, and a generation-based attack treating inversion as a translation task. The results are striking: a 4,112-token medical consulting prompt was nearly perfectly inverted with 86.88% F1 token matching from the middle layer of Llama-3. The paper evaluated four practical defenses and found none could fully prevent internal state inversion. This has direct implications for requirement 11.4.2: organizations sharing intermediate model states (collaborative inference, split learning, model-as-a-service with internal state access) face inversion risks that rate limiting alone cannot address. The attack surface extends beyond output logits to any exposed internal representation.

### LLM Recommender System Inversion (ACM RecSys 2025)

As of late 2025, model inversion attacks have been extended to LLM-empowered recommender systems. Research presented at ACM RecSys 2025 demonstrated that adversaries can reconstruct original prompts containing personal preferences, interaction histories, and demographic attributes by exploiting the output logits of recommendation models. Using a Similarity-Guided Refinement optimization of the vec2text framework, the attack recovered nearly 65% of user-interacted items and correctly inferred age and gender in 87% of cases. A key finding is that privacy leakage depends heavily on domain consistency and prompt complexity rather than victim model performance -- inversion models trained on domain-aligned data achieved ItemMatch of 61% (movies) and ProfileMatch exceeding 83%, while generic instruction-trained inversion models failed. This represents a practical inversion threat for deployed recommendation systems where output logits are accessible.

### CENSOR: Orthogonal Subspace Gradient Defense (NDSS 2025)

CENSOR (Zhang et al., NDSS 2025) introduces a gradient defense mechanism for federated learning that samples gradients in a subspace orthogonal to the original gradient, layer by layer, selecting the update that achieves the lowest loss. By leveraging cold posteriors over orthogonal subspaces, CENSOR disrupts gradient inversion attacks while maintaining model utility -- the orthogonal perturbation makes it fundamentally harder for attackers to map gradients back to training data without significantly degrading convergence. Evaluated across three datasets against five state-of-the-art attacks, CENSOR outperformed four existing defenses. The code is available in the [CENSOR repository](https://github.com/KaiyuanZh/censor). This complements output-level controls (requirement 11.4.1) by offering a utility-preserving alternative for federated settings where full DP-SGD noise is too costly.

### Diffusion-Based Model Inversion (2025--2026)

Generative model inversion attacks based on diffusion models have emerged as a significant upgrade over GAN-based approaches. By replacing GAN generators with single-step generators distilled from pretrained diffusion models, these attacks achieve higher-fidelity reconstructions of private training data with fewer queries. The superior generative quality of diffusion models means that reconstructed samples are more visually convincing and leak more sensitive detail than earlier GAN-based inversions. This escalation in attack capability reinforces the need for proactive defenses rather than relying on reconstruction difficulty -- organizations should assume inversion quality will continue to improve as generative model architectures advance.

### Privacy-Preserving LLM Inference (2026)

A comparative survey published in early 2026 examines cryptographic approaches for protecting LLM inference, directly relevant to the inference-time gap that output-level controls (requirement 11.4.1) alone cannot address. The main approaches include Secure Multi-Party Computation (MPC), Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE), hybrid HE/MPC designs, and Trusted Execution Environments (TEEs). The practical trajectory moves from TEE-based solutions (deployable today for large-scale confidential inference) through crypto-augmented designs (reducing hardware trust at higher compute cost) toward FHE as a long-term endpoint. Notable practical advances include the Fission framework, which evaluates linear layers under MPC and nonlinear layers in the clear, achieving up to 8x speedups over prior privacy-preserving methods on BERT, GPT, and Llama 3. FHE-based inference remains approximately 1000x slower than plaintext, but GPU acceleration is closing the gap. For organizations processing sensitive data at inference time (RAG over medical records, PII-bearing queries), these approaches address the gap where training-time DP provides no protection.

As of February-March 2026, split and confidential inference work is becoming more directly testable against inversion attacks rather than described only as architecture hardening. Privacy-Aware Split Inference keeps embedding and unembedding layers on a trusted local device, sends only intermediate activations to an untrusted cloud GPU, and uses lookahead decoding to offset WAN latency; its empirical inversion tests recovered about 59% of tokens with a shallow two-layer split and about 35% at an eight-layer split, making split depth an auditable privacy-performance parameter. Talaria takes a stricter confidential-inference route by running sensitive weight-independent operations in a client-controlled confidential virtual machine and masking outsourced intermediates through Reversible Masked Outsourcing, reducing reported token reconstruction accuracy from above 97.5% to 1.34% while preserving token-identical outputs. OSNIP proposes a lighter client-side obfuscated semantic null-space mapping for embeddings. The verification implication is that "private inference" claims should be backed by token-reconstruction tests against the actual split point, masking scheme, and cloud trust boundary, not by an architecture diagram alone.

### Gradient Inversion: Practical Feasibility Reassessment (2025--2026)

A systematic evaluation by Valadi et al. (August 2025, revised February 2026) challenges the severity of gradient inversion attacks under realistic conditions. Testing across image classification and object detection tasks on contemporary vision architectures, they found that modern, performance-optimized models consistently resist meaningful visual reconstruction. Many previously reported successes relied on upper-bound settings -- inference-mode operation, architectural simplifications, or batch-size-one assumptions that do not reflect production training pipelines. Under honest-but-curious server assumptions with realistic training configurations, high-fidelity reconstruction did not constitute a critical privacy risk. Separately, a comprehensive IEEE TPAMI survey (Guo et al., March 2025) categorized gradient inversion attacks into three types -- optimization-based (most practical), generation-based (too many dependencies), and analytics-based (easily detectable) -- and proposed a three-stage defense pipeline for federated learning frameworks. These findings do not eliminate gradient inversion as a threat, but they contextualize the risk: legacy architectures, small batch sizes, and simplified training setups remain vulnerable, while production configurations with large batches and modern architectures provide inherent resistance.

### Verifiable and Hierarchical Gradient Inversion (April 2026)

Two April 2026 preprints narrow the gap between "visually plausible" inversion and operationally provable leakage. VGIA (Verifiable Gradient Inversion Attack) targets federated tabular models by using ReLU activation boundaries as hyperplanes; when the algebraic test certifies that a region contains exactly one record, the attacker analytically recovers the feature vector and reconstructs the target with a lightweight optimization step. This matters for C11.4 because tabular deployments have often been treated as lower-risk than image or text deployments simply because reconstruction quality is harder to inspect; a certificate of correctness makes that assumption weaker. GIFD (Gradient Inversion over Feature Domains) improves image reconstruction by optimizing over hierarchical GAN feature domains rather than only the initial latent vector, and reports pixel-level reconstruction across federated settings including out-of-distribution and label-inconsistent scenarios. Auditors should therefore test both "realistic configuration" resistance and attack families that can certify recovery or search richer feature spaces, especially before accepting secure aggregation, large batch size, or GAN-prior mismatch as sufficient defenses.

### Adapter-Based Federated LLM Gradient Inversion (WWW 2026)

Chen et al. (January 2026, accepted at WWW 2026) challenge the assumption that parameter-efficient adapters reduce federated LLM privacy risk. Their Unordered-word-bag-based Text Reconstruction (UTR) attack targets adapter-based FedLLMs by inferring token presence from frozen-layer attention patterns, then reconstructing sentences within the low-rank adapter-gradient subspace. Across GPT2-Large, BERT, and Qwen2.5-7B on CoLA, SST-2, and Rotten Tomatoes, the paper reports ROUGE-1/2 above 99 even under large batch settings where prior gradient inversion attacks fail. For C11.4 verification, the practical point is that LoRA-style or adapter-only fine-tuning should not be counted as an inversion defense by itself. Auditors need to inspect whether adapter gradients leave the tenant boundary, whether DP noise applies to adapter updates rather than only full-model training, and whether federated aggregation assumptions still hold when the backbone is frozen but attention patterns remain informative.

### SOMP: Scalable Aggregated-Gradient Inversion (March 2026)

SOMP (Subspace-Guided Orthogonal Matching Pursuit, March 2026) reframes text recovery from aggregated transformer gradients as sparse signal recovery. The method exploits head-wise geometric structure and sample-level sparsity in aggregated gradients to avoid exhaustive search, then progressively narrows candidate text. The authors report better reconstruction fidelity than prior methods for long sequences at batch size 16 and meaningful text recovery even at batch size 128. This narrows the comfort zone created by older batch-size-one attack assumptions: aggregation still helps, but it should be treated as a risk reducer rather than a privacy boundary. C11.4 evidence for federated or collaborative training should therefore include batch-size sensitivity tests and attacker models that operate on aggregated updates, not only single-client gradient leakage tests.

### AdaDPIGU: Importance-Aware Adaptive Clipping (July 2025)

AdaDPIGU (Zhang and Xie, July 2025) addresses a long-standing scalability weakness in DP-SGD: noise requirements grow with parameter dimensionality, so uniform gradient clipping on large models injects more noise than necessary. The method combines three mechanisms -- importance-based binary masking that prunes low-importance coordinates using a DP Gaussian estimator during pretraining, adaptive per-coordinate clipping thresholds tuned to the selected gradient distribution, and progressive sparsification that gradually restores full model capacity as training stabilizes. Reported results: 99.12% test accuracy on MNIST at epsilon=8 using only 60% of gradients, and 73.21% on CIFAR-10 at epsilon=4 -- exceeding the 71.12% non-private baseline, an unusual case where the regularization effect of sparse DP training improved generalization. The theoretical contribution is a convergence proof for sparse-gradient DP-SGD under adaptive clipping, and the practical contribution is that organizations can apply stricter epsilon budgets on deeper models without the utility collapse that has historically pushed teams toward weaker privacy guarantees. This expands the set of models where formal DP is feasible at production utility, directly strengthening the DP training foundation that underlies output generalization (11.4.1) and rate-limiting controls (11.4.2).

### Automated Model Inversion Assessment Pipeline (September 2025)

Shumaker et al. (September 2025, arXiv:2509.04214) released an automated developmental test and evaluation (DT&E) pipeline for MI risk. The pipeline directly addresses a gap in verification: previous inversion audits required human inspection of reconstructed outputs, which is subjective, difficult to standardize, and does not scale. The system integrates vision-language models configured for zero-shot classification and image captioning to interpret reconstructed images automatically, benchmarks against state-of-the-art MI attacks, and produces four quantitative adversarial risk dimensions that convert subjective "does this reconstruction leak?" judgments into measurable privacy-loss scores. Originally motivated by defense use cases (protecting sensitive training imagery from extraction), the approach generalizes to any image-classification deployment where a reproducible inversion audit is required -- directly useful as verification evidence for requirements 11.4.1 and 11.4.2. The main limitation in its current form is scope: image classifiers only, with VLM interpretation that inherits biases from the captioning model. Text and tabular inversion auditing still require MIBench-style integration with domain-specific evaluation protocols.

### Model-Inversion Evaluation Reliability (CVPR 2026)

FMLLM (CVPR 2026 Findings) challenges the dominant model-inversion success metric used in many image MI papers. The authors reassessed 27 attack setups and found that reconstructions counted as successful by the conventional secondary-evaluator framework often did not preserve the true visual identity of the private training subject; reported false-positive rates reached 89--97% across evaluation models, and attacks previously reported above 90--100% accuracy never exceeded 80% true success under the multimodal-LLM evaluation framework. For verification, this is a caution rather than a reason to dismiss inversion risk: a passing or failing MI red-team result is only as good as its evaluator. C11.4 evidence should document the evaluation framework, false-positive controls, positive/negative pair calibration, and whether the assessor is independent from the target model's private training task.

### Scale-MIA: MI Against Secure Federated Aggregation (NDSS 2025)

Scale-MIA (NDSS 2025) demonstrates that secure aggregation -- widely assumed to be the cornerstone defense for federated learning against gradient inversion -- does not prevent model inversion when attackers compromise the aggregation server or exploit aggregation metadata. The attack scales to large federations by exploiting structure in the aggregated update rather than requiring client-side access, meaning that FL deployments that treat secure aggregation as a privacy guarantee need to layer DP noise or orthogonal-subspace defenses (e.g., CENSOR, NDSS 2025) on top. For FL contexts, Scale-MIA tightens the verification standard for all C11.4 requirements: it is no longer sufficient to document that secure aggregation is in place; teams need evidence that DP noise or equivalent gradient protection holds under aggregation compromise assumptions.

### Embedding Inversion Defenses: Obfuscated Embeddings (2024--2026)

Beyond the EGuard approach already documented, recent work has consolidated a simpler defense pattern for embedding-level inversion: injecting Laplace noise into pooled embeddings during inference and training a decoder that operates on obfuscated embeddings. Reported reconstruction accuracy drops sharply while downstream task utility remains within a few percentage points of clean baselines. For deployments that expose sentence or document embeddings over an API (vector search, RAG retrieval, similarity services), this offers a deployment-time defense that does not require retraining the underlying LLM. It complements -- and is often combined with -- query-rate limiting from requirement 11.4.2, since even with noisy embeddings an unlimited query budget enables iterative refinement attacks. Organizations deploying shared embedding endpoints should treat embedding obfuscation as part of their MI defense layer rather than relying solely on access controls.

### MI Defense via Feature Purification (IEEE TDSC 2025)

A 2025 IEEE TDSC paper introduced feature purification as a complementary MI defense: rather than perturbing outputs or gradients, the method trains the target model with an auxiliary loss that removes private attributes from intermediate feature representations while preserving task-relevant information. The key practical value is that feature purification can be retrofitted to already-trained models via short fine-tuning runs, whereas full DP-SGD retraining is often infeasible for large production models. It sits between deployment-time defenses (output generalization, stand-in models) and training-time DP, offering a middle path when teams have DP training budget constraints but can afford a fine-tuning pass.

### Disparate Privacy Vulnerability and Targeted Attribute Inference (USENIX Security 2025)

Kabir et al. (USENIX Security 2025, arXiv:2504.04033) revealed a critical gap in how output-level defenses for requirement 11.4.1 are typically evaluated: aggregate privacy metrics systematically understate risk. Their "Disparate Privacy Vulnerability" paper demonstrates that attribute inference attacks exhibit group-level disparities -- while overall inference accuracy on a full dataset may appear low, adversaries can achieve up to 100% accuracy against high-correlation demographic subgroups by computing angular differences across confidence score distributions for records with identical non-sensitive features but varying sensitive attribute values. The attack exploits that models train on data with correlation imbalances across groups, causing them to output measurably different confidence distributions depending on the sensitive attribute value of a queried record. The proposed defense, **Balanced Correlation Defense (BCorr)**, addresses the root cause by equalizing the correlation between sensitive and output attributes across demographic groups during training rather than simply restricting output precision. BCorr preserves primary task utility while eliminating the correlation signal attackers exploit. The practical verification implication is significant: evaluating requirement 11.4.1 compliance at the dataset level is insufficient -- auditors must compute angular-difference variance across demographic groups to identify vulnerable subpopulations, and group-level privacy auditing should be treated as a minimum standard for tabular and structured-output models.

### Comparative Analysis of Defense Mechanisms on Tabular Data (2025)

A comparative study published in 2025 (MDPI Cybersecurity) evaluated multiple defense approaches against model inversion on benchmark tabular datasets (ADULT, GSS, FTE). The study found that combined defenses -- integrating differential privacy, federated learning, adaptive noise injection, and ensemble-based obfuscation -- can mitigate up to 50% of MI attacks while maintaining F1 scores above 0.85. Notably, no single defense was sufficient: API output restriction alone (returning top-k labels rather than full probability vectors) reduced but did not eliminate attribute inference risk, particularly for records near decision boundaries. The practical guidance for implementing requirement 11.4.1 is that output generalization should be one component of a layered defense including DP training, and that the effectiveness of output-only defenses depends heavily on how tight the generalization (bucket width) is relative to the information the adversary can infer from the output class itself. For multi-class classifiers where the output class is itself a sensitive attribute proxy, label suppression or re-mapping may be necessary alongside bucketing.

### Regulatory Alignment: ISO/IEC 42005 and EU AI Act Timeline (2025--2026)

ISO/IEC 42005:2025 (AI system impact assessment) was published in 2025 as a companion to ISO/IEC 42001 (AI management system). Unlike 42001, which specifies the AIMS process, 42005 provides concrete guidance for pre-deployment impact assessment -- mapping purpose, stakeholders, and potential harms including model inversion and data leakage risks -- and mandates that this assessment precede formal risk evaluation. For C11.4 requirements, this means that regulated organizations operating under 42001 are expected to produce MI risk documentation as an input to their AIMS risk register, not as ad-hoc security evidence -- covering output sanitization controls (11.4.1) and query throttling evidence (11.4.2). In parallel, the EU AI Act's enforcement timeline tightens through 2026: GPAI provider obligations began August 2, 2025, and Commission enforcement powers take effect August 2, 2026. High-risk AI systems are explicitly required to undergo red-teaming for privacy vulnerabilities including model inversion, making MIBench-style or Automated MI Pipeline audits a likely component of compliance evidence for covered deployments.

### PILS: Language Model Inversion via Compact Next-Token Distributions (NeurIPS 2025)

A line of work that directly targets the inference surface of API-served LLMs is Prompt Inversion from Logprob Sequences (PILS), published in late 2025. The core insight is that next-token probability distributions occupy a low-dimensional subspace, which makes it possible to losslessly compress the full distribution into a sequence of pseudo-embeddings and feed those to an encoder-decoder inverter. The result is a 2--3.5x improvement in exact prompt recovery over the previous Logit2Text-style baseline -- on one held-out benchmark, exact recovery jumped from 17% to 60% -- and the inverter generalises across generation lengths (a model trained at 16 steps gains an additional 5--27 points of accuracy when evaluated at 32 steps). The threat model is reconstruction of hidden user prompts and (more concerning for deployments) hidden system messages from any API endpoint that returns logprobs. The paper proposes no defenses, leaving providers with two practical mitigations from the existing toolbox: suppress logprob exposure entirely, or sample stochastically rather than returning argmax/top-k probabilities so the inverter sees noisier evidence per query. For requirement 11.4.1 verification, this means treating any `logprobs=true` API parameter as a privacy-relevant configuration toggle, not just a debugging convenience -- it materially expands what an adversary can reconstruct from a fixed query budget.

### AgentLeak: Full-Stack Privacy Leakage Benchmark for Multi-Agent Systems (February 2026)

AgentLeak (arXiv 2602.11510) is the first full-stack benchmark for privacy leakage in multi-agent LLM systems, instrumenting seven distinct data channels: final outputs, inter-agent messages, tool inputs and outputs, shared memory, logs, and artifacts. Across 1,000 scenarios spanning healthcare, finance, legal, and corporate domains, the benchmark evaluated GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Mistral Large, and Llama 3.3 70B, producing 4,979 validated execution traces. The headline finding reframes how MI risk should be measured for agentic deployments: per-channel output leakage drops in multi-agent topologies (27.2% on the C1 channel versus 43.2% in single-agent), but total OR-aggregated system exposure across C1, C2, and C5 channels rises to 68.9%. Output-only audits -- the implicit assumption underlying most existing 11.4.1 verification approaches -- miss the inter-agent messages and shared-memory channels where a substantial fraction of the leakage occurs. For organizations deploying coordinator-worker LLM agents, this benchmark provides reproducible test scenarios and a 32-class attack taxonomy that auditors can use directly; the harness adapts to LangChain, CrewAI, AutoGPT, and MetaGPT. The practical implication for C11.4 verification is that scope must extend beyond final API outputs to include any internal channel that crosses a trust boundary.

### Differential Privacy Bounds for Agent Outputs (March 2026)

Yang and Zhu (March 2026) model enterprise AI agents as stochastic mechanisms that map prompts and internal datasets to token-sequence distributions, then define both token-level and message-level differential privacy for generated responses. The work is useful for C11.4 because it connects leakage risk to concrete generation parameters such as temperature and response length, making "output generalization" more than a schema-control question. In practice, an auditor can ask whether sensitive-data agents document privacy budgets at the response level, whether longer messages consume more privacy budget, and whether temperature or sampling policies are intentionally chosen for privacy-utility tradeoffs rather than tuned only for answer quality. This does not replace inversion testing, but it gives a quantitative way to reason about repeated adaptive queries where each response leaks a bounded amount of information.

### Activation Inversion on Decentralized LLM Training (ACL 2025)

Dai, Lu, and Zhou (ACL 2025, arXiv 2502.16086) provide the first systematic activation inversion attack against decentralized LLM training. The attacker assembles a shadow dataset of public text labels paired with their corresponding activations on a reference model, then trains an inverter that maps activations back to text. Applied to victim decentralized pipelines where intermediate activations cross node boundaries, the inverter reconstructs sensitive training data including names, phone numbers, and email addresses. This formalises the AIA threat described in earlier sections and makes it concrete: organizations using decentralized or split-learning architectures (multi-party fine-tuning consortiums, on-device personalisation pipelines that send activations to a central aggregator) need to treat activation transmission as equivalent to raw data transmission for privacy-impact-assessment purposes. Rate limiting (requirement 11.4.2) does not apply -- there are no per-principal queries to throttle -- so defense must come from activation-level noise injection, secure enclaves, or homomorphic encryption on the transmitted intermediates.

### MoE Routing Leakage (February 2026)

Expert Selections in MoE Models Reveal (Almost) As Much As Text extends inversion concern from activations and logits to routing metadata. The attack reconstructs tokens from mixture-of-experts expert-selection traces alone: a three-layer MLP reached 63.1% top-1 token recovery, while a transformer sequence decoder reached 91.2% top-1 and 94.8% top-10 token recovery on 32-token OpenWebText sequences after training on 100M tokens. The practical leakage scenarios include distributed inference, telemetry, and side channels where operators may log or expose expert-routing decisions while assuming they are harmless operational metadata. For C11.4 verification, MoE routing traces, load-balancer logs, and per-token expert IDs should be scoped as sensitive inference artifacts; masking or rate limiting final model outputs does not protect data if routing metadata is independently available to an adversary.

### Multi-GPU DP for Production LLMs: Sharding-Compatible DP-SGD

A persistent gap in the DP tooling stack has been that Opacus and similar frameworks lose efficiency above roughly 1.5B parameters because per-sample gradient computation does not compose cleanly with model sharding. Recent multi-GPU DP frameworks built on DeepSpeed ZeRO -- DynamoEnhance is one example, with public claims of supporting Llama-70B and Mistral-8x7B under DP-SGD -- demonstrate that sharded DP training has become practically deployable, integrating with Hugging Face, LoRA, BitsAndBytes quantisation, flash attention, and mixed precision. This expands the set of deployments where formal DP can be the foundational defense for requirement 11.4.1 rather than an output-level workaround. As of 2026, organisations training or fine-tuning large LLMs no longer have a credible engineering excuse for skipping DP entirely on grounds of model size; the conversation shifts to the privacy-utility tradeoff at chosen epsilon budgets and to whether the DP implementation is correctly applied (which is exactly what VERIDP-style cryptographic verification is designed to attest).

### OpenDP Deployments Registry (Harvard, March 2026)

Harvard's OpenDP project published a Differential Privacy Deployments Registry on March 30, 2026, cataloguing real-world DP implementations across companies and government agencies. The [registry](https://registry.opendp.org/deployments-registry/) is the first standardised public catalogue of in-production DP deployments and serves three concrete purposes for C11.4 verification: (1) provides comparable epsilon budgets and scope examples that auditors can reference when evaluating whether an organisation's DP parameters are reasonable for their deployment context, (2) supports evidence-based ISO/IEC 42005 impact assessments by giving organisations sectoral baselines rather than producing privacy budgets in a vacuum, and (3) reduces the asymmetric-information problem where deployers cannot tell whether their epsilon choice is conservative or aggressive relative to peer practice. The registry is maintained by Salil Vadhan's group at Harvard's John A. Paulson School of Engineering and Applied Sciences and is intended to evolve as new deployments are documented.

### EU AI Act Timeline After the May 2026 Political Agreement

As of May 2026, the European Commission's AI Act implementation page reflects the Digital Omnibus political agreement reached on May 7, 2026. The AI Act still entered into force on August 1, 2024, prohibited-practice and AI-literacy obligations applied from February 2, 2025, and GPAI governance obligations applied from August 2, 2025. The key change is the high-risk timeline: rules for systems in certain high-risk areas, including biometrics, critical infrastructure, education, employment, migration, asylum, and border control, are now listed for application from December 2, 2027, while high-risk systems embedded in regulated products such as lifts or toys are listed for August 2, 2028. For C11.4 planning, the delay should be treated as extra evidence-building time, not as permission to defer inversion testing. The same artifacts -- model-inversion red-team reports, DP epsilon budgets, output-schema reviews, query-throttle calibration, and incident logging -- remain relevant to GDPR Article 17 erasure handling, Article 32 security-of-processing arguments, ISO/IEC 42005 impact assessments, NIST AI RMF GenAI profile reviews, and AI Act technical documentation once the high-risk obligations apply.

### Medical Imaging: Retinal Image Inversion Attacks (2026)

A January 2026 study demonstrated the first gradient inversion attack framework specifically targeting retinal age prediction models in federated learning. Using public datasets to train the attack model, the researchers achieved identification of 92% of participants from reconstructed retinal images -- a stark result for healthcare settings where patient re-identification constitutes a serious privacy violation. Critically, moderate differential privacy (at standard epsilon budgets) was insufficient to prevent identifiable reconstructions, suggesting that medical imaging federated learning requires either stricter DP budgets or complementary defenses. Related work on sensitivity-aware differential privacy (2025) proposed adaptive noise calibration that applies stronger protection to high-sensitivity image regions while reducing noise on low-sensitivity areas, achieving 13.5% average performance improvement over uniform DP methods on real-world medical imaging datasets.

### DP-OPD: Synthesis-Free Private Distillation (April 2026)

DP-OPD (Khadem et al., April 6, 2026, arXiv:2604.04461) collapses two expensive steps that previously stood between practitioners and DP-trained student models. Prior pipelines either applied DP-SGD to both teacher and student (which compounds the noise floor and degrades utility) or generated DP synthetic data from a privately fine-tuned teacher and trained a non-private student on the synthetic corpus (which doubles the engineering cost and bottlenecks on synthesis quality). DP-OPD discards both: the teacher remains frozen and non-private, the student is trained directly with DP-SGD on student-generated rollouts using the frozen teacher's token-level distributions as targets. At a strict privacy budget of epsilon=2.0, this reduced perplexity from 44.15 to 41.68 on Yelp and from 32.43 to 30.63 on BigPatent versus standard DP fine-tuning, while eliminating the offline DP-synthesis stage entirely. The practical implication for requirement 11.4.1 is that DP-trained students -- the "stand-in model" pattern documented earlier as a deployment-time inversion defense -- are now substantially cheaper to produce, making it harder to justify exposing the original DP-untreated model behind a public API on cost grounds alone.

### FlashDP: Memory-Efficient DP-SGD at LLM Scale (NeurIPS 2024 / 2025)

FlashDP (Wang et al., NeurIPS 2024, arXiv:2507.01154) addresses the engineering bottleneck that has historically pushed teams to skip DP-SGD on large LLMs: per-sample gradient accounting blows up GPU memory at the parameter scales where MI risk is highest. FlashDP fuses per-layer clipping and gradient accumulation into a single cache-friendly kernel, reducing memory movement by up to 50% and redundant computation by approximately 20%, with no increase in peak memory versus non-DP training. On a four-A100 system pre-training Llama-13B, FlashDP reaches 90% of non-DP throughput while preserving the accuracy of standard per-layer-clipped DP-SGD; on GPT-2 large at batch size 1 it uses approximately 38% less memory than Opacus and roughly doubles Opacus throughput. The code is available in the [FlashDP repository](https://github.com/kaustpradalab/flashdp). Combined with DynamoEnhance-style ZeRO-sharded DP-SGD documented earlier, this further closes the gap between "DP is theoretically appropriate" and "DP is operationally affordable" for the production LLM training that underlies requirement 11.4.1's output-level controls.

### Trap-MID and TrapNet: Trapdoor-Based MI Defense (NeurIPS 2024 / IEEE TIFS 2025)

Trapdoor-based MI defenses introduce a fundamentally different attack-surface manipulation than DP, output generalization, or noise injection. Trap-MID (Liu and Chen, NeurIPS 2024, arXiv:2411.08460) integrates a trigger-label association into the trained model: when an input matches the trapdoor trigger pattern, the model predicts a specific designated label. Because the trapdoor offers a stronger gradient signal than the underlying training data manifold, optimization-based MI attacks are pulled toward reconstructing the trigger rather than genuine training samples. The defense is data-free (no auxiliary dataset needed), has minimal computational overhead, and achieved state-of-the-art protection across multiple MI attack benchmarks with negligible primary-task utility loss. The follow-up TrapNet (IEEE TIFS, 2025, doi:10.1109/TIFS.2025.3560557) generalised the technique by injecting trapdoors into the classification manifold itself rather than relying on a single trigger, broadening protection across a wider range of advanced MI attacks. The practical attraction for requirement 11.4.1 verification is operational simplicity: trapdoor injection can be added during ordinary fine-tuning without the privacy-budget engineering of DP-SGD, and unlike output sanitization it does not constrain what an API may return. The trade-off is that trapdoors offer no formal privacy guarantee -- their effectiveness is empirical, attack-class-specific, and may degrade against trapdoor-aware adaptive attackers, so trapdoor defenses should be treated as a complementary layer alongside DP rather than a substitute. Code is available in the [Trap-MID repository](https://github.com/ntuaislab/Trap-MID).

### RePAIR: Inference-Time Unlearning via Natural Language (April 2026)

RePAIR (Rachapudi et al., April 14, 2026, arXiv:2604.12820) shifts machine unlearning from a model-provider operation to a user-driven inference-time control. The system uses three coordinated models -- a watchdog that detects unlearning intent in user prompts, a surgeon that generates repair procedures, and the patient model whose MLP activations are steered toward a refusal subspace via closed-form pseudoinverse updates (the STAMP mechanism). Across harmful-knowledge suppression, misinformation correction, and personal-data erasure tasks, RePAIR reached near-zero forget scores (Acc_f = 0.00) while preserving up to 84.47% retained-task accuracy, outperforming six prior unlearning baselines without requiring fine-tuning. A low-rank variant provides approximately 3x speedup over training-based alternatives. The relevance to requirement 11.4.1 is that natural-language unlearning interfaces convert what has been an operations-team workflow (issue erasure ticket -> retrain or patch model -> redeploy) into something closer to an in-band data-subject control, which is increasingly what GDPR and the EU AI Act expect when an inversion attack confirms that a deployed model retains personal data. The open question is auditability: a closed-form activation steer that affects MLP outputs only at inference time leaves the underlying weights unchanged, so verifiers must distinguish "the data was removed from the model" from "the data is masked at runtime but still retrievable if the steering layer is bypassed."

### RAG Security Taxonomy and Knowledge Exfiltration (April 2026)

A comprehensive RAG security taxonomy (Xu et al., April 9, 2026, arXiv:2604.08304) organises the RAG workflow into six stages across three trust boundaries and four security surfaces: pre-retrieval knowledge corruption, retrieval-time access manipulation, downstream context exploitation, and -- most directly relevant to C11.4 -- knowledge exfiltration. The exfiltration surface includes embedding inversion attacks against vector databases, where reconstruction attacks have been shown to recover 50--70% of original input words from compromised embedding vectors (consistent with OWASP LLM08:2025 sensitivity ratings for vector and embedding weaknesses). The taxonomy's contribution to verification practice is the explicit boundary it draws between inherent LLM flaws (already covered by output-level controls in 11.4.1) and RAG-introduced or RAG-amplified leakage paths that originate in the retrieval pipeline. The authors observe that current defenses remain "largely reactive and fragmented," pointing to a gap where MI verification scope for RAG-backed deployments must extend across all four surfaces rather than testing only the LLM endpoint. For organizations operating coordinator-worker LLM stacks with RAG retrieval (the architecture pattern AgentLeak instrumented for multi-agent leakage), this taxonomy provides a missing structural map: 11.4.2 rate limiting applies to the LLM endpoint, but separate throttling, embedding obfuscation, and retrieval auditing controls are needed at each upstream surface to prevent the inversion attack surface from migrating into the RAG layer.

### Zero2Text: Training-Free Cross-Domain Embedding Inversion (February 2026)

Zero2Text (Kim et al., February 2026, arXiv:2602.01757) materially raises the bar for RAG and vector-database privacy reviews because it removes two assumptions that made earlier embedding inversion attacks easier to dismiss: access to in-domain training pairs and large optimization budgets. The attack uses recursive online alignment, combining an LLM prior with dynamic ridge regression to steer generated text toward a target embedding in a strict black-box, cross-domain setting. On MS MARCO against an OpenAI embedding model, the authors report 1.8x higher ROUGE-L and 6.4x higher BLEU-2 than baselines, recovering sentences from unknown domains without leaked data pairs; they also report that standard differential-privacy-style defenses were not enough against the adaptive attack. For C11.4 verification, this pushes auditors to test embedding endpoints directly, not just the final LLM response: rate limits, per-tenant vector access controls, embedding-noise parameters, and retrieval audit logs all become evidence for inversion resistance.

### Conditional Masked Diffusion Embedding Inversion (February 2026)

Xiao (February 2026, arXiv:2602.11047) shows that embedding inversion can be performed through conditional masked diffusion rather than autoregressive token generation. The attack conditions a masked diffusion language model on the target embedding through adaptive layer normalization, then recovers all token positions in parallel through iterative denoising. The notable operational detail is efficiency: the paper reports recovery on 32-token sequences across three embedding models with only 8 forward passes and no access to the target encoder at inference time. The accompanying implementation supports Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B, jina-embeddings-v3, and embeddinggemma-300m configurations. This matters for C11.4 because embedding inversion is becoming cheaper, more encoder-agnostic, and easier to operationalize; rate limits for embedding APIs should be calibrated for low-query reconstruction methods rather than older iterative re-embedding attacks that required many correction loops.

### Active Data Reconstruction for Training-Data Detection (February 2026)

Yin et al. (February 2026, arXiv:2602.19020) introduced Active Data Reconstruction Attack (ADRA), a membership-inference technique that actively fine-tunes a policy initialized from the target model so it reconstructs candidate text. The result is adjacent to C11.3, but it matters for C11.4 because it reframes "training data detection" as a reconstruction workflow rather than a passive likelihood test. The authors report average detection improvement of 10.7% over the previous runner-up, including gains on both pre-training and post-training data. Practically, this means reconstruction risk can move outside the production inference API: organisations that expose fine-tuning, RLHF, evaluation, or distillation interfaces should include those interfaces in inversion threat models and not rely solely on production query-rate limits.

### Separable Expert Architecture for Privacy-Preserving Personalization (April 2026)

Microsoft Research's Separable Expert Architecture (SEA, April 2026, arXiv:2604.21571) proposes an architectural mitigation for personalized LLMs: keep shared base weights static, use domain LoRA adapters that contain no user-specific data, and store per-user personalization only in deletable proxy artifacts. The authors evaluated Phi-3.5-mini and Llama-3.1-8B, reporting 82--89% verification pass rates after proxy deletion and near-zero cross-user contamination. For model inversion resistance, the useful pattern is the invariant rather than the exact architecture: user-specific gradients must not flow into shared weights, proxy deletion must return behavior toward baseline, and auditors should be able to inspect routing, adapter, and storage boundaries. SEA does not prove that the base model is free of memorized data, but it offers a practical design for keeping new personalization data out of invertible shared parameters.

### OpenDP Output Privacy Attacks Repository (December 2025)

OpenDP's Attacks Working Group launched the Community Guide to Output Privacy Attacks in December 2025, cataloguing more than 80 papers and classifying them by threat model, success metric, and related dimensions. This is not a model-inversion benchmark by itself, but it is useful verification infrastructure for C11.4 because it gives auditors a maintained map of privacy-attack variants to consider before declaring a test plan complete. For mature pages like this one, the practical use is gap analysis: compare the system's tested threat models against the repository taxonomy, then document why excluded attack classes are out of scope or covered by adjacent controls.

## Open Research Questions

- LLM inversion attacks have been demonstrated (Llama 3.2 PII extraction, split-learning attacks), but how do attack success rates compare across model families and scales? Early evidence suggests vulnerability grows with scale.
- Is gradient clipping without noise addition a meaningful defense, or does it merely increase attack cost without fundamentally limiting leakage?
- How should model inversion risk be assessed for models that process sensitive data only at inference time (e.g., RAG over medical records) rather than training time?
- Can model inversion attacks be reliably detected through query-pattern analysis, or are they indistinguishable from legitimate exploratory queries? FedGuard-CI's MIRE offers one quantitative approach, but its applicability beyond federated settings is unproven.
- What output generalization granularity provides a useful privacy-utility trade-off for different domains (healthcare, finance, HR)?
- Are multilingual models fundamentally more vulnerable to inversion than monolingual models, and should multilingual deployments require stricter defenses?
- Can embedding-level protections like EGuard substitute for full DP-SGD in scenarios where training-time DP is infeasible?
- How should MIBench-style standardized benchmarks be incorporated into verification procedures for requirements 11.4.1 and 11.4.2?
- VaultGemma shows DP is viable at 1B parameters -- can the approach scale to 7B+ models without prohibitive compute costs, and how do Poisson sampling techniques generalize beyond the Gemma architecture?
- Model inversion attacks on VLMs achieved 75%+ accuracy with no proposed defenses. What multimodal-specific countermeasures are needed beyond text-only DP approaches?
- How effective are stand-in model distillation approaches at preventing inversion compared to direct DP training, and can the fidelity-privacy trade-off be quantified systematically?
- DFSU demonstrated that model inversion can be repurposed defensively for PII removal -- can this approach generalize beyond text to image and multimodal models where inversion attacks are also effective?
- TTLoRA-DP shows adapter architecture structure alone can suppress membership signals. What other structural constraints beyond tensor train decomposition might offer similar privacy benefits?
- VERIDP provides cryptographic verification of DP-SGD, but at what scale does per-iteration proof generation become impractical for production training runs with millions of iterations?
- As GDPR enforcement increasingly treats invertible models as data copies, how should organizations quantify inversion risk to determine whether retraining or unlearning is required for compliance?
- KV-cache inversion (NDSS 2026) opens an inference-time attack surface independent of training-time DP. Should KV-cache obfuscation (e.g., KV-Cloak) become a standard requirement for multi-tenant LLM deployments, and how does it interact with prefix caching performance optimizations?
- Data poisoning defenses like LPA shift the attack surface from the victim model to the attacker's inversion model. How robust are these defenses against adaptive attackers who anticipate output perturbation, and do they compose well with DP training?
- The retinal imaging study showed 92% participant identification despite moderate DP. For high-sensitivity medical imaging, what epsilon thresholds are needed to prevent re-identification, and is sensitivity-aware DP (non-uniform noise) sufficient as an alternative to stricter uniform budgets?
- Gradient inversion feasibility varies dramatically between legacy and modern architectures. Should verification approaches for C11.4 incorporate architecture-specific risk assessment rather than applying uniform DP requirements regardless of inherent model resistance?
- Internal state inversion (USENIX Security 2025) showed 86.88% F1 reconstruction from middle layers. For collaborative inference and split-learning deployments, should internal state obfuscation be a distinct requirement alongside output-level controls?
- LLM recommender system inversion recovered 87% of user demographics from output logits alone. Should recommendation-specific output sanitization be treated as a distinct control, or do existing output generalization requirements (11.4.1) sufficiently cover this attack surface?
- CENSOR's orthogonal subspace approach offers a utility-preserving alternative to DP noise for federated gradient protection. Under what conditions does orthogonal perturbation provide formal privacy guarantees comparable to DP, and can the approach be composed with DP for layered defense?
- Diffusion-based model inversion attacks produce significantly higher-fidelity reconstructions than GAN-based predecessors. How should organizations update their inversion risk models as generative architectures continue to improve, and at what fidelity threshold does reconstruction constitute a privacy breach under GDPR?
- Privacy-preserving inference via MPC/FHE/TEE addresses the inference-time gap that output-level controls alone cannot cover, but current FHE approaches are 1000x slower. What performance threshold makes cryptographic inference practical for latency-sensitive applications, and how should organizations choose between TEE-based and crypto-based approaches?
- AdaDPIGU's importance-based masking achieved better-than-non-private utility on CIFAR-10 at epsilon=4. Does the regularization effect persist on large language models, or is it specific to smaller image architectures where overfitting dominates?
- Scale-MIA shows secure aggregation alone is insufficient against MI in federated learning. Should verification for FL deployments require explicit evidence of layered defense (DP noise plus orthogonal-subspace or feature-purification defenses), and what combined-defense testing methodology is appropriate?
- The Automated MI Assessment Pipeline uses VLMs for zero-shot interpretation of reconstructed images. How should its four adversarial risk dimensions be standardized into audit thresholds, and can the approach generalize beyond computer vision to text and tabular inversion auditing?
- ISO/IEC 42005 (2025) mandates MI risk in pre-deployment impact assessment. What evidence artifacts -- MIBench scores, DP epsilon budgets, red-team reports -- should organizations produce to satisfy 42005 auditors, and how do these align with EU AI Act high-risk system obligations taking effect in August 2026?
- PILS demonstrated that next-token logprobs collapse to a low-dimensional subspace that admits 60% exact prompt recovery. Should `logprobs=true` API parameters be disabled by default for production deployments, and how should auditors evaluate the privacy cost of exposing logprobs to third-party tooling that legitimately needs them (evaluation harnesses, calibration tooling, downstream stochastic decoding)?
- AgentLeak shows multi-agent topologies leak 68.9% across internal channels even when per-channel output leakage drops. How should C11.4 verification scope expand to cover inter-agent messages, shared memory, and tool I/O, and what minimum subset of AgentLeak's 1,000 scenarios should auditors run for representative coverage?
- Activation inversion on decentralized training (Dai et al., ACL 2025) recovers PII from intermediates even with no per-principal queries to throttle. Should split-learning and federated deployments be required to apply activation-level noise or homomorphic encryption to transmitted intermediates, and what are the convergence costs at production scale?
- Multi-GPU sharded DP-SGD frameworks (DynamoEnhance and successors) make DP training feasible for 70B+ models. As this becomes deployable, should regulators raise the bar for what counts as "appropriate technical measures" under GDPR Article 32 for sensitive-data fine-tuning, treating the absence of DP as a presumptive failure rather than a discretionary choice?
- The EU AI Act's August 2026 high-risk activation creates demand for standardised inversion-robustness red-team artifacts. How should the MI red-team report format align with existing EU AI Act technical documentation templates, and how often should rotating-attacker testing be required to keep pace with attack literature improvements (PILS, KV-cache, activation inversion all post-dating most existing red-team playbooks)?
- DP-OPD eliminates DP teacher training and offline synthesis, but only evaluated at small-to-medium model scales and on text generation tasks. Does the synthesis-free advantage hold for multi-billion-parameter students distilled from frontier teachers, and how does it interact with PEFT adapters versus full-parameter student training?
- Trapdoor-based MI defenses (Trap-MID, TrapNet) provide empirical protection without DP's privacy budget, but offer no formal guarantee. How robust are trapdoors to trapdoor-aware adaptive attackers who explicitly target the trigger pattern, and can trapdoor defenses be composed with DP to provide both empirical and formal protection at lower combined cost than either alone?
- RePAIR's natural-language unlearning operates via inference-time activation steering rather than weight modification, raising an audit question: should "the data is masked at runtime" satisfy GDPR right-to-erasure obligations, or does compliance require demonstrable removal from model parameters that survives bypass of the steering layer?
- The RAG security taxonomy (Xu et al., April 2026) identifies four security surfaces but notes that defenses remain "largely reactive and fragmented." For RAG-backed deployments, should C11.4 verification scope be formally extended to cover the embedding-inversion exfiltration surface alongside the LLM endpoint, and what minimum evidence (per-surface throttling, embedding obfuscation parameters, retrieval audit logs) should auditors expect?
- FlashDP-style fused per-layer DP-SGD reaches 90% of non-DP throughput on 13B-parameter pretraining. As this engineering gap closes further, at what scale does "we couldn't afford DP-SGD" stop being a credible justification for skipping formal privacy in fine-tuning runs that touch personal data?
- Zero2Text shows that black-box embedding inversion can work without leaked in-domain pairs. Should vector databases expose per-query privacy budgets and embedding-noise metadata so auditors can reason about reconstruction risk the same way they reason about API output precision?
- SEA-style personalization isolates user data in deletable proxies, but verification depends on architectural discipline. What evidence should prove that user-specific gradients never reached shared adapters or base weights, especially in systems with continuous learning or analytics feedback loops?
- OpenDP's output-privacy attack catalogue is broader than model inversion. Which attack classes should be mandatory in a C11.4 test plan, and which belong only in adjacent C11.3 membership-inference or C12 privacy reviews?
- Adapter-based FedLLM attacks show that frozen backbones and LoRA-style updates can still leak training text through low-rank gradients. Should federated fine-tuning reviews require DP noise or secure computation specifically on adapter gradients?
- SOMP recovers meaningful text from aggregated gradients at batch sizes where earlier attacks degraded. What batch-size and aggregation thresholds should count as sufficient risk reduction for collaborative training deployments?
- Conditional masked diffusion makes embedding inversion faster and less dependent on target-encoder access. How should embedding API rate limits and privacy budgets change when practical attacks need only a small number of forward passes?
- Token-level and message-level DP for agents ties privacy leakage to temperature and response length. Can this become an auditable control for repeated adaptive queries, or does real-world tool use break the assumptions needed for meaningful response-level privacy budgets?
- If MoE expert-selection traces can reconstruct tokens, should expert-routing logs be classified as sensitive content logs, and what retention limits or aggregation thresholds are appropriate for production telemetry?
- FMLLM shows that conventional image-MI success metrics can overcount false positives. What evaluator calibration evidence should be mandatory before accepting an MI red-team result as either passing or failing?
- VGIA can certify tabular record reconstruction from federated gradients. Should tabular FL reviews require algebraic/certified attacks in addition to visual or loss-based privacy audits?
- The May 2026 AI Act timeline shift gives high-risk providers more implementation time. How should inversion-resistance test reports be kept current when attack literature moves faster than regulatory deadlines?
