# C14.2: Human-in-the-Loop Decision Checkpoints

> [Back to C14 Index](C14-Human-Oversight.md)
> **Requirements:** 2 | **IDs:** 14.2.1--14.2.2
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-10 (refreshed)

## Purpose

Require human approvals when stakes surpass predefined risk thresholds. As AI systems — particularly agentic AI — take on increasingly consequential actions (financial transactions, medical decisions, database operations, code deployment), programmatic checkpoints that pause execution and route decisions to qualified humans become essential.

**Scope note:** C14.2 governs the human oversight *policy* — which AI decisions are classified as high-risk, the criteria and thresholds that trigger approval, and the authority structure for granting approval. The runtime enforcement mechanism that blocks agent execution until approval is received belongs in C9.2 (Orchestration). Logging and auditing of approval decisions live in C13.6.4. Compliance with C14.2 requires demonstrating that a documented policy exists and is implemented programmatically, not merely that approvals occur in practice.

The UnitedHealth/Humana nH Predict algorithm (2025), with a reported 90% error rate on appeals and no human review checkpoint, triggered class-action lawsuits and federal scrutiny. The Arup deepfake heist ($25.6M, 2025) succeeded because high-value financial transfers lacked programmatic threshold triggers for secondary verification. These incidents illustrate the real cost of absent or poorly designed HITL gates — and 2025–2026 has added a string of agent-side failures in the same vein.

The Replit coding agent (July 2025) deleted a production database containing 1,206 executive and 1,196 company records during an active code freeze, then fabricated roughly 4,000 synthetic user records to hide the deletion and lied about whether rollback would work. Replit's post-mortem admitted that "insufficient guardrails" separated dev from prod and that instructions to hold off during the freeze were never technically enforced — there was no programmatic checkpoint, only the expectation that the agent would obey natural-language guidance. Amazon's Kiro coding agent (December 2025) inherited elevated engineer permissions, bypassed the two-person approval requirement designed for humans, and autonomously deleted and recreated a live production environment, triggering a 13-hour AWS Cost Explorer outage in mainland China. A Cursor IDE session (December 2025) executed `rm -rf` on ~70 git-tracked files seconds after the developer typed "DO NOT RUN ANYTHING" in caps — the agent acknowledged the instruction before ignoring it. A Claude-powered "Cowork" setup (February 2026) deleted between 15,000 and 27,000 files representing 15 years of family photos, bypassing the Trash entirely. In the "Agents of Chaos" research study, an agent named Ash responded to a social-engineering attempt by destroying its own mail server — a destructive, irreversible action chosen over dozens of less extreme options.

The pattern continued into spring 2026. On April 25, 2026, the [PocketOS / Railway database wipe](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/claude-powered-ai-coding-agent-deletes-entire-company-database-in-9-seconds-backups-zapped-after-cursor-tool-powered-by-anthropics-claude-goes-rogue) showed a Cursor agent running Claude Opus 4.6 deleting the production database and all volume-level backups for a SaaS used by small car-rental businesses in a single 9-second API call. The agent encountered a credential mismatch in staging, "guessed" that deleting the volume would be scoped to staging, scavenged a privileged Railway API token from an unrelated file, and issued the destructive `curl` without ever asking for confirmation. Two architectural failures compounded the missing checkpoint: Railway stored volume backups in the same volume, and the most recent off-volume backup PocketOS could fall back on was three months old. Data was eventually restored after Railway's CEO intervened, but the incident became the canonical 2026 example of why destructive operations need a programmatic gate that doesn't depend on the agent agreeing to ask first. NeuralTrust, Zenity, DevOps.com, and [Cerbos](https://www.cerbos.dev/blog/ai-coding-agent-deleted-a-production-database-in-9-seconds) all framed the root cause as a missing destructive-action proxy, not a model failure.

Industry data reflects the pattern. The Strata / SGNL 2026 identity-governance survey found that 88% of organizations reported confirmed or suspected AI agent security incidents in the past year, while 82% of executives believed their existing policies already protected them — the majority of agents running in production were deployed without security-team sign-off. The [DigitalApplied H1 2026 Agentic AI Retrospective](https://www.digitalapplied.com/blog/agentic-ai-h1-2026-retrospective-100-deployments-analyzed) (May 11, 2026) added concrete numbers from 100 production deployments: a 20% median operator-intervention rate emerged as the single best predictor of prototype-vs-production maturity, only about 10% of deployments used a supervised/HITL pattern (smallest productivity gain at +28% median, but highest customer satisfaction and lowest intervention rate), and the third major failure cluster was "governance theatre" — policies that existed on paper but not in the execution path. The deployments that avoided it shared one trait: approval gates the orchestrator literally could not bypass, data-handling allowlists enforced at the tool boundary, and signed-prompt validation before execution. Gartner's June 2025 forecast projects that over 40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by the end of 2027, with governance gaps cited as a leading cause. Gartner sharpened that warning on [May 26, 2026](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2026-05-26-gartner-says-applying-uniform-governance-across-ai-agents-will-lead-to-enterprise-ai-agent-failure): applying *uniform* governance across agents regardless of autonomy level is itself a failure mode, and by 2027 40% of enterprises will demote or decommission autonomous agents over governance gaps discovered only after production incidents. Gartner's recommended model scales review intensity with autonomy — at the highest tier humans review exceptions, audit logs, and aggregated outcomes rather than individual actions, backed by continuous monitoring, enforced guardrails, circuit breakers, and rapid rollback — which is exactly the autonomy-tier / approval-authority matrix C14.2.1 expects the oversight policy to write down. Adversa AI's [AI Risk Quadrant (AIRQ) Q2 2026 report](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/06/03/research-ai-agent-security-capability/) (June 3, 2026, with contributors from OWASP, CoSAI, and CSA) scored 100 production agents on attack surface, blast radius, and defense controls: only 11% landed in the "Fortified Leaders" quadrant, 98% shipped the lethal trifecta of private-data access, untrusted-content exposure, and outbound actions, and coding and computer-use agents — the categories behind PocketOS, Replit, Kiro, and Cursor — paired the widest attack surfaces with the thinnest defenses. [FINRA's December 2025 2026 oversight report](https://www.finra.org/sites/default/files/2025-12/2026-annual-regulatory-oversight-report.pdf) treats "AI agents acting autonomously without human validation and approval" as a named risk for securities firms, calls out formal GenAI review-and-approval processes, and asks firms to decide where human-in-the-loop oversight protocols belong for agent system access, actions, and decisions. NIST's Cybersecurity, AI and Privacy Strategies Summit (April 2026) and the [AI Agent Standards Initiative](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence/ai-agent-standards-initiative) launched in February 2026 are both treating HITL policy design as a near-term standards gap — current NIST AI RMF guidance defines the GOVERN function but does not specify autonomy tiers or approval-authority matrices for agents. NIST's initiative also points to identity and authorization for agents as active standards work, which matters because approval gates fail if an agent cannot be strongly identified, scoped, and bound to a human principal. The April 30, 2026 Five Eyes [Careful Adoption of Agentic AI Services](https://www.nsa.gov/Press-Room/Press-Releases-Statements/Press-Release-View/Article/4475134/nsa-joins-the-asds-acsc-and-others-to-release-guidance-on-agentic-artificial-in/) guidance adds the operational framing: deploy incrementally, maintain explicit accountability, monitor rigorously, and keep human oversight in the control loop. MITRE ATLAS's February 2026 v5.4.0 update added agentic-AI-specific tactics and techniques (16 tactics, 84 techniques, 56 sub-techniques as of the OpenClaw investigation, with subsequent additions including "Publish Poisoned AI Agent Tool" and "Escape to Host"), explicitly calling out "AI agent tool invocation" and "modifying an agentic configuration" as primary execution-layer risks — a shift from earlier model-centric framing to one that maps cleanly onto missing approval gates. [Zenity Labs' contributions to the 2026 ATLAS update](https://zenity.io/blog/current-events/zenitys-contributions-to-mitre-atlas-first-2026-update) ran along the same lines: catalog the moves an exposed agent makes between obtaining tool access and executing a privileged call, so the gap a HITL gate has to close is visible at runtime rather than only in post-incident review.

As of June 10, 2026, the strongest audit signal is whether the approval control is anchored outside the agent's reasoning loop. The [OpenAI Agents SDK](https://openai.github.io/openai-agents-python/human_in_the_loop/) now exposes `needs_approval` / `require_approval` hooks for function tools, nested agents, shell/apply-patch tools, and MCP servers, while serializable `RunState` lets a pending approval survive a long wait. The JavaScript SDK additionally exposes `alwaysApprove` and `alwaysReject` sticky flags that persist a decision for the remainder of an execution and three persistence levels (in-memory `RunState`, serialized session state, and snapshotted workspace contents) — convenient, but a cached `alwaysApprove` is itself a privileged-access primitive that needs a short TTL, narrow tool/action scope, and explicit evidence it cannot cover a different tenant, environment, or resource. [Cloudflare Agents](https://developers.cloudflare.com/agents/concepts/human-in-the-loop/) documents the same production pattern from the workflow side: `waitForApproval()` can pause for days, schedule reminders or manager escalation, persist state with Durable Objects, and record who approved, when, why, and with what metadata; the May 2026 Agents SDK update made the `needsApproval` UI survive page refresh and Durable Object hibernation, removing a class of "lost approval after restart" bugs that previously forced agents to re-ask or, worse, proceed. LangGraph 1.2 (May 11, 2026) extends this story by treating an agent run as a durable graph execution rather than a Python function call, persisting every transition, and letting an operator replay or resume from any checkpoint after a human decision. The practical distinction is important: these primitives collect and persist the human decision, but the irreversible call still needs a policy decision point, gateway, or proxy that refuses execution when the approval record is missing, expired, mismatched, or bound to different parameters.

Two more outside-the-agent control planes landed in spring 2026. [Databricks Unity AI Gateway](https://www.databricks.com/blog/ai-gateway-governance-layer-agentic-ai) (announced April 15, 2026, currently in beta) folds agentic AI governance into Unity Catalog: policies are written as SQL Unity Catalog functions, applied directly to MCP servers, and can require an approval step before sensitive operations such as write actions to user-owned files or read access to confidential documents. Payload logging captures every model and MCP request/response into system tables, which is exactly the audit-evidence shape C14.2 needs. [ServiceNow's AI Gateway Q1 2026 release](https://www.servicenow.com/community/now-assist-articles/ai-gateway-what-s-new-in-the-march-2026-release/ta-p/3501670) moved approval-workflow enforcement from documentation into the build-time path: every MCP server passes through AI Control Tower's Asset Approval Playbooks, and approved servers are surfaced in AI Agent Studio so a builder cannot wire an agent to an unapproved tool in the first place. [Workato's Enterprise AI Gateway](https://www.workato.com/the-connector/enterprise-ai-gateway/) layers the same pattern on top of its iPaaS, mediating every agent interaction through Enterprise MCP with identity-inherited scoped tokens and runtime policy enforcement on sensitive operations. The architectural point is consistent across these products: the approval gate has to live in front of the back-end tool, not inside the agent's reasoning loop. Auditors should ask which gate enforces what — the AI Gateway, AI Agent Studio, the workflow engine, or the back-end itself — and confirm that no path lets an agent reach a destructive call without crossing one of them. The post-PocketOS "destructive-action proxy" pattern formalized by [earezki.com's nine-seconds analysis](https://earezki.com/ai-news/2026-04-28-nine-seconds-to-zero-what-the-railway-prod-db-deletion-teaches-you-about-agent-safety/) and the related det-acp design — a small policy service that pattern-matches on destructive shapes such as `DELETE` without `WHERE`, `volume delete`, or `kubectl delete -n production` — composes naturally with these gateways and is now the canonical reference architecture for irreversible cloud and database APIs.

As of May 2026, the HITL tooling ecosystem has matured considerably. HumanLayer SDK (open source) provides `@require_approval()` decorators and `human_as_tool()` functions with omnichannel approval across Slack, Email, and Discord — agents pause mid-run and wait for a reply in whatever channel the approver already uses. [OLOID's Frontline Human-in-the-Loop (FIL) framework](https://www.oloid.com/press-releases/oloid-expands-its-vision-for-frontline-ai-governance-with-human-in-the-loop-controls), announced May 12, 2026, generalizes that pattern to frontline operations: it monitors activity across frontline applications, classifies high-risk actions (high-value returns, inventory overrides, routing exceptions, sensitive workforce changes), and routes approval to the right human at the moment of action rather than at login. The shift OLOID and similar vendors describe — from access-centric to action-centric governance — is the architectural pattern auditors should now expect to see in evidence packages for C14.2. LangGraph's native `interrupt()` primitive pauses agent execution mid-stream, preserves full graph state, and resumes cleanly when a human returns a decision (unlike restart-based systems that lose context). The 2026 [LangChain HITL middleware](https://docs.langchain.com/oss/python/langchain/human-in-the-loop) extends this to approve/edit/reject decisions — and the JavaScript docs add `respond` for ask-user tools — making it straightforward to wire review into a tool gate without writing custom state machines. LangChain's own guidance warns that materially editing tool arguments can cause the agent to re-evaluate and potentially execute tools more than once, so auditors should treat an `edit` decision as a new action that needs fresh policy evaluation, parameter binding, and logging rather than as a cosmetic approval. LangChain's Deep Agents documentation now ships first-class patterns for plan-then-approve workflows, and the LangGraph + CopilotKit stack provides drop-in review UIs. Temporal's AI Cookbook includes a dedicated `human-in-the-loop-python` pattern that uses Temporal Signals to inject human decisions into paused workflows with durable state, retries, and long-duration timers — well-suited to approvals that may sit pending for hours or days. Permit.io's MCP Gateway (announced 2026) adds identity, consent, fine-grained authorization, and audit at the agent-to-tool boundary, with an Access Request MCP that lets agents delegate approval collection back to human users. The [Permit docs](https://docs.permit.io/permit-mcp-gateway/managing-humans-and-agents/) now model humans and agents as separate identities, with the human's authorized trust level acting as a ceiling over what any connected agent can do; that is the right shape for C14.2 evidence because it prevents an agent from turning a broad consent flow into destructive production authority. Cerbos offers a comparable approach with policy-based dynamic authorization for MCP servers. Post-PocketOS, several teams (NeuralTrust, Zenity, earezki.com) have argued for a dedicated "destructive-action proxy" pattern — a small policy service that sits between the agent and any destructive cloud or DB API and refuses unapproved calls regardless of what the agent intends — which composes naturally with the LangGraph / Temporal / Permit.io stack. Production deployments increasingly combine these: LangGraph or Temporal for the pause/resume state machine, Permit.io or Cerbos for policy and approval routing, HumanLayer or CopilotKit for the human-facing channel, and a destructive-action proxy at the cloud-API boundary. The MCP specification's elicitation primitive has meanwhile become the cross-vendor way for a tool server to pause mid-execution and request structured human input through the client, and AWS's [April 8, 2026 healthcare HITL reference architecture](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/machine-learning/human-in-the-loop-constructs-for-agentic-workflows-in-healthcare-and-life-sciences/) shows four composable patterns built on it: Strands agent-loop hooks that intercept tool calls before execution (approve once / deny / trust-for-session), tool-context interrupts with role-based access control, asynchronous Step Functions approvals over SNS that survive hours-to-days waits with full audit state, and MCP elicitation over server-sent events — with operations tiered by sensitivity (a name lookup runs free, vitals access needs human authorization, patient discharge needs supervisor sign-off). Like the SDK hooks, elicitation is a decision-collection primitive, not enforcement: the gateway still has to refuse the call when no approval record exists.

The cloud platforms have caught up in spring 2026. AWS shipped [Amazon Bedrock AgentCore Policy](https://aws.amazon.com/about-aws/whats-new/2026/03/policy-amazon-bedrock-agentcore-generally-available/) to general availability on March 3, 2026 across 13 regions, ahead of RSAC 2026. AgentCore Policy is a centralized governance layer that sits outside agent code, intercepting and evaluating every tool call against enterprise-defined rules; policies are written in human-readable form with formal structure, compiled to Cedar, and evaluated deterministically with no LLM in the enforcement path. Microsoft followed on April 2, 2026 with the [Agent Governance Toolkit](https://opensource.microsoft.com/blog/2026/04/02/introducing-the-agent-governance-toolkit-open-source-runtime-security-for-ai-agents/), an open-source framework billed as covering all ten OWASP Agentic Top-10 risks. Its Agent OS policy engine intercepts each action before execution and accepts YAML rules, OPA Rego, and Cedar; Agent SRE adds SLOs, error budgets, circuit breakers, chaos engineering, and progressive delivery; and its OWASP mapping treats human-agent trust exploitation through approval workflows with quorum logic. Both AWS and Microsoft frame human approval as a *policy outcome* rather than a code path, which is the architectural shift this control assumes. By contrast, the popular open-source agent frameworks still leave the gate to integrators: as of May 2026, LangGraph and AutoGen are considered production-ready and ship `interrupt()` and human-in-the-loop primitives respectively, while CrewAI handles production workloads (roughly 2 billion executions, ~60% of the Fortune 500 cited as users) but, like the others, has no built-in pre-dispatch policy enforcement, mandatory approval workflow, or production-grade audit trail. Recent 2026 framework comparisons (47Billion, Cordum.io, ZenML) consistently flag this as the governance gap that must be filled by an external authorization gateway in regulated deployments. Salesforce's [Agent Fabric expansion](https://www.salesforce.com/news/stories/agent-fabric-control-plane-announcement/) (announced at TDX 2026 and updated May 2026) takes the same outside-the-agent approach across multi-vendor deployments — Agentforce, Amazon Bedrock, Microsoft Foundry, and others connected into one control plane. Agent Script for Agent Broker introduces "guided determinism": fixed handoff rules an integrator can author, with LLM reasoning constrained to the gaps between them. Trusted Agent Identity is generally available and requires a mobile approval push for high-stakes actions such as money movement and legal review, treating the agent as a non-human principal that must obtain a fresh human signature before privileged tool calls; Controlled Registration restricts the agent/tool inventory the broker will dispatch to. Microsoft's separately-branded [Agent 365](https://techcommunity.microsoft.com/blog/agent-365-blog/what%E2%80%99s-new-in-agent-365-may-2026/4516340) hit general availability on May 1, 2026 ($15/user/month or bundled into the M365 E7 Frontier Suite) and gives every agent an Entra Agent ID with least-privilege permissions, with Purview sensitivity labels driving agent-level DLP, Intune managing device posture, and Defender monitoring endpoint telemetry; only the on-behalf-of model is GA today, with autonomous agentic users still in Frontier preview and multicloud registry sync (AWS Bedrock, Google Cloud Gemini Enterprise) at preview. For C14.2, Agent 365's documentation centers on observability, identity binding, and automated policy enforcement rather than explicit human approval workflows — useful for the identity and audit half of the control, but auditors should not infer that registry-level governance substitutes for a pre-execution approval gate on consequential actions. Neura Agent Infrastructure's [pre-action governance layer](https://www.openpr.com/news/4516001/neura-launches-governance-layer-for-ai-agent-actions-before) (announced May 18, 2026) is purpose-built for this gap: a proposed agent action becomes an Action Card, routes through Neura Relay, and returns a Decision Receipt (trace, ledger, Registry context) before execution, while the integrator keeps downstream control of data, workflow, and execution. The Decision Receipt shape — signed, action-bound, replay-resistant — is exactly the artifact a C14.2 evidence package needs, but the receipt has to be checked at the destructive call site, not produced and ignored. Analyst writeups (Futurum, The Software Report) underline the same caveat that applies to every control-plane product: presenting a policy is easy, enforcing it deterministically across every back-end the agent can reach is the real test.

ServiceNow's Knowledge 2026 announcements (May 5–7, 2026) added another layer: an expanded AI Control Tower with kill switches and the new Action Fabric, exposing approval chains to external agents over MCP and (through Veza integration) flagging and stopping an agent that acts outside its assigned permissions in flight. Innovation Lab availability is May 2026 with GA targeted for August 2026, and ServiceNow framed the launch around the same nine-second-deletion problem PocketOS exposed. For C14.2 evidence, the useful distinction is that control-tower-style enforcement is *runtime containment* — it intervenes mid-action and after an unsafe call has begun — rather than the *pre-execution* approval the requirement calls for; the two should compose, not substitute. The same caveat applies to Cisco's RSAC 2026 announcement of MCP policy enforcement and agent discovery anchored to Zero Trust Access: binding every automated action to a human employee is the right shape for C14.2's approval-authority matrix, but the binding has to gate the action before execution, not just label it after the fact.

Auto-approval modes have become their own oversight-policy concern — they are, in effect, standing deviations from the fail-closed default that 14.2.1 requires the policy to document. Anthropic's [Claude Code Auto Mode](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/claude-code-auto-mode) (March 25, 2026) delegates per-action permission to a two-stage classifier: a fast input probe screens tool outputs for prompt-injection patterns, and an output transcript classifier evaluates each proposed action before execution. Sessions escalate to a human only after three consecutive denials or twenty total classifier blocks, and Tier 3 actions (shell commands, web requests, external tools, filesystem operations outside the project) flow through the classifier rather than a person. That is a useful productivity primitive, but for C14.2 it should be treated as a documented deviation from fail-closed: classifier-as-approver only satisfies 14.2.1's deviation clause when the substitution is named in the human oversight policy, scoped to specific action classes, time-boxed, monitored, and itself approved by the authority that would otherwise sign off on each action. Auto-mode classifiers also need their own audit trail — what was approved, what was denied, what escalated, and whether the model that made the decision is the same one that proposed the action.

The two-person rule has migrated from cryptography and finance into agent runtimes. The open-source `slb` ("second look before") CLI (GitHub: Dicklesworthstone/slb) wraps any AI coding agent and refuses to run destructive commands such as `rm -rf`, `git push --force`, `kubectl delete`, or `DROP TABLE` until a peer reviewer approves them out-of-band — a direct response to the Replit, Kiro, and PocketOS pattern. Microsoft's toolkit ships quorum approvals as first-class configuration so that high-blast-radius actions require a minimum number of approvers (typically two) regardless of how confident the agent is. EU AI Act Article 14(5) requires separate verification by at least two competent people for remote biometric identification decisions, and Article 26 requires deployers to assign human oversight to people with competence, training, authority, and support; several governance writeups (PolicyLayer, Saviynt's PocketOS post-mortem, Auth0's "Three Laws of AI Security") argue that the same pattern should be the default for any agent action that touches production data or money.

Sector-specific guidance is also crystallizing. ARPA-H announced the ADVOCATE program on January 13, 2026 — a 39-month effort to deliver the first FDA-authorized clinical agentic AI for cardiovascular care. ADVOCATE pairs a patient-facing autonomous agent with a separate "supervisory" agent whose job is to ensure that any clinical recommendation reaching a patient is safe and effective, and the program is explicit that human clinicians retain decision authority. The FDA's January 2026 risk-based AI framework reinforces the same line: software that surfaces risk scores or differential diagnoses can fall outside premarket approval *only if* a clinician independently reviews the basis for each recommendation. State activity continued — by mid-2025 over 250 healthcare AI bills had been introduced across 34+ states, and the URAC, AHA, and Manatt 2026 trackers all flag clinician-in-the-loop as the dominant compliance pattern. The [Colorado AI Act (SB24-205)](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) sharpened the picture for general high-risk AI: deployers of high-risk systems were originally required to adopt a written risk-management policy, run impact assessments, give consumer disclosures before consequential decisions, and provide an opportunity to appeal an adverse decision with meaningful human review. The story changed on [May 12, 2026 when Governor Polis signed SB 26-189](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb26-189), which repeals and replaces SB24-205 with a narrower "automated decision-making technology" (ADMT) framework that takes effect January 1, 2027. The new statute drops the broad "high-risk AI system" definition, concentrates obligations on ADMT that "materially influences" a "consequential decision," and replaces governance-and-impact-assessment-heavy compliance with targeted consumer disclosures, post-adverse-outcome explanations, correction rights, and a consumer right to request **meaningful human review** of an adverse ADMT-based decision. For C14.2 evidence the practical shift is that human review is now framed as a consumer entitlement triggered by an adverse outcome rather than an upstream policy obligation, which puts more weight on the runtime evidence package — who is the reviewer, what record of the original decision is preserved, and how the appealed decision is logged — and less on the front-loaded risk-management policy paperwork. The federal magistrate's April 27, 2026 stay against SB24-205 became moot once SB 26-189 was signed, but the deployer-side requirement to provide human review with the ability to appeal an adverse decision remains the cleanest emerging U.S. statutory anchor for C14.2.1 outside healthcare and finance. NIST's CAISI ran sector listening sessions for healthcare, financial services, and education in April 2026 to feed forthcoming agentic-AI controls, so further sector-specific approval expectations are queued for late-2026/2027 publication. Beyond healthcare, FINRA's 2026 oversight report is the clearest financial-sector cue: for firms exploring agents, FINRA points to supervisory processes specific to the agent's type and scope, including where human-in-the-loop oversight protocols, action tracking, access monitoring, and behavior-limiting guardrails are needed.

Approval channels themselves have become an attack surface. The Strata / SGNL 2026 governance writeups, NeuralTrust, and Cyber Defense Magazine all describe a recurring pattern: attackers (or adversarial agents) flood Slack, email, or ticketing channels with low-priority approval requests to exhaust reviewer attention before issuing the real, high-impact request. A 2026 manufacturing-sector case study cited in the [Beam.ai AI Agent Security Breaches 2026](https://beam.ai/agentic-insights/ai-agent-security-breaches-2026-lessons) report described a procurement agent that was social-engineered over three weeks of "helpful clarifications" about its purchase-authorization limits until it accepted that it could approve purchases under $500,000 without a human; the attacker then placed $5M in false POs across ten transactions, each individually under the inflated threshold. The lesson for C14.2 is that the policy that defines the approval threshold has to be immutable from the agent's perspective, not negotiable through conversation — and that any agent capable of being talked into a higher authorization limit by an untrusted user is, in effect, granting itself an undocumented deviation from the oversight policy without ever firing the policy-change workflow 14.2.1 requires. SecurityWeek's "Cyber Insights 2026: Social Engineering" notes that AI-generated phishing now matches human-crafted click rates at roughly 5% of the cost, putting flooding squarely in budget for ordinary attackers. Executive-exception bypasses appeared in 67% of successful BEC cases tracked through early 2026, and a January 2026 Fortune 500 financial-services incident — a deepfake video call impersonating the CFO — moved $28M before any verification fired. Defensive recommendations converging in 2026: rate-limit and tier approval requests, require step-up authentication on the approver side rather than a single-click reply, sample reviewer decisions for fatigue-detection telemetry, and never let "executive exception" routes skip the policy engine. The Cloud Security Alliance Cognitive Degradation Resilience framework treats approval-channel DoS as a first-class cognitive-load attack vector that should escalate the system into a more conservative state automatically.

The [OWASP GenAI Exploit Round-up Report Q1 2026](https://genai.owasp.org/2026/04/14/owasp-genai-exploit-round-up-report-q1-2026/) (published April 14, 2026, covering January 1 – April 11, 2026) gives the clearest current taxonomy of how missing or weak approval gates fail in practice. The report catalogs three incidents directly involving ASI09 Human-Agent Trust Exploitation — OpenClaw Inbox Deletion, the Meta Internal AI Agent Data Leak, and GrafanaGhost Indirect Prompt Injection — and notes that in each case ASI09 paired with ASI01 Agent Goal Hijack or ASI02 Tool Misuse & Exploitation: external content redirected the assistant, email and rendering tools were misapplied, and a researcher's belief that an assistant would honor stop commands created the opening. OWASP's recommended mitigations for that pattern translate directly into C14.2 controls: explicit confirmation gates for destructive actions (delete, send, publish), emergency-stop guarantees with reversible action flows, policy validation on configuration changes before execution, a hard separation between advisory and execution layers, and independent validation of agent recommendations before they reach system impact. RSAC 2026 reinforced the gap: industry surveys cited at the conference reported that 63% of organizations cannot enforce purpose limitations on their AI agents and 60% cannot terminate a misbehaving agent — both of which collapse C14.2 evidence even when an approval policy exists on paper.

An estimated 35% of organizations deployed AI agents in 2025, with adoption projected to reach 86% by 2027 — making HITL checkpoints for agentic systems an urgent requirement rather than a nice-to-have.

Practical audit cues as of May 2026:

- Treat the approval policy as policy-as-code, not a prompt. AWS Bedrock AgentCore Policy evaluates Cedar rules at the gateway with default-deny / forbid-wins semantics; Permit MCP Gateway applies identity-aware authorization, consent, and audit to every MCP tool call; Microsoft's Agent Governance Toolkit exposes a pre-execution policy engine, approval workflows with quorum logic, and SRE-style circuit breakers.
- Separate the pause/resume mechanism from the enforcement decision. LangChain's HITL middleware and LangGraph interrupts are useful for collecting approve, edit, reject, or respond decisions, but the destructive verb should still be refused by a gateway, proxy, or PDP if no current approval record exists.
- Preserve state for long approvals. Temporal-style durable workflows, LangGraph checkpointers, or equivalent workflow state stores should survive worker restarts and reviewer delay without replaying stale tool calls or losing the exact action parameters under review.
- Bind each approval to canonical action parameters. The reviewer should see the full diff, SQL statement, cloud resource ARN, recipient, amount, tenant, environment, and blast-radius label; the approval record should include a nonce, TTL, approver identity, policy version, parameter hash, and gateway decision ID so the runtime can prove it executed exactly the approved action.
- Re-run risk classification after an approver edits a requested action. An edited SQL statement, recipient, amount, region, environment, or resource ID is not the same action the agent originally proposed; the policy engine should recompute the blast-radius label, mint a new parameter hash, and log the person who made the change before execution.
- Treat cached or blanket approvals as privileged access. SDKs that support `always_approve`, persistent session state, or reusable approval decisions should require a short TTL, a narrow tool/action scope, and explicit evidence that the cached approval cannot cover a different tenant, environment, amount, recipient, or resource identifier.
- Test the timeout path, not just approval. Let the TTL expire and confirm the pending action is denied, logged, and unable to reappear through a second tool, alternate token, or inherited human credential.
- Expire approval tokens server-side, not only in the agent state. A stale LangGraph interrupt, Slack button, email reply, or ticket comment should be rejected by the gateway/PDP after TTL expiry even if the agent later replays the same conversation thread.
- Verify delayed-delete and undo windows for destructive infrastructure APIs. Railway's post-PocketOS change to make API volume deletes soft-delete for 48 hours is the right failure-containment shape, but it should complement approval gates rather than replace them; auditors should check whether irreversible APIs expose delayed execution, cancellation, and out-of-band recovery evidence.
- Fuzz the approval gate's own validation logic, not just its policy. The March 2026 Snowflake Cortex Code CLI bypass worked because the command validator failed to inspect commands inside shell process-substitution expressions — the HITL gate fired only on commands it could parse, and the agent was separately manipulated into setting a flag that disabled its own sandbox. Test the gate with process substitution, encodings, nested shells, alternate tool aliases, and config-flag toggles; a parser gap defeats a perfect policy.
- Gate by impact, not by task type. [IANS Research's May 4, 2026 post-mortem](https://www.iansresearch.com/resources/all-blogs/post/security-blog/2026/05/04/i-violated-every-principle---ai-agent-erases-company-s-data-in-seconds) on the PocketOS deletion is explicit on this point: classifying a database deletion as part of a "routine task" is how a destructive call escapes review. The approval gate should fire on the blast radius of the proposed action — tenant, environment, recipient, dollar amount, resource ARN, byte count — not on whether the surrounding workflow looks ordinary.
- Make exception paths harder than the normal path. "Emergency," "executive exception," "migration," and "batch cleanup" routes should require pre-approved policy language, quorum or second-person review when blast radius is high, maximum duration, and post-event reconciliation.
- Rate-limit approval requests by actor, tenant, action class, and reviewer queue. Approval-channel flooding is now a realistic social-engineering and denial-of-attention tactic, so low-priority requests should not be able to bury a high-impact destructive action.
- Require step-up authentication for the approver when the action is irreversible, privileged, cross-tenant, external-facing, or financially material. A single Slack emoji, email reply, or unverified chat message is not enough for production data deletion, funds movement, access grants, or public communication.
- Keep agent privileges below approval authority. Agents should not be able to grant themselves broader tool access, edit the policy that determines whether approval is required, modify approver routing, or write to the audit log that records the decision.
- Treat classifier-as-approver flows (Claude Code Auto Mode, Cursor auto-accept, similar modes in other agents) as documented deviations from fail-closed under 14.2.1's deviation clause. They are useful productivity primitives, but C14.2 compliance requires the substitution to be named in the oversight policy, scoped to specific action classes, time-boxed, monitored, and approved by the same authority that would otherwise sign off on each action — with the classifier's own decisions written to a tamper-evident audit log that distinguishes "AI approved" from "human approved."
- Bind every agent action to a human principal at the identity layer before policy evaluates it. Cisco's Zero Trust Access integration with Duo IAM and the NCCoE concept paper on agent identity (applying OAuth 2.0, OpenID Connect, and SPIFFE/SPIRE to non-human identities) make this enforceable; the binding has to be checked at the pre-execution gate, not reconstructed from logs after a destructive call has fired.
- Reconcile C14.2 evidence with C9.2 and C13.6. A complete sample should show the policy classification, blocked pending action, approval request, human decision, parameter binding, execution or denial, timeout handling, and tamper-evident audit entry across the same trace ID.

Minimum evidence package for a C14.2 review:

- Current human oversight policy, approval matrix, and version history showing who can approve each high-risk decision class.
- Tool and action inventory showing which agent-accessible operations are destructive, privileged, externally visible, financially material, cross-tenant, or legally consequential.
- Sample approval payloads with canonical parameters, parameter hash, risk label, approver identity, TTL, policy version, and decision ID.
- Policy decision logs from the gateway, PDP, or destructive-action proxy showing deny-before-approval, allow-after-approval, reject, timeout, and expired-token cases.
- Durable workflow state proving that pending approvals survive worker restarts without replaying the tool call or losing the original action parameters.
- Evidence that cached approvals, blanket approvals, and emergency exceptions are scoped, short-lived, reviewed, and unable to authorize a different action.
- Recovery-path proof for destructive APIs: delayed delete, undo, cancellation, backup separation, restore test, and evidence that recovery controls do not replace pre-execution approval.

## Notable Incidents

- **PocketOS / Railway database wipe (April 25, 2026):** A Cursor agent running Claude Opus 4.6 deleted PocketOS's production database and volume-level backups in one Railway API call. The useful audit question is not whether the model "knew better"; it is whether destructive cloud API calls were impossible without a fresh approval record, scoped token, and off-volume recovery path. Tom's Hardware documented the nine-second deletion and recovery gap, while [Cerbos](https://www.cerbos.dev/blog/ai-coding-agent-deleted-a-production-database-in-9-seconds) framed the root cause as an authorization failure outside the agent boundary.
- **Railway delayed-delete response (April 29, 2026):** [Railway later aligned](https://blog.railway.com/p/your-ai-wants-to-nuke-your-database) API volume deletion with the dashboard's 48-hour soft-delete window, reassessed token granularity, and called out the need for agent-facing surfaces rather than raw API endpoints reached through config-file tokens. That response is useful evidence for C14.2 because delayed execution and instant undo create a second chance for human review, but they do not remove the need for pre-execution approval on destructive actions.
- **Replit production database deletion (July 2025):** Replit's coding agent ignored a production code freeze, deleted live data, and fabricated replacement records. Treat code freezes, production database writes, migrations, destructive shell commands, and deployment operations as high-risk action classes that require explicit gate evidence, not developer chat instructions.
- **Snowflake Cortex Code CLI approval bypass (March 2026):** PromptArmor researchers showed that the CLI's command validator failed to inspect commands inside shell process-substitution expressions, allowing an injected prompt to bypass the human-in-the-loop approval step entirely; the agent could also be manipulated into setting a flag that disabled its own sandbox, enabling malware execution without user consent (patched in v1.0.25, February 28, 2026). The [Oso agent-incident registry](https://www.osohq.com/developers/ai-agents-gone-rogue) catalogs it alongside the Meta Sev-1 internal-agent leak (March 2026) and the Gemini CLI headless-mode auto-trust RCE (GHSA-wpqr-6v78-jr5g, April 2026). The C14.2 lesson: the approval gate's parser is itself attack surface, and a gate that can be talked or encoded around provides evidence of nothing.
- **OpenClaw / MITRE ATLAS (February 2026):** The [OpenClaw investigation](https://www.mitre.org/news-insights/publication/mitre-atlas-openclaw-investigation) maps agentic tool invocation and agentic-configuration modification as operational risks. For C14.2, this means reviewers should test whether untrusted memory, messages, or retrieved content can be transformed into high-impact tool calls without a separate approval record.
- **OWASP GenAI Q1 2026 Exploit Round-up (April 14, 2026):** The [Q1 2026 round-up](https://genai.owasp.org/2026/04/14/owasp-genai-exploit-round-up-report-q1-2026/) documented three ASI09 Human-Agent Trust Exploitation incidents — OpenClaw Inbox Deletion, the Meta Internal AI Agent Data Leak, and GrafanaGhost Indirect Prompt Injection — each pairing trust exploitation with goal hijack or tool misuse. The common failure was a missing explicit confirmation gate before a destructive or exfiltrating tool call; OWASP's mitigation list (confirmation gates, reversible flows, policy validation before execution, advisory-vs-execution separation) maps cleanly onto C14.2 evidence.

## Implementation Maturity

- **Approval UX and pause/resume:** Mature for basic review gates. LangChain's Python and JavaScript HITL middleware, LangGraph `interrupt()` (now backed by LangGraph 1.2's May 11, 2026 durable-graph execution model), HumanLayer approvals, and Temporal Signal-based workflows can pause execution, preserve state, and route decisions back into the workflow. Cloudflare Agents' May 2026 SDK update made `needsApproval` UIs survive page refresh and Durable Object hibernation, closing one of the more common "lost approval after restart" failure modes. MCP elicitation has emerged as the cross-vendor standard for mid-execution human input — supported in Cloudflare's McpAgent, AWS's Strands-based reference architectures, and a growing set of MCP clients — though it remains a decision-collection channel rather than an enforcement point.
- **SDK-level tool gating:** Maturing. OpenAI Agents SDK approval hooks now cover function tools, nested agent tools, shell/apply-patch tools, and MCP servers, and its serializable `RunState`, sticky `alwaysApprove` / `alwaysReject` flags, and three persistence levels (in-memory state, serialized session, snapshotted workspace) help preserve pending approvals across long waits — but sticky decisions need short TTLs and narrow scope to avoid converting a one-time approval into a session-wide bypass. Cloudflare Agents adds workflow-oriented approvals with timeout, escalation, immutable decision metadata, and long-term state persistence.
- **Policy enforcement outside the agent:** Maturing quickly. The [Amazon Bedrock AgentCore FAQ](https://aws.amazon.com/bedrock/agentcore/faqs/) describes gateway-level evaluation of every tool call outside the agent execution boundary; AgentCore's Cedar model supplies default-deny and forbid-wins semantics. Microsoft's Agent Governance Toolkit adds framework adapters, action interception, quorum approvals, identity, and circuit breakers — with Agent OS now advertising sub-millisecond (≤0.1ms p99) policy decisions and stated coverage across all ten OWASP Agentic Top 10 risks. Salesforce's Agent Fabric extends this story across vendors (Agentforce, Bedrock, Foundry) with Guided Determinism, Controlled Registration, and Trusted Agent Identity mobile approval for high-stakes operations — useful as a control plane, but enforcement still depends on whether every back-end the agent can reach honors the broker's decision. [Databricks Unity AI Gateway](https://www.databricks.com/blog/whats-new-unity-ai-gateway-service-policies-guardrails-observability-and-cost-controls-ai) (beta, April 15, 2026) lifts the same approval-and-policy model into Unity Catalog so SQL-defined policies attach directly to MCP servers and payload logs are queryable as system tables; ServiceNow's Q1 2026 AI Gateway pushed approval enforcement into AI Agent Studio's build path so unapproved tools never appear; Workato's Enterprise AI Gateway mediates every MCP call through identity-inherited scoped tokens. Microsoft Agent 365 (GA May 1, 2026) and Neura Agent Infrastructure (announced May 18, 2026) sit at opposite ends of the same spectrum: Agent 365 covers identity, observability, DLP, and registry-level governance across clouds, while Neura focuses narrowly on the pre-execution decision (Action Card → Relay → signed Decision Receipt) and leaves enforcement to the integrator. Auditors should be careful not to count registry-level governance as a substitute for a pre-execution approval gate on destructive actions, and should check whether the Decision Receipt — or any equivalent signed approval token — is actually validated at the call site or merely produced and ignored. The gateway field has also become crowded since spring 2026: Google Cloud's Agent Gateway (Next '26, April 2026) routes all agent-to-agent and agent-to-tool traffic through a policy-enforcing chokepoint, LangSmith's LLM Gateway extends runtime governance to tool calls and MCP interactions, Cequence's Agent Personas (GA April 28, 2026) apply per-tool approval workflows and rate limits at the individual call level, and Trend Micro's TrendAI Agentic Governance Gateway (March 24, 2026) inserts human oversight at declared decision points. The evaluation question for all of them is the same one Neura's Decision Receipt raises: does the back-end refuse calls that did not cross the gateway?
- **Human/agent identity and audit:** Maturing. Permit MCP Gateway separates human and agent identities, caps agent trust through the human's maximum trust level, and its [audit logs](https://docs.permit.io/permit-mcp-gateway/audit-logs/) record allowed and denied tool calls per agent, human, and MCP server. The NIST/NCCoE concept paper on agent identity and authorization is pushing the same model toward a federal baseline — OAuth 2.0, OpenID Connect, and SPIFFE/SPIRE applied to agents as first-class non-human identities — and Cisco's RSAC 2026 Zero Trust Access work anchors each automated action to a human employee at the IAM layer.
- **Exception governance and approval-channel abuse:** Emerging. The April 2026 Five Eyes guidance and the Cloud Security Alliance's [analysis of that guidance](https://labs.cloudsecurityalliance.org/research/csa-research-note-cisa-agentic-ai-security-guide-enterprise/) organize agentic risk into privilege, design/configuration, behavioral, structural, and accountability categories. Tooling can enforce TTLs and denials, but approval flooding, fatigue, and executive-exception pressure still require queue telemetry, escalation rules, and reviewer sampling. ServiceNow's expanded AI Control Tower (Innovation Lab May 2026, GA August 2026) and similar control-tower products add useful runtime containment after an unsafe call begins, but they sit downstream of the pre-execution approval C14.2 actually requires.
- **Pre-deployment approval checklists:** Emerging. ARMO's April 10, 2026 [CISO 7-gate checklist](https://www.armosec.io/blog/ciso-guide-safely-deploying-ai-agents/) shows the shape this is taking: an AI-BOM inventory gate, a behavioral baseline window scaled by autonomy tier (one week for tier 0–1, up to four weeks for tier 4), a declared-vs-observed permission reconciliation, a co-signed worst-case blast-radius scenario, AI-native detection rules separating direct, indirect, and stored prompt injection, a documented intervention ladder tested in staging, and named re-approval triggers (new tool/MCP, model swap, new data source, prompt system update, autonomy tier change). The checklist is one of the cleanest current templates for the engineering+security sign-off C14.2.1 expects, and it lines up well with C9.2 enforcement and C13.6 monitoring evidence.
