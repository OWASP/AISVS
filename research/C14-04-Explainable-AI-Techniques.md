# C14.4: Explainable-AI Techniques

> [Back to C14 Index](C14-Human-Oversight.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-03-29

## Purpose

AI systems making consequential decisions must be able to explain why. Opaque decision-making prevents users and operators from identifying errors or biases, blocks regulatory compliance in domains requiring explanation (ECOA, GDPR Article 22, EU AI Act Annex 22), and undermines trust.

The UnitedHealth/Humana nH Predict algorithm (2025) — where "the model said so" was the sole justification for denials with a 90% appeal error rate — exemplifies the harm of unexplained AI decisions. As of 2026, only 20% of enterprises deploying AI report meaningful revenue growth; most organizations bolt on explainability after model training rather than building it into the lifecycle, producing shallow explanations regulators can challenge.

Enterprise-grade explainability requires five capabilities most platforms lack: training data attribution, influence scoring, complete audit trails with timestamps, contestability (allowing human review and correction), and model certification before production deployment. For LLMs, explanation fidelity is an especially acute challenge — chain-of-thought explanations are susceptible to confabulating plausible but incorrect reasoning.

Production deployments as of 2026 typically use SHAP for offline audit and LIME for real-time explanations, since SHAP computation scales poorly (hours for large models) while LIME produces faster but less stable explanations (Salih et al. 2025).

## Requirements

|---|-------------|:-----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| **14.4.1** | **Verify that** AI systems provide basic explanations for their decisions in human-readable format. | 1 | Opaque decision-making that prevents users or operators from understanding why an action was taken; inability to identify errors or biases; regulatory non-compliance in domains requiring explanation (ECOA, GDPR Article 22). The UnitedHealth/Humana nH Predict algorithm (2025) — where "the model said so" was the sole justification for denials with a 90% appeal error rate — exemplifies the harm of unexplained AI decisions. | Confirm that every decision-producing endpoint returns an explanation alongside its output. Evaluate explanation readability with non-technical stakeholders. Verify explanations are generated for all decision paths, not just common ones. For agentic AI, verify that the agent's chain-of-thought or reasoning trace is exposed to the human reviewer, not just the final output. | "Human-readable" is subjective and audience-dependent. A data scientist and an end user need different explanation formats. For LLMs, this may mean citing source documents or highlighting reasoning steps (chain-of-thought). For classification models, it may mean showing top contributing features. EU AI Act Article 14 requires that persons assigned to human oversight can "correctly interpret the high-risk AI system's output" — this mandates explanations calibrated to the overseer's expertise level. Regulatory frameworks like Annex 22 now require model explainability using techniques such as SHAP or LIME to justify decisions. A practical challenge: enterprise-grade explainability requires five capabilities most platforms lack as of 2026 — training data attribution (tracing outputs to specific data points), influence scoring (ranking data contributions by impact), complete audit trails with timestamps, contestability (allowing human review and correction), and model certification before production deployment. Organizations that bolt on explainability after training rather than building it into the AI lifecycle produce shallow explanations regulators can challenge. |
| **14.4.2** | **Verify that** explanation quality is validated through human evaluation studies and metrics. | 2 | Explanations that are technically present but meaningless, misleading, or incomprehensible to the intended audience; "explanation washing" where systems produce plausible-sounding but inaccurate rationales. LLM chain-of-thought explanations are particularly susceptible to confabulating plausible but incorrect reasoning (a form of explanation washing at scale). | Review human evaluation study design and results. Check that studies include representative users from the target audience. Verify that metrics such as explanation fidelity, comprehensibility, and actionability are measured. Confirm evaluations are repeated after model updates. For LLM-based systems, validate that chain-of-thought explanations are faithful to the actual decision process, not post-hoc rationalizations. | Metrics for explanation quality are an active research area. Common approaches include: simulatability (can humans predict model behavior from the explanation), fidelity (does the explanation accurately reflect the model's reasoning), and sufficiency (does the explanation contain enough information for the user's task). The International AI Safety Report 2026 notes that AI agents can fabricate information — this applies to explanations as well as outputs. Explanation fidelity testing should include adversarial cases where the model is incentivized to produce misleading explanations. |
| **14.4.3** | **Verify that** feature importance scores or attribution methods (SHAP, LIME, etc.) are available for critical decisions. | 3 | Inability to diagnose model errors or biases in critical decisions; lack of technical transparency for auditors and regulators who need to understand model behavior at a feature level. | Verify that SHAP, LIME, Integrated Gradients, or equivalent attribution methods are integrated. Test that attributions are generated for sample critical decisions. Check that attribution outputs are stored and retrievable for audit. Validate that attributions are consistent across similar inputs (stability). | SHAP and LIME have known limitations: SHAP is computationally expensive for large models; LIME explanations can be unstable across similar inputs. For deep learning, Integrated Gradients or attention-based attributions may be more appropriate. For LLMs, attribution is an open research problem — token-level attention maps and probing methods are emerging but not production-ready. Captum (PyTorch) continues active development with support for Integrated Gradients, DeepLIFT, and GradCAM. InterpretML provides a unified API across methods. ConformaSight (2025) combines conformal prediction with model-agnostic explainability for global explanations. Rated L3 because tooling for production-grade attribution is still maturing, though the regulatory push (EU AI Act Annex 22 requiring SHAP/LIME-based justification) may accelerate adoption. A 2025 perspective paper (Salih et al., Advanced Intelligent Systems) provides a thorough comparison of SHAP vs LIME production characteristics: SHAP considers all feature combinations for attribution but computation scales poorly (hours for production-size models), while LIME fits local surrogate models quickly but produces unstable explanations across minor parameter adjustments. This tradeoff means most production deployments as of 2026 use SHAP for offline audit and LIME for real-time explanations — neither alone satisfies enterprise requirements. |
| **14.4.4** | **Verify that** counterfactual explanations show how inputs could be modified to change outcomes, if applicable to the use case and domain. | 3 | Users who receive adverse decisions (loan denial, insurance rejection) have no actionable recourse because they cannot understand what would need to change to get a different outcome. Directly relevant to the UnitedHealth/Humana case (2025) where denied patients had no way to understand what would change the algorithm's decision. | Verify that counterfactual generation is implemented for relevant decision types. Test that counterfactuals are actionable (suggest changes to mutable features only, not immutable characteristics). Confirm counterfactuals are plausible (within realistic data distributions). | Counterfactual explanation libraries include DiCE (Microsoft), Alibi, and Carla. Key challenge: counterfactuals must only suggest changes to features the user can actually modify (e.g., "increase income" is actionable; "change age" is not). Most applicable to tabular classification tasks; harder to apply to unstructured data or generative models. For LLM-based decisions, counterfactual explanations might take the form of "if the prompt had included X, the output would have been Y" — tooling for this is nascent. |
