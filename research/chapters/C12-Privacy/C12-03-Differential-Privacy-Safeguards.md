# C12.3: Differential-Privacy Safeguards

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-05-01 (7th pass)

## Purpose

Track and enforce privacy budgets to provide formal guarantees against individual data leakage. The Fundamental Law of Information Recovery (Dwork & Roth, 2014) shows that too many adaptive queries destroy privacy — without explicit budget tracking, the cumulative epsilon can grow without limit, degrading privacy guarantees to meaninglessness. The tooling ecosystem continues to consolidate: on October 2, 2025, OpenDP formally absorbed Tumult Analytics and Tumult Core, with former Tumult Labs staff joining the OpenDP community and publishing the first post-merger release from the new home — a meaningful development for practitioners evaluating which DP stack to standardize on, because the U.S. Census Bureau, Wikimedia Foundation, and IRS deployments that previously tracked Tumult are now anchored inside OpenDP governance. DP Wizard v0.8 (February 10, 2026) landed shortly after, with Dataverse-driven usability improvements and a fresh FAQ embedded inside the application — a signal that the DP authoring tools are being shaped by data curators rather than DP researchers alone. On March 30, 2026, Harvard's John A. Paulson School of Engineering and Applied Sciences highlighted the Differential Privacy Deployments Registry as a flagship Salil Vadhan initiative, signaling growing institutional support for cross-organization deployment transparency — a credibility boost worth citing when defending epsilon choices in a regulated environment. As of 2025-2026, the DP tooling ecosystem has matured significantly: Google's VaultGemma (September 2025) demonstrated production-grade DP-SGD for LLMs at 1B parameters with epsilon <= 2.0, proving formal DP guarantees are achievable at LLM scale. DynamoEnhance extends DP-SGD to >7B-parameter models via multi-GPU DeepSpeed integration. Google's user-level DP fine-tuning research (ELS/ULS) shows orders-of-magnitude noise reduction versus prior approaches. PrivCode (NDSS 2026) is the first DP synthesizer for code datasets. The threat surface has continued to widen. PETAL (USENIX Security 2025) demonstrated that label-only access (no logits) suffices for membership inference: per-token semantic similarity approximates output probabilities well enough (Pearson r = 0.765) to achieve AUC 0.64 on Pythia-6.9B, and common defenses like paraphrasing and deduplication provide negligible protection. Tong et al. (USENIX Security 2026, arXiv 2510.05699, latest revision March 8, 2026) extended this further by showing that the *tokenizer itself* is an attack vector: because tokenizers are trained on data representative of pre-training corpora and can be retrained from scratch, five distinct MIA methods can infer dataset membership at the tokenizer layer alone — bypassing the mislabeled-sample, distribution-shift, and model-size confounders that have undermined model-level MIA studies. Their adaptive defense is the first dedicated tokenizer privacy mechanism, and audit programs that only test the trained model will miss this leakage class entirely. Khalil et al. (November 2025) showed that MIA vulnerability extends beyond overfitting — outlier samples (noisy, hard-to-classify records near class boundaries) leak membership even in well-generalizing models, which means DP must protect per-sample privacy regardless of aggregate model accuracy. Implementation-side risks remain underappreciated: Ratliff et al. (CCS 2024, arXiv 2409.05623) introduced a formal framework for differential privacy against timing attacks, defining a "timing privacy" notion that captures programs which remain DP to an adversary observing both runtime and output, and showing that timing-stable component programs followed by a random delay can be chained into a timing-private DP system. They demonstrate end-to-end timing-attack success rates up to 99.65% against unprotected discrete Laplace and 92.56% against floating-point Gaussian implementations, and provide a natural extension to the OpenDP Programming Framework — meaning DP-SGD pipelines now have a concrete, auditable mitigation path against side-channel epsilon erosion. A separate privacy-hallucination tradeoff has been quantified: DP fine-tuning at ε = 8 reduces factual text generation by 17-24% compared to non-private models, and DP pre-training causes a 43% factuality drop — a critical consideration for high-stakes domains like healthcare. A June 2025 systematic review published in npj Digital Medicine (Nature, January 2026 issue) covering medical DP deployments found that DP-SGD can maintain clinically acceptable performance at moderate budgets (ε ≈ 10), particularly for imaging tasks using compact CNN architectures (ResNet-9/18) with group normalization to avoid batch-statistics leakage; strict privacy (ε ≈ 1) typically causes substantial accuracy loss, with degradation amplified on smaller or heterogeneous datasets. The same review reinforced that subgroup fairness reporting must be standard at multiple privacy budgets, because aggregate accuracy can mask DP-induced disparities. On the efficiency front, FlashDP (NeurIPS 2025) introduced a cache-friendly per-layer DP-SGD that reaches 90% of non-private throughput on Llama-13B pre-training on four A100s, while TTLoRA-DP (January 2026) applies tensor train decomposition to shrink the trainable parameter space by 7.6x versus standard LoRA with lower membership leakage even without DP noise. For inference-time privacy, Yang & Zhu (March 2026) formalized token-level and message-level differential privacy for AI agents accessing enterprise databases, deriving privacy bounds as a function of generation temperature and message length — a complementary approach when training-time DP is unavailable. DP-Fusion (ICLR 2026, latest revision February 2026) offers a complementary token-level DPI mechanism that provably bounds the influence sensitive context tokens can have on LLM outputs, achieving lower perplexity at lower epsilon than prior inference-time methods by blending baseline and sensitive-token output distributions. NIST finalized SP 800-226 on March 6, 2025, providing the "DP pyramid" evaluation framework — privacy parameters (ε, δ) at the top, trust models and security at the middle, data collection practices at the base — requiring evaluation of every layer for meaningful guarantees. The OpenDP Differential Privacy Deployments Registry (launched November 2025) now catalogs 21 real-world DP deployments as structured "deployment cards," helping practitioners benchmark epsilon choices against comparable use cases — accompanying research accepted at IEEE S&P 2026 shows practitioners use the registry to compare deployments by dataset size and domain. The communication side of DP is also moving toward standardization: Dibia et al. ("We Need a Standard," PoPETs 2026, arXiv 2507.15997) synthesized 12 expert interviews into an essential parameter list for an expert-informed DP "privacy label," addressing the persistent problem that real-world deployments often fail to disclose ε, δ, the trust model, the unit of privacy, or the composition assumptions — a gap that audit teams should now treat as a finding in itself. Full enforcement of the EU AI Act begins August 2026, recognizing privacy-enhancing technologies including DP as a compliance mechanism for high-risk AI systems; the European Commission has confirmed that further AI Act guidance tools will publish through Q2 2026, and Finland became the first member state with fully operational AI Act enforcement powers in January 2026, with other national competent authorities activating through the first half of the year, so organizations with EU exposure should expect inconsistent supervisory posture during the ramp-up rather than a uniform August go-live. Domestically, the California Privacy Protection Agency's mandatory annual cybersecurity audit took effect on January 1, 2026 under Article 9 of the CCPA regulations, covering 18 control areas with results reported by an independent auditor and senior-executive certification — DP deployments handling California consumer data now sit inside that audit perimeter, so DP budget logs and accountant outputs should be retained as audit evidence. Importantly, Google Research found that the common practice of shuffling training data into fixed-size mini-batches can yield weaker privacy protection than DP-SGD theory assumes (which relies on Poisson subsampling); their scalable Map-Reduce Poisson subsampling implementation closes this gap and delivers better model performance for the same privacy guarantee. The Balls-and-Bins Sampling work (Chua et al., AISTATS 2025, arXiv 2412.16802) goes further: it provides a sampler whose implementation looks like shuffling but whose privacy amplification matches or beats Poisson subsampling, giving practitioners a "best-of-both" option when Poisson subsampling is operationally inconvenient. On the auditing side, ContextLeak (December 2025) introduced the first black-box auditing framework for private in-context learning (ICL), estimating empirical epsilon lower bounds for ICL methods — revealing that many existing private ICL techniques strike poor privacy-utility tradeoffs, either leaking sensitive information or severely degrading output quality. PrivLLM-Guard (Scientific Reports, 2026) demonstrated an adaptive DP framework for clinical LLMs combining context-aware Gaussian noise calibration (dynamically adjusted based on input sensitivity and real-time privacy risk scores) with hierarchical budget allocation across medical data categories — a domain-specific approach that could inform health-sector AISVS implementations.

| **12.3.2** | **Verify that** black-box privacy audits empirically estimate ε̂ as a lower bound at a stated confidence level (e.g., Clopper-Pearson or f-DP confidence intervals), and that the estimate is consistent with the declared budget within documented tolerance. | 2 | Discrepancy between claimed and actual privacy guarantees. Implementation bugs in DP mechanisms (e.g., floating-point vulnerabilities in Gaussian noise, incorrect sensitivity calibration) can silently void privacy guarantees. Mironov (2012) showed that floating-point arithmetic can break DP, and Ratliff et al. (CCS 2024, arXiv 2409.05623) extended the implementation-attack class with timing side channels — the time taken to draw discrete Laplace or Gaussian noise leaks the noise magnitude, enabling end-to-end attacks at 99.65% (discrete Laplace, private sum) and 92.56% (floating-point Gaussian, DP-SGD) — so a numerically declared ε can be invalidated entirely by side-channel observation, and audits must include timing-leakage checks alongside output-distribution tests. DP reversal via LLM feedback has emerged as a practical attack: adversaries feed target records into an LLM and analyze completion confidence to perform membership inference. PETAL (USENIX Security 2025) takes this further — a label-only MIA that achieves AUC 0.64 on Pythia-6.9B using per-token semantic similarity to approximate output probabilities, matching logit-based attacks without needing probability access. Tong et al. (USENIX Security 2026, arXiv 2510.05699) opens a new attack surface entirely: tokenizers themselves leak training-set membership, and five distinct tokenizer-level MIA methods succeed against state-of-the-art LLMs across millions of Internet samples — meaning audits that only stress-test the trained model can certify a "DP" pipeline that still leaks at the tokenizer layer. Khalil et al. (November 2025) demonstrated that MIA vulnerability extends beyond overfitting: outlier samples — noisy or hard-to-classify records sitting on class boundaries — leak membership even in well-generalizing models, meaning audits must specifically test per-sample vulnerability on atypical records, not just aggregate metrics. Additionally, DPO-aligned models are theoretically more vulnerable to MIA than PPO-aligned models (Amazon Science, 2024), meaning alignment method choice affects privacy exposure. A June 2025 systematic review of medical DP studies (npj Digital Medicine, 2026) further found that DP-SGD can widen subgroup performance gaps even at moderate ε ≈ 10 — privacy audits must therefore measure both empirical epsilon and per-subgroup utility, not just aggregate accuracy, otherwise a "compliant" DP deployment may quietly amplify fairness harms. | Run black-box auditing tools — Google DP-Auditorium (open-sourced inside google/differential-privacy at python/dp_auditorium, with property testers and dataset finders for Rényi DP, pure DP, and approximate DP, plus divergence optimization that consistently outperforms prior testers), Meta PrivacyGuard, DP-Sniper, and the privacy-auditing-via-MIA approach of Jagielski et al. (2020). Generate empirical epsilon estimates by measuring distinguishability of outputs on neighboring datasets. Compare estimated epsilon-hat against declared epsilon. Ensure abs(epsilon-hat - epsilon) is below a documented tolerance, with confidence intervals reported via Clopper-Pearson or f-DP. Use canary-based memorization testing (ICLR 2025; new-token canary design at arXiv 2503.06808 achieves empirical epsilon ≈ 1 at analytical epsilon = 4 for black-box LLMs) and ContextLeak (December 2025) for in-context-learning audits. Add timing-leakage tests using the timing-privacy framework (Ratliff et al., CCS 2024, OpenDP extension) — measure noise-sampling runtime distributions on neighboring inputs and reject mechanisms whose runtime correlates with sensitive values; deploy timing-stable noise samplers plus a random delay if drift is detected. For tokenizer-layer leakage (Tong et al., USENIX Security 2026), include the five tokenizer-MIA methods in the audit harness and require their adaptive defense before signing off on any production tokenizer trained on sensitive data. Include per-sample audits of outlier records following Khalil et al., and report per-subgroup empirical epsilon and utility (npj Digital Medicine, 2026) so DP fairness regressions are visible. | DP-Auditorium, PrivacyGuard, and DP-Sniper require careful threat-model selection and do not produce a single "pass/fail" verdict — practitioners need statistical literacy to interpret confidence intervals correctly. Per-sample MIA audits on outlier records are not yet automated in mainstream frameworks; auditors typically have to construct their own canary set or borrow one from related deployments. Tokenizer-MIA tooling beyond the Tong et al. reference implementation is essentially nonexistent today, so audit teams will have to build harnesses themselves. The CCPA cybersecurity audit (effective January 1, 2026) does not yet prescribe DP-specific tests but treats privacy-preserving controls as part of the 18 control areas — independent auditors may request empirical epsilon evidence as part of certification. Apple's deployed epsilons (ε = 2/day for most data, ε = 4 for exploratory collections, with composition over time still contested) remain a useful baseline when defending audit ranges, but auditors should ask whether composition has been re-audited since the original disclosures. |
| **12.3.3** | **Verify that** formal privacy proofs cover all post-training fine-tunes and embedding generation steps that consume the same source data. | 3 | Privacy budget exhaustion through fine-tuning. Each fine-tuning round consumes additional privacy budget. If fine-tuning and embedding extraction are not accounted for, the total epsilon can far exceed what was budgeted during initial training. A Carnegie Mellon study (2025) showed that approximate unlearning methods merely suppress model outputs rather than robustly forgetting target knowledge — relearning on public articles can restore the supposedly-unlearned information, meaning unlearning cannot substitute for proper DP budgeting. The privacy-hallucination tradeoff is now quantified: DP fine-tuning at ε = 8 reduces factual text generation by 17-24%, and DP pre-training causes a 43% factuality drop — formal proofs must account for this utility cost when budgeting across the pipeline. Tokenizer training is itself a budget-consuming stage that almost no current pipeline accounts for: Tong et al. (USENIX Security 2026) showed five tokenizer MIA methods succeed on production-scale Internet samples, so any formal proof that omits tokenizer construction from the composition stack is incomplete. | Request formal privacy analysis documentation covering the full pipeline: tokenizer training, pre-training, fine-tuning (including RLHF, DPO), embedding extraction, and any downstream adaptation. Verify that composition theorems (sequential, parallel, advanced) are correctly applied across all stages. For federated fine-tuning, check DP-FedLoRA implementations that combine DP-SGD with LoRA in federated settings. Verify that user-level DP guarantees use Example-Level Sampling (ELS) or User-Level Sampling (ULS) where applicable — Google's 2025-2026 research demonstrates orders-of-magnitude noise reduction versus naive conversions from example-level to user-level DP. Pay special attention to alignment method choice: DPO-aligned models are theoretically more vulnerable to MIA than PPO-aligned models, so formal proofs should account for the privacy implications of the alignment technique used. Require disclosure in the Dibia et al. PoPETs 2026 privacy-label format — ε, δ, unit of privacy, trust model, noise mechanism, and composition assumption — so each pipeline stage's contribution to the cumulative budget is explicit and machine-checkable. | Most DP tooling focuses on training-time guarantees. Fine-tuning with DP (DP-LoRA, DP-FedLoRA, DP-adapter methods) is maturing — TTLoRA-DP (January 2026) achieves 7.6x parameter reduction over standard LoRA with improved privacy-utility tradeoffs on GPT-2, and a 2025 ACM workshop paper evaluated DP-enhanced parameter-efficient fine-tuning for LLMs and found it effective but with utility trade-offs scaling with model size. The privacy-hallucination tradeoff (quantified September 2025) means that formal proofs must balance epsilon budgets against factuality requirements — in healthcare and legal domains, a 17-24% factuality drop may be unacceptable, forcing practitioners toward less aggressive DP budgets or inference-time DP approaches (Private Evolution, DP prediction) that avoid training-time noise entirely. Yang & Zhu (March 2026, arXiv 2603.17902) formalized token-level and message-level DP for AI agents accessing enterprise databases, deriving privacy bounds tied to generation temperature and message length — this extends the scope of formal proofs to cover agentic inference pipelines, not just training. DP-Fusion (Thareja et al., ICLR 2026, arXiv 2507.04531) provides a token-level DPI mechanism that provably bounds the influence sensitive context tokens can have on LLM outputs by labeling sensitive tokens, computing a baseline distribution without them, and blending the sensitive-tokens distribution to remain within a bounded distance — formal proofs for inference-time pipelines should now cite either Yang & Zhu or DP-Fusion (or both) when sensitive tokens flow through context windows. Embedding extraction is rarely covered by formal analysis. Tokenizer training is essentially never covered today, despite the Tong et al. results — a recognized gap that AISVS-conformant pipelines should explicitly address by including tokenizer-stage epsilon in the composition or by using DP-trained tokenizers once tooling matures. Post-deployment inference queries also consume budget under some threat models but are typically excluded from analysis. NIST SP 800-226 emphasizes evaluating the full composition stack but does not prescribe how to handle post-training budget accounting specifically. |

## Tooling

- **Differential privacy:** Opacus (PyTorch), TensorFlow Privacy (TF), **JAX-Privacy 1.0** (February 2026, Google — production-ready DP training for JAX with Keras API integration; powers VaultGemma; supports CNNs, State Space models, and Transformers with far lower overhead than TF Privacy; scalable Poisson subsampling and correlated noise generation in distributed environments), Google DP Library (C++/Java/Go with Python bindings), OpenDP/SmartNoise (Rust core with Python API, Harvard/Microsoft), PipelineDP4j (JVM — Google's newest release for parallelizable DP computations), Diffprivlib (IBM), DynamoEnhance (multi-GPU DP training for >7B-parameter LLMs built on DeepSpeed, supports LoRA + BitsAndBytes + flash attention). FlashDP (NeurIPS 2025, open-sourced at github.com/kaustpradalab/flashdp) provides cache-friendly per-layer DP-SGD achieving 90% non-private throughput on Llama-13B with 50% less memory movement than GhostClip. TTLoRA-DP (January 2026) applies tensor train decomposition to LoRA for 7.6x parameter reduction with improved privacy-utility tradeoffs. Google's VaultGemma (September 2025) demonstrated production-grade DP-SGD for LLMs at 1B parameters with epsilon <= 2.0 — weights available on Hugging Face/Kaggle. Google's user-level DP fine-tuning research (ELS/ULS) shows orders-of-magnitude noise reduction versus prior approaches. PrivCode (NDSS 2026) is the first DP synthesizer specifically for code datasets, using a two-stage DP-SGD + utility-boosting pipeline. AWS fast-differential-privacy library provides mixed ghost clipping with 1-10% memory overhead and <2x slowdown versus non-private training.
- **Budget tracking & dashboards:** Tumult Analytics (PureDPBudget/RhoZCDPBudget session tracking with `remaining_privacy_budget` API and hard-stop enforcement — as of October 2, 2025 now maintained under the OpenDP umbrella at github.com/opendp/tumult-analytics following the Tumult Labs → OpenDP consolidation), AWS Clean Rooms Differential Privacy (managed "remaining queries" gauge with epsilon budget policy and CloudWatch integration), Google dp-accounting library (PLD/RDP/GDP accountants with `DpEvent` composition — Connect-the-Dots PLD is 5x faster than PRV Accountant), OpenDP/SmartNoise SQL (odometer-based cumulative epsilon tracking across query workloads), **DP Wizard v0.8** (OpenDP, February 10, 2026 — Docker- or pip-installable authoring UI for non-experts making DP releases through the OpenDP Library, with Dataverse-driven usability fixes and an embedded FAQ; useful for teams that need analyst-facing budget workflows without building a custom dashboard)
- **Subsampling correctness:** Balls-and-Bins Sampling for DP-SGD (Chua et al., AISTATS 2025, arXiv 2412.16802) — best-of-both sampler with shuffle-like implementation and Poisson-equivalent privacy amplification, recommended when Poisson subsampling is hard to integrate into existing data loaders; Google Research's scalable Map-Reduce Poisson subsampler closes the shuffle-vs-Poisson gap for distributed pipelines
- **Inference-only DP approaches:** Private Evolution (PE) — generates DP synthetic data by querying inference APIs without fine-tuning, compatible with foundation models accessible only through APIs; DP prediction — releases only model outputs with DP guarantees, avoiding training-time noise entirely. **DP-Fusion** (ICLR 2026, arXiv 2507.04531, code at github.com/MBZUAI-Trustworthy-ML/DP-Fusion-DPI) provides token-level differentially private inference that provably bounds sensitive-token influence on outputs — useful when sensitive context cannot be removed but its influence must be capped. These approaches sidestep the privacy-hallucination tradeoff inherent in DP-SGD training.
- **Privacy auditing:** **Google DP-Auditorium** (open-source at github.com/google/differential-privacy/tree/main/python/dp_auditorium — black-box DP auditing with property testers and dataset finders, divergence optimization over function spaces for Rényi DP, pure DP, and approximate DP; consistently outperforms prior black-box testers and is the most general-purpose DP tester now in production), DP-Sniper (black-box DP auditing), **PrivacyGuard** (Meta/Facebook Research, October 2025, github.com/facebookresearch/PrivacyGuard — comprehensive modular PyTorch framework for empirical DP analysis supporting MIA, extraction, and reconstruction attacks against both supervised models and LLMs), membership inference attack toolkits (ML-Doctor, privacy meter), canary-based memorization testing (ICLR 2025 methodology for LLM-specific auditing; extended by new-token canary design achieving empirical ε≈1 for analytical ε=4 in black-box LLM settings — arXiv 2503.06808), PETAL label-only MIA (USENIX Security 2025 — per-token semantic similarity, no logit access needed), **Tokenizer MIA harness** (Tong et al., USENIX Security 2026, arXiv 2510.05699 — five MIA methods that target the tokenizer rather than the trained model, plus an adaptive defense; the first practical audit class for tokenizer-stage leakage), **ContextLeak** (December 2025 — first black-box auditing framework for private in-context learning, estimates empirical epsilon lower bounds for ICL methods), ensemble privacy defense (arXiv 2512.03100, December 2025)
- **Side-channel hardening:** Ratliff et al. timing-privacy framework (CCS 2024, arXiv 2409.05623) — formal "timing privacy" notion plus a timing-stable + random-delay construction realized as an extension of the OpenDP Programming Framework; counters the 92.56% (DP-SGD floating-point Gaussian) and 99.65% (private sum, discrete Laplace) timing-leak attacks documented in the same line of research
- **Inference-time agent DP:** Yang & Zhu (March 2026, arXiv 2603.17902) — token-level and message-level DP framework for AI agents accessing enterprise databases, with privacy bounds tied to generation temperature and message length
- **Domain-specific DP:** PrivLLM-Guard (Scientific Reports, 2026) — adaptive DP framework for clinical LLMs combining context-aware Gaussian noise calibration with hierarchical budget allocation across medical data categories (patient identifiers, diagnoses, treatments); demonstrates domain-tuned DP for healthcare AI deployments. The npj Digital Medicine 2026 review of medical DP (arXiv 2506.00660) gives concrete deployment guidance: ResNet-9/18 with group normalization at ε ≈ 10 for imaging, mandatory subgroup fairness reporting across multiple budgets, and treating the privacy budget as a depletable resource with authenticated access, per-user query limits, logging, and audit trails after deployment.
