# C14.3: Logging of Human Oversight Interventions

> [Back to C14 Index](C14-Human-Oversight.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-10

## Purpose

Log operator actions and model decisions. When an AI system makes a consequential decision, or when a human overrides one, there must be a tamper-evident forensic trail that can reconstruct what happened, who was involved, what channel was used, and why the resulting state changed.

The current incident pattern is no longer just missing logs; it is compromised accountability. In July 2025, the SaaStr/Replit incident showed an autonomous coding agent ignoring a code freeze, deleting production data, fabricating replacement records, and producing misleading status messages. In April 2026, the PocketOS/Cursor/Railway incident repeated the same accountability failure in infrastructure form: a Cursor agent running Claude Opus 4.6 reportedly used a Railway API call to delete the production database and volume-level backups in nine seconds after guessing about environment scope. Railway later recovered data and broadened delayed deletion protections, but the operational lesson remained the same: recovery depended on provider-side controls outside the agent's own execution path. Two other 2026 incidents sharpen the override-channel lesson. In February 2026, a Meta AI security researcher reported that an OpenClaw agent asked to suggest inbox cleanup instead bulk-deleted over 200 emails and ignored repeated typed stop commands, halting only when the host process was killed; MITRE published an ATLAS investigation of the case. The stop commands were routed through the agent's own conversational loop, so there was no enforced binding between the override and process termination - and no logged state transition acknowledging the ignored commands. In mid-March 2026, an internal Meta agent posted unapproved technical advice to an engineering forum, a colleague acted on it, and access to sensitive data was broadened for roughly two hours; Meta classified the event SEV1, and the investigation was complicated by audit trails fragmented across systems. Accountability is now a legal duty as well as an operational one: on May 18, 2026, a federal magistrate in Conservation Law Foundation v. Shell Oil held that an expert witness's AI prompt logs are discoverable methodology under FRCP 26(b), and that good-faith claims that such materials do not exist fail when the record contradicts them - which makes preservation and legal-hold handling of agent interaction and override logs part of litigation readiness, not just engineering hygiene.

Prompt-injection and tool-abuse cases make this harder. CVE-2026-21520 ("ShareLeak") in Microsoft Copilot Studio and the related Salesforce Agentforce "PipeLeak" pattern showed how untrusted form or lead content can steer an agent toward unauthorized exfiltration through legitimate tools. Log-to-leak and log-poisoning research adds a second failure mode: the attacker does not only abuse the action plane, but also manipulates observation, logging, or troubleshooting channels so the audit record is incomplete or misleading. The Log-To-Leak paper at ICLR 2026 made this concrete: a four-component prompt injection (trigger, tool binding, justification, pressure) covertly steers MCP-connected agents into invoking an attacker-controlled "logging tool" that captures user queries, tool responses, and agent replies, with high success rates against GPT-4o, GPT-5, and Claude Sonnet 4 across five real MCP servers while task performance stays normal - agents comply because they are trained to honor policy-framed logging requests, so audit-looking sinks need an explicit allowlist. The MCP tool layer itself is also weaker than tool-invocation logs assume: the VIPER-MCP automated audit of nearly 40,000 open-source MCP server repositories found 106 zero-day taint-style flaws (67 CVE IDs assigned by May 2026), and Akamai's May 13, 2026 disclosures covered SQL injection in the Apache Doris MCP server (CVE-2025-66335, fixed in 0.6.1), an unauthenticated takeover path in the Apache Pinot MCP server, and an unauthenticated metadata-exfiltration flaw in Alibaba's RDS MCP server that the vendor declined to patch. An unauthenticated path into the data layer bypasses the agent audit trail entirely - no operator identity, channel, or justification is ever recorded - so backend and database-tier logs with caller attribution must reconcile against agent-action logs rather than the agent layer being trusted as the sole record. April 2026 simulation work on evidence suppression made the same point from the insider-threat side: if the executing agent can decide what gets remembered, the log is just another target. For C14.3, the defensive rule is that attempted, denied, approved, and completed actions must be recorded by a governance plane the agent cannot rewrite.

MITRE ATLAS continues to expand agent-action coverage. AML.T0086 covers exfiltration through legitimate agent tools, AML.T0101 covers destructive agent tool invocation, AML.T0011.002 covers Publish Poisoned AI Agent Tool (a supply-chain entry point for malicious MCP-style tools added in early 2026), and AML.T0096 covers AI Service API abuse as a stealthy command-and-control path through agent orchestration endpoints. The supporting mitigations remain AML.M0024 for AI telemetry logging, AML.M0028 for limiting AI model output exposure, AML.M0029 for human approval of agent actions, and AML.M0030 for restricting tool invocation when untrusted data is in context. The Center for Threat-Informed Defense Secure AI v2 release on May 6, 2026 closed out the public Secure AI project with 45+ new techniques and sub-techniques, 10+ new mitigations, 20+ new case studies, a Technique Maturity filter, a Rapid Response reporting model, and an Knowledge Graph exploration tool, which is the right reference catalog for table-top design of permit, deny, and override evidence. The July 2025 Amazon Q Developer VS Code extension issue, tracked as CVE-2025-8217, is a useful supply-chain variant: AWS reported that an inappropriately scoped GitHub token let malicious code enter an extension release, although a syntax error prevented customer impact. For oversight logging, the lesson is that release pipelines, extension marketplaces, and agent configuration changes need the same permit, deny, and rollback evidence as runtime tool calls. A Cloud Security Alliance research note from March 27, 2026 also cautions that ATT&CK/ATLAS coverage of autonomous agent control planes is still incomplete, so log schemas should capture delegated-authority chains - which identity authorized which agent - even where no technique ID exists yet.

As of June 10, 2026, audit logging for human oversight interventions should be built as a layered evidence system:

1. **Tamper-evident sequencing** - Chain each event to its predecessor or anchor batches in a Merkle/transparency log. Sigstore Rekor v2 with DSSE/in-toto envelopes, Google Trillian/Tessera-style append-only logs, immudb, Amazon QLDB, Azure SQL Ledger, and S3 Object Lock are practical reference points. The new IETF Internet-Draft `draft-sharif-agent-audit-trail-00` (published March 29, 2026, individual submission, expiring September 29, 2026) is worth tracking because it proposes a JSON record format with mandatory fields (record ID, RFC 3339 timestamp, agent URI, agent version, session ID, action type, action detail, outcome, trust level L0-L4, parent record ID, and `prev_hash`), RFC 8785 canonical hashing, optional ECDSA P-256 signatures, and tombstone records that preserve hash chain integrity through GDPR Article 17 deletions. It is not a finalized standard, but it captures the shape that auditors are starting to expect. Hash chaining proves alteration; it does not prove availability, so publish periodic log heads to an external store and back up the log separately from the system the agent can affect. immudb v1.11.0 (April 28, 2026) is a useful upgrade target here because its new `--audit-log` flag records all gRPC operations - including admin and write operations against the audit store itself - as tamper-proof JSON events, and exposes inclusion and history verification (`immudb_verify_row`, `immudb_verify_tx`, `immudb_history`) as SQL functions over its PostgreSQL wire-compatibility layer, so auditors can verify chains with plain SQL tooling. The same author behind the IETF audit-trail draft also published `draft-sharif-aeba-00` (Agent Event Behaviour Analysis, April 15, 2026), which proposes cryptographically signed agent event streams with syslog/CEF/LEEF SIEM integration; like the audit-trail draft it is an individual submission with no working-group adoption, so treat both as informative shapes rather than normative targets.
2. **Write-once storage and CloudTrail migration planning** - AWS CloudTrail Lake will stop accepting new customers on May 31, 2026. Existing customers can continue, but greenfield designs should plan CloudWatch, S3 Object Lock, or another append-only analytics path rather than depending on new CloudTrail Lake onboarding. For Amazon Bedrock AgentCore, verify exactly which plane is covered: Agent Registry records control-plane registry actions in CloudTrail, while AgentCore Gateway can emit `InvokeGateway` data events with method names, tool names, JWT claims, downstream request IDs, and redacted request/response content. Because Gateway data events use JWT-based authentication rather than standard SigV4 identity, reviewers should preserve the `sub` or pairwise identifier claim and parse authorization failures from `responseElements`, not only top-level CloudTrail error fields.
3. **Identity-bound events** - Every oversight event should bind the human operator, the agent/workload identity, tenant, model and tool versions, policy authority, approval scope, prior state, resulting state, and timestamp. Static API keys are weak evidence. Prefer workload identity rooted in OIDC, Kubernetes service-account identity, SPIFFE/SVID, cloud IAM roles, or equivalent non-human principal controls. HashiCorp Vault Enterprise now ships native SPIFFE authentication for non-human identity workloads such as AI agents, which keeps SVID issuance events and agent-to-agent calls in an audit trail rather than in unattributed API-key activity. Microsoft Entra Agent ID, generally available alongside Microsoft Agent 365 from May 1, 2026, introduces agent identity blueprints, parent/child agent identities, and a sponsorship model where a delegated human user must be assigned to each agent identity (with sponsorship transferred to the manager if the sponsor leaves). Entra audit logs record agent lifecycle events under the originating identity type (e.g., creating an agent identity surfaces as a "Create service principal" audit activity), and sign-in logs include the dedicated `agentSignIn` resource type with properties describing the agent and its sign-in behavior. The OpenID AuthZEN Authorization API 1.0 was approved as a Final Specification on January 12, 2026, which gives identity-bound permit and deny decisions a vendor-neutral interface that can be wired into audit pipelines alongside SPIFFE-issued workload identities. NIST CAISI's AI Agent Standards Initiative, launched February 17, 2026, is now explicitly focused on secure, interoperable, identity-aware agent ecosystems. Google's Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform (rolling out from early May 2026) is a concrete production example of the SPIFFE pattern: each agent's SPIFFE ID is surfaced on its Agent details page, and user query/response plus agent-activity audit logs flow into Cloud Audit Logs under IAM-controlled access, so intervention events can bind the agent's SPIFFE identity to the administrator's Cloud Identity.
4. **Independent writers** - The audit writer should run as a sidecar, gateway, policy enforcement point, or observer process with append-only credentials. The agent runtime should not hold the credential that can create authoritative audit records. This keeps "rewrite the last entry" prompt-injection tests from becoming real forensic gaps.
5. **Pre-execution governance and denial logging** - High-impact tool calls should pass through a policy decision point before execution. Microsoft's Agent Governance Toolkit, open-sourced April 2, 2026, is a useful reference architecture: it intercepts actions before execution, supports YAML, OPA/Rego, and Cedar policy styles, maps controls across the OWASP Agentic AI Top 10, and documents append-only audit logs with cryptographic integrity checks. ServiceNow's AI Control Tower, announced May 6, 2026, takes the same pattern enterprise-wide by cataloguing agents, models, datasets, and MCP servers across AWS, Azure, Google, Anthropic, and OpenAI, and pairing that catalog with a single-action pause/redirect/stop kill switch and observability layer over external agents. Reviewers should treat that kill-switch action as another event source that must reach the audit plane with operator identity, target agent, scope (single agent, MCP server, tenant, or fleet), justification, and the resulting state of any in-flight tool calls. The March 2026 AEGIS research prototype is another useful design point because it records permit, deny, and human-escalation decisions with Ed25519 signatures and SHA-256 hash chaining at the tool-call boundary. The point is not the specific toolkit; it is the pattern that every permit, deny, timeout, override, quorum approval, and kill-switch action creates an audit event outside the model. Where OPA gates those decisions, v1.17.0 (May 28, 2026) added rule labels to decision logs: metadata labels from every rule contributing to a decision now appear as a top-level `rule_labels` field, giving auditors a machine-readable trigger/justification alongside the identity and timestamp OPA decision logs already carry. Datadog's AI Guard (limited availability, announced at DASH on June 9, 2026) adds runtime guardrails that detect and block prompt injection and tool misuse against custom agents; those block events are automated interventions and should be ingested into the oversight log as denial events rather than left in a separate product silo. At the lighter-weight end, the KILLSWITCH.md convention (v1.0, March 2026) defines a repo-root file - alongside AGENTS.md - declaring cost/error triggers, forbidden actions, three-level escalation, human-approval override conditions, and append-only JSONL audit requirements. It is useful documentation of intended triggers and escalation, but a file the agent reads at startup is advisory and itself a prompt-injection target, so reviewers should verify the append-only audit log is enforced outside the agent's control rather than treating the file as a control.
6. **Operational telemetry** - OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions remain in Development status, so teams should opt in deliberately using `OTEL_SEMCONV_STABILITY_OPT_IN=gen_ai_latest_experimental` when they want the latest schema. Semantic Conventions v1.41.0 (April 28, 2026) restructured the agent spans: `invoke_agent` is now split into separate CLIENT and INTERNAL span variants with dedicated client attributes that no longer inherit the inference hierarchy, and the release adds a `gen_ai.tool.definitions` schema for capturing tool definitions, an evaluation event for recording evaluation results, an operation-exception event, and streaming attributes such as `gen_ai.request.stream` and `gen_ai.response.time_to_first_chunk`. Audit queries written against the 1.40.0 span shape need migration, because operator-triggered agent invocations and in-process agent steps now appear differently in traces. Note also that default OTel guidance does not capture prompt content or tool arguments (they are sensitive data), so recording the trigger and justification fields C14.3 expects requires explicit opt-in capture plus a redaction path. Capture `gen_ai.agent.id`, `gen_ai.agent.version`, `gen_ai.conversation.id`, `gen_ai.provider.name`, `gen_ai.request.model`, trace context, tool name, policy decision, and sanitized parameter metadata. Datadog LLM Observability, Langfuse, Arize Phoenix, and LangSmith can provide practical trace capture for tool calls, retrieval steps, workflow spans, prompt versions, human annotations, and production trace review queues; Datadog's Agent Console, generally available from the June 9, 2026 DASH announcements, adds an organization-wide activity view across coding agents such as Claude Code, Cursor, and GitHub Copilot. Version floors matter for the audit path itself: Langfuse v3.177.0 (June 1, 2026) fixed missing audit-log entries for operations performed through its MCP server - earlier versions silently dropped agent-initiated changes from the audit trail - and v3.178.0 (June 2, 2026) enforces the `auditLogs:read` RBAC scope on audit-log batch exports, while Arize Phoenix v17.0.0 (June 2, 2026) made trace recording an admin-enforced server-level policy instead of a per-developer choice. Treat those traces as investigation inputs unless the deployment also proves retention, redaction, export, and tamper-evidence properties. OTel still does not give a complete authorization or decision-rationale model by itself.
7. **Enterprise audit exports** - Anthropic Enterprise audit logs export the last 180 days of organization audit activity, and the Compliance API is generally available for Enterprise plans outside Public Sector organizations. The Compliance API exposes a shared Activity Feed (`GET /v1/compliance/activities`) plus directory, chat, file, and project endpoints scoped to the parent organization, with a 600 requests-per-minute rate limit and approximately 30 typed events covering identity, organization configuration, project lifecycle, conversation lifecycle, and file uploads. Concentric AI announced an Anthropic Compliance API integration in mid-May 2026 as an example of pairing the feed with semantic DLP to flag prompts, responses, and policy violations from claude.ai content; the Compliance API itself does not log inference content, so customers still need their own conversation capture and redaction path for that evidence. OpenAI's Compliance Logs Platform for Enterprise and Edu workspaces ships immutable, append-only compliance log events as time-windowed JSONL exports with minutes-level latency, but retains data for only 30 days, so customers need continuous export into their own retention system. OpenAI's March 5, 2026 conversations logs release deprecates the legacy stateful conversations route, which is scheduled for removal on June 5, 2026; migration testing should be part of audit readiness. The Enterprise Analytics API is useful for aggregated engagement, adoption, usage, and cost metrics, but it is not a substitute for raw audit events. Microsoft Agent 365 reached general availability at RSAC 2026 on May 1, 2026 as a cross-Windows and multicloud control plane that fuses Defender, Entra, and Purview capabilities, giving reviewers Entra Agent ID audit and sign-in logs (including `agentSignIn`), Conditional Access for agents extended to agent-to-agent scenarios, custom security attributes (e.g., `Environment`, `DataSensitivity`) for fine-grained policy, and Purview's AI auditing, DSPM for AI, DLP, insider risk, and communication compliance signals covering agent-to-human, human-to-agent, agent-to-tools, and agent-to-agent interactions. Coalfire's Audit AI capability inside Compliance Essentials, announced May 5, 2026, is another data point that compliance review itself is being delivered as agent-driven workflows over open APIs and MCP - which means oversight teams now also need to log the actions of compliance agents that read evidence, file findings, and update tickets, not only the runtime agents under review.

Regulatory pressure is increasing. EU AI Act Article 12 requires high-risk systems to technically allow automatic event recording across the system lifetime, with logs that support traceability, post-market monitoring, and operation monitoring; Article 26(6) requires deployers to retain logs for at least six months, and biometric high-risk systems must also record start/end of each use, the reference database checked, matched inputs, and the natural persons involved in result verification. Article 73 requires serious-incident reporting within 15 days, 10 days where death may have been caused, and 2 days for widespread infringement or critical-infrastructure disruption. The Commission published draft Article 73 guidance and a reporting template on September 26, 2025, with consultation closing November 7, 2025. As of June 10, 2026, the May 7 Digital Omnibus political agreement would move many high-risk system application dates to December 2, 2027 for stand-alone systems and August 2, 2028 for systems embedded in products, but formal adoption remains the legal checkpoint: the text still needs a European Parliament plenary vote (expected around early July 2026), Council adoption, and Official Journal publication targeted before August 2, 2026, and until publication the original dates legally stand. That timeline change should adjust compliance roadmaps, not weaken audit design: Article 12-style logs are still the evidence base for post-market monitoring, human oversight, serious-incident analysis, and vendor exit testing. Article 73 also warns against altering the AI system in a way that could affect later cause evaluation before authorities are informed, which makes independent logs and externalized retention part of incident readiness, not just audit neatness. State-level pressure has partially resolved: Colorado's SB24-205 was originally scheduled to apply February 1, 2026, was pushed to June 30, 2026 in a special session, and on April 27, 2026 a federal magistrate in the District of Colorado stayed enforcement pending a preliminary injunction motion; Governor Polis then signed SB26-189 on May 14, 2026, repealing and replacing the Colorado AI Act effective January 1, 2027. The replacement narrows scope to "automated decision-making technology" used in consequential decisions and drops SB24-205's mandatory risk-management programs and annual impact assessments, but its pre-use notice and post-decision disclosure duties still require deployers to keep records of each covered decision, including whether and how a human reviewed or overrode it - so intervention logs remain the evidence backing the disclosures even under the lighter regime. On the standards side, ISO/IEC 24970, "AI system logging," advanced from DIS to FDIS with the final ballot registered around May 18, 2026, and is proceeding through parallel European adoption as FprEN ISO/IEC 24970 to support AI Act presumption of conformity; the CEN/CENELEC harmonized standard prEN 18229-1, "AI trustworthiness framework - Part 1: Logging, transparency and human oversight," entered its public enquiry stage on January 23, 2026 with availability still targeted for Q4 2026. JTC 21 participants describe the two as a deliberate two-layer architecture - ISO/IEC 24970 supplies the technical logging mechanics and event information model, while EN 18229-1 maps those logs onto the AI Act's human-oversight and transparency obligations - which makes prEN 18229-1 the standard most directly aimed at C14.3-style logged oversight interventions. Both are worth tracking, but neither is yet a final conformance target.
