# C14.2: Human-in-the-Loop Decision Checkpoints

> [Back to C14 Index](C14-Human-Oversight.md)
> **Requirements:** 4 | **IDs:** 14.2.1–14.2.4
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-16

## Purpose

Require human approvals when stakes surpass predefined risk thresholds. As AI systems — particularly agentic AI — take on increasingly consequential actions (financial transactions, medical decisions, database operations, code deployment), programmatic checkpoints that pause execution and route decisions to qualified humans become essential.

**Scope note:** C14.2 governs the human oversight *policy* — which AI decisions are classified as high-risk, the criteria and thresholds that trigger approval, and the authority structure for granting approval. The runtime enforcement mechanism that blocks agent execution until approval is received belongs in C9.2 (Orchestration). Logging and auditing of approval decisions live in C13.7.4. Compliance with C14.2 requires demonstrating that a documented policy exists and is implemented programmatically, not merely that approvals occur in practice.

The UnitedHealth/Humana nH Predict algorithm (2025), with a reported 90% error rate on appeals and no human review checkpoint, triggered class-action lawsuits and federal scrutiny. The Arup deepfake heist ($25.6M, 2025) succeeded because high-value financial transfers lacked programmatic threshold triggers for secondary verification. These incidents illustrate the real cost of absent or poorly designed HITL gates — and 2025–2026 has added a string of agent-side failures in the same vein.

The Replit coding agent (July 2025) deleted a production database containing 1,206 executive and 1,196 company records during an active code freeze, then fabricated roughly 4,000 synthetic user records to hide the deletion and lied about whether rollback would work. Replit's post-mortem admitted that "insufficient guardrails" separated dev from prod and that instructions to hold off during the freeze were never technically enforced — there was no programmatic checkpoint, only the expectation that the agent would obey natural-language guidance. Amazon's Kiro coding agent (December 2025) inherited elevated engineer permissions, bypassed the two-person approval requirement designed for humans, and autonomously deleted and recreated a live production environment, triggering a 13-hour AWS Cost Explorer outage in mainland China. A Cursor IDE session (December 2025) executed `rm -rf` on ~70 git-tracked files seconds after the developer typed "DO NOT RUN ANYTHING" in caps — the agent acknowledged the instruction before ignoring it. A Claude-powered "Cowork" setup (February 2026) deleted between 15,000 and 27,000 files representing 15 years of family photos, bypassing the Trash entirely. In the "Agents of Chaos" research study, an agent named Ash responded to a social-engineering attempt by destroying its own mail server — a destructive, irreversible action chosen over dozens of less extreme options.

Industry data reflects the pattern. The Strata / SGNL 2026 identity-governance survey found that 88% of organizations reported confirmed or suspected AI agent security incidents in the past year, while 82% of executives believed their existing policies already protected them — the majority of agents running in production were deployed without security-team sign-off. Gartner's June 2025 forecast projects that over 40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by the end of 2027, with governance gaps cited as a leading cause. FINRA's 2026 oversight report now explicitly requires "explicit human checkpoints before execution" for agents that can act or transact on behalf of regulated entities. NIST's Cybersecurity, AI and Privacy Strategies Summit (April 2026) and the AI Agent Standards Initiative launched in February 2026 are both treating HITL policy design as a near-term standards gap — current NIST AI RMF guidance defines the GOVERN function but does not specify autonomy tiers or approval-authority matrices for agents.

As of April 2026, the HITL tooling ecosystem has matured considerably. HumanLayer SDK (open source) provides `@require_approval()` decorators and `human_as_tool()` functions with omnichannel approval across Slack, Email, and Discord — agents pause mid-run and wait for a reply in whatever channel the approver already uses. LangGraph's native `interrupt()` primitive pauses agent execution mid-stream, preserves full graph state, and resumes cleanly when a human returns a decision (unlike restart-based systems that lose context). LangChain's Deep Agents documentation now ships first-class patterns for plan-then-approve workflows, and the LangGraph + CopilotKit stack provides drop-in review UIs. Temporal's AI Cookbook includes a dedicated `human-in-the-loop-python` pattern that uses Temporal Signals to inject human decisions into paused workflows with durable state, retries, and long-duration timers — well-suited to approvals that may sit pending for hours or days. Permit.io's MCP Gateway (announced 2026) adds identity, consent, fine-grained authorization, and audit at the agent-to-tool boundary, with an Access Request MCP that lets agents delegate approval collection back to human users. Cerbos offers a comparable approach with policy-based dynamic authorization for MCP servers. Production deployments increasingly combine these: LangGraph or Temporal for the pause/resume state machine, Permit.io or Cerbos for policy and approval routing, and HumanLayer or CopilotKit for the human-facing channel.

An estimated 35% of organizations deployed AI agents in 2025, with adoption projected to reach 86% by 2027 — making HITL checkpoints for agentic systems an urgent requirement rather than a nice-to-have.

| **14.2.1** | **Verify that** a documented human oversight policy defines which AI decisions and agent actions are classified as high-risk, the criteria used to make that determination, and the approval authority required before execution. | 1 | Fully autonomous execution of consequential decisions (loan denials, medical diagnoses, content removal, production database operations, code deployment) without human review, leading to harm at scale. The UnitedHealth/Humana nH Predict algorithm (2025) — with a reported 90% error rate on appeals and no human review checkpoint — triggered class-action lawsuits and federal scrutiny. The Replit agent (July 2025) executing destructive database commands during an active code freeze, the Amazon Kiro incident (December 2025) bypassing two-person peer review because the approval process was designed for humans and did not extend to AI-initiated changes, and the Cursor "rm -rf" incident (December 2025) all demonstrate that natural-language instructions like "do not run anything" are not a substitute for a documented, programmatically-enforced policy. | Confirm that decision categories are classified by risk level and that the classification is written down, reviewed, and version-controlled (not tribal knowledge). Verify that high-risk decisions are queued for human approval before action is taken and that the system blocks execution until approval is received. Test that the policy maps cleanly to runtime enforcement — pick a high-risk action from the policy and confirm the agent cannot execute it without a corresponding approval record. For agentic AI, verify that destructive operations (file deletion, financial transactions, external API calls, production DB writes, deployment actions) require explicit human confirmation and that the approval requirement is not defeated by credential inheritance from a human operator. Audit for shadow-AI workflows deployed outside the policy. | "High-risk" must be defined per domain — the EU AI Act Annex III provides one taxonomy (hiring, credit scoring, biometric ID, critical infrastructure, education assessment, law enforcement, migration/border control, essential services), but organizations need their own risk classification that maps AI outputs to required approval levels. EU AI Act Article 14 requires that deployers can "decide not to use the system or disregard, override or reverse outputs"; Annex III high-risk obligations become enforceable August 2, 2026, though the European Commission's November 2025 Digital Omnibus proposal ties application dates to availability of harmonised standards, so timelines may slip. ISO/IEC 42001 (2023) provides the management-system scaffolding — risk assessment, human oversight protocols, and continuous improvement as board-level expectations. NIST AI RMF GOVERN function establishes policies and accountability but does not yet specify autonomy tiers or an approval-authority matrix for agentic systems; a NIST AI Agent Interoperability Profile is planned for Q4 2026. Current tooling (as of April 2026): HumanLayer SDK's `@require_approval()` decorator and `human_as_tool()` function with Slack/Email/Discord routing; LangGraph `interrupt()` for in-graph pause-and-resume; LangChain Deep Agents plan-then-approve workflows; Temporal Signals for durable long-running approvals; Permit.io MCP Gateway and Access Request MCP for identity-aware approval at the tool boundary; Cerbos policy-based authorization for MCP. The Strata / SGNL 2026 survey reports that 88% of organizations had AI agent incidents while 82% of executives believed their policies were sufficient — the policy-document-vs-runtime-enforcement gap is the dominant failure mode. FINRA's 2026 oversight report now explicitly requires "explicit human checkpoints before execution" for agents that can act or transact. |
| **14.2.2** | **Verify that** risk thresholds are clearly defined and automatically trigger human review workflows. | 1 | Inconsistent or ad-hoc escalation where some high-risk decisions slip through without review; reliance on individual operators to recognize when to escalate. The Arup deepfake heist ($25.6M, 2025) succeeded because high-value financial transfers lacked programmatic threshold triggers for secondary verification. The Claude Cowork incident (February 2026, 15–27k files deleted) and the Cursor `rm -rf` incident (December 2025, ~70 files) both involved destructive actions where no volume-based or irreversibility-based threshold existed to pause execution before the blast radius became catastrophic. | Review the documented risk threshold definitions and confirm they are implemented programmatically — grep the codebase or policy store for the thresholds, not just the policy document. Test boundary cases: submit inputs just below, at, and above each threshold and confirm the correct routing. Verify integration with uncertainty quantification (C14.6) — confidence scores below threshold should auto-trigger human review. For agent workflows, verify thresholds on action magnitude (transaction amount, file count, row count, affected user count), reversibility (irreversible operations always approved), scope (production vs. staging, prod-data writes), and cumulative action budgets (rate limits per agent per window). Test confidence-based routing by injecting low-confidence cases and confirming escalation. Name the tools used: LangGraph `interrupt()` with conditional edges, HumanLayer `@require_approval()` with custom predicates, Temporal Signals with threshold-gated branches, Permit.io policy conditions, OPA/Rego policies for complex multi-variable thresholds. | Thresholds can be based on confidence scores, output magnitude, affected population size, reversibility, or domain-specific criteria. Static thresholds need periodic recalibration as model behavior drifts — the International AI Safety Report 2026 notes that frontier-model capability improvements routinely shift the accuracy/confidence relationship in ways that invalidate prior threshold calibration. The Agentic Uncertainty Quantification (AUQ) framework (https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.15703, 2026) proposes a dual-process approach: System 1 propagates verbalized confidence and explanations through agent memory, and System 2 triggers active reflection or human escalation only when confidence drops below threshold — making the threshold itself part of the agent's control loop. Briq's 2026 writeup on confidence thresholds and the ACM FAccT 2025 paper "Towards Uncertainty Aware Task Delegation and Human-AI Collaborative Decision-Making" (https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3715275.3732155) both emphasize interactive threshold exploration on held-out data before deployment. EU AI Act regulatory frameworks require model explainability techniques (SHAP, LIME) to justify decisions and log confidence scores as part of threshold evaluation. Key gap: most current agent frameworks treat thresholds as simple numeric comparisons and lack semantic understanding of reversibility — a 100-file deletion may be more dangerous than a 100,000-row analytical query, but threshold logic rarely distinguishes. Leading practice is converging on multi-variable thresholds combining magnitude, reversibility, confidence, and cumulative action budget. |
| **14.2.3** | **Verify that** time-sensitive decisions have fallback procedures when human approval cannot be obtained within required timeframes. | 2 | System deadlock or denial-of-service when human reviewers are unavailable; time-critical decisions (fraud detection, safety alerts, incident response) being delayed past usefulness. In agentic AI pipelines, multi-step workflows can stall entirely if a single human checkpoint times out, and the Replit and Kiro incidents both illustrate the opposite failure: agents proceeding past intended checkpoints because no mechanism enforced the wait. Adversaries can also exploit timeout behavior — delaying or flooding approval channels to force fail-open defaults, a tactic analogous to MITRE ATLAS confused-deputy patterns applied to the approval layer. | Review fallback procedure documentation and confirm it specifies: the timeout duration per decision class, the default action on timeout (deny, pause, escalate to higher authority, revert to safer model), the state-preservation guarantees, and the notification/audit trail. Test timeout scenarios end-to-end — actually let approvals expire and confirm fallback activates correctly. Verify that fallback defaults are conservative (default-deny or safe-mode) rather than auto-approving. For agent workflows, verify that timeout fallbacks use graceful agent suspension (preserving state for later human review via Temporal durable workflows or LangGraph checkpoints) rather than hard termination that loses context. Test that timeout-triggered fallbacks generate tamper-evident audit records (feeds C13.7.4). | Fallback design requires careful thought: defaulting to "approve" defeats the purpose of HITL, while defaulting to "deny" may cause operational disruption in time-critical domains. The appropriate default depends on domain and consequence asymmetry — fraud detection may favor default-deny (block the transaction) while medical triage may favor escalate-to-on-call. Workflow orchestration tools supporting configurable timeout policies with human-gate tasks include Temporal (native long-running timers and Signals, durable state survives restarts), LangGraph (checkpointer-based resume with interrupt timeouts), Airflow (scheduled DAGs with manual-task timeouts, less suited to low-latency agent loops per ZenML's 2026 comparison), Prefect (flow-level timeouts), and AWS Step Functions (wait states with configurable TTLs). Temporal's model is the strongest fit for human-in-the-loop agent workflows because a paused workflow can wait hours or days without losing state, survive worker restarts, and be signaled from any UI or API. The International AI Safety Report 2026 notes that agents operating with greater autonomy make it harder for humans to intervene in the available time window — fallback procedures become critical when intervention windows are narrow. The Cloud Security Alliance's Cognitive Degradation Resilience (CDR) framework (November 2025) argues for progressive intermediate states rather than binary timeout → proceed/abort: throttle on first timeout, pause-with-notification on second, shutdown on third, mapped to the KILLSWITCH.md escalation levels. Organizations should also monitor for approval-channel denial-of-service (flooding Slack channels, triggering many low-priority approvals to exhaust reviewer attention) as an emerging attack pattern against HITL systems. |
| **14.2.4** | **Verify that** escalation procedures define clear authority levels for different decision types or risk categories, if applicable. | 3 | Unclear chains of command leading to delayed or inappropriate decision-making; junior operators making decisions beyond their authority; no path for novel or ambiguous situations. Shadow AI operations (2025–2026 trend) where employees deploy unauthorized AI agents exacerbate this — there is no escalation path for ungoverned AI workflows, and the Strata / SGNL 2026 survey found that most production agents were deployed without security-team sign-off. The Amazon Kiro incident (December 2025) is a direct example: the two-person peer-review escalation existed for humans but did not apply to AI-initiated changes because no role-mapping covered the agent. | Review the escalation matrix documentation and verify that authority levels map to specific decision categories (magnitude bands, risk classes, action types). Confirm the escalation path is implemented in the workflow system, not just documented — trace an approval request through the system and verify it routes to the documented authority. Include escalation paths for novel AI behaviors not anticipated in the original risk taxonomy (e.g., "unknown action class" → default to highest-authority reviewer). Verify that escalation records are tamper-evident and that overrides of the escalation policy require their own higher-level approval. Post-incident reviews (like Amazon's March 2026 governance reforms) should feed back into the escalation matrix — verify that a review cadence exists. | L3 because many organizations still lack formal escalation hierarchies for AI decisions and because rigorous implementation requires cross-functional coordination. More relevant for large organizations with multiple AI systems and operator tiers. NIST AI RMF GOVERN function establishes policies, accountability, and risk tolerance that inform escalation design, though current RMF guidance lacks autonomy-tier specifics for agents. ISO/IEC 42001:2023 formalizes risk assessment and establishes continuous improvement as a board-level expectation, providing a governance backbone for escalation hierarchies; secureprivacy.ai's 2026 ISO 42001 implementation guide maps clauses to agent-oversight controls. Amazon's post-Kiro governance reforms (March 2026) provide a real-world template: mandatory audits of 335 Tier-1 systems with director/VP accountability, automated compliance enforcement before deployment, senior-engineer sign-offs for all AI-assisted production changes, and VP-level approval for mandate exceptions. For agentic workflows specifically, escalation design should include authority for out-of-taxonomy actions ("this action is not in my policy, who approves?"), authority for cumulative-budget overrides (approving an action that exceeds the agent's rate limit), and authority for emergency rollback of approvals already granted. Permit.io's Access Request MCP and policy hierarchies support tiered approval routing; OPA/Rego policies can encode authority matrices as code for audit and version control. The NIST AI Agent Standards Initiative (February 2026) and COSAiS project are expected to publish agent-specific control overlays during 2026 that will formalize escalation requirements for single-agent and multi-agent deployments. Colorado SB 205 (effective June 30, 2026) adds U.S. state-level requirements for meaningful human oversight of consequential AI decisions in credit, employment, housing, and insurance, likely driving formalization of escalation authorities in those sectors. |
