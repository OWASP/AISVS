# C12.4: Purpose-Limitation & Scope-Creep Protection

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-10 (thirteenth pass)

## Purpose

Prevent models and datasets from being used beyond their originally consented purpose. GDPR Article 5(1)(b) establishes the purpose limitation principle, and the EDPB's 2024 opinion requires controllers to articulate "sufficiently clear purposes" and ensure processing remains within "reasonable data subject expectations." Without explicit purpose tags and runtime enforcement, models trained on data collected for one purpose can be silently repurposed — for example, a customer service chatbot model reused for credit scoring. The LinkedIn enforcement action (October 2024, €310 million fine by the Irish DPC) illustrates the regulatory cost: LinkedIn's behavioral profiling for targeted advertising violated purpose limitation because consent was "not freely given, sufficiently informed or specific, or unambiguous," and neither legitimate interest nor contractual necessity provided valid cover. Clearview AI's cumulative enforcement actions — €20M from CNIL (2022), €20M from Italian Garante (2022), €30.5M from Dutch DPA (2024), and the UK Upper Tribunal's October 2025 ruling reviving the ICO's jurisdiction over Clearview's £7.5M enforcement action — demonstrate that purpose limitation principles apply regardless of where processing occurs or whom the controller serves. The UK tribunal rejected the argument that processing for foreign law-enforcement customers put Clearview outside UK data protection law, then sent the substantive appeal back for determination; that nuance matters because the jurisdictional point is strong, while final penalty posture can still move through appeal. Despite ~€100M in cumulative EU and UK enforcement exposure, Clearview has largely ignored regulators' orders, exposing a glaring enforcement gap in cross-border AI privacy regulation.

The CNIL's 2026 guidance further sharpens expectations — organizations must specify excluded functionalities by design and maintain documentation of known use cases and deployment conditions, with general-purpose AI systems explicitly prohibited from relying on vague purposes like "development and improvement of an AI system." The EU's General-Purpose AI Code of Practice (published July 10, 2025, effective August 2, 2025) adds a Transparency Chapter requiring providers to complete a comprehensive Model Documentation Form detailing technical specifications, training data characteristics, and intended purposes — with full compliance required by August 2, 2027 for models placed on the market before the effective date.

Scope creep is compounded by shadow AI: IBM's 2025 Cost of a Data Breach research found that one in five organizations reported a breach due to shadow AI, only 37% had policies to manage or detect it, and high shadow-AI exposure added about $670,000 to breach costs. Earlier enterprise visibility surveys still show broad unsanctioned use: 75% of large enterprises report shadow AI usage among employees, 78% of employees bring their own AI tools to work, and 45% do not disclose usage to their employer. The McDonald's McHire incident (2025) illustrates the intersection of purpose creep, vendor oversight, and basic security control failure — default credentials and an IDOR in the Paradox.ai hiring platform left up to 64 million applicant chat/application records accessible; the researchers reportedly accessed seven records, five with personal information, and the vendor said the issue was fixed the same day. As of April 2026, 81% of AI agents are already operational, yet only 14.4% have full security approval, and 88% of organizations report AI-agent security incidents. The EU AI Act's high-risk framework will require purpose documentation for regulated AI systems, with penalties up to €35M or 7% of global revenue; the May 2026 Digital Omnibus agreement may move high-risk application dates, but it does not relax GDPR purpose-limitation duties. Tooling for purpose-limitation enforcement remains immature — runtime guardrails (NVIDIA NeMo Guardrails, Guardrails AI) can enforce topic-level scope restrictions, but mapping these to legal purpose definitions requires custom integration. However, a new enforcement surface is emerging: AI gateways. As of mid-2026, enterprise AI gateway layers (Lasso Security, Portkey) are being positioned as centralized policy choke points — intercepting every agent tool invocation and evaluating it against enterprise policy before execution, covering agent permissions, PII/DLP controls, provenance checks, and purpose enforcement. Lasso Security's 2026 analysis notes that agentic security challenges now stem "less from novel attacks and more from unclear purpose and weak oversight," with post-deployment drift (prompt changes, workflow expansion, integration sprawl) eroding agent boundaries over time.

The EDPB's Coordinated Enforcement Framework (CEF) 2026, launched March 19, 2026, adds further urgency: 25 Data Protection Authorities across the EEA are simultaneously examining compliance with GDPR transparency obligations (Articles 12-14), with aggregated findings expected in H2 2026 and follow-up enforcement actions to follow. While this enforcement action targets general transparency rather than AI specifically, AI systems with opaque purpose boundaries are likely to draw scrutiny. Additionally, deployers of high-risk AI systems now face a dual assessment requirement: a DPIA under GDPR Article 35 plus a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA) under AI Act Article 27, with penalties stacking to €55M (€20M GDPR + €35M AI Act). The AI Act explicitly allows the FRIA to complement the DPIA (Recital 96), and practical guidance recommends combining both into a single document, but the obligation is cumulative — neither substitutes for the other.

Enforcement outcomes remain unpredictable. Italy's Garante fined OpenAI €15M in December 2024 for processing users' personal data to train ChatGPT without adequate legal basis — a purpose-limitation violation at its core — but the Court of Rome annulled the fine in March 2026, and the full judgment reasoning published on May 28, 2026 shows the annulment rests solely on one-stop-shop jurisdictional grounds: the Garante lost competence once OpenAI's Irish establishment made the Irish DPC the lead supervisory authority (February 2024), and the court never examined the substantive lawful-basis, transparency, or purpose questions. Commentators warn this creates a replicable "establish, then escape" pattern where non-EU AI providers can shift enforcement to slower lead authorities mid-proceeding. Notably, the EDPB's own Opinion 28/2024 (December 2024) on AI models and personal data explicitly declined to address purpose limitation, data protection impact assessments, and data protection by design — signaling that authoritative guidance on purpose limitation in AI contexts remains a gap at the EU level, even as enforcement proceeds at the national level. The US state-level picture reversed sharply in May 2026: Colorado repealed and replaced its AI Act (SB24-205) before it ever took effect. Governor Polis signed SB 26-189 ("Automated Decision-Making Technology") on May 14, 2026, which eliminates the impact assessment, risk-management program, and algorithmic-discrimination duty-of-care requirements and retains only disclosure obligations — pre-interaction consumer notice, post-adverse-decision disclosure with appeal and human-review rights, developer documentation, and three-year record retention — effective January 1, 2027, with Attorney General rulemaking required first and no enforcement until rulemaking completes. What had been the strongest US state-level purpose-documentation hook is now a disclosure-only statute, leaving Texas's TRAIGA as the operative state purpose-disclosure law. At the federal level, the FTC's AI Policy Statement (March 11, 2026) interprets FTC Act Section 5 as applying across the AI lifecycle with agents explicitly in scope — a deception/unfairness hook that can reach purpose misrepresentation even without AI-specific legislation.

On the observability front, Microsoft's March 2026 guidance on AI system observability argues that traditional health metrics (uptime, error rates, latency) are insufficient for detecting purpose drift in non-deterministic AI systems, recommending instead behavioral baselines (tool call frequencies, retrieval volumes, token consumption, evaluation score distributions) with alerts on meaningful departures. OpenTelemetry (OTel) and its GenAI semantic conventions are emerging as the instrumentation standard for this kind of AI-specific observability. Arthur AI's Agent Development Lifecycle (ADLC) framework codifies this into a feedback loop: observability surfaces failure modes, evaluation suites capture them as test cases, and policy updates prevent recurrence — treating observability as a governance enabler rather than an afterthought.

NIST's March 2026 publication of **AI 800-4: Challenges to the Monitoring of Deployed AI Systems** (the first federal-level report on post-deployment monitoring, drawing on three practitioner workshops with 200+ experts from academia, industry, and 10+ federal agencies plus an 87-paper literature review) directly validates the purpose-drift problem. The report organizes post-deployment monitoring into six categories and identifies five cross-cutting challenges: lack of trusted methods and tools, immature information sharing across the value chain, difficulty keeping pace with rapid change, misaligned organizational incentives, and the significant resource burden of comprehensive monitoring. For federal agencies specifically, NIST flags "monitoring blind spots and overconfidence" and notes that "many agencies lack critical capacity regarding algorithmic oversight" — a frank admission that operational monitoring maturity lags the regulatory expectations being set. The report treats monitoring as fundamentally a standards problem rather than a tooling problem, reinforcing that purpose-limitation enforcement will require industry-wide convention before it becomes systematically auditable.

The US state-level landscape is also expanding beyond Colorado. The **Texas Responsible AI Governance Act (TRAIGA, HB 149)** took effect January 1, 2026, creating an intent-based liability framework that diverges from Colorado's impact-based approach. TRAIGA prohibits AI systems "intentionally designed" to harm persons, engage in criminal activity, impair constitutional rights, unlawfully discriminate against protected classes, or produce illegal sexual content. Purpose disclosure is central: businesses must provide a high-level description of intended use, deployment context, and benefits, and governmental entities must give clear, conspicuous, plain-English disclosure (free of dark patterns) before consumer interaction with AI. The intent standard raises the bar for prosecutors but also creates an argument that organizations with documented, constrained purposes are better insulated from liability than those operating without explicit scope.

A second concern surfaced in 2026 industry reporting is the operational disconnect between purpose declaration and runtime enforcement. The Microsoft Cyber Pulse AI Security Report and parallel work from FireTail (April 2026) note that least-privilege scoping for AI agents — "ensure agents can access only the data, systems, and workflows required to fulfill their purpose, no more, no less" — is now consensus advice, but enterprises consistently grant broader scopes during onboarding than the agent's purpose actually requires, creating standing privilege that outlasts the original use case. This is the operational mechanism behind the CISO survey gap (71% AI access vs 16% effective governance): permissions get granted by capability, not by purpose, and there is no scheduled re-binding when the purpose changes.

As of May 2026, the strongest practical evidence pattern is to bind purpose at three layers at once: declared model/data purpose tags, authorization-scoped tool access, and telemetry that proves the binding held during execution. MCP authorization gives auditors a concrete protocol surface to inspect: every HTTP request should carry an audience-bound Bearer token for the MCP server, not a recycled token for another resource. AWS's MCP guidance makes the same idea operational with short-lived session policies and session tags that mark AI-driven access, allowing IAM rules to deny sensitive actions such as destructive S3 operations when the access path is an agent. OpenTelemetry's GenAI semantic conventions then provide the observation layer — agent identity/version, conversation ID, tool definitions, and `execute_tool` spans — so purpose drift can be investigated from trace evidence rather than inferred after the fact from output logs.

The joint Five Eyes guidance **Careful Adoption of Agentic AI Services** (published April 30, 2026) turns that pattern into a government-backed security baseline for agents: do not grant broad or unrestricted access, prefer low-risk and non-sensitive tasks, and treat privilege, design/configuration, behaviour, structural, and accountability risks as separate review areas. Its practical recommendations map cleanly to purpose limitation: tool allow lists and version controls prevent unapproved capability expansion, unified inter-agent audit logs preserve scope evidence, trigger-action protocols can automatically restrict permissions when unexpected behaviour emerges, and agents should not be able to modify their own privileges or create unapproved delegation chains without explicit expiry and recorded grants. NIST's AI Agent Standards Initiative, launched February 17, 2026 and updated April 20, 2026, points in the same direction by prioritizing agent authentication, identity infrastructure, authorization, interoperable protocols, and security evaluations — all prerequisites for proving that an autonomous agent stayed within its declared purpose.

On the legal-basis front, the **CNIL's June 19, 2025 recommendations** on legitimate interest as a legal basis for AI development acknowledge that purpose limitation "applies flexibly" to general-purpose AI systems — operators who cannot precisely define all future applications at training may describe the type of system and illustrate its key functionalities. Examples of qualifying legitimate interests include scientific research, facilitating public access to information, offering a conversational assistant, product improvement, and fraud detection. This flexibility narrows the gap between the EDPB's strict reading of Article 5(1)(b) and the practical reality of foundation-model training, but does not eliminate the downstream deployer obligation to bind concrete uses to specific purposes. For agentic systems, the **Model Context Protocol (MCP) authorization model** provides an emerging purpose-binding pattern: per-user OAuth identities with role-scoped permissions, and per-tool-invocation scope enforcement via the AssumeRole-with-session-policy pattern (effective permissions = intersection of role policies and session policy). AWS's April 2026 guidance on securing MCP agent access to AWS resources articulates the design principle explicitly: "design permissions based on the acceptable scope of impact, not intended functionality alone" — because any permission granted can be exercised regardless of the intended use case.

The AI gateway market is consolidating quickly. On **April 30, 2026, Palo Alto Networks announced its intent to acquire Portkey**, an AI gateway already processing trillions of tokens per month, with plans to integrate it into the Prisma AIRS platform as a unified control plane for autonomous agents. The press release explicitly frames agents as "highly privileged insiders, executing a large volume of automated decisions" — a useful framing for purpose-limitation thinking, since the privilege of an agent must be bounded by the purpose it was authorized for. The deal is expected to close in PANW's fiscal Q4 2026. The OWASP **GenAI Security Project's Top 10 for Agentic Applications (2026 edition)** also crystallizes purpose-drift as a first-class risk category — "Rogue Agents" describes agents that drift from their intended purpose without external manipulation (self-repeating actions, persisting across sessions, silently approving unsafe operations), with mitigations including locked system prompts, human approval for goal-changing actions, memory isolation per user/session/task, and periodic re-baselining of agent memory to remove drift or contamination. The OWASP guide pairs with the **AI Security Solutions Landscape for Agentic AI (Q2 2026)**, which maps the full agent lifecycle and surfaces purpose-binding requirements at the DevOps/SecOps intersection.

The regulatory timeline changed again in early May 2026. The **EU Digital Omnibus trilogue initially failed on April 28, 2026**, but Parliament and Council reached a provisional agreement on May 7, 2026. As of June 10, 2026 the deal is moving through formal adoption: Member State ambassadors (Coreper) endorsed the text on May 13, 2026, the Parliament's IMCO and LIBE committees approved it on June 2, 2026, and a plenary vote is expected in the June 2026 session with Council formal adoption to follow before the August 2 deadline. The agreed dates move high-risk obligations to **December 2, 2027** for Annex III use cases such as biometrics, critical infrastructure, education, employment, law enforcement, migration, asylum, and border control, and to **August 2, 2028** for AI systems used as safety components in regulated products such as lifts, toys, machinery, and medical devices; national AI sandboxes shift to August 2, 2027. That reduces near-term AI Act timing pressure, but it does not relax GDPR purpose-limitation, DPIA, lawful-basis, or transparency duties. Article 50 transparency obligations remain closer: the agreement cut the grace period for AI-generated-content transparency solutions from six to three months (new deadline December 2, 2026), and the Commission's consultation on draft Article 50 guidance (opened May 8, 2026) closed on June 3, 2026 with final guidelines pending. The EDPB's **CEF 2026 action on transparency** (Articles 12-14 GDPR) is in active execution: 25 DPAs are launching formal investigations and fact-finding exercises in H1 2026, with consolidated findings expected to be adopted by the EDPB in H2 2026 — AI systems with vague or undocumented purposes are likely to feature prominently. Meanwhile, the **2026 CISO AI Risk Report** highlights that 71% of CISOs say AI now has access to core business systems, but only 16% govern that access effectively — pointing to a structural gap where scope creep happens because permissions outlive the purpose they were granted for. Updated shadow AI telemetry from March 2026 sharpens the picture further: 65% of organizations still detect unauthorized shadow AI even after rolling out visibility programs, 86% report they lack visibility into how data flows to and from AI tools (Reco, 2025-2026), and average breach costs at firms with high shadow AI exposure have risen to **$4.63M** — roughly $670K higher than firms with low or no shadow AI. While 62% of organizations now report a formal AI governance framework and 67% an AI/risk committee, 20% concede their policies are not actively enforced and more than a third lack dedicated AI detection capabilities — purpose limitation cannot survive that gap.

---

As of May 2026, the clearest technical failure mode is purpose drift through over-scoped agent identity rather than a model deciding to be malicious. Teleport's 2026 infrastructure-security survey found organizations with over-privileged AI systems reporting a 76% incident rate versus 17% for least-privilege deployments, making access scope the strongest predictor of outcomes. The Cloud Security Alliance's May 12, 2026 industry survey adds quantitative weight: only 8% of organizations report AI agents never exceed intended permissions, 53% see agents exceeding permissions occasionally or regularly, 47% had an AI-agent security incident in the past 12 months, and 58% take five or more hours to detect and respond — scope violations are no longer rare, they are a routine operational pattern. A parallel finding from 2026 vendor reporting is that **63% of organizations cannot technically enforce purpose limitations on their AI agents** even when they know what those agents should and should not do — the gap between declared scope and enforced scope is structural, not aspirational. MITRE's OpenClaw investigation (February 2026) shows the same mechanism in attack form: untrusted web results, messages, and third-party skills can flow into shared agent memory, then drive file I/O, email, messaging, and shell tools to exfiltrate or destroy data. The January 12, 2026 MITRE ATLAS v5.x release (Zenity Labs collaboration) hardens this picture with concrete technique IDs that auditors can now cite directly: **AML.T0085 (AI Agent Tools)** for collection-phase abuse of agent tooling, **AML.T0086 (Exfiltration via AI Agent Tool Invocation)** for using legitimate tool calls as the exfil channel, **AML.T0096 (AI Service API)** for piggybacking on agent infrastructure, **AML.T0098 (AI Agent Tool Credential Harvesting)** for extracting secrets from agent-accessible systems like SharePoint, **AML.T0099 (AI Agent Tool Data Poisoning)** for placing malicious content where agents will invoke it, **AML.T0100 (AI Agent Clickbait)** for content engineered to manipulate machine decision-making, and **AML.T0101 (Data Destruction via AI Agent Tool Invocation)** for agent-mediated destructive operations — plus the case study **AML.CS0042 (SesameOp)** documenting a backdoor that used the OpenAI Assistants API for command and control. That maps purpose limitation to concrete control evidence: each tool call needs a user/agent identity, declared purpose, allowed resource set, destination constraint, and approval state before execution.

Gateway evidence is improving. Cloudflare AI Gateway DLP (updated April 20, 2026) can scan prompts, responses, and JSON tool-call arguments/results, but it does not decode base64 attachments or dereference external URLs; auditors should treat it as a useful inline check, not a full data-lineage control. TrueFoundry's OPA Guardrails pattern is closer to purpose-binding because Rego policies can evaluate LLM inputs, outputs, and MCP pre-tool hooks with user identity, tool name, server, and request arguments. Palo Alto's May 12, 2026 Secure AI Agents update makes the same architectural point for MCP: centralize agent identity, inventory remote MCP servers, require admin enablement before connection, and preserve audit trails showing which agent, user, permission set, server, and tool were involved.

Late May and early June 2026 brought three developments worth tracking. First, **Palo Alto Networks closed the Portkey acquisition on May 29, 2026**, ahead of schedule — Portkey becomes the foundational AI gateway for Prisma AIRS, integrating runtime inspection of AI traffic with agent identity (Idira) and observability (Chronosphere); integrated-product GA dates are not yet announced, so for now this is consolidation signal rather than deployable capability. Second, **Microsoft's Build 2026 security announcements (June 2, 2026)** moved several purpose-relevant controls from concept to product: the Agent 365 SDK (GA) bundles observability, access controls, and compliance enforcement for agents; the Agent 365 Agent Registry (preview) discovers unmanaged agents including MCP servers and lets Intune block execution by agent category — a direct shadow-agent countermeasure; Defender AI Model Scanning (preview) detects and blocks vulnerable or compromised models in registries and CI/CD before deployment, which is the first mainstream vendor implementation of a model-deployment gate; and Purview runtime DLP now inspects agent prompts (preview). Third, **MITRE ATLAS shifted to a monthly release cadence**: v5.6.0 (May 4, 2026) added agent-relevant techniques and the case study **AML.CS0051 (OpenClaw Command & Control via Prompt Injection)**, and the v2026.05 release (May 27, 2026, data format v6.0.0) made every technique carry machine-readable platform tags (Predictive AI, Generative AI, Agentic AI, Enterprise) with typed relationships and STIX/Navigator exports — a useful precedent for the machine-readable asset tagging that 12.4.1 requires. On the incident-evidence side, the **Verizon 2026 DBIR (May 28, 2026)** reports shadow-AI incidents up roughly 4x year-over-year — now the third most common non-malicious insider action — with 67% of users accessing AI services through non-corporate accounts on corporate devices and source code the most commonly uploaded IP type. A Gravitee survey adds that 70% of organizations grant AI agents higher access than a human would need for the same task and only 21% have full visibility into agent permissions. The mechanism is no longer hypothetical: an AWS internal "Kiro" coding agent with over-broad permissions deleted and recreated a production environment in December 2025, contributing to a roughly 13-hour outage in the mainland China region — a concrete case of an agent exercising permissions far beyond its purpose.

"Intent-based" runtime authorization has matured rapidly in 2026 and is now a distinct enforcement layer rather than a marketing label. **Microsoft's Agent Governance Toolkit** (open source, April 2, 2026) ships seven packages — most relevant here, Agent OS provides a stateless policy engine evaluating every action at sub-0.1ms p99 latency using YAML, OPA Rego, or Cedar policies, plus a semantic intent classifier that detects goal-hijacking and is explicitly mapped against the OWASP Agentic AI Top 10. Agent Runtime adds dynamic execution rings (CPU-privilege-style isolation), saga orchestration, and emergency termination, and Agent Compliance auto-maps controls to regulatory frameworks — the first vendor-grade open implementation that puts purpose-bound runtime policy on the same footing as service mesh policy. Commercial intent-aware platforms are converging on the same model: **Akeyless Agentic Runtime Authority** (March 31, 2026) gates actions on intent + runtime context alignment with pre-approved parameters, **Proofpoint AI Security** (March 17, 2026) combines intent-based detection with multi-surface control points across human-AI and AI-AI interactions, and **Token Security** (March 18, 2026) issues task-scoped tokens whose effective permissions are derived from the agent's declared intent rather than its principal. The architectural point auditors should look for: authorization decisions are made by an external policy engine, out-of-process and inaccessible to the LLM, with permissions cryptographically tied to a declared intent and a time-bound task — not delegated to the model itself.

On governance evidence, the ICO's generative-AI purpose-limitation consultation remains a useful audit lens because it separates core-model training, adaptation/fine-tuning, and application deployment as distinct processing purposes. OECD's February 2026 Responsible AI Due Diligence guidance adds a management-system loop: embed policy, identify impacts, cease/prevent/mitigate, track results, communicate, and remediate. ISO/IEC 42001 is not a privacy-law safe harbor, but it gives assessors a certifiable management-system wrapper for AI use cases; the useful audit question is whether the AIMS scope and risk register are tied to concrete purposes, data categories, deployment contexts, and owners rather than a generic enterprise AI policy.
