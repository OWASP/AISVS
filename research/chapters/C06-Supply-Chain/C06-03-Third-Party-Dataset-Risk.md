# C6.3: Third-Party Dataset Risk Assessment

> **Parent:** [C06: Supply Chain Security](C06-Supply-Chain.md) | **Requirements:** 4 | **IDs:** 6.3.1–6.3.4

## Purpose

Datasets are a uniquely dangerous supply-chain vector for AI systems. Unlike code dependencies where vulnerabilities are typically detectable, poisoned training data can embed subtle behavioral changes into a model that are extremely difficult to detect post-training. Third-party datasets also carry legal risks — copyrighted content, personally identifiable information, and biased distributions can create liability. This section ensures external datasets are assessed for poisoning, legal compliance, and bias before use, and monitored throughout their lifecycle.

---

## 2025-2026 Landscape Update

### Web-Scale Dataset Poisoning is Practical and Cheap

Carlini et al. (2023) demonstrated two concrete attack methods against web-scale datasets that remain relevant and under-mitigated:

- **Split-view poisoning**: Many distributed datasets (LAION-400M, COYO-700M, Conceptual 12M) store URLs rather than actual data. Domain names in these URL lists continuously expire. An attacker can purchase expired domains for as little as $10 each and serve malicious content to any future downloader. The researchers found that all ten tested datasets contained between 0.14% and 6.48% purchasable expired domains. Poisoning 0.01% of LAION-400M or COYO-700M costs approximately $60 USD. Critically, nearly all third-party download tools for these datasets ignore the hash checksums that were provided.

- **Frontrunning poisoning**: For crowdsourced datasets derived from Wikipedia snapshots, attackers can predict snapshot timing to the minute and inject malicious edits that are captured in the dataset but quickly reverted. This could theoretically poison approximately 6.5% of English Wikipedia-derived documents, with multilingual Wikipedias showing vulnerability rates up to 25.3%.

Even poisoning rates as low as 0.00025% of a dataset can achieve 60% success in targeted misclassification attacks. These findings mean that any organization using URL-based or web-scraped datasets without hash verification is accepting a materially exploitable risk.

### LAION Dataset Controversies and Remediation

The LAION-5B dataset saga illustrates the full spectrum of third-party dataset risks:

- **CSAM discovery (December 2023)**: The Stanford Internet Observatory found 3,226 suspected instances of links to child sexual abuse material in LAION-5B, with 1,008 externally validated. LAION temporarily removed LAION-5B and LAION-400M.
- **Sensitive personal data**: An investigation by Bayerischer Rundfunk found that LAION datasets contain large amounts of private and sensitive data harvested from public websites without consent.
- **Hateful and harmful content**: Research (Birhane et al. 2023) documented widespread instances of rape, pornography, malign stereotypes, racist and ethnic slurs, and other extremely problematic content within LAION-5B.
- **Re-LAION-5B (August 2024)**: LAION released a cleaned version, removing 2,236 links after matching against known-abuse lists. However, the fundamental architecture of URL-based datasets means that the content behind remaining links continues to change over time.

The LAION case is instructive: even well-intentioned, widely-used datasets can harbor serious content and legal risks that are only discovered years after initial release and widespread adoption.

### Platform Supply Chain Attacks: Hugging Face and Model Hubs

As of April 2026, model hub platforms have become a significant attack surface for dataset and model poisoning:

- **Malicious model and dataset uploads**: A monitoring study tracking over 705,000 models and 176,000 datasets on Hugging Face uncovered 91 malicious models and 9 malicious dataset loading scripts containing reverse shells, browser credential theft, and system reconnaissance payloads. Separately, researchers identified approximately 100 models on the platform enabling remote code execution on user machines.
- **Protect AI Guardian platform-wide scanning (April 2025 milestone, expanded through 2026)**: Six months after the Hugging Face partnership launch, Protect AI Guardian had scanned 4.47 million unique model versions across 1.41 million repositories, flagging roughly 352,000 unsafe or suspicious issues across 51,700 models. Four new detection modules shipped in the April 2025 update and remain the canonical checks heading into 2026: PAIT-ARV-100 (archive-slip writes at load time), PAIT-JOBLIB-101 (joblib deserialization code execution), PAIT-TF-200 (TensorFlow SavedModel architectural backdoors), and PAIT-LMAFL-300 (Llamafile malicious code execution during inference). Guardian also caught CVE-2025-1550 (Keras critical vulnerability) before public disclosure, illustrating the value of zero-trust scanning regardless of stated intent.
- **Android RAT distribution (late 2025)**: A Hugging Face repository barely a month old was weaponised to host Android remote access trojan payloads, with new payloads regenerated approximately every 15 minutes across 6,000+ commits before takedown — showing how rapidly model-hub infrastructure can be abused when platform-side detection lags.
- **Model namespace reuse** (Palo Alto Unit 42): When authors delete accounts on platforms like Hugging Face, Google Vertex AI Model Garden, Microsoft Azure AI Foundry, or Kaggle, attackers can re-register the same namespace and upload compromised models. Existing pipelines that pull models by name automatically download the malicious version. Mitigations: pin models to specific commit revisions, clone trusted repositories to internal storage, and scan codebases for name-based model references.
- **Typosquatting**: Threat actors upload models with names that are common misspellings of popular models, targeting developers who do not verify model identity before downloading.
- **Cisco Foundation AI partnership (2026)**: Cisco's Foundation AI group announced an expanded AI supply chain security collaboration with Hugging Face, pushing platform-side scanning and attestation standards for both models and dataset loading scripts — a signal that major networking and security vendors now treat model-hub artifacts as first-class supply-chain dependencies.
- **Estimated impact**: One 2025 report estimated $12 billion in losses from compromised machine learning models across the supply chain, with 23% of the top 1,000 most-downloaded models having been compromised at some point.

These findings reinforce that datasets downloaded from public hubs require the same vetting rigor as third-party code dependencies — hash verification, namespace validation, and quarantine-before-use policies.

### Emerging Attack Techniques (2025-2026)

Several new poisoning vectors have materialized beyond the traditional web-scale approaches. Check Point's 2026 Tech Tsunami report and Datadog's AI supply chain abuse research have both labelled prompt injection and data poisoning the "new zero-day" threats for AI systems — a framing that is now driving procurement language and vendor-assessment checklists at larger enterprises.

- **Backdoor trigger injection**: Hidden prompts embedded in training repositories activate malicious model behaviors months after training (the "Basilisk Venom" pattern). These triggers are invisible during standard data review.
- **Synthetic data propagation**: The "Virus Infection Attack" (September 2025) demonstrated that contamination in synthetic training data spreads invisibly across model generations, compounding with each fine-tuning cycle. Once baked into synthetic datasets, the poison quietly amplifies across downstream models.
- **Multimodal poisoning**: Two CVPR papers revealed that image-generation models can be hijacked: "Silent Branding" made diffusion models reproduce logos unprompted, while "Losing Control" showed that ControlNets could be poisoned so subtle triggers force NSFW content generation while appearing normal.
- **Safetensors conversion hijacking** (HiddenLayer): Researchers demonstrated that the Hugging Face safetensors conversion service could be compromised to inject malicious code into models during format conversion and send unauthorized pull requests to any repository while impersonating the conversion bot. This highlights that even security-enhancing infrastructure can become an attack vector.
- **CVE-2026-1839 — Hugging Face Transformers Trainer RCE (disclosed April 7, 2026)**: A CWE-502 insecure-deserialization flaw in `Trainer._load_rng_state()` (src/transformers/trainer.py:3059) called `torch.load()` without `weights_only=True`. Any malicious `rng_state.pth` shipped inside a checkpoint archive executes arbitrary Python at load time. The bug affects all Transformers versions prior to `v5.0.0rc3` when paired with `torch>=2.2` and PyTorch `<2.6` (where `safe_globals()` does not enforce isolation), and is directly relevant to dataset/model ingestion pipelines that resume training from third-party checkpoints. Fix: upgrade to Transformers `v5.0.0rc3` (adds `weights_only=True` and a new `check_torch_load_is_safe()` guard) or PyTorch 2.6+. CVSS 6.5 (AV:L/AC:H/PR:N/UI:R/C:H/I:L/A:H) — requires a user to load the crafted file, which is exactly the workflow during fine-tuning on hub-sourced artifacts.
- **Medical domain poisoning**: A 2025 multi-university study (NYU, Washington, Columbia) and the comprehensive 2026 analytical framework by Abtahi, Seoane, Pau, and Vega-Barbas (*Journal of Medical Internet Research*, January 23, 2026 — Karolinska Institutet and Universidad Politécnica de Madrid) found that as few as 100–500 poisoned samples can compromise clinical LLMs, medical imaging systems, and agentic healthcare AI. The JMIR 2026 paper pinned down architecture-specific compromise ranges — 60–80% for clinical LLMs and 70–95% for medical imaging — and reinforced that attack success depends on the **absolute** number of poisoned samples rather than their proportion of the corpus, so scaling the training set does not automatically dilute the threat. Detection delays commonly range 6 to 12 months and extend to years in federated or privacy-constrained environments. A *Nature Medicine* study (cited in the Lakera 2026 poisoning review) went further, showing that replacing just 0.001% of training tokens with misinformation produced 7–11% more harmful completions — a vanishingly small poisoning ratio that still moved the needle on safety metrics, reinforcing that corpus-level poisoning budgets remain extraordinarily cheap for attackers.
- **Near-constant pretraining poisoning (Anthropic + UK AISI + Alan Turing Institute, October 2025)**: Souly et al. ran the largest pretraining poisoning study to date — models from 600M to 13B parameters trained on chinchilla-optimal corpora ranging from 6B to 260B tokens — and showed that **roughly 250 malicious documents** reliably installed a denial-of-service backdoor across the full model range. The 13B model sees more than 20× the clean data of the 600M model, yet the absolute number of poisons required did not scale with corpus size. This generalises the medical-domain finding to general-purpose LLMs and demolishes the common assumption that large training sets are inherently safer. For dataset risk programmes it means the relevant unit of vetting is **document count** (and document provenance), not **poisoning percentage** — an attacker targeting a flagship pretraining run does not need to compromise millions of URLs, just a few hundred persistent ones.
- **Privacy-regulation paradox (JMIR 2026)**: The same analytical framework raised a point that most security literature has ignored — HIPAA, GDPR, and equivalent regimes restrict cross-patient correlation and multi-institutional auditing, which is exactly the telemetry needed to catch distributed clean-label poisoning. Privacy controls required for lawful operation therefore create detection blind spots that sophisticated attackers can exploit. Mitigation candidates the authors propose include ensemble disagreement monitoring (MEDLEY — Medical Ensemble Diagnostic system with Leveraged Diversity), Byzantine-robust federated aggregation, and constraint-driven neuro-symbolic architectures (e.g., Dynasmile for orthodontics) where non-anatomical deviations surface as immediate, auditable anomalies rather than silent drift.
- **Medical device firmware AI poisoning (2026)**: A multinational medical device manufacturer disclosed a poisoning campaign in which attackers compromised the ML system used to verify firmware updates, causing hidden backdoors to be signed into deployed devices — an early example of dataset poisoning propagating directly into cyber-physical systems rather than staying confined to chat-style models.
- **PoisonBench (ICML)**: A benchmark specifically designed to measure LLM vulnerability to data poisoning, providing standardized evaluation across attack types and model architectures.
- **MCP tool poisoning**: Hidden instructions embedded in Model Context Protocol tool descriptions can poison the context that agents consume, representing a new data poisoning vector in agentic AI systems.

### Legal Landscape for Training Data

The legal environment for training data has become significantly more complex, particularly with the EU AI Act entering enforcement:

- **NYT v. OpenAI** and **Getty v. Stability AI** established that training on copyrighted content carries real litigation risk.
- **LAION v. Kneschke (2024)**: A German court ruled that building public datasets is covered by the text and data mining (TDM) exception under EU copyright law, providing some legal cover for research datasets, but the ruling's applicability to commercial use remains contested.
- **GDPR and CCPA enforcement**: Models that memorize and regurgitate PII from training data face regulatory action.
- **EU AI Act Article 10 (enforcing August 2026)**: High-risk AI systems must use training data that is "relevant, representative, free of errors and complete." Organizations must document all datasets used, maintain full records of data origin, publish summaries of training data for general-purpose AI models, and respect copyright opt-outs under the EU Copyright Directive. Penalties reach up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover.
- **EU AI Act timeline**: General-purpose AI obligations began August 2025; high-risk system requirements become fully applicable August 2026. Organizations need data source inventories, quality results, lineage diagrams, and usage approvals ready for enforcement.

### 2026 Cross-Domain Supply Chain Incidents

The first four months of 2026 have also produced several non-AI-specific supply chain compromises that are directly relevant to dataset-ingestion pipelines, because most AI teams rely on the same PyPI/npm ecosystem to download data-loading scripts, augmentation libraries, and evaluation frameworks:

- **LiteLLM compromise (24 March 2026)**: The TeamPCP threat actor used a Trivy supply-chain compromise to steal maintainer credentials, then published `litellm==1.82.7` and `1.82.8` to PyPI with base64-encoded malware embedded directly in the proxy server code and executing on import. The packages were live for roughly 40 minutes before PyPI quarantined them — long enough to hit a tool that reports ~97 million monthly downloads and an estimated 36% presence in cloud environments. Datadog's write-up treats this as a direct dataset-/model-loader threat because many teams call LiteLLM from training and evaluation pipelines.
- **Axios npm compromise (31 March 2026)**: Microsoft Threat Intelligence attributed malicious `axios@1.14.1` and `axios@0.30.4` to the Sapphire Sleet North Korean threat cluster. Those versions quietly dropped a malicious dependency that fetched payloads from known C2 servers — and Axios is a near-universal HTTP client inside dataset downloaders.
- **eScan supply chain attack (20 January 2026)**: Attackers breached a regional update server for MicroWorld's eScan antivirus and delivered malware to customer systems — a reminder that anti-malware agents themselves sit on the critical path of dataset-curation workstations.
- **European Commission breach via Trivy compromise**: The EC's Europa.eu platform disclosed a breach traced back to the same Trivy supply-chain chain that enabled the LiteLLM attack, amplifying concerns that container-scanning tooling used in ML CI can itself become the poisoning vector.

These incidents matter for dataset risk programs because third-party dataset ingestion runs on top of exactly this software stack. An AI BOM that lists only datasets and models, without the data-loading libraries and CI tooling that touch them, will miss a substantial share of the realistic compromise surface.

### NIST AI RMF Critical Infrastructure Profile (April 2026)

On April 7, 2026 NIST released a concept note for an AI RMF Profile on Trustworthy AI in Critical Infrastructure, signaling additional structure around third-party AI and dataset risk for regulated sectors. The profile is expected to tie the existing AI RMF 1.0 Govern/Map/Measure/Manage functions to sector-specific data-supply-chain controls, including dataset provenance, poisoning resistance, and vendor monitoring — complementing the NIST AI 600-1 Generative AI profile published in 2024. Organisations in energy, healthcare, finance, and transportation should track the profile as it moves toward finalisation; its categories are likely to appear in contractual third-party risk questionnaires throughout 2026.

### ISO/IEC 42001 Annex A Controls for Third-Party Datasets

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 is now the most widely referenced AI management-system standard in third-party risk questionnaires. Three Annex A controls matter directly for dataset-risk programmes and have been mostly absent from AISVS supplier checklists so far:

- **Annex A.4.3 — Documentation of data**: Organisations must document how each dataset is acquired, including source, data-subject characteristics, potential biases, data rights, and provenance events (creation, updates, abstraction, sharing). For third-party datasets this means the vendor's own acquisition records must be captured in the receiving organisation's documentation, not just referenced.
- **Annex A.7.5 — Data provenance**: Requires end-to-end event logging of who acquired each dataset (with signed consent and licence evidence), exact logs of labelling, cleaning, modification, and merge operations (with script versions and operator IDs), and tamper-resistant change/access records that can be exported for regulatory, customer, or audit review "in minutes, not weeks." Auditors verify this by exporting live provenance records, requesting evidence for randomly selected third-party datasets, and running gap analyses for periods when logging lapsed. Manual workarounds, disconnected logging systems, and untracked vendor or API integrations are explicit non-compliance indicators.
- **Annex A.10.3 — AI suppliers**: Distinguishes external supplier governance from internal responsibility allocation (A.10.2). Requires contractual clauses covering data provenance disclosure, performance and fairness reporting, incident-notification SLAs, bias-mitigation evidence, and security/privacy guarantees against model inversion and unauthorised access. Monitoring intensity must scale with organisational maturity — from lightweight pre-adoption security reviews at startups, through structured provenance and bias risk assessments at scaleups, up to continuous automated drift and bias monitoring plus comprehensive upstream-supplier audits at enterprise level. Sub-processor agreements must flow the same responsible-AI requirements down to dataset sub-suppliers, and auditors expect retained evidence of selection criteria, risk assessments, corrective-action requests, and updated contractual clauses.

For most organisations, mapping EU AI Act Article 10 documentation evidence to ISO 42001 A.4.3, A.7.5, and A.10.3 is now the cheapest way to satisfy both regimes from a single evidence base. ISO/IEC 23894:2023 (AI risk-management guidance) remains the companion process standard — it is not a certifiable management system, but its risk-source taxonomy (data quality, bias, adversarial attacks, privacy) is useful vocabulary when writing third-party dataset risk assessments for A.10.3 evidence packs.

### GSA "Basic Safeguarding of AI Systems" Proposed Clause (March 2026)

On March 6, 2026 the U.S. General Services Administration published a proposed contract clause, **GSAR 552.239-7001 "Basic Safeguarding of Artificial Intelligence Systems,"** that materially affects any dataset or model supplier selling to federal agencies. Key dataset-relevant provisions:

- **Model provenance statement**: Contractors must submit a model provenance statement, a model-specific risk assessment, documented mitigation measures, and proposed government use-rights on timelines set within the clause, for every AI system used in contract performance.
- **NIST AI RMF-aligned documentation**: System documentation must be "consistent with the NIST AI Risk Management Framework," including information on known biases and other data needed for government AI impact assessments — effectively pulling dataset bias evaluation into the contract deliverable set.
- **AI system disclosure**: Contractors must disclose all AI systems used in contract performance, including modifications made to comply with foreign or commercial regulatory frameworks — a transparency requirement that surfaces non-US training corpora and jurisdiction-driven dataset variants.
- **Government data restriction**: Government-provided data cannot be used to train, fine-tune, or improve any AI model for any other customer or commercial purpose, closing a common back-channel for cross-tenant contamination.
- **Evaluation window**: A 30-day evaluation period is required before implementing major AI model version changes, which pushes dataset-change impact analysis into a scheduled cadence rather than release-gated approval.
- **Public comment**: The original comment deadline of April 3, 2026 was extended to May 31, 2026; the clause's definition of "American AI Systems" (systems "developed and produced in the United States") has no further test for "produced," and contractors are submitting clarification requests aimed at training-data sourcing.

The practical implication is that any organisation distributing datasets into federal AI pipelines will need to furnish provenance statements, bias disclosures, and data-use restrictions that look a lot like ISO 42001 A.7.5/A.10.3 evidence — aligning the two regimes in a single document pack is a realistic 2026 engineering target.

### FINRA 2026 Annual Regulatory Oversight Report — Dataset Governance

FINRA's 2026 Annual Regulatory Oversight Report (published December 2025) made generative AI and third-party AI vendor oversight standing priorities for member firms. Dataset-relevant guidance:

- **Vendor data hygiene**: When vetting a vendor — whether a CRM with built-in AI or a specialised research tool — firms must understand the vendor's "data hygiene" and whether the vendor uses client data to train their global models. Firms are reminded that reliance on limited or outdated datasets can produce incorrect or skewed outputs, and that outdated training data is an explicit source of "concept drift."
- **Contractual updates**: FINRA expects firms to update vendor contracts to cover AI usage, training-data rights, security controls, sub-processors, logging access, model-change notifications, and incident reporting — essentially the same scope ISO 42001 A.10.3 requires, translated into US securities-industry vocabulary.
- **Supervisory framework (Notice 21-29 tie-in)**: The report reiterates that supervisory obligations for outsourcing to third-party vendors (Regulatory Notice 21-29) apply to AI vendors, and firms must maintain detailed inventories of vendor services, connected systems, and the firm data vendors can access.
- **Model risk management**: Firms should establish supervision/governance/model-risk frameworks that establish clear policies for developing, implementing, using, and monitoring GenAI — with robust testing covering capabilities, limitations, privacy, integrity, reliability, and accuracy, plus ongoing monitoring of prompts, responses, and outputs with stored logs, model-version tracking, and human-in-the-loop validation.
- **Notable gap**: The report is thin on prescriptive language for contractual clauses governing training-data provenance validation and data-residency controls for proprietary data used in model training, and cross-references NIST AI RMF for those details. Firms that build their training-data provenance evidence against NIST AI RMF 1.0 plus AI 600-1 are therefore producing the same artefacts FINRA examiners will ask to see.

### MITRE ATLAS v5.4.0 Updates (February 2026)

MITRE ATLAS v5.4.0 (February 2026) expanded to **16 tactics, 84 techniques, and 56 sub-techniques** — up from 15 tactics and 66 techniques in October 2025. Techniques most relevant to third-party dataset risk:

- **AML.T0048 — ML Software Dependency Compromise**: Attached as the canonical Initial Access technique for the March 2026 LiteLLM PyPI incident; now the reference pattern auditors cite when asking whether an organisation's data-loader dependency tree is scanned and pinned.
- **AML.T0020 — Poison Training Data**: Continues to cover the split-view, frontrunning, and near-constant-poison attack families described above; still the anchor technique for dataset-poisoning controls in AISVS 6.3.2.
- **Publish Poisoned AI Agent Tool (added February 2026)**: Adversaries create malicious versions of legitimate MCP tools that execute harmful actions when invoked — relevant to dataset-loading scripts published as MCP tools on model hubs.
- **AML.T0096 — AI Service API (2026)**: Exploiting AI orchestration APIs for stealthy command and control, including data-pipeline APIs that fetch third-party datasets at runtime.

For dataset-risk programmes, the v5.4.0 updates justify explicit ATLAS technique mappings in third-party dataset risk registers — AML.T0020 plus AML.T0048 is now the minimum plausible mapping for any externally-sourced dataset.

### NSA/CISA AI Supply Chain Guidance (March 2026)

In March 2026, the NSA alongside allied intelligence agencies published joint guidance on AI/ML supply chain risks and mitigations. Key recommendations relevant to dataset security:

- **Quarantine and test** externally sourced data before moving it into internal systems. Preprocess and review for quality and bias issues.
- **Integrity verification**: Use checksums, cryptographic hashes, digital signatures, and lineage tracking methods for all datasets.
- **Vendor assessment**: Evaluate third-party data providers' security practices and vulnerability management processes. Monitor their security posture over time.
- **Contractual controls**: Include cybersecurity requirements in contracts with dataset suppliers — covering data handling restrictions, audit rights, incident response obligations, and retention policies.
- **AI Bill of Materials**: Maintain documentation of all components including datasets, analogous to Software Bills of Materials (SBOMs) for code dependencies.
- **Threat modeling**: Perform threat modeling specific to AI/ML data pipelines, including data poisoning and model inversion attack scenarios.

### Dataset Vetting Tools: Current State

The tooling landscape for dataset security has matured considerably but still has gaps:

- **Cleanlab**: Identifies label errors, outliers, and near-duplicates in datasets. Useful for detecting dirty-label poisoning but limited against clean-label attacks. Now integrates with major ML frameworks for pipeline-embedded validation.
- **Great Expectations**: General-purpose data validation framework that can enforce schema, statistical, and distribution constraints on incoming datasets.
- **Microsoft Presidio**: PII detection and redaction for unstructured text. Supports multiple languages but has significant false-negative rates for non-English content and domain-specific identifiers.
- **DVC (Data Version Control)**: Open-source version control for datasets providing Git-like tracking of data changes, integrity verification through hash-based deduplication, and audit trails via pull-request-style reviews of data modifications. As of late 2025, lakeFS acquired DVC to combine lightweight file-based versioning with production-scale data lake management.
- **Hawk-Eye**: Open-source broad-spectrum scanner covering databases, cloud storage, and files with OCR support for 350+ file types (DOCX, PDF, images, videos) — useful for detecting PII in multimodal training datasets.
- **PiiCatcher**: CLI scanner that detects PII in databases using regex pattern matching and NLP-based analysis with spaCy, catching both obviously-named columns and columns with generic names but sensitive content.
- **Croissant (MLCommons)**: Metadata format for ML datasets gaining adoption, providing standardized fields for provenance, license, and content description. Complementary to the "Datasheets for Datasets" framework.
- **ScanCode / REUSE**: License detection for code datasets, important for detecting copyleft or restricted-license code in training corpora.
- **Data Provenance Initiative**: Academic effort to catalog and document the provenance of major ML training datasets, providing a reference for organizations performing due diligence.
- **Socket AI Model Scanner (2026)**: Socket extended its supply-chain security platform to Hugging Face in experimental mode, scanning model artifacts (PyTorch, TensorFlow, Keras, TFLite/LiteRT, GGUF, Llamafile) for hidden malware, backdoors, and malicious deserialization payloads, plus a Package Scanner that inspects loader scripts for typosquatting and credential leakage.
- **Picklescan (Hugging Face, 2026 update)**: Hugging Face updated Picklescan to catch threats in intentionally "broken" pickle files that previously bypassed scanning. Still not a complete defence against executable model formats — pair with mandatory safetensors conversion and sandboxed loading.
- **MalHug pipeline**: End-to-end detection pipeline (dataset loading-script extraction, model deserialization, taint analysis, heuristic pattern matching) deployed on a mirrored Hugging Face instance; remains the most publicly documented platform-wide dataset/model-scanning telemetry source.

### Poisoning Detection Frameworks (2025-2026)

Beyond the general dataset vetting tools above, several specialized poisoning detection frameworks have emerged:

- **GBDR (Generalizable Backdoor Detection and Removal)**: A peer-reviewed framework that detects and removes backdoors from datasets without knowledge of attack specifications. It exploits the Model Capacity Effect (MCE) — backdoor and clean samples exhibit distinct performance across models of varying capacity. A lower-capacity detection model differentiates between them, then a diffusion-based purification phase removes triggers and corrects labels. Validated across four benchmark datasets, outperforming prior state-of-the-art defenses. Open-source implementation available on GitHub.
- **BaDLoss**: Addresses a gap in prior defenses by handling multiple simultaneous backdoor attacks. Leverages the observation that poisoned images exhibit anomalous loss trajectories during training — they rely on unnatural features for classification, which manifests as detectable loss patterns.
- **MEDLEY (Ensemble Disagreement Monitoring)**: Proposed for healthcare AI, this approach uses architectural and vendor diversity to detect poisoning through systematic disagreement patterns across heterogeneous model ensembles. Particularly relevant for high-stakes domains where a single compromised foundation model could propagate to hundreds of institutions.
- **MalHug**: End-to-end detection pipeline deployed on a mirrored Hugging Face instance, monitoring over 705K models and 176K datasets. Uncovered 91 malicious models and 9 malicious dataset loading scripts containing reverse shells, credential theft, and reconnaissance payloads.

### AI BOM Standards and Provenance Tools

As of early 2026, AI Bill of Materials standards have matured substantially, though adoption remains a challenge:

- **CycloneDX 1.6** (April 2024) added ML-BOM support, and **CycloneDX 1.7** (2025) expanded with patent and intellectual property metadata, citations for data provenance, and enhanced cryptographic transparency.
- **SPDX 3.0** (April 2024) introduced formal AI and Dataset profiles extending the traditional SBOM model to describe ML components — models, datasets, training configurations, and provenance data — in a machine-readable format.
- **TAIBOM (Trusted AI Bill of Materials)** (Oxford, October 2025): A research framework that extends SBOM principles to AI with cryptographic attestation. Each artifact is hashed and digitally signed on creation with full provenance (source URI, timestamp, license, compliance details). Derived artifacts must embed signed hashes of all direct ancestors, ensuring a chain of trust from training data through deployed inference.
- **AIBoMGen** (ACM/IEEE CAIN 2026): A proof-of-concept platform that automates AI BOM generation, acting as a neutral third-party observer during model training. Each worker node runs training inside a read-only container; every job is forced to return a valid in-toto attestation before any artifact enters storage or a registry. The attestation forms a cryptographically rooted extension of the CycloneDX SBOM, recording the exact datasets, base models, container images, and hyperparameters that contributed to each trained model — with negligible performance overhead reported in the paper.
- **Atlas (Intel Labs, 2025-2026)**: Open-source framework and Rust-based CLI for ML lifecycle provenance and transparency. Uses a sidecar attestation client to monitor Kubeflow pipelines and produces digitally signed manifests across data-processing, training, evaluation, and deployment stages. Integrates hardware TEE attestation for stronger integrity guarantees. Released by Spoczynski, Melara, and Szyller at Intel Labs with minimal required changes to existing ML workflows.
- **OWASP AI-BOM Project**: The Prerequisites workstream is assessing how well CycloneDX 1.6 and SPDX 3.0 address AI-system requirements and where extensions are needed. Serves as a reference for organizations choosing an AI BOM standard.
- **SPDX 3.0 ISO standardisation track**: SPDX 3.0 (including the AI and Dataset profiles) is being prepared for formal submission to ISO as part of updating ISO/IEC 5962. Member-body and Technical Committee review is expected to drive broader 2026-2027 adoption — worth tracking for organisations standardising on a single format rather than maintaining dual CycloneDX + SPDX pipelines.
- **Open Trusted Data Initiative (OTDI, AI Alliance, v0.1.7)**: A dataset-specification layer built on top of Hugging Face Dataset Cards that mandates explicit ownership affirmation, CDLA Permissive 2.0 licensing for redistribution, upstream-source declaration for derived datasets, and a "most restrictive upstream license" propagation rule so permissive relicensing of derivatives cannot strip obligations. OTDI introduces four transformation categories (Raw, Filtered, Structured, Derived) that map cleanly into CycloneDX and SPDX provenance fields. It is explicitly *not* a cryptographic framework — pair it with TAIBOM, AIBoMGen, Atlas, or Sigstore-based model signing when integrity attestation is needed.
- **Sigstore for models and datasets (Red Hat Emerging Tech, 2025)**: Sigstore's model-transparency library now signs serialized manifests that enumerate every file in a model or dataset artifact with a cryptographic hash. It supports SHA-256 and BLAKE2 hashing and is the path Red Hat and several upstream communities are pushing as a common signing substrate for Hugging Face-style artifacts — complementing, rather than replacing, the CycloneDX/SPDX metadata layer.
- **Data Bill of Materials (DBOM) as a distinct layer**: A December 2025 industry Q&A with Bruno Kurtic (Bedrock Data) argued that a DBOM is complementary to — not a replacement for — an ML-BOM: the ML-BOM describes model components and training configuration, while the DBOM is an ingredient label for the data itself (source, classification, processing steps, access controls). Kurtic's core point is that governance must happen **before** the model runs, because "you can't govern retroactively," and framed DBOM adoption as a response to EU AI Act Article 10 and emerging SEC disclosure expectations around AI-influenced decisions. The DBOM Project (Linux Foundation incubation) maintains an open specification that is starting to surface in enterprise procurement language for 2026.
- **OpenSSF MLSecOps whitepaper (August 2025)**: The OpenSSF published *Visualizing Secure MLOps (MLSecOps): A Practical Guide for Building Robust AI/ML Pipeline Security* as a vendor-neutral reference architecture. It integrates CI/CD, ML lifecycle stages, persona-based controls, and mapped risks — including dataset provenance, poisoning defence, and BOM generation — and is now one of the few consensus documents that explicitly wires dataset security into a DevSecOps-style pipeline rather than leaving it as an ML-team concern.
- **Adoption gap**: A June 2025 Lineaje survey found 48% of security professionals admit their organizations are falling behind on SBOM requirements; ML-BOM adoption is significantly lower still. Elevate Consult's April 2026 AI data-protection guidance recommends pairing CycloneDX ML-BOM with a Data Bill of Materials (DBOM) and cryptographic signing for full data-to-model auditability — an explicit acknowledgement that dataset-side provenance is the weakest link in most AI BOM implementations today.

### Bias Detection and Drift Monitoring Tools

For requirements 6.5.4 and 6.5.5, the tooling ecosystem has expanded beyond research prototypes into production-ready platforms:

- **Fairlearn (Microsoft)**: Open-source toolkit for assessing and improving fairness in ML models. Provides demographic parity, equalized odds, and calibration metrics with integration into scikit-learn pipelines.
- **AI Fairness 360 (IBM)**: Comprehensive bias detection library with 70+ fairness metrics and 13 bias mitigation algorithms covering pre-processing, in-processing, and post-processing stages.
- **EvidentlyAI**: Open-source platform with built-in drift reports, data quality monitoring, and model performance dashboards. Supports statistical drift detection using KS tests, PSI, and Jensen-Shannon divergence.
- **WhyLabs**: AI observability platform providing continuous dataset monitoring, drift detection, and anomaly alerting. Released under Apache 2 license in January 2025.
- **Arthur AI**: Production AI monitoring with bias drift alerts, providing continuous fairness monitoring rather than point-in-time assessments.
- **Fiddler AI**: Shifts bias detection from pre-deployment testing to continuous production monitoring, alerting teams before biased predictions cause compliance issues.
- **Arize AI**: ML observability with embedding drift detection, particularly useful for monitoring vector-based datasets used in RAG systems.

### Data Supply Chain Security

Organizations should treat datasets with the same supply-chain rigor applied to code dependencies:

1. **Hash-verify on download**: Always verify dataset integrity using cryptographic hashes. Do not rely on URL stability. Use tools like DVC for hash-based integrity tracking.
2. **Mirror locally**: Download and snapshot datasets to internal storage rather than re-fetching from external URLs at training time. This eliminates split-view and namespace reuse attacks.
3. **Version-pin datasets**: Reference specific dataset versions or commits (e.g., Hugging Face dataset revisions with commit hashes) rather than "latest." This is the dataset equivalent of dependency pinning.
4. **Quarantine before use**: Following NSA guidance, quarantine externally sourced data and run automated content scanning (PII, CSAM, copyright, poisoning indicators) in an isolated environment before any training use.
5. **Namespace validation**: Verify model and dataset author identity on platforms like Hugging Face. Check for typosquatting and namespace reuse by confirming the publishing account's history and activity.
6. **Document in AI BOM**: Capture provenance, license, preprocessing steps, known limitations, and data subject descriptions for every dataset. Align with EU AI Act Article 10 documentation requirements.
7. **Contractual controls**: When procuring datasets from third parties, include security requirements in contracts covering data handling, audit rights, incident response, and retention policies per the NSA/CISA guidance.

- How quickly are enterprises actually patching `torch.load()`-derived RCEs like CVE-2026-1839 in their training pipelines, and how should dataset providers signal safe-to-load status (e.g., safetensors-only, signed manifest, pinned minimum `transformers`/`torch` versions) as part of distribution metadata?
- Souly et al. (October 2025) show ~250 documents suffice to backdoor models from 600M to 13B parameters. If the attack budget is a fixed document count rather than a poisoning ratio, what auditable thresholds should regulators set for dataset vetting — inspect every document above a certain provenance tier, require signed source attestations for a minimum fraction of tokens, or something else entirely? How do we reconcile this with web-scale corpora where per-document human review is economically impossible?
- Does a standalone Data Bill of Materials (DBOM) belong beside CycloneDX ML-BOM and SPDX 3.0, or should the dataset layer live entirely inside the existing ML-BOM? If both persist, which fields are authoritative when they disagree — and who is the custodian of record for third-party data?
- With ISO/IEC 42001 Annex A.7.5 demanding provenance evidence exportable "in minutes, not weeks," and the GSA March 2026 clause pulling NIST AI RMF documentation into federal contract deliverables, what is the minimum common evidence pack that satisfies EU AI Act Article 10, ISO 42001 A.4.3/A.7.5/A.10.3, NIST AI RMF 600-1, NSA/CISA AI BOM guidance, and FINRA Regulatory Notice 21-29 simultaneously — and which fields are still regime-specific enough to require separate artefacts?
- MITRE ATLAS v5.4.0 now couples AML.T0020 (Poison Training Data) with AML.T0048 (ML Software Dependency Compromise) via the LiteLLM case. Should third-party dataset risk registers treat the data-loader dependency tree as an in-scope dataset component (so a single ATLAS mapping covers both the data and the code that fetches it), or keep them as separate risk entries with distinct mitigations?
