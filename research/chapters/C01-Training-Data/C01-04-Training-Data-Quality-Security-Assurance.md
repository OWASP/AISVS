# C1.4: Training Data Quality and Security Assurance

> [Back to C01 Index](C01-Training-Data.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-09

## Purpose

Data quality validation is the last defensive line before poisoned, corrupted, or malformed data enters the training pipeline. This section treats quality checks as a security control, not a hygiene concern: the same statistical and schema checks that catch upstream pipeline bugs are also the cheapest broad-spectrum detector for adversarial contamination.

Several lines of research have reset the threat model since 2024:

- **Absolute-count poisoning.** Anthropic, the UK AI Security Institute, and the Alan Turing Institute published [*Poisoning Attacks on LLMs Require a Near-constant Number of Poison Samples*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07192) in October 2025. Across 72 models from 600M to 13B parameters trained on 6B–260B tokens, **250 crafted documents consistently produced a working backdoor** regardless of clean-data volume. In the 13B regime that is about 0.00016% of the corpus — well below the sensitivity of most percentage-based contamination audits. [Anthropic's research summary](https://www.anthropic.com/research/small-samples-poison) frames the asymmetry bluntly: defenders must detect fixed-count contamination, not dilute it.
- **Clinical and code-gen blast radius.** The [*Nature Medicine* study on medical LLMs (Jan 2025)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-024-03445-1) showed that replacing just **0.001% of training tokens** with targeted misinformation produced measurably more harmful completions while passing standard medical QA benchmarks. [CodeScan (Mar 2026, arXiv:2603.17174)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.17174) then demonstrated that token-level consistency scans are weak for source code; AST normalization and vulnerability-oriented black-box scanning reached 97%+ detection accuracy in the authors' test set, but only for code-generation poisoning.
- **Preference and synthetic-data poisoning.** [PoisonBench (ICML 2025)](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v267/fu25e.html) found that poisoned preference data can affect 22 widely used models across eight scenarios, that larger parameter counts do not consistently improve resilience, and that effects can generalize to triggers not present in the poisoned set. [Virus Infection Attack (NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041) then showed poisoning content propagating through synthetic-data generation even when the downstream synthetic-data queries are clean.

The practical implication for Section 1.4 is layered detection. Format validation catches obviously broken records, statistical/embedding analysis catches clumpy or out-of-distribution poisoning, confidence gates stop auto-labels from laundering upstream model errors into fresh training data, adversarial training raises the bar for evasion at inference, clean-label-specific defenses (purification, k-NN filtering, partition aggregation) cover the poisoning family that label checks structurally cannot see, and security-exploitable-bias probing closes the evaluation loop for abuse-detection and fraud models. As of June 2026, no single tool covers all of this; a research-grade check list means naming specific libraries, versions, thresholds, owners, and what each one does **not** catch.

PureVQ-GAN's published numbers are vision-only; text and code modalities still lack a comparable bottleneck defense. ConfGuard is excellent for the inference-time signal, but a clean confidence trace does not imply a clean training set — pair it with offline screening. Datasig is still proof-of-concept-stage and MinHash by design misses semantic manipulation. No single tool wins; a layered check with documented coverage gaps is the minimum defensible posture. |
| **1.4.3** | **Verify that** automatically generated labels (e.g., via models or weak supervision) are subject to confidence thresholds and consistency checks to detect misleading or low-confidence labels. | 2 | Propagation of model errors into training data (feedback loops); adversarial manipulation of auto-labeling models to inject systematic mislabels; quality degradation from overreliance on weak supervision; *Virus Infection Attack* (VIA, NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight) showing poisoning propagates through synthetic data generation and amplifies across generations ([arXiv:2509.23041](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041)). | Review the auto-labeling pipeline for explicit confidence gates. Confirm the pipeline uses Confident Learning (Prune-by-Class or Prune-by-Noise-Rate variants — see the [noisy-label-detection benchmark, arXiv:2510.16211](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.16211)) or equivalent statistical screening. For weak-supervision stacks, check [Snorkel](https://github.com/snorkel-team/snorkel) or [Snuba](https://www.vldb.org/pvldb/vol12/p223-varma.pdf)-style labeling-function aggregation and that conflicting votes are flagged, not silently averaged. Cross-model agreement (ensemble disagreement > X% = quarantine), temporal-stability checks against a held-out canary set, and documented human-review sampling rates for auto-generated labels should all be evidenced. Confirm synthetic data produced from potentially poisoned upstream models is screened against the dataset-level detection stack in 1.4.2 before re-ingest. | [Snorkel](https://github.com/snorkel-team/snorkel) and [Cleanlab](https://github.com/cleanlab/cleanlab) support confidence thresholds and CL-style joint-matrix screening. Gap: there is no standard methodology for *choosing* the threshold; teams often pick it empirically per project, which makes the control easy to satisfy on paper and weak in practice. Model collapse from recursive auto-labeling is a known failure mode ([Shumailov et al., *Nature* 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07566-y)). VIA means synthetic pipelines can silently inherit upstream poisoning; screening the *output* of synthetic generation against the 1.4.2 stack is load-bearing. |
| **1.4.4** | **Verify that** automated tests catch label skews on every ingest or significant data transformation. | 2 | Class imbalance introduced by poisoning (flooding one class to shift decision boundaries); drift in label distributions indicating a compromised or tampered pipeline; adversarial manipulation of class proportions to weaken minority-class detection (e.g., injecting "benign" labels to dilute abuse-detection training). | Confirm label-distribution monitoring is wired into the ingest DAG and not just run ad hoc. [Great Expectations](https://github.com/great-expectations/great_expectations), [Deepchecks](https://github.com/deepchecks/deepchecks), and [Evidently](https://docs.evidentlyai.com/metrics/preset_data_drift) all support distribution or drift checks; confirm a statistical test (chi-squared, KL divergence, Jensen-Shannon distance, Wasserstein distance, or population stability index) fires on each ingest with alerting thresholds set above expected legitimate drift. Verify that alerts require human sign-off before downstream training proceeds and that the baseline distribution is versioned in source control (so "the baseline moved" is itself a reviewable change). | Distinguishing malicious skew from legitimate distribution shift requires domain context; no tool does this automatically. Threshold tuning is manual. The control is also bypassable if the adversary poisons across classes proportionally; skew detection only fires when class counts move, not when class *semantics* move. Pair with 1.4.2 embedding/outlier analysis for coverage. |
| **1.4.5** | **Verify that** models used in security-relevant decisions (e.g., abuse detection, fraud scoring, automated trust decisions) are evaluated for systematic bias patterns that an adversary could exploit to evade controls (e.g., mimicking a trusted language style or demographic pattern to bypass detection), before deployment and after any significant model update. | 2 | Adversarial exploitation of demographic, stylistic, regional, device, or behavioral biases in security models; evasion of abuse detection by mimicking under-detected styles; systematic blind spots in fraud detection that attackers can probe and weaponize; cross-model consistency attacks where the same input routes through a biased classifier. The [DHS 2023 adversarial AI risk memo](https://www.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/2023-12/23_1222_st_risks_mitigation_strategies.pdf), [NIST AI RMF](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework), and [EU AI Act Article 10](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-10) all treat bias, representativeness, and feedback loops as security and governance concerns, not just fairness concerns. | Review bias-evaluation reports for each security-critical model. Confirm disaggregated performance metrics across relevant subgroups (style, dialect, geography, device class, account age, activity pattern — whatever matters for the deployment). [Fairlearn](https://github.com/fairlearn/fairlearn) and [AI Fairness 360](https://github.com/Trusted-AI/AIF360) provide the measurement plumbing. Confirm an adversarial-probing step goes beyond measurement: red-team exercises that attempt to bypass abuse detection or fraud scoring using under-performing subgroup patterns, with findings logged and remediated. Mature red-team toolchains in 2026 — [Giskard](https://github.com/Giskard-AI/giskard) for ML-model evasion and bias scans, plus [Promptfoo](https://www.promptfoo.dev/) and prompt-level fuzzers for language-style bypass attempts — can automate the probe-and-replay step, but the threat model still has to be designed by humans. Require regression tests so fairness fixes do not open a new evasion path. | No dedicated "security-exploitable-bias" tooling exists. Fairness tools measure the gap; they do not tell you which gaps are weaponizable. This still requires human red-team design and domain-specific threat modeling. Also in scope: ensuring remediation does not itself create new exploitable classes, such as a fraud model that over-trusts a protected or high-reputation subgroup after a fairness adjustment. |
| **1.4.6** | **Verify that** training-time defenses against data poisoning are selected and applied based on a documented risk assessment, and that the chosen defense (e.g., adversarial training, data augmentation with perturbed inputs, or robust optimization) and its tuning rationale are recorded alongside the model artifact. | 2 | Adversarial examples and perturbation-based evasion (MITRE ATLAS [AML.T0042](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0042)); clean-label poisoning, label flipping, and targeted backdoors that survive ordinary data-quality gates; fragile "defenses" that only hide gradients or work for one perturbation family while leaving the deployed model exploitable. The risk assessment matters because PGD/TRADES-style hardening mainly covers bounded evasion, while clean-label poisoning defenses such as deep k-NN filtering or partition aggregation address different failure modes. | Review the model risk assessment and confirm each high-risk model has an explicit threat model: modality, attacker knowledge, perturbation norm/budget, poisoning budget, protected classes, and expected deployment transformations such as quantization. Inspect training configs for PGD adversarial training, [TRADES](https://arxiv.org/abs/1901.08573), adversarial weight perturbation, randomized smoothing or certified training where appropriate, plus data augmentation strategies such as MixUp/CutMix and perturbed-input tests. For implementation evidence, use [IBM ART 1.17](https://adversarial-robustness-toolbox.readthedocs.io/en/latest/modules/defences/trainer.html) trainers and poison detectors, [Foolbox 3.3](https://foolbox.readthedocs.io/en/stable/), [AutoAttack](https://github.com/fra31/auto-attack), and [RobustBench](https://robustbench.github.io/) rather than only bespoke notebooks. For clean-label poisoning, verify deep k-NN filtering, input purification, or [Deep Partition Aggregation](https://openreview.net/forum?id=YUGG2tFuPM) style controls on canary poison sets, with documented k values, class-imbalance handling, acceptance thresholds, and false-positive review. Vision pipelines should look at [PureVQ-GAN (arXiv:2509.25792)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.25792) as a fast purification option — 0% poison success against Gradient Matching and Bullseye Polytope on CIFAR-10 with ~91–95% clean accuracy and ~50× the throughput of diffusion-based purifiers — but require the team to re-evaluate against an adaptive attacker rather than rely on author-reported numbers. For certified clean-label guarantees, [Hong, Carlini & Kurakin's *Certified Robustness to Clean-Label Poisoning Using Diffusion Denoising* (arXiv:2403.11981)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.11981) reports attack success driven down to 0–16% across seven clean-label attacks with negligible clean-accuracy loss by sanitizing training data through an off-the-shelf diffusion denoiser inspired by randomized smoothing. [ECLIPSE (ESORICS 2024, arXiv:2406.15093)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.15093) targets clean-label *indiscriminate* poisons specifically and uses sparse Gaussian-plus-diffusion purification to assimilate the perturbation, reporting a ~38% improvement over prior defenses on CIFAR-10 / ResNet18 — useful when the attacker's goal is broad generalization degradation rather than a single target trigger. [FLORAL (ICLR 2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.17121) is worth tracking for label-poisoning-specific adversarial training, but treat it as a research signal unless it matches the deployment model class. | The control is expensive and easy to overclaim. Adversarial training can reduce robustness to attacks outside the selected norm, lower clean accuracy, and materially increase training cost. AutoAttack/RobustBench are strong empirical checks for vision-style perturbation robustness, not proof of LLM or tabular-model safety. Deep k-NN and DPA evidence is strongest for image/classification settings; adaptive clean-label attacks and triggerless code or text poisoning remain much less mature. Input purification can become security theater unless evaluated with adaptive attacks. Auditors should require pre/post-defense attack success rates, robust accuracy, clean accuracy, poison-detection precision/recall, and a rationale for models where the risk assessment chooses monitoring and rollback over hardening. |
| **1.4.7** | **Verify that** defenses against clean-label poisoning attacks (e.g., input purification, k-NN filtering, data partitioning and aggregation) are implemented for models exposed to untrusted or partially trusted training data sources. | 3 | Clean-label poisoning: correctly labeled, minimally perturbed samples that pass every label-quality and schema gate because the labels are genuinely right (MITRE ATLAS [AML.T0020](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0020)). Canonical attack families: feature-collision (Poison Frogs), [Bullseye Polytope (arXiv:2005.00191)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.00191), Gradient Matching / Witches' Brew, Sleeper Agent, and Narcissus. [Carlini et al. (arXiv:2302.10149)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.10149) showed the delivery path is cheap for any pipeline that scrapes the web, and [*Sequential Data Poisoning in LLM Post-Training* (arXiv:2606.04929, Jun 2026)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04929) extends the threat to multi-stage pipelines: adversaries who split a poison budget across SFT and DPO/PPO stages each look negligible in isolation but succeed in combination. | Scope first: identify which models ingest untrusted or partially trusted sources (web scrapes, user submissions, third-party datasets, model-generated data) — those are the ones this Level 3 control applies to. Then confirm at least one dedicated clean-label layer per such model, from three families. **Filtering:** [Deep k-NN defense (arXiv:1909.13374)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.13374) flags samples whose label disagrees with the mode of their k nearest neighbors in feature space; detected >99% of feature-collision and convex-polytope poisons in the authors' evaluation. [FLARE (IEEE TIFS, arXiv:2411.19479)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.19479) clusters aggregated all-layer activations and was evaluated against 22 backdoor attack variants including adaptive ones. **Purification:** [Friendly Noise (NeurIPS 2022, arXiv:2208.10224)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.10224) ([code](https://github.com/tianyu139/friendly-noise)) breaks Gradient Matching, Bullseye Polytope, and Sleeper Agent with minimal accuracy loss; [Hong–Carlini–Kurakin certified diffusion denoising (arXiv:2403.11981)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.11981) drives attack success to 0–16% across seven clean-label attacks with formal guarantees; [PureVQ-GAN (arXiv:2509.25792)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.25792) and [ECLIPSE (arXiv:2406.15093)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.15093) cover targeted and indiscriminate variants respectively; [Mendata (arXiv:2312.01281)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.01281) re-distributes untrusted inputs toward a trusted reference set. **Partition aggregation:** [Deep Partition Aggregation (arXiv:2006.14768)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.14768) ([reference code](https://github.com/alevine0/DPA)) gives certified bounds on tolerated insertions/deletions; [Finite Aggregation (ICML 2022, arXiv:2202.02628)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.02628) and [Run-Off Election (ICML 2023, arXiv:2302.02300)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.02300) improve certified accuracy by up to 12–27% on boosted DPA; [arXiv:2306.16415](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.16415) covers the practical scaling trade-offs (base-model count vs. accuracy vs. cost). Audit evidence: canary runs with known clean-label poison sets (Gradient Matching, Bullseye Polytope, Narcissus), documented k values / partition counts / certified radii, poison-detection precision-recall, and re-evaluation under an adaptive attacker rather than author-reported numbers alone. | Nearly all published evidence is image-classification on CIFAR-scale data. For LLM fine-tuning and multi-modal pipelines there is no certified clean-label defense, and filtering/purification evidence is thin — the honest posture there is source vetting (C6.3), dataset fingerprinting (1.4.2), canary-based behavioral testing, and staged rollout rather than a claimed clean-label guarantee. Aggregation defenses cost real accuracy and multiply training compute by the partition count, which is why this is Level 3. Certified bounds cover bounded insertion/deletion counts, not the fixed-250-document LLM regime. Quantization can break detection layers downstream ([arXiv:2512.06243](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.06243)), and per-stage screening underestimates compound risk from sequential multi-stage poisoning ([arXiv:2606.04929](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04929)). Purification layers that are never re-tested against adaptive attacks degrade into security theater. |

## Recent Research and Incident Context (2025-2026)

- **Feb 2023 / 2024 — Carlini et al.** [*Poisoning Web-Scale Training Datasets is Practical* (arXiv:2302.10149)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.10149). Introduced *split-view* and *frontrunning* attacks on URL-based web-scale corpora such as LAION-400M and COYO-700M, showing that an adversary could have poisoned 0.01% of the datasets for roughly $60. Foundational reference for why dataset fingerprints (1.4.2) and lineage controls (C1.5) are now mandatory and not optional.
- **Oct 2024 — Rando & Tramèr (ETH Zurich SPYLab, ICLR 2025).** [*Persistent Pre-Training Poisoning of LLMs* (arXiv:2410.13722)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.13722). 0.1% poison during pre-training survives full SFT + DPO alignment up to 7B parameters across denial-of-service, jailbreak, prompt-stealing, and belief-manipulation objectives; even 0.001% produces measurable belief manipulation. Wikipedia alone can plausibly cover 0.27% of OLMo-style pretraining corpora, well above this threshold. Reinforces the case for treating poisoning as a *pre-training* concern, not just a fine-tuning one.
- **Oct 2025 — Anthropic/UK AISI/Turing.** [*Poisoning Attacks on LLMs Require a Near-constant Number of Poison Samples*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07192). 250-document threshold across 600M–13B parameter models. Resets the defender's math from percentages to absolute counts.
- **Jan 2025 — *Nature Medicine*.** [Medical LLMs vulnerable to data poisoning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-024-03445-1). 0.001% token replacement produces measurably harmful completions while evading standard medical QA benchmarks. A [PMC case study in breast-cancer clinical LLMs](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10984073/) extended the finding to specialty models.
- **May 2025 — Trail of Bits.** [Datasig release](https://blog.trailofbits.com/2025/05/02/datasig-fingerprinting-ai/ml-datasets-to-stop-data-borne-attacks/). MinHash-based dataset fingerprinting specifically designed for AIBOM / supply-chain consumption. [Source repo](https://github.com/trailofbits/datasig).
- **ICML 2025 — PoisonBench.** [Poisoned preference data benchmark](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v267/fu25e.html) across 22 models, two attack types, and eight scenarios. Larger models are not consistently more resilient, attack effects scale with poison ratio, and poisoned behavior can generalize to unseen triggers.
- **Aug 2025.** Triggerless code-generation poisoning study — spectral signatures, activation clustering, and static analysis all failed on AST-normalized attacks; [CodeScan (Mar 2026, arXiv:2603.17174)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.17174) reported ~97% detection for this one attack class and is currently the only defense with published numbers.
- **NeurIPS 2025 — Virus Infection Attack.** [arXiv:2509.23041](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041). Poisoning propagates through synthetic-data generation pipelines; organizations that generate training data from other models inherit and amplify upstream contamination.
- **Dec 2025 — Quantization Blindspots.** [arXiv:2512.06243](https://www.arxiv.org/pdf/2512.06243). INT8 quantization drops classic backdoor defenses (Activation Clustering, STRIP, Spectral Signatures, Fine-Pruning) to 0% detection while the backdoor remains fully functional. Only Neural Cleanse survived, and only at full precision. Changes the risk picture for edge deployments that quantize after training.
- **Jan 2026 — Cleanlab 2.9.** Latest open-source release at the time of this review; useful for label errors, outliers, duplicates, multi-annotator quality, and OOD screening ([project page](https://github.com/cleanlab/cleanlab)).
- **ICLR 2025 — FLORAL.** [Adversarial Training for Defense Against Label Poisoning Attacks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.17121) frames label poisoning as a Stackelberg-style training problem and reports stronger robust accuracy than several baselines under increasing attacker budgets. The work is promising for classical classification settings, but not a drop-in defense for LLM fine-tuning or multi-modal pipelines.
- **Robustness evaluation baseline.** [AutoAttack](https://github.com/fra31/auto-attack) remains a practical empirical check for perturbation robustness, while [RobustBench](https://robustbench.github.io/) gives a maintained benchmark and model zoo. These tools help catch weak or gradient-masking defenses, but they do not certify poisoning resistance.
- **Apr 2026 — NIST critical-infrastructure profile concept note.** NIST published a [concept note for an AI RMF Profile on Trustworthy AI in Critical Infrastructure](https://www.nist.gov/programs-projects/concept-note-ai-rmf-profile-trustworthy-ai-critical-infrastructure), a useful signal for organizations that treat data quality checks as operational resilience controls rather than one-off model-development tasks. NIST is opening a Community of Interest, so quality-gate requirements for high-impact pipelines are still in flux.
- **Feb 2026 — MITRE ATLAS v5.4.0.** The release added [AML.T0104 Publish Poisoned AI Agent Tool](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0104) and additional agent-focused techniques. Training-data quality programs that previously scoped only ingest-time content should extend the threat model to tool, plugin, and agent-skill registries.
- **AAAI 2026 — ConfGuard.** [arXiv:2508.01365](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.01365). Detects backdoored generations by spotting "sequence lock" — abnormally high and consistent token confidence across a sliding window. Reports near-100% TPR and negligible FPR with almost no inference latency, useful as a runtime tripwire that complements offline data screening.
- **Sep 2025 — PureVQ-GAN.** [arXiv:2509.25792](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.25792). Vector-quantized bottleneck purification for image data. 0% poison success against Gradient Matching and Bullseye Polytope and 1.64% against Narcissus on CIFAR-10, ~91–95% clean accuracy, and ~50× the throughput of diffusion-based purifiers. Vision-only; no text or code numbers yet.
- **Mar 2024 — Hong, Carlini & Kurakin.** [*Certified Robustness to Clean-Label Poisoning Using Diffusion Denoising* (arXiv:2403.11981)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.11981). Off-the-shelf diffusion denoiser used as a randomized-smoothing-style sanitizer. Drops attack success to 0–16% across seven clean-label poisoning attacks with negligible clean-accuracy loss; one of the few defenses with formal certified guarantees rather than empirical-only numbers.
- **ESORICS 2024 — ECLIPSE.** [*Expunging Clean-label Indiscriminate Poisons via Sparse Diffusion Purification* (arXiv:2406.15093)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.15093). Aimed at indiscriminate availability-degrading poisons (the inverse of targeted backdoors). Combines theoretical analysis of Gaussian-noise absorption with sparse diffusion purification and reports ~38% improvement over prior baselines on CIFAR-10/ResNet18.
- **Mar 2026 — LiteLLM supply-chain compromise.** [Trend Micro analysis](https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/26/c/inside-litellm-supply-chain-compromise.html) and [TechCrunch coverage of the Mercor breach](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/31/mercor-says-it-was-hit-by-cyberattack-tied-to-compromise-of-open-source-litellm-project/). Two backdoored PyPI releases of LiteLLM (1.82.7 / 1.82.8) harvested credentials and installed persistent backdoors across thousands of AI stacks. Primarily a supply-chain story (see C06), but a useful reminder that the data ingest and labeling pipelines covered by this section often run on top of compromisable middleware — a poisoned proxy can rewrite labels, drop integrity checks, or quietly mirror datasets to attacker-controlled stores.
- **Jun 2026 — Sequential Data Poisoning in LLM Post-Training.** [arXiv:2606.04929](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04929). Models the realistic case where different adversaries poison different post-training stages. In SFT→DPO pipelines the contributions are additive (splitting a poison budget across stages beats concentrating it); in SFT→PPO they are complementary (neither stage succeeds alone, the combination does). The authors call the resulting blind spot the "single-attacker illusion": evaluating each stage's data screening in isolation systematically underestimates compound vulnerability. Directly relevant to how 1.4.2/1.4.7 audits should be scoped — per-stage gates are necessary but not sufficient.
- **TIFS 2025 — FLARE.** [*Universal Dataset Purification against Backdoor Attacks* (arXiv:2411.19479)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.19479). Aggregates abnormal activations across *all* hidden layers (not just the final layer, where separability varies by attack) and clusters the dataset, treating the more stable cluster as poisoned. Evaluated against 22 backdoor attack variants including all-to-all, untargeted, and adaptive attacks; implementations ship in the BackdoorBox and backdoor-toolbox repositories.
- **Clean-label defense lineage (for 1.4.7).** [Deep k-NN filtering (arXiv:1909.13374)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.13374) → [Deep Partition Aggregation (arXiv:2006.14768)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.14768) → [Finite Aggregation (ICML 2022, arXiv:2202.02628)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.02628) → [Run-Off Election (ICML 2023, arXiv:2302.02300)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.02300), with [Friendly Noise (NeurIPS 2022, arXiv:2208.10224)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.10224) and [Mendata (arXiv:2312.01281)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.01281) on the purification side and [arXiv:2306.16415](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.16415) documenting the practical cost/accuracy trade-offs of aggregation at scale. Worth knowing the lineage because vendors routinely cite the 2020-era numbers without the later corrections.
- **ReSAISE 2025 — Stealthy Code-Gen Poisoning.** [arXiv:2508.21636](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.21636). Independent evaluation of spectral signatures, activation clustering, and static analysis on CodeBERT, CodeT5+, and AST-T5 found all three classes struggle against triggerless code-generation poisoning. Reinforces the case for CodeScan-style AST-normalized scanning plus end-to-end behavioral testing.
- **EU AI Act high-risk enforcement milestone.** High-risk AI systems must demonstrate documented data governance, bias measurement, traceability, and human oversight under the [EU AI Act high-risk obligations](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai) — originally due 2 August 2026, deferred to 2 December 2027 by the May 2026 Digital Omnibus (AI embedded in Annex I regulated products: 2 August 2028) — backed by penalties up to €35M or 7% of global turnover for the most serious violations and up to €15M or 3% for non-compliance with high-risk obligations. Training-data quality controls are now audit targets, not internal hygiene. See the [Cloud Security Alliance mapping of ISO/IEC 42001 and NIST AI RMF onto the EU AI Act](https://cloudsecurityalliance.org/blog/2025/01/29/how-can-iso-iec-42001-nist-ai-rmf-help-comply-with-the-eu-ai-act).
- **May 2025 — CISA/NSA/FBI joint guidance.** The [AI Data Security CSI](https://media.defense.gov/2025/May/22/2003720601/-1/-1/0/CSI_AI_DATA_SECURITY.PDF) (rolled into [CISA's resource page](https://www.cisa.gov/resources-tools/resources/ai-data-security-best-practices-securing-data-used-train-operate-ai-systems)) directs operators to track data provenance, verify integrity with checksums and cryptographic hashes during storage and transport, and use quantum-resistant digital signatures to authenticate trusted revisions across training, fine-tuning, and alignment.

| Adversarial robustness | PGD-AT, TRADES, AWP, IBM ART trainers, Foolbox, AutoAttack, RobustBench | Bounded perturbation/evasion attacks. Expensive, threat-model-specific, and not a substitute for poisoning detection. |
| Clean-label / poisoning hardening | Deep k-NN filtering, Friendly Noise and Mendata purification, FLARE all-layer activation clustering, ART poison detectors, Deep Partition Aggregation + Finite Aggregation + Run-Off Election (certified), FLORAL-style label-poisoning training, PureVQ-GAN bottleneck purification (vision), Hong-Carlini-Kurakin certified diffusion denoising, ECLIPSE sparse diffusion purification | Targeted clean-label, indiscriminate clean-label, and label-flip poisoning in classification settings. | Security-exploitable bias | Fairlearn, AI Fairness 360, Giskard, Promptfoo, adversarial red-team probing | Measurement + exploit probing. Automated probe-and-replay tools exist; weaponization analysis still human-led. |

## Audit Evidence Checklist

For this section, a paper control is not enough. Ask for artifacts that prove the quality gates run at the right points in the pipeline:

- Ingest DAG screenshots or configuration showing schema, null, type, and label-distribution checks before data lands in the training set.
- Current expectation suites, Pandera schemas, TFX schemas, or equivalent contracts, with owners and review history.
- Alert records showing failed data-quality checks, who approved any override, and whether downstream training was blocked.
- Baseline label distributions and embedding/outlier thresholds stored with dataset versions, not just in a dashboard.
- Canary poisoned samples or synthetic corruption tests that exercise poisoning detectors without entering production data.
- Auto-labeling confidence thresholds, ensemble-disagreement rules, and human-review sampling rates.
- Dataset fingerprints, checksums, or AIBOM entries tying each model version back to the exact dataset snapshot used.
- Robustness evaluation runs showing attack budget, norm, seeds, AutoAttack/Foolbox/ART configuration, clean accuracy, robust accuracy, and attack success rate before and after hardening.
- Clean-label poison canary results showing k-NN/filtering thresholds, DPA certificate or ensemble settings, poison-detection precision/recall, and manual review outcomes for quarantined samples.
- Bias and subgroup-evasion test reports for security-critical models, including the red-team prompts or samples used to probe bypass patterns.
