# C11.3: Model-Extraction Defense

> **Chapter:** [C11 Adversarial Robustness & Attack Resistance](C11-Adversarial-Robustness.md)
> **Requirements:** 4 | **IDs:** 11.3.1--11.3.4

## Purpose

Detect and deter unauthorized model cloning through API abuse. Model extraction (model stealing) attacks create a functionally equivalent copy of a target model by querying it systematically and training a surrogate on the input-output pairs. Successful extraction compromises intellectual property, enables white-box adversarial attacks against the original model, and bypasses access controls. The primary defenses are rate limiting, query-pattern analysis, and post-hoc identification via watermarking.

## Recent Research (2024--2026)

### Expanded LLM Extraction Threat Model

A comprehensive survey on model extraction attacks and defenses for LLMs (June 2025) identifies three primary attack categories beyond traditional functionality extraction: **training data extraction** (exploiting memorization to recover sensitive training examples via crafted prompts), **prompt-targeted attacks** (stealing system prompts, few-shot examples, and other prompt IP), and **parameter recovery** (reverse-engineering internal weights). This expanded threat model means that rate limiting and query-pattern analysis (requirement 11.3.1) must account for all three attack vectors, not just functionality cloning.

### Adaptive Watermarking: ModelShield

**ModelShield** (IEEE TIFS, January 2025) introduces adaptive and robust watermarking that dynamically adjusts protection strength based on query patterns. Unlike static watermarking schemes that can be characterized and removed, ModelShield strategically perturbs responses while maintaining high utility for legitimate users. This addresses a key gap in requirement 11.3.3: static watermarks are increasingly vulnerable to removal through fine-tuning and pruning, while adaptive approaches resist these attacks more effectively.

### Explanation-Based Watermarking (EaaW)

**Explanation as a Watermark (EaaW)** (2024) introduces a novel paradigm that embeds multi-bit watermarks into feature attribution explanations rather than model predictions. This means verification behaviors are implanted in the explanation layer rather than the output layer, making the watermark invisible to standard extraction pipelines that only replicate input-output mappings. EaaW supports multi-bit encoding, enabling richer ownership claims than single-bit backdoor watermarks.

### Watermark Vulnerability Research

USENIX Security 2024 research ("How to Break White-Box DNN-Watermarking Schemes") systematically demonstrated attacks against existing white-box watermarking schemes, showing that many can be removed or forged. This directly validates requirement 11.3.3's emphasis on protecting watermark verification keys and trigger sets -- if an attacker gains access to verification materials, watermark schemes are fundamentally compromised. Organizations implementing watermarking should assume adversaries will attempt both extraction-then-removal and direct watermark attacks.

### Semantically Conditioned Watermarks for LLMs

A May 2025 paper introduces **Semantically Conditioned Watermarks (SCW)**, a paradigm shift in LLM fingerprinting. Instead of teaching a model to memorize fixed query-key pairs (which break under fine-tuning and quantization), SCW uses an entire semantic domain (e.g., a specific language or topic area) as the trigger. The model diffuses a statistical watermarking signal throughout all responses generated from that domain. This makes the fingerprint both stealthier and more robust — it survives standard deployment modifications that compromise traditional approaches. Model owners verify ownership by querying the predetermined semantic domain and detecting the watermark statistically.

### FingerMarks: Multi-Bit Edge Model Watermarking

**FingerMarks** (MDPI Electronics, 2025) addresses a gap that earlier edge-deployment protections (TransLinkGuard, CoreGuard) left open: combining model attribution with user traceability. FingerMarks embeds multi-bit watermarks using a progressive adversarial training strategy, enabling organizations to identify both which model was stolen and which edge node user was responsible. Evaluation across multiple datasets confirms robustness against common removal attacks.

### Edge Deployment Protection

Earlier work on **TransLinkGuard** and **CoreGuard** addresses model extraction defense for edge-deployed models where rate limiting and API-level defenses are not available. These techniques embed architectural-level watermarks that persist even when models are deployed on untrusted hardware, extending the protection scope beyond API-served models.

### Operation Bizarre Bazaar: Real-World LLMjacking at Scale

In December 2025–January 2026, Pillar Security documented **Operation Bizarre Bazaar**, the first publicly attributed systematic campaign targeting exposed LLM and MCP endpoints. Across 35,000 attack sessions averaging 972 daily attempts, attackers scanned for Ollama (port 11434) and OpenAI-compatible APIs (port 8000), validated access with placeholder API keys, then resold access through silver.inc — a marketplace offering discounted access to 30+ LLM providers without authorization. The threat actor ("Hecker") operated from Netherlands-based bulletproof hosting. This incident validates that model extraction and API abuse are not theoretical — unprotected inference endpoints are actively hunted and monetized.

### Falling Inference Costs and Extraction Economics

As of early 2026, LLM inference costs have dropped approximately 50x per year (per Epoch AI analysis), with providers like DeepSeek offering processing at $0.70 per million tokens total. This cost collapse changes the extraction threat model: surrogate training becomes cheaper, making even large-scale extraction economically rational for attackers who previously would have trained from scratch. Rate limits and query-pattern detection (11.3.1) become more important as the economic barrier to extraction falls.

### Defense Taxonomy

The 2025 LLM extraction survey organizes defenses into three categories that map well to AISVS requirements:
1. **Model Protection** (architectural defenses, output control) -- maps to requirements 11.3.1, 11.3.2, and 11.3.3
2. **Data Privacy Protection** (preventing training data extraction, output sanitization) -- connects to C11.2 and C11.4
3. **Prompt Protection** (watermarking prompts, query monitoring for prompt theft) -- an emerging concern not yet explicitly covered in the current requirements

### ModelGuard: Information-Theoretic Output Perturbation

**ModelGuard** (USENIX Security 2024) introduces a principled approach to output perturbation that goes beyond ad-hoc noise injection. Rather than simply adding random noise to model predictions, ModelGuard formulates extraction defense as an optimization problem that minimizes information leakage to attackers while maximizing utility for legitimate queries. The framework resists adaptive attackers who adjust their strategies based on observed perturbations — a critical advancement since naive perturbation schemes can be reverse-engineered. ModelGuard supports multiple defense modes (perturbation-based and quantization-based) and has been evaluated against six different extraction attack strategies including KnockoffNet and JBDA-TR across eight datasets. An open-source implementation is available on GitHub. This directly strengthens requirement 11.3.1 by providing a concrete, implementable output-level defense.

### API Gateway Evidence: Counting Dimensions and Actions

As of May 2026, rate-limit evidence should show more than the presence of a generic throttle. Auditors should ask what identity and traffic attributes the gateway counts on: API key, organization, authenticated session, model endpoint, batch job, SDK client, source ASN, bot or TLS fingerprint, request body operation, response status, and estimated request cost. This matters because coordinated distillation campaigns can rotate IPs, split traffic across accounts, and mix extraction traffic with ordinary usage.

Cloudflare's Advanced Rate Limiting documentation gives a useful evidence pattern: authenticated API traffic can be counted by session identifiers carried in cookies, headers, or query values rather than by IP alone; traffic can be bucketed using attributes such as ASN or JA3; and enforcement can issue challenges, custom responses, timeouts, or blocks. For 11.3.1 and 11.3.4 reviews, the practical test is whether extraction-specific detector signals can drive those gateway actions and whether the rule history shows decisions by principal, model, endpoint, and policy reason.

### DistillGuard: Task-Specific Limits of Output Defenses

**DistillGuard** (arXiv, March 2026) evaluates output-level defenses against LLM knowledge distillation with a standardized teacher/student pipeline using Qwen3-14B as teacher and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct as student across MATH-500, HumanEval+, and MT-Bench. The findings are useful for auditors because they separate "plausible" controls from controls that actually reduce student-model quality: paraphrasing perturbation barely degrades a distilled model in same-family settings, data poisoning mostly damages conversational fluency, and chain-of-thought removal substantially reduces math performance while leaving code generation much less affected. For 11.3.2 and 11.3.4, this means output minimization and adaptive degradation should be evaluated per capability family rather than accepted as generic model-theft protection.

### Dynamic Neural Fortresses: Architectural Extraction Defense

**Dynamic Neural Fortresses (DNF)** (ICLR 2025) represents the first application of early-exit neural networks to model extraction defense. DNF dynamically routes queries through the network: benign queries proceed to later layers for full-fidelity predictions, while suspected extraction queries are diverted to early-exit points that return deliberately degraded responses. This architectural approach achieves a 2x improvement in computational efficiency (since attack queries consume fewer resources) and reduces clone model accuracy by up to 12% compared to prior state-of-the-art defenses. DNF also provides protection against neural architecture theft — an attack vector where adversaries attempt to reverse-engineer the target model's structure, not just its behavior. This is notable because most existing defenses focus solely on functionality extraction.

### GreyNoise Intelligence: Systematic LLM Infrastructure Targeting

Between October 2025 and January 2026, GreyNoise's Ollama honeypot infrastructure captured **91,403 attack sessions** revealing two distinct campaigns targeting AI infrastructure. The first campaign exploited SSRF vulnerabilities in Ollama's model pull functionality, peaking at 1,688 sessions in 48 hours over Christmas 2025. A single JA4H fingerprint appeared in 99% of traffic, suggesting shared automation tooling (likely Nuclei). The second campaign, starting December 28, 2025, was a methodical LLM reconnaissance operation: two IPs generated 80,469 enumeration requests across 73+ model endpoints in eleven days, probing for OpenAI, Anthropic, Meta, DeepSeek, Google, Mistral, Alibaba, and xAI models. Together with Operation Bizarre Bazaar, this data confirms that exposed inference endpoints face sustained, professional-grade scanning — organizations should treat unprotected model endpoints as equivalent to exposed databases.

### Practical Extraction Economics: Low-Cost Demonstrations

A January 2026 Praetorian demonstration quantified just how accessible model extraction has become: using standard knowledge distillation via PyTorch, they achieved 80.1% accuracy and 80.8% prediction agreement on a Fashion-MNIST model after only 1,000 API queries. Simple categories like trousers and sneakers reached over 90% agreement. Separately, Model Leeching research extracted 73% similarity from ChatGPT-3.5-Turbo for approximately $50 in API costs over 48 hours. These results confirm that extraction is no longer a theoretical concern requiring massive resources — it is a practical attack within reach of individual actors with modest budgets.

### EU AI Act: Regulatory Dimensions of Model Protection

As of May 2026, the EU AI Act's obligations for general-purpose AI (GPAI) model providers have been in effect since August 2025, with AI Office enforcement for new GPAI models scheduled for August 2026. The final General-Purpose AI Code of Practice was published on July 10, 2025 and confirmed by the Commission and AI Board as an adequate voluntary tool for demonstrating compliance. Its Safety and Security chapter is directly relevant to extraction defense: systemic-risk model providers commit to cybersecurity protection for models and physical infrastructure against unauthorized releases, unauthorized access, and model theft. Auditors should map 11.3 controls to the provider's safety and security framework, systemic-risk scenarios, post-market monitoring, incident reporting, and model reports instead of treating extraction defense as a purely technical API-gateway issue.

### EU AI Act Article 50: Marking and Labelling

The European Commission published the **final Code of Practice on Marking and Labelling of AI-generated Content on June 10, 2026**, closing the drafting process that ran through first (December 2025) and second (March 5, 2026) drafts. The final code is separate from the GPAI Code and focuses on Article 50 transparency duties. It is organized in two sections — providers commit to marking and detection of AI-generated and manipulated content (machine-readable marks that are "effective, interoperable, robust, and reliable as far as technically feasible"), while deployers commit to labelling deepfakes and AI-generated text — retaining the two-layered secured-metadata-plus-watermarking approach from the drafts, with optional fingerprinting/logging and detection protocols. The code is now undergoing an adequacy assessment by the Commission and AI Board, and the Commission released accompanying labelling icons for deployers. Article 50 becomes applicable on August 2, 2026, so signatories have a narrow window to operationalize the commitments. This matters for 11.3.3 because content provenance marks can complement model-ownership watermarking, but they do not prove that a deployed model was stolen; verification still needs model-behavior fingerprints, controlled trigger sets, contractual evidence, and investigation telemetry.

### Watermarking Adoption Gap in Practice

Despite regulatory momentum, a March 2026 study analyzing 50 popular AI image generation systems found that only 38% implement any form of machine-readable watermark, with just 8 systems using robust invisible watermarking. Visible disclosures appear in only 18% of systems. Adoption is heavily concentrated among well-funded end-to-end platforms (Google, Adobe, OpenAI) — smaller providers and those using APIs from upstream model developers rarely maintain watermark protections. Even when base model developers embed watermarks, downstream API consumers often fail to preserve them through their pipeline. Only 3 of the 40 analyzed systems originate from EU countries, raising questions about enforcement reach. This gap between regulatory expectation and industry reality means that requirement 11.3.3 verification must account for supply-chain watermark integrity — confirming that watermarks survive the full deployment pipeline, not just that the base model supports them.

### Adversarial Robustness of LLM Fingerprints

A September 2025 paper by Nasery et al. ("Are Robust LLM Fingerprints Adversarially Robust?") exposed a fundamental weakness in the current generation of model fingerprinting: while these schemes are designed to survive benign modifications like fine-tuning and quantization, they fail catastrophically against adaptive adversaries. The researchers tested ten fingerprinting methods — including Chain-and-Hash, FPEdit, Implicit Fingerprints, EditMF, Perinucleus, Instructional FP, MergePrint, RoFL, ProFLingo, and Domain Specific Watermarks — and achieved 94–100% attack success rates against memorization-based schemes and 100% against GCG-based intrinsic fingerprints using simple perplexity filtering. Even statistical watermarks were compromised at 65% success while retaining 90% model utility. The attacks exploit three weaknesses: overconfidence in memorized responses (detectable and suppressible), unnatural query patterns (filterable by perplexity thresholds), and learnable statistical signatures (stealable via bigram analysis). This directly challenges the assumptions behind requirement 11.3.3 — organizations should treat current fingerprinting as a deterrent rather than a reliable ownership proof, and evaluate hybrid approaches combining multiple verification strategies.

### ATOM: RL-Based Extraction Detection for MLaaS

**ATOM** (KDD 2025) introduces a real-time extraction detection framework for graph-based Machine Learning as a Service platforms. Unlike static detectors like PRADA, ATOM uses reinforcement learning with Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) to dynamically adapt its detection policy as attackers refine their query strategies. The framework's differential query feature encoding mechanism tracks changes across consecutive interactions, while k-core embedding captures structural properties of query graphs. While designed for GNNs rather than LLMs, the architectural approach — combining sequential modeling with adaptive RL-based detection — is transferable to any API-served model and represents the direction extraction detection is heading. Source code is available at https://github.com/LabRAI/ATOM.

### Embedding Service Protection: GuardEmb and SemMark

As embedding-as-a-service (EaaS) becomes a major deployment model, two complementary watermarking techniques have emerged. **GuardEmb** (EMNLP Findings 2024) selects special tokens and perturbs their embeddings to inject watermarks, while simultaneously training a verifier to detect them. When an attacker trains a surrogate on stolen embeddings, the watermark transfers to the clone, and verification is conducted by querying the suspect service with constructed test texts. **SemMark** (2025) takes a more sophisticated approach: it partitions embedding space using locality-sensitive hashing to identify semantic regions, then injects adaptive watermarks with weights determined by local outlier factor analysis. SemMark achieves verification p-values below 10⁻¹⁰ even under clustering-based, dimensionality-reduction, and detect-sampling attacks, while maintaining 94%+ similarity to original embeddings. These techniques fill a gap left by requirement 11.3.3, which focuses on model outputs rather than embedding endpoints.

### LiteLLM Supply Chain Compromise (March 2026)

On March 24, 2026, the widely used LiteLLM Python package (3.4 million daily PyPI downloads) was compromised when threat actor TeamPCP exfiltrated PyPI publishing credentials from the project's CI/CD pipeline via a poisoned Trivy GitHub Action. The backdoored versions (1.82.7 and 1.82.8) harvested SSH keys, cloud tokens, Kubernetes secrets, and .env files, then attempted lateral movement across Kubernetes clusters. While primarily a supply-chain attack (see C06), the incident is directly relevant to extraction defense: LiteLLM serves as a unified proxy for 100+ LLM providers, meaning a compromised proxy could silently intercept and exfiltrate all inference traffic — effectively enabling model extraction without querying the model API at all. The attack was live for approximately three hours before PyPI quarantine. This underscores that extraction defense must extend beyond API-level controls to include supply-chain integrity of inference infrastructure.

### Distributed Computing and Cross-Environment Extraction Threats

A February 2025 survey on model extraction in distributed computing environments (arXiv:2502.16065) highlights that extraction threats vary significantly by deployment context. Cloud-hosted models face API query exploitation with rich confidence score outputs. Edge-deployed models face physical side-channel attacks (power analysis, electromagnetic emanations). Federated learning models face gradient leakage during iterative training rounds. The survey identifies emerging cross-environment defenses including fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) for encrypted inference, hardware-assisted security features (secure enclaves, TPMs), and differential privacy frameworks with formal privacy budgets. As of March 2026, organizations deploying models across hybrid cloud-edge environments should evaluate environment-specific extraction risks rather than applying a one-size-fits-all defense.

### MITRE ATLAS and SAFE-AI Framework Alignment

As of February 2026, MITRE ATLAS has expanded to v5.4.0 with 16 tactics, 84 techniques, and 56 sub-techniques — a significant growth from the October 2025 baseline (15 tactics, 66 techniques). The October 2025 update added 14 agent-focused techniques in collaboration with Zenity Labs, followed by the February 2026 release adding techniques for AI agent exploitation including "Publish Poisoned AI Agent Tool" (AML.T0104), "Escape to Host" (AML.T0105), and "AI Agent" (AML.T0108). MITRE's **SAFE-AI** framework provides organizational controls mapped to the ATLAS taxonomy, including specific guidance on model extraction defense through output anomaly monitoring and access restriction to training environments. Model extraction falls under AML.T0024 with sub-techniques for training data membership inference (AML.T0024.000) and model inversion/extraction (AML.T0024.001).

On May 6, 2026, MITRE's Center for Threat-Informed Defense announced the conclusion of the Secure AI project's initial phase with the April 2026 ATLAS updates: 45+ new techniques and sub-techniques, 10+ new mitigations, 20+ new case studies, and a Technique Maturity filter that lets defenders distinguish emerging from mature threats. ATLAS has shifted to a monthly release cadence with a Rapid Response Report process for emerging AI security incidents, an interactive Knowledge Graph, and threat-emulation playbooks (including Ansible-based scenarios) with planned MITRE Caldera integration during 2026. For 11.3 audits, the practical change is that extraction-defense mappings now need re-validation on a monthly cycle rather than against an annual taxonomy snapshot, and the maturity filter helps justify which extraction techniques warrant production-grade controls versus monitoring-only treatment.

### Edinburgh Study: Fingerprint Removal at Scale (IEEE SaTML 2026)

A March 2026 University of Edinburgh study — the largest independent evaluation of AI fingerprinting to date — tested 14 detection methods across 12 generative models and found that fingerprints can be eliminated with striking effectiveness: over 80% success rate with full-access attacks, and over 50% success using simple techniques like JPEG compression or image resizing. Critically, fingerprints could not only be removed but **forged to falsely attribute content to a generator that never produced it**. The peer-reviewed findings, presented at IEEE SaTML 2026, establish that fingerprint-based detection represents a "structural weakness" rather than a fixable software flaw — none of the 14 techniques examined guarantees both high accuracy and robustness across all scenarios. This complements the Nasery et al. (Sept 2025) findings on LLM fingerprint bypass and further challenges the reliability assumptions behind requirement 11.3.3. Together, these results suggest that watermarking should be treated as one layer in a defense-in-depth strategy rather than a standalone ownership proof.

### ProbeSpec: Behavioral Fingerprinting via Perturbation Response (Feb 2026)

**ProbeSpec** (MDPI Electronics, February 2026) introduces a novel black-box model fingerprinting approach based on dynamic perturbation response spectrum analysis. Rather than relying on memorized query-key pairs (which the Nasery et al. and Edinburgh studies have shown to be vulnerable), ProbeSpec captures a model's unique "behavioral spectrum" — the response patterns under multi-level perturbations that originate from implicit decision mechanisms learned during training. The method uses three probe types (Gabor textures, boundary samples, and cross-class mixup samples), applies multi-level perturbations to construct dynamic trajectory response matrices, and extracts spectral fingerprints via 2D-DCT compression. ProbeSpec identifies stolen models with only 80 queries, achieving AUC greater than 0.97 across six architectures while maintaining extremely low false positive rates for independently trained models — even under label-only output restrictions. While currently demonstrated on vision models, the principle of dynamic perturbation response analysis provides a foundation for extending to LLM fingerprinting that may prove more robust than memorization-based schemes.

### Watermark Verification Under Adversarial and Privacy Pressure (April 2026)

April 2026 watermarking research sharpened both sides of requirement 11.3.3. **SIF** (Semantically In-Distribution Fingerprints), accepted at CVPR 2026, shows that vision-language model ownership checks based on semantically abnormal or out-of-distribution probes can be detected and filtered by a Semantic Divergence Attack. Its proposed defense keeps fingerprint queries semantically aligned with normal multimodal use, which gives auditors a concrete test question: do verification prompts look like real customer traffic, or are they easy for a stolen model operator to identify and suppress?

At the same time, **Adaptive Stealing Watermark** shows that attackers can derive watermark information from watermarked text and dynamically choose attack perspectives during generation, making fixed watermark-stealing assumptions too weak. **Functional Subspace Watermarking** takes the opposite defensive path by anchoring ownership signals in a stable low-dimensional functional subspace so the mark is more likely to survive fine-tuning, quantization, and distillation. The practical takeaway is that watermark reviews should include explicit adversarial testing against watermark stealing, semantic-divergence filtering, fine-tuning, quantization, distillation, paraphrasing, and ensemble use rather than only checking that a watermark exists.

**VOW** (Verifiable and Oblivious Watermark Detection, April 30, 2026) adds a privacy and evidentiary angle. Conventional watermark verification often requires users or investigators to reveal suspect text to the provider and then trust the provider's detection result. VOW reframes detection as secure two-party computation using a verifiable oblivious pseudorandom function, allowing private text checks with verifiable outcomes. For 11.3.3 audits, this means the watermark verification process itself needs review: who sees the suspect content, how detection results are independently verified, and whether the evidence chain would survive legal or regulatory challenge.

### Model Provenance Fingerprinting for Suspect Copies (May 2026)

Cisco AI Defense's open-source Model Provenance Kit gives investigators a practical triage path when they can inspect a suspected open-weight model or local checkpoint. The toolkit compares models using a multi-signal fingerprint that includes architecture metadata, tokenizer features, and weight-level signals; its bundled reference database covers about 150 base models across more than 45 model families. This is useful evidence for 11.3.3 when watermark probes are inconclusive or unavailable, especially for third-party models whose documentation may have been stripped or falsified.

The limitation is just as important as the capability: provenance scans are artifact-based, while many extraction cases involve API-only clones, heavily transformed derivatives, or private deployments that investigators cannot access. Treat a high-similarity result as an investigative lead to combine with watermark challenges, behavioral probes, access logs, account-link analysis, and contractual evidence. Audit evidence should include the compared model artifacts, toolkit version, reference database date, signal-level scores, threshold rationale, and retesting after fine-tuning, quantization, merge, or distillation transformations.

### CoSAI AI Incident Response Framework (2026)

The Coalition for Secure AI (CoSAI) released the **AI Incident Response Framework v1.0** in early 2026, the first comprehensive open-source framework addressing AI-specific incident response. The framework identifies model extraction as a distinct incident archetype and maps five common AI architecture patterns to component-specific vulnerabilities, MITRE ATLAS techniques, and response procedures. Aligned with the NIST SP 800-61r3 incident response lifecycle and the OASIS CACAO standard for playbook documentation, the framework defines four AI-adapted phases: preparation (AI asset inventory, specialized response teams, AI-specific telemetry), detection and analysis (monitoring for model drift, suspicious prompt patterns, unusual retrieval behaviors), containment/eradication/recovery (model version rollback, poisoned memory purging), and post-incident activities. Separately, the GenAI-IRF framework (MDPI, January 2026) formalizes six recurrent GenAI incident archetypes with pre-defined decision gates for SOAR integration. These frameworks directly support requirement 11.3.4's mandate for extraction-specific incident response playbooks.

On May 6, 2026, OASIS/CoSAI added two agentic security papers covering Agentic Identity and Access Management and autonomous swarm defense. The useful extraction-defense update is operational: agents need unique, machine-readable identities, narrowly delegated credentials, continuous authorization, and action-level visibility. CoSAI also identifies intent-based authorization and the semantic mosaic effect as unresolved problems and proposes Agent Detection and Response (ADR) for environments where agents can act, spawn work, and move data at machine speed. For 11.3.1 and 11.3.4, extraction alerts should therefore preserve the acting agent identity, delegated authority chain, tool or router path, credential scope, and human approval state -- not just the human account that launched the workflow.

### LoRD: Alignment-Aware LLM Extraction (ACL 2025)

**Locality Reinforced Distillation (LoRD)**, published at ACL 2025, marks a step change in LLM extraction efficiency. Prior extraction methods adapted techniques from smaller DNNs and ignored the alignment training that makes LLMs useful — LoRD closes that gap by treating extraction as a policy-gradient reinforcement learning task, using the victim model's responses as implicit reward signals. The result: a pre-trained 8B-parameter local model can approximate a 175B-parameter commercial LLM with as few as 100 API queries. LoRD also explicitly defeats watermark protections through exploration-based stealing that avoids the fixed query patterns that watermark verification relies on. Source code is available at https://github.com/liangzid/LoRD-MEA. This directly challenges the assumptions behind requirements 11.3.1 (rate limits set for thousands-of-queries budgets may be far too generous) and 11.3.3 (static watermarks may not survive alignment-aware extraction).

### RADEP: Resilient Adaptive Defense Framework (IEEE IWCMC 2025)

**RADEP** addresses limitations of earlier defense frameworks like AMAO (IEEE TDSC 2023) by combining four interlinked layers: progressive adversarial training to weaken extraction effectiveness, uncertainty-quantified malicious query detection using behavioral pattern analysis, an adaptive response mechanism that degrades output quality proportional to suspicion scores, and embedded ownership verification via watermarking. Where AMAO's label-flipping poisoning was a coarse-grained response to detected attackers, RADEP's graduated degradation preserves utility for borderline-suspicious users while maintaining strong defense against confirmed extraction campaigns. Testing shows minimal impact on legitimate users — a practical concern that hampered adoption of earlier all-or-nothing defense approaches.

### Hardware Side-Channel Extraction: NVBleed and BarraCUDA

Two research lines demonstrate that model extraction threats extend well beyond API queries into the physical layer. **NVBleed** (March 2025) reverse-engineers NVIDIA's NVLink interconnect and identifies two leakage sources: contention-based timing variations and accessible performance counters that disclose communication patterns. Using these channels, researchers achieved 97.78% F1 score in fingerprinting deep learning models across multi-GPU systems — and critically, the attack works remotely across VM instances in cloud environments like GCP (F1 > 88%), meaning co-tenants on shared GPU clusters can identify which models neighbors are running. **BarraCUDA** (2024) takes a complementary approach on edge devices: it uses correlation electromagnetic analysis (CEMA) to recover neural network weights and biases directly from NVIDIA Jetson hardware, requiring about five minutes of trace collection per weight. NVIDIA's official mitigation advice — prevent physical access — is inadequate for shared cloud and edge deployments. Together, these attacks mean that extraction defense must consider not just API-level controls but also infrastructure isolation: dedicated GPU instances, side-channel-resistant scheduling, and hardware performance counter access restrictions.

### Dual-Layer VLM Side-Channel Attacks (March 2026)

A March 2026 paper (arXiv:2603.25403) demonstrates that local Vision-Language Models using dynamic high-resolution preprocessing (like LLaVA-NeXT's AnyRes) leak information through two side channels simultaneously. The first tier exploits execution-time variation: square images require ~110 seconds (five patches) while rectangular images need ~48 seconds (three patches), revealing input geometry with a 2.3x timing difference. The second tier monitors Last-Level Cache (LLC) misses via hardware performance counters to distinguish visual density — dense structures like medical X-rays create more cache contention than sparse content. Combined, these channels classify input content with 84% accuracy. While not full model extraction, this demonstrates that multimodal architectures create novel side-channel surfaces that traditional API-level defenses do not cover.

### On-Device Inference: The Emerging Extraction Blind Spot

As of April 2026, a growing concern in model extraction defense is the proliferation of on-device and local AI inference — developers running models locally via Ollama, llama.cpp, and similar tools outside of API-controlled environments. When models are deployed on developer workstations or edge devices, traditional API-level defenses (rate limiting, query-pattern analysis, watermarking) are entirely bypassed. HiddenLayer's 2026 AI Threat Landscape Report found that 1 in 8 AI breaches are linked to agentic systems, while the broader consolidation of the AI security market (Check Point's acquisition of Lakera in early 2026) signals growing enterprise demand for runtime protection that extends beyond API endpoints. HiddenLayer's 2026 survey also found that 76% of organizations now identify shadow AI as a definite or probable problem — a 15-point increase from 2025 — yet only 34% partner externally for threat detection. Organizations should inventory all model deployment surfaces — including shadow AI deployments on developer machines — and evaluate whether extraction controls are proportionate to the deployment context.

### Industrial-Scale Distillation: The Anthropic and Google Disclosures (February 2026)

In February 2026, Anthropic published a detailed report accusing three Chinese AI labs — DeepSeek, Moonshot AI, and MiniMax — of conducting coordinated, industrial-scale distillation attacks against Claude. The campaigns used over 24,000 fraudulent accounts to generate more than 16 million exchanges, with each lab targeting distinct capabilities: DeepSeek focused on foundational logic and alignment across 150,000+ exchanges, Moonshot AI targeted agentic reasoning and tool use across 3.4 million exchanges, and MiniMax concentrated on agentic coding capabilities across 13 million exchanges. Attackers operated through "hydra cluster" proxy networks distributing requests across thousands of accounts to evade per-principal rate limits, and MiniMax demonstrated the ability to redirect traffic within 24 hours of model updates — indicating near-real-time adaptation.

Anthropic attributed each campaign through IP address correlation, request metadata, infrastructure indicators, and corroboration from industry partners. Defense measures included classifiers and behavioral fingerprinting systems that identify distillation patterns in API traffic, strengthened verification for educational and research accounts, and model-level safeguards designed to reduce the efficacy of outputs for illicit distillation without degrading legitimate use. Anthropic emphasized that "no company can solve this alone," calling for coordinated action across AI labs, cloud providers, and policymakers.

Separately, Google's Threat Intelligence Group (GTIG) disclosed that attackers sent over 100,000 reasoning trace coercion prompts against Gemini, attempting to force the model to output full internal reasoning processes to provide extra signal for knowledge distillation. Google responded by disabling offending accounts and deploying classifier and model-level improvements to refuse similar attacks. These incidents collectively demonstrate that model extraction has transitioned from a research concern to an active, well-resourced adversarial operation — directly validating the need for requirement 11.3.1.

On April 23, 2026, the White House OSTP issued NSTM-4 on adversarial distillation of American AI models, treating large-scale distillation as a national-security and industrial-policy issue rather than only an API abuse problem. The memo cited coordinated campaigns using tens of thousands of proxy accounts and jailbreaking techniques, and directed federal agencies to share intelligence with model providers, support private-sector coordination, develop best practices for identifying and remediating distillation campaigns, and explore accountability measures. For auditors, this raises the bar for extraction controls: rate limits, query-pattern detectors, and response playbooks should be able to describe how they detect coordinated proxy-account behavior, not just single-account overuse.

### Malicious LLM API Router Intermediaries: "Your Agent Is Mine" (CCS 2026)

An April 2026 paper accepted at ACM CCS ("Your Agent Is Mine: Measuring Malicious Intermediary Attacks on the LLM Supply Chain") presents the first systematic analysis of security risks from third-party LLM API routers. The researchers analyzed 428 routers across Taobao, Shopify, and free markets, finding real-world malicious operators: 1 of 28 paid routers injected malicious code, 8 of 400 free routers deployed adaptive evasion triggers, and 17 routers abused researcher-owned AWS credentials. The study identifies four attack classes: response-side payload injection (AC-1, 100% rewrite compatibility across four major agent frameworks), passive secret exfiltration (AC-2, 100% coverage of secrets in payloads), dependency-targeted injection (AC-1.a, 99.6% compatibility), and conditional delivery (AC-1.b, evasion triggers activating only after N benign requests or during YOLO-mode sessions). Critically, "no end-to-end integrity mechanism binds the provider's tool-calling output to what the client finally receives" — a compromised router at any layer in a chain can silently intercept all inference traffic, enabling model extraction without querying the model API directly. The study found that 401 of 440 observed Codex sessions operated in YOLO mode, making sophisticated evasion unnecessary. Defense recommendations include high-risk tool policy gates (100% detection at 1.0% FP for basic attacks), response-side anomaly screening (89% detection at 6.7% FP), and append-only transparency logging. Long-term, the authors propose provider-signed response envelopes analogous to DKIM signatures — currently no major provider offers this capability. This directly extends extraction defense beyond API-level controls to supply-chain integrity of the inference routing layer.

### Transfer Station and Shadow API Markets (March-May 2026)

May 2026 reporting on China's "transfer station" economy shows how shadow API brokers can turn ordinary customer traffic into extraction-ready datasets. These proxies advertise access to restricted or expensive frontier models at as low as 10% of official pricing, while monetizing through account farming, subscription arbitrage, model substitution, and logging full prompts, responses, tool calls, and coding-agent traces. The key verification lesson is practical: extraction defense has to inspect where requests actually flow. Reviews should check SDK base URLs, proxy configuration, egress allowlists, billing/account provenance, and whether internal users can silently route sensitive workloads through untrusted relay services.

Useful control evidence includes code and configuration searches for provider base-url overrides, network telemetry showing direct provider endpoints rather than relay domains, procurement records for API brokers, and DLP rules that treat prompt/response logs as sensitive training data. These checks are especially important for coding agents, where a single relayed session can include repository context, build failures, tool outputs, and human-approved fixes.

CISPA's March 2026 "Real Money, Fake Models" audit gives a complementary measurement angle. The researchers identified 17 shadow APIs used by 187 academic papers and found direct and indirect evidence of deception in three representative providers, including performance divergence up to 47.21% and model-fingerprint failures in 45.83% of tests. For model-extraction defense, this means third-party API routers create two risks at once: provider-side extraction detection loses sight of the real downstream user, and customer-side teams may unknowingly feed proprietary prompts and validated outputs into a relay that can resell them for distillation. Where intermediaries are unavoidable, require contractual data-use limits, strong logging, model-identity checks, and separate extraction monitoring on the customer side.

Model-identity testing should be treated as a guardrail, not a complete assurance mechanism: it can flag likely substitution or drift, but it does not prove that a broker is preserving confidentiality or that logged outputs are excluded from downstream training datasets.

### Neural Honeytrace: Training-Free Watermarking (January 2025)

**Neural Honeytrace** introduces a training-free, plug-and-play watermarking framework built on an information-theoretic model of watermark transmission through extraction attacks. Unlike prior approaches (DAWN, EWE, Composite Backdoor, MEA-Defender) that require model retraining, Neural Honeytrace operates at the inference layer by calculating feature similarity between input queries and predefined watermark samples, then encoding watermark information into output predictions through logit mixing. Its multi-step transmission strategy encodes watermark information into the label distribution itself via probability-based label flipping, making the watermark transfer to attacker-trained surrogates through the extraction process. The framework reduces samples required for copyright claims from approximately 12,000 to 200, achieves 36.6% minimum watermark success rate under worst-case adaptive attacks (versus 2.1% for MEA-Defender), and is effective even in hard-label scenarios — the most common MLaaS configuration where competing methods fail. The framework classifies attackers into three categories (naive, adaptive, oracle) and maintains robustness against pruning-based removal. Open-source implementation: https://github.com/NeurHT/NeurHT. This is particularly relevant for organizations that cannot retrain production models to embed watermarks, directly addressing a practical barrier to adopting requirement 11.3.3.

### Information-Theoretic Logit Purification (May 2026)

Fang et al., "Towards Distillation-Resistant Large Language Models: An Information-Theoretic Perspective" (arXiv 2602.03396, last revised May 6, 2026) proposes a defense that learns a transformation matrix to purify output logits before they leave the inference path. The objective minimizes the conditional mutual information between teacher logits and queries, conditioned on ground-truth labels, so the post-processed outputs retain task accuracy but carry much less information useful for distillation. This is a more principled alternative to ad-hoc top-k truncation or logprob suppression for requirement 11.3.2, and complements ModelGuard's information-theoretic perturbation by acting on logits rather than perturbing predictions. Auditors evaluating output minimization should check whether the deployment uses a measured information-leakage objective (CMI, mutual information bound) rather than only structural removal of fields like `logprobs` or `top_logprobs`. The paper does not yet ship reference code, so production adopters should plan their own implementation and regression tests against KnockoffNet, CopycatCNN, and LoRD-style alignment-aware extraction.

### Pre- and Post-Distillation Watermark Removal (ACL 2025)

Pan et al., "Can LLM Watermarks Robustly Prevent Unauthorized Knowledge Distillation?" (ACL 2025, arXiv 2502.11598) directly challenges the assumption that a watermark embedded in teacher outputs will reliably survive into a distilled student. The authors introduce two attack families that together eliminate inherited watermarks across the schemes they tested: targeted data paraphrasing applied to the teacher-generated training set before distillation (pre-distillation removal), and inference-time watermark neutralization applied to the student's outputs (post-distillation removal). Both achieve near-complete removal while preserving knowledge transfer, with low computational overhead. For 11.3.3, this means watermark-based ownership claims against distilled students must be paired with detection of the paraphrasing or neutralization process itself, evidence of teacher-query patterns consistent with extraction (rate, diversity, JA4H fingerprints, account-cluster signals), and corroborating provenance signals such as Cisco's Model Provenance Kit fingerprints. Treat student-side watermark verification as a single signal in a chain, not as standalone proof.

### HoneypotNet: Defense by Output Poisoning (AAAI 2025)

HoneypotNet (Wang et al., AAAI 2025, arXiv 2501.01090) takes the "attack as defense" stance: instead of trying to keep watermarks intact through extraction, it intentionally poisons the substitute. The victim model's final classification layer is replaced with a honeypot layer fine-tuned via bi-level optimization against a shadow surrogate, so any model trained on the victim's outputs inherits a backdoor that both verifies ownership when triggered and degrades the surrogate's accuracy on the attacker's intended tasks. The authors report high backdoor-injection rates across four benchmarks while preserving original model performance. For 11.3.3 and 11.3.4, this is useful evidence that defenders can choose between preservation-style watermarking (ModelShield, Neural Honeytrace, CFW, SemMark) and degradation-style traps (HoneypotNet, label-flipping poisoning in earlier AMAO work) — but degradation-style defenses raise ethical and safety review questions when the poisoned outputs could affect legitimate downstream users, and they may not generalize cleanly from classifiers to open-ended LLM generation.

### Trace Rewriting and Thief-Controlled Verification (February–April 2026)

Two 2026 papers fill gaps that earlier defenses left open. **Trace rewriting** (Ma, Yeoh, Zhang, and Vorobeychik, arXiv 2602.15143, February 2026, revised April 2026) targets the reasoning-trace distillation vector directly: rather than suppressing chain-of-thought entirely (which DistillGuard showed costs substantial math performance), the defense dynamically rewrites the teacher's reasoning traces — via instruction-prompted LLM rewriting or gradient-based optimization — so they remain correct and coherent for human readers but lose most of their value as distillation training data. The same rewriting embeds a watermark that transfers into any student trained on the traces, and the authors report reliable student-side watermark detection with essentially no false alarms while teacher benchmark performance is maintained or improved. This gives 11.3.2 deployments a defensible option between "expose full reasoning" and "remove reasoning," particularly relevant after the 100,000+ reasoning-trace coercion prompts Google disclosed against Gemini.

**iSeal** (AAAI 2026) addresses the verification-side weakness that Nasery et al. exposed: most fingerprints assume the verifier controls the query path, but a model thief serving a stolen model controls inference and can unlearn fingerprints or manipulate outputs to break exact-match checks. iSeal injects unique identifiers into both the model and an external module, reinforced by error correction and similarity-based (rather than exact-match) verification, and reports a 100% fingerprint success rate across 12 LLMs against more than 10 attacks — including collusion-based fingerprint unlearning and response manipulation — where baseline schemes fail. Together with weight-intrinsic approaches like SELF and provenance scans like Cisco's Model Provenance Kit, this points toward layered verification portfolios: behavioral probes for API-only suspects, intrinsic fingerprints for inspectable weights, and manipulation-resistant schemes for adversary-served models.

### Distribution-Shift Detection: An "Embarrassingly Simple" Extraction Detector (June 2026)

Liu, Guo, and Dong ("An Embarrassingly Simple Detector for Model Extraction Attacks in LLM API Traffic," June 2026) make a useful argument for 11.3.1: because every extraction or distillation campaign shares the operational requirement of repeatedly querying the target, the most reliable signal lives in the *aggregate distribution* of traffic rather than in any single query. Their detector embeds incoming queries with a lightweight sentence encoder (BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5) and applies a Maximum Mean Discrepancy two-sample test against a window of historical benign traffic, calibrating thresholds purely from benign-versus-benign comparisons so no labeled attack data is needed. Across fourteen attacker/normal pairs spanning four extraction families (Query-Efficient, Model Leeching, MeaeQ, and BERT-based API extraction) over WildChat, SQuAD, and GLUE queries, it reports a 0.3% benign false-positive rate, 100% detection of pure-attacker streams, 90.5% average true-positive rate under mixed traffic, and 95.1% balanced accuracy. The practical lesson for auditors is that a credible extraction-attempt detector should perform batch- or window-level distributional testing — not just classify isolated prompts — and that detector calibration can be grounded in normal traffic alone, which sidesteps the usual lack of labeled extraction examples. The residual weakness is the mixed-traffic gap: a patient adversary diluting extraction queries among genuine users still erodes the true-positive rate.

### Ensemble-of-Distilled-Models Serving: MISLEADER (2025)

MISLEADER (Cheng, Zheng, Zhu, and Dong, arXiv 2506.02362, 2025) is relevant to 11.3.2 as a structural alternative to ad-hoc output perturbation. Instead of serving the protected model directly, MISLEADER answers queries through an ensemble of deliberately diversified distilled student models. The diversity injects distributional inconsistencies into the response stream, so an attacker training a surrogate on the observed outputs inherits a blurred, internally inconsistent target rather than a faithful copy — the authors report degraded extraction success across knockoff- and prediction-poisoning-style attacks while preserving utility for legitimate users, with supporting theory. For output-exposure reviews this offers a deployment pattern where the externally visible behavior is decoupled from any single high-value model, though it adds serving cost and its evaluation to date is on classification rather than open-ended LLM generation. Reference code is published under the LabRAI repository.

### Extraction-Rehearsed Watermarking: T2S (June 2026)

T2S treats watermark survival as a property to train and test against extraction, rather than assuming that a watermark present in the protected model will transfer to a stolen copy. It alternates fine-tuning of the protected classifier with a simulated stolen model and back-propagates the simulated model's trigger-set loss into the protected model. In the authors' image-classification evaluation, T2S reached a 97.56% watermark success rate against hard-label Knockoff on CIFAR-10 and 68.68% on CIFAR-100, compared with 21.60% and 15.41% for the pretrained SSW baseline.

For 11.3.3, the useful audit pattern is the evaluation design: measure watermark success in the extracted model across hard-label, soft-label, and data-free attacks; change the stolen architecture and query distribution; then apply a second extraction, quantization, pruning, and distillation. The result is not yet a general answer for generative models: the experiments cover image classifiers, use large query budgets, and show that ground-truth-assisted distillation still degrades watermark transfer. A deployment should therefore preserve the same extraction-and-removal regression suite used to qualify its exact model family.

### Model Multiplicity Changes What Counts as a Copy (June 2026)

Baltz, Hara, and Aïvodji show that high aggregate fidelity does not make an extracted surrogate behaviorally interchangeable with its source. Across tabular, medical-imaging, and NLP experiments, near-equivalent extracted models occupied a wider Rashomon set: models with similar victim agreement still differed on individual predictions and, for tabular tasks, group-fairness behavior. Their analysis uses dropout-based local sampling and fixed architecture families, so it is evidence of underdetermination rather than a complete enumeration of possible copies.

This sharpens 11.3.3 verification in both directions. Similar accuracy or label agreement alone is weak evidence of derivation, while a watermark or fingerprint test should report false-positive controls against independently trained near-equivalent models and should not overclaim full behavioral equivalence. Where an ownership or incident decision depends on impact, auditors should pair watermark evidence with task fidelity, predictive multiplicity, calibration, robustness, and relevant group-level metrics.

### Calibrating Distillability and Structural Logit Leakage (July 2026)

Two July 2026 papers turn output calibration into concrete negative tests for 11.3.2. [Boyle et al.](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10794) target the Carlini et al. hidden-layer-dimension attack directly, perturbing the logits exposed by a production-style language-model API and evaluating the protection-versus-utility tradeoff. [Zhan et al.'s ACL paper](https://aclanthology.org/2026.acl-long.908/) takes a different route: reinforcement fine-tuning combines task utility, a KL anchor, and an across-tokenizer calibration reward to make a teacher deliberately hard to distill. Across math, knowledge-QA, and instruction-following tasks, the authors report that the protected teacher retains task performance while students trained from its outputs collapse.

These approaches protect different assets and should not be collapsed into a single "output filtering" check. An auditor should first enumerate every field and parameter that exposes logits, probabilities, or reasoning traces, then attempt structural recovery through those exact interfaces. Separately, the auditor should train matched students across the deployment's capability families and tokenizers, comparing ordinary and risk-calibrated response policies. Acceptance criteria need both sides of the tradeoff: preserved authorized-user utility and a measured reduction in attacker recovery. Evidence from one task, tokenizer, or attack family is not enough to generalize to the entire endpoint.

### Reasoning Watermarks Need Spoofing Controls (2026)

[ReasMark (ACL 2026)](https://aclanthology.org/2026.acl-long.2185/) addresses reasoning-model attribution by tying watermark activation to frequent prompts in a target domain and using score-conditioned training to create a black-box-detectable reasoning-length gap. That is a useful verification design for a distilled model: test prompts should resemble the traffic an extractor would actually collect, and the verifier should measure a statistical behavior rather than depend on exact output matching.

[DITTO (EACL 2026)](https://aclanthology.org/2026.eacl-long.229/) demonstrates the complementary failure case. Its attacker distills from a watermarked teacher so the malicious student inherits and reproduces an authentic-looking watermark signal, turning watermark radioactivity into a spoofing mechanism. An ownership test therefore needs negative controls against independently trained near-equivalent models and deliberately spoofed students, plus corroborating query records, provenance evidence, and key custody. Detecting the expected watermark alone establishes neither unique origin nor an unbroken chain of derivation.

### Implementation Maturity

Extraction *detection* is moving from research to practice but remains uneven. Query-pattern and distribution-shift detection (11.3.1) is **emerging-to-practical** — PRADA, SEAT, ATOM, and the 2026 MMD detector show the techniques work, and frontier labs (Anthropic, Google) now run production distillation classifiers, but commodity, drop-in detectors for self-hosted APIs are still thin and rate limits are widely mis-calibrated against few-query extraction. Output minimization and calibration (11.3.2) is **mature in mechanism, immature in rigor**: disabling `logprobs` and capping outputs is trivial, but principled, information-theoretic minimization (ModelGuard, Fang et al.) is rarely deployed and is task-dependent. Watermarking and fingerprinting (11.3.3) is **deterrent-grade only** — multiple independent 2025–2026 studies defeat or forge the leading schemes, so it should sit inside a defense-in-depth evidence chain, not stand alone. Response and incident handling (11.3.4) is the **least mature**: frameworks exist (CoSAI, GenAI-IRF) but most organizations have no extraction-specific playbook, and cross-industry indicator sharing is still limited to a handful of frontier labs.

- How much evidentiary weight should model-provenance similarity scans carry when a suspected copy has been fine-tuned, quantized, merged, or partially distilled?
- The Frontier Model Forum now shares adversarial-distillation indicators across founding labs, but enterprises and smaller providers are not yet inside that channel — what minimum-viable schema for distillation indicators (account-cluster IDs, JA4H fingerprints, prompt structures, capability targeting) would let CAISI, sector ISACs, or OASIS/CoSAI act as a parallel sharing layer without leaking customer data?
- Class-Feature Watermarks survive a combined MEA + Watermark Removal Attack at 70%+ success while sample-level schemes drop by ~90% — does the class-level synthetic-class approach generalize to LLMs and embedding services, or does the lack of well-defined "classes" in generative settings require a different anchoring construct?
- Trace rewriting claims anti-distillation with maintained-or-improved teacher performance — does that hold for adversaries who paraphrase or re-derive rewritten traces (the Pan et al. pre-distillation attack), and what is the latency and serving-cost overhead of rewriting every reasoning trace at production scale?
- Can extraction-rehearsed watermarks retain their advantage for generative LLMs, heterogeneous student architectures, and attackers that mix queried outputs with ground-truth or independently generated training data?
- What evidentiary standard should distinguish derivation from coincidental similarity when high-fidelity surrogates occupy a broad set of behaviorally different near-equivalent models?
- What negative-control suite can distinguish a genuinely derived reasoning watermark from DITTO-style copied watermark radioactivity without assuming that watermark detection proves unique provenance?
