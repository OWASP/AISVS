# C1.4: Training Data Quality and Security Assurance

> [Back to C01 Index](C01-Training-Data.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-05-02

## Purpose

Data quality validation is the last defensive line before poisoned, corrupted, or malformed data enters the training pipeline. This section treats quality checks as a security control, not a hygiene concern: the same statistical and schema checks that catch upstream pipeline bugs are also the cheapest broad-spectrum detector for adversarial contamination.

Several lines of research have reset the threat model since 2024:

- **Absolute-count poisoning.** Anthropic, the UK AI Security Institute, and the Alan Turing Institute published [*Poisoning Attacks on LLMs Require a Near-constant Number of Poison Samples*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07192) in October 2025. Across 72 models from 600M to 13B parameters trained on 6B–260B tokens, **250 crafted documents consistently produced a working backdoor** regardless of clean-data volume. In the 13B regime that is about 0.00016% of the corpus — well below the sensitivity of most percentage-based contamination audits. [Anthropic's research summary](https://www.anthropic.com/research/small-samples-poison) frames the asymmetry bluntly: defenders must detect fixed-count contamination, not dilute it.
- **Clinical and code-gen blast radius.** The [*Nature Medicine* study on medical LLMs (Jan 2025)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-024-03445-1) showed that replacing just **0.001% of training tokens** with targeted misinformation produced measurably more harmful completions while passing standard medical QA benchmarks. [CodeScan (Mar 2026, arXiv:2603.17174)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.17174) then demonstrated that token-level consistency scans are weak for source code; AST normalization and vulnerability-oriented black-box scanning reached 97%+ detection accuracy in the authors' test set, but only for code-generation poisoning.
- **Preference and synthetic-data poisoning.** [PoisonBench (ICML 2025)](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v267/fu25e.html) found that poisoned preference data can affect 22 widely used models across eight scenarios, that larger parameter counts do not consistently improve resilience, and that effects can generalize to triggers not present in the poisoned set. [Virus Infection Attack (NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041) then showed poisoning content propagating through synthetic-data generation even when the downstream synthetic-data queries are clean.

The practical implication for Section 1.4 is layered detection. Format validation catches obviously broken records, statistical/embedding analysis catches clumpy or out-of-distribution poisoning, confidence gates stop auto-labels from laundering upstream model errors into fresh training data, adversarial training raises the bar for evasion at inference, and security-exploitable-bias probing closes the evaluation loop for abuse-detection and fraud models. As of May 2026, no single tool covers all of this; a research-grade check list means naming specific libraries, versions, thresholds, owners, and what each one does **not** catch.

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| 1.4.1 | **Verify that** automated tests catch format errors and nulls on every ingest or significant data transformation. | 1 | Malformed records causing silent training failures or model degradation; adversarial samples disguised as "just noisy" records sneaking past manual review; pipeline reliability issues that mask more serious tampering (for example, a poisoned batch arriving inside an otherwise broken day of ingests). | Review data validation code in ingest DAGs. Confirm schema validation runs via [Great Expectations](https://github.com/great-expectations/great_expectations) (GX Core 1.16+ Checkpoints, validation definitions, actions, and Data Docs; legacy 0.18 deployments should have a migration plan), [Pandera](https://github.com/unionai-oss/pandera) for typed Pandas/Polars/Dask contracts, or [TFX Data Validation](https://www.tensorflow.org/tfx/data_validation/get_started) with StatisticsGen, SchemaGen, and ExampleValidator. Verify these tests run in CI/CD on every data change, not just at release. Google's published TFX deployment across 700+ pipelines found real data errors in 6% of training runs over a 30-day window; use that as a baseline for "expected" issue rate. | Tabular coverage is mature. Gap: schema validation for unstructured modalities (images, audio, long-form text, multi-modal) is still ad hoc. Format validation does not catch semantically poisoned-but-syntactically-valid records, which is the dominant LLM poisoning pattern. Also does not catch adversarial Unicode, zero-width, homoglyph, or encoding-normalization tricks used to evade lexical filters unless those checks are explicitly added. |
| 1.4.2 | **Verify that** training and fine-tuning pipelines implement data integrity validation and poisoning detection techniques (e.g., statistical analysis, outlier detection, embedding analysis) to identify potential data poisoning or unintentional corruption in training data. | 2 | Data poisoning (MITRE ATLAS [AML.T0020](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0020)). Headline results: Anthropic/UK AISI/Turing (Oct 2025) — 250 documents backdoor any-size LLM ([arXiv:2510.07192](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07192)); Carlini et al. — web-scale poisoning for ~$60; CyLab/CMU ICLR 2025 — 0.1% poisoning survives safety alignment; *Nature Medicine* (Jan 2025) — 0.001% medical-token poisoning evades benchmarks while raising harmful-output rates; [PoisonBench (ICML 2025)](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v267/fu25e.html) — scaling model size does not reliably improve poisoned-preference resilience across 22 models; Virus Infection Attack ([arXiv:2509.23041](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041), NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight) — poisoning propagates through synthetic-data pipelines. | Inventory the detection stack and confirm something covers each attack family. Label/outlier/spurious-correlation screening: [Cleanlab 2.9](https://github.com/cleanlab/cleanlab) (Jan 2026, strong for label errors, outliers, duplicates, multi-annotator quality, and OOD samples). Classic backdoor defenses: [IBM ART](https://github.com/Trusted-AI/adversarial-robustness-toolbox) poisoning modules for Neural Cleanse, Activation Clustering, Spectral Signatures, and STRIP. Dataset-level provenance: [Datasig](https://github.com/trailofbits/datasig) (Trail of Bits, [May 2025 release](https://blog.trailofbits.com/2025/05/02/datasig-fingerprinting-ai/ml-datasets-to-stop-data-borne-attacks/)) — MinHash fingerprints that let AIBOMs compare datasets without redistributing raw content. Code generation: [CodeScan (Mar 2026)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.17174) for AST-normalized, vulnerability-oriented triggerless-backdoor detection. Audit procedure: feed known-poisoned and clean canary sets through the pipeline, include a poisoned-preference scenario modeled after PoisonBench where relevant, confirm alerts fire, and review outlier thresholds plus override approvals. | Critical gap: the 250-document threshold means defenses must be sensitive to vanishingly small proportions. Clean-label and triggerless attacks remain hardest to catch — a Dec 2025 study ([arXiv:2512.06243](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.06243)) found that INT8 quantization drops five representative backdoor defenses to 0% detection while the backdoor stays above 99% attack success; INT4 results were dataset-dependent. Triggerless code-gen poisoning evades spectral signatures, activation clustering, and static analysis. Datasig is still proof-of-concept-stage and MinHash by design misses semantic manipulation. VIA amplifies through synthetic-data generation, so organizations generating training data from other models inherit upstream contamination. No single tool wins; a layered check with documented coverage gaps is the minimum defensible posture. |
| 1.4.3 | **Verify that** automatically generated labels (e.g., via models or weak supervision) are subject to confidence thresholds and consistency checks to detect misleading or low-confidence labels. | 2 | Propagation of model errors into training data (feedback loops); adversarial manipulation of auto-labeling models to inject systematic mislabels; quality degradation from overreliance on weak supervision; *Virus Infection Attack* (VIA, NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight) showing poisoning propagates through synthetic data generation and amplifies across generations ([arXiv:2509.23041](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041)). | Review the auto-labeling pipeline for explicit confidence gates. Confirm the pipeline uses Confident Learning (Prune-by-Class or Prune-by-Noise-Rate variants — see the [noisy-label-detection benchmark, arXiv:2510.16211](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.16211)) or equivalent statistical screening. For weak-supervision stacks, check [Snorkel](https://github.com/snorkel-team/snorkel) or [Snuba](https://www.vldb.org/pvldb/vol12/p223-varma.pdf)-style labeling-function aggregation and that conflicting votes are flagged, not silently averaged. Cross-model agreement (ensemble disagreement > X% = quarantine), temporal-stability checks against a held-out canary set, and documented human-review sampling rates for auto-generated labels should all be evidenced. Confirm synthetic data produced from potentially poisoned upstream models is screened against the dataset-level detection stack in 1.4.2 before re-ingest. | [Snorkel](https://github.com/snorkel-team/snorkel) and [Cleanlab](https://github.com/cleanlab/cleanlab) support confidence thresholds and CL-style joint-matrix screening. Gap: there is no standard methodology for *choosing* the threshold; teams often pick it empirically per project, which makes the control easy to satisfy on paper and weak in practice. Model collapse from recursive auto-labeling is a known failure mode ([Shumailov et al., *Nature* 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07566-y)). VIA means synthetic pipelines can silently inherit upstream poisoning; screening the *output* of synthetic generation against the 1.4.2 stack is load-bearing. |
| 1.4.4 | **Verify that** automated tests catch label skews on every ingest or significant data transformation. | 2 | Class imbalance introduced by poisoning (flooding one class to shift decision boundaries); drift in label distributions indicating a compromised or tampered pipeline; adversarial manipulation of class proportions to weaken minority-class detection (e.g., injecting "benign" labels to dilute abuse-detection training). | Confirm label-distribution monitoring is wired into the ingest DAG and not just run ad hoc. [Great Expectations](https://github.com/great-expectations/great_expectations), [Deepchecks](https://github.com/deepchecks/deepchecks), and [Evidently](https://docs.evidentlyai.com/metrics/preset_data_drift) all support distribution or drift checks; confirm a statistical test (chi-squared, KL divergence, Jensen-Shannon distance, Wasserstein distance, or population stability index) fires on each ingest with alerting thresholds set above expected legitimate drift. Verify that alerts require human sign-off before downstream training proceeds and that the baseline distribution is versioned in source control (so "the baseline moved" is itself a reviewable change). | Distinguishing malicious skew from legitimate distribution shift requires domain context; no tool does this automatically. Threshold tuning is manual. The control is also bypassable if the adversary poisons across classes proportionally; skew detection only fires when class counts move, not when class *semantics* move. Pair with 1.4.2 embedding/outlier analysis for coverage. |
| 1.4.5 | **Verify that** models used in security-relevant decisions (e.g., abuse detection, fraud scoring, automated trust decisions) are evaluated for systematic bias patterns that an adversary could exploit to evade controls (e.g., mimicking a trusted language style or demographic pattern to bypass detection). | 2 | Adversarial exploitation of demographic, stylistic, regional, device, or behavioral biases in security models; evasion of abuse detection by mimicking under-detected styles; systematic blind spots in fraud detection that attackers can probe and weaponize; cross-model consistency attacks where the same input routes through a biased classifier. The [DHS 2023 adversarial AI risk memo](https://www.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/2023-12/23_1222_st_risks_mitigation_strategies.pdf), [NIST AI RMF](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework), and [EU AI Act Article 10](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-10) all treat bias, representativeness, and feedback loops as security and governance concerns, not just fairness concerns. | Review bias-evaluation reports for each security-critical model. Confirm disaggregated performance metrics across relevant subgroups (style, dialect, geography, device class, account age, activity pattern — whatever matters for the deployment). [Fairlearn](https://github.com/fairlearn/fairlearn) and [AI Fairness 360](https://github.com/Trusted-AI/AIF360) provide the measurement plumbing. Confirm an adversarial-probing step goes beyond measurement: red-team exercises that attempt to bypass abuse detection or fraud scoring using under-performing subgroup patterns, with findings logged and remediated. Require regression tests so fairness fixes do not open a new evasion path. | No dedicated "security-exploitable-bias" tooling exists. Fairness tools measure the gap; they do not tell you which gaps are weaponizable. This still requires human red-team design and domain-specific threat modeling. Also in scope: ensuring remediation does not itself create new exploitable classes, such as a fraud model that over-trusts a protected or high-reputation subgroup after a fairness adjustment. |
| 1.4.6 | **Verify that** appropriate defenses, such as adversarial training, data augmentation with perturbed inputs, or robust optimization techniques, are implemented and tuned for relevant models based on risk assessment. | 3 | Adversarial evasion at inference time (MITRE ATLAS [AML.T0015](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0015) / [AML.T0042](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0042)); model brittleness under distribution shift; targeted attacks exploiting known model weaknesses. | Review training code for adversarial components: [PGD-AT (Madry et al.)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.06083), [TRADES](https://github.com/yaodongyu/TRADES), or newer dummy-class variants that claim to reduce the robustness-accuracy trade-off ([arXiv:2410.12671](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.12671v1)). Verify adversarial augmentation in the data pipeline and that evaluation uses the AutoAttack battery (APGD-CE, APGD-DLR, FAB, Square) rather than PGD alone — AutoAttack remains the accepted "defense-audit" benchmark. [IBM ART](https://github.com/Trusted-AI/adversarial-robustness-toolbox) and [Foolbox](https://github.com/bethgelab/foolbox) provide reference implementations. Confirm there is a documented risk assessment justifying the chosen defense (matching attack model to deployment surface) — a Level 3 control is expected to cite which threats it does and does not mitigate. | Adversarial training raises compute cost 2–10× and typically reduces clean accuracy 2–6 points. The robustness-accuracy trade-off is well characterized but poorly tooled for automated tuning. Appropriately Level 3 because of cost, skill required, and because many deployed systems do not face meaningful adversarial input. For LLMs specifically, adversarial-training analogues (safety RLHF, constitutional AI, red-team fine-tuning) are the practical substitute but are not covered by classical AT/PGD benchmarks. |

## Recent Research and Incident Context (2025-2026)

- **Oct 2025 — Anthropic/UK AISI/Turing.** [*Poisoning Attacks on LLMs Require a Near-constant Number of Poison Samples*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07192). 250-document threshold across 600M–13B parameter models. Resets the defender's math from percentages to absolute counts.
- **Jan 2025 — *Nature Medicine*.** [Medical LLMs vulnerable to data poisoning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-024-03445-1). 0.001% token replacement produces measurably harmful completions while evading standard medical QA benchmarks. A [PMC case study in breast-cancer clinical LLMs](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10984073/) extended the finding to specialty models.
- **May 2025 — Trail of Bits.** [Datasig release](https://blog.trailofbits.com/2025/05/02/datasig-fingerprinting-ai/ml-datasets-to-stop-data-borne-attacks/). MinHash-based dataset fingerprinting specifically designed for AIBOM / supply-chain consumption. [Source repo](https://github.com/trailofbits/datasig).
- **ICML 2025 — PoisonBench.** [Poisoned preference data benchmark](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v267/fu25e.html) across 22 models, two attack types, and eight scenarios. Larger models are not consistently more resilient, attack effects scale with poison ratio, and poisoned behavior can generalize to unseen triggers.
- **Aug 2025.** Triggerless code-generation poisoning study — spectral signatures, activation clustering, and static analysis all failed on AST-normalized attacks; [CodeScan (Mar 2026, arXiv:2603.17174)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.17174) reported ~97% detection for this one attack class and is currently the only defense with published numbers.
- **NeurIPS 2025 — Virus Infection Attack.** [arXiv:2509.23041](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041). Poisoning propagates through synthetic-data generation pipelines; organizations that generate training data from other models inherit and amplify upstream contamination.
- **Dec 2025 — Quantization Blindspots.** [arXiv:2512.06243](https://www.arxiv.org/pdf/2512.06243). INT8 quantization drops classic backdoor defenses (Activation Clustering, STRIP, Spectral Signatures, Fine-Pruning) to 0% detection while the backdoor remains fully functional. Only Neural Cleanse survived, and only at full precision. Changes the risk picture for edge deployments that quantize after training.
- **Jan 2026 — Cleanlab 2.9.** Latest open-source release at the time of this review; useful for label errors, outliers, duplicates, multi-annotator quality, and OOD screening ([project page](https://github.com/cleanlab/cleanlab)).
- **Apr 2026 — NIST critical-infrastructure profile concept note.** NIST published a concept note for a profile on trustworthy AI in critical infrastructure, a useful signal for organizations that treat data quality checks as operational resilience controls rather than one-off model-development tasks.
- **Aug 2026 EU AI Act enforcement milestone.** High-risk AI systems must demonstrate documented data governance, bias measurement, traceability, and human oversight. Training-data quality controls are now audit targets, not internal hygiene. See the [Cloud Security Alliance mapping of ISO/IEC 42001 and NIST AI RMF onto the EU AI Act](https://cloudsecurityalliance.org/blog/2025/01/29/how-can-iso-iec-42001-nist-ai-rmf-help-comply-with-the-eu-ai-act).

## Defensive Tooling Cheat Sheet

| Layer | Primary tools | Covers |
|---|---|---|
| Schema / format | Great Expectations / GX Core 1.16+, Pandera, TFX Data Validation, Deepchecks, Evidently | Structural corruption, nulls, type drift, label drift, value ranges. Not semantic poisoning. |
| Label / outlier | Cleanlab 2.9, Confident Learning, SplitNet, Snorkel/Snuba | Label errors, low-confidence auto-labels, OOD samples. Not coordinated clean-label attacks. |
| Backdoor forensics | IBM ART poisoning defenses (Neural Cleanse, Activation Clustering, Spectral Signatures, STRIP), CodeScan (code-gen specific) | Trigger-based backdoors at full precision. Degrades sharply under quantization. |
| Dataset provenance | Datasig (MinHash fingerprints), AIBOM tooling, dataset-versioning hashes | Detecting silent dataset swaps and tampering. Not content-level poisoning within a known dataset. |
| Adversarial robustness | PGD-AT, TRADES, IBM ART, Foolbox, AutoAttack for evaluation | Evasion attacks at inference. Expensive; orthogonal to training-data poisoning. |
| Security-exploitable bias | Fairlearn, AI Fairness 360, adversarial red-team probing | Measurement + exploit probing. No automated tool yet; human-led exercise. |

## Audit Evidence Checklist

For this section, a paper control is not enough. Ask for artifacts that prove the quality gates run at the right points in the pipeline:

- Ingest DAG screenshots or configuration showing schema, null, type, and label-distribution checks before data lands in the training set.
- Current expectation suites, Pandera schemas, TFX schemas, or equivalent contracts, with owners and review history.
- Alert records showing failed data-quality checks, who approved any override, and whether downstream training was blocked.
- Baseline label distributions and embedding/outlier thresholds stored with dataset versions, not just in a dashboard.
- Canary poisoned samples or synthetic corruption tests that exercise poisoning detectors without entering production data.
- Auto-labeling confidence thresholds, ensemble-disagreement rules, and human-review sampling rates.
- Dataset fingerprints, checksums, or AIBOM entries tying each model version back to the exact dataset snapshot used.
- Bias and subgroup-evasion test reports for security-critical models, including the red-team prompts or samples used to probe bypass patterns.
