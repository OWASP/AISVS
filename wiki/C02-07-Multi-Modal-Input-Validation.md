# C2.7 Multi-Modal Input Validation

> **Parent:** [C02: User Input Validation](C02-User-Input-Validation)

## Purpose

Modern AI systems accept inputs beyond text -- images, audio, video, PDFs, spreadsheets, and other file types. Each modality introduces unique attack surfaces: images can contain steganographic payloads or adversarial perturbations invisible to humans; audio can include ultrasonic commands; documents can embed hidden instructions in metadata, comments, or invisible layers. Multi-modal validation must handle format verification, content extraction, malware scanning, and adversarial detection for each modality, while also detecting coordinated cross-modal attacks where benign inputs in individual modalities combine to form an attack.

As of March 2026, multi-modal attacks have matured significantly. Transfer-based adversarial attacks achieve concerning success rates against production vision-language models (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5, Gemini). Research published at CVPR 2025 and ICLR 2025 demonstrates chain-of-attack techniques against VLMs, and a 2025 practitioner survey (arXiv:2505.03084) confirms that defense literature remains fragmented with no unified multi-modal defense framework in production.

A January 2026 CSA research note quantifies image-based prompt injection success rates: typographic text injection achieves 64% success against GPT-4V, Claude 3, and Gemini; steganographic encoding reaches 24.3% with near-invisible pixel-level payloads; and the CrossInject framework (ACM MM 2025) improves adversarial perturbation success by at least 30.1% over prior methods through Visual Latent Alignment and Textual Guidance Enhancement using surrogate open-source LLMs. The "Invisible Injections" study (arXiv:2507.22304, July 2025) provides the first comprehensive evaluation of steganographic prompt injection across spatial, frequency, and neural embedding methods, finding neural steganography reaches 31.8% attack success while maintaining visual imperceptibility (PSNR > 38 dB, SSIM > 0.94) across 8 VLMs and 12 datasets. AnyAttack (CVPR 2025) demonstrates that self-supervised adversarial learning pre-trained on LAION-400M can generate targeted adversarial images without label supervision, transferring seamlessly to commercial systems including GPT, Claude, Gemini, and Microsoft Copilot -- representing a scalability leap over prior targeted attacks. The MFHA framework (Scientific Reports, March 2025) achieves a 16.05% average improvement in adversarial transferability through triple contrastive feature heterogenization and multi-domain cross-modal variance aggregation.

On the defense side, the Cross-Agent Multimodal Provenance-Aware Defense Framework (arXiv:2512.23557) demonstrates 94% detection accuracy compared to 52% for keyword filtering and 66% for safety fine-tuning alone, using cooperating sanitizer agents with a provenance ledger. DMAST (Dual-Modality Multi-Stage Adversarial Safety Training, March 2026) reduces attacker success on multimodal web agents from 41.2% to 21.4% through three-stage adversarial co-evolution training. For audio, the SUAD defense (arXiv:2508.02116, August 2025) blocks inaudible ultrasonic voice attacks with over 98% success on six smartphones without impacting normal speech, while SUAD Attack itself achieves 89.8%+ activation success via solid-channel ultrasonic injection -- highlighting both the severity and the viability of defending against ultrasonic attack vectors.

The 2025 practitioner survey confirms that cross-modal attacks -- where manipulating one modality influences processing of another -- represent a distinct and growing threat class. Current practical approach: flag requests where any single modality scores above a suspicion threshold and the combination of modalities is unusual for the user's history. A pipeline-wide defense framework (arXiv:2509.05883) recommends eight defense stages from ingestion through monitoring, including context firewalls and trust-aware formatting. As of March 2026, the Cross-Agent Multimodal Provenance-Aware Defense Framework (arXiv:2512.23557) represents the closest approximation to production-grade cross-modal correlation: its provenance ledger tracks trust levels and influence relationships across modalities, achieving 94% accuracy and reducing trust leakage by 70%. HITL approval workflow requires operational design: queue management, reviewer training, SLA for review time, and fallback behavior if no reviewer is available. The HITL requirement adds operational cost but is appropriate for the highest-risk scenarios. |

## Open Research Questions

- Can cross-modal adversarial attacks be reliably detected with current tooling, or is this fundamentally a research problem? As of March 2026, the Provenance-Aware Defense Framework (94% detection) and DMAST (41.2% to 21.4% attacker success reduction) suggest detection is becoming feasible, but these remain research prototypes requiring custom integration -- no turnkey production solution exists yet.
- How should multi-modal validation handle new modalities (e.g., 3D models, haptic input, sensor data) as AI systems expand?
- What is the performance overhead of comprehensive multi-modal validation, and how does it impact real-time applications? CrowdStrike AIDR demonstrates sub-30ms is achievable for text-based detection, but multi-modal scanning adds significant latency.
- How do unified vision-language model architectures (e.g., GPT-4o, Gemini 2.5 Pro) change the attack surface compared to pipeline architectures with separate vision and language models? CVPR 2025 research on chain-of-attack techniques suggests unified architectures may be more susceptible to cascading cross-modal attacks.
- What are effective defenses against typographic attacks on vision models beyond text detection in images? MAT (2025) shows promise at training time, but runtime defenses remain limited.
- How should organizations prioritize adversarial detection investment given that embedding-space attacks are particularly difficult to detect even for experienced practitioners?
- What role should the eight-stage pipeline defense framework (ingestion through monitoring) play in standardized multi-modal validation architectures?
- How should organizations balance the DMAST finding that visual attacks (34.4% success) significantly outperform text-only injections (24.1%), given that most current safety training remains text-centric?
- What is the practical overhead and latency impact of multi-agent sanitization architectures (Text Sanitizer + Visual Sanitizer + Output Validator) compared to single-model defense approaches?
- How should defenders adapt to self-supervised adversarial attacks (AnyAttack) that eliminate the target-label constraint, enabling any image to serve as a targeted attack vector against any VLM without prior knowledge of the model's label space?
- Can dormant Visual Memory Injection payloads (arXiv:2602.15927) be detected pre-emptively, or does defense require continuous monitoring across multi-turn conversation state?
- What countermeasures are effective against Virtual Scenario Hypnosis attacks that combine deceptive narrative framing with adversarial images, given their 82% success rate?
- How should organizations defend against solid-channel ultrasonic injection (SUAD) in physical environments where attackers can access shared surfaces (desks, tables) near voice-enabled AI systems?
