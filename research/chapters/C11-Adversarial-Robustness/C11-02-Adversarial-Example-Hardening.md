# C11.2: Adversarial-Example Hardening

> **Chapter:** [C11 Adversarial Robustness & Attack Resistance](C11-Adversarial-Robustness.md)
> **Requirements:** 9 | **IDs:** 11.2.1--11.2.9

## Purpose

Increase resilience to manipulated inputs designed to cause misclassification or policy bypass. Adversarial examples -- inputs crafted with small, often imperceptible perturbations -- remain one of the most studied attack vectors in ML security. This section requires evaluation against known attack techniques, detection mechanisms in production, and (at higher levels) formal verification of robustness properties. The emphasis is on adaptive evaluation: testing defenses against attacks specifically designed to defeat them, not just generic benchmarks.

## Recent Research (2024--2026)

### Adversarial Training Advances for LLMs and VLMs

Adversarial training remains the most empirically validated hardening technique, but recent work has adapted it substantially for foundation models. **Prompt Adversarial Tuning (PAT)** enhances robustness through systematic adversarial example exposure during fine-tuning, enabling improved resistance without architectural modifications. For LLMs, **Adversarial Preference Learning** (ACL 2025 Findings) integrates adversarial robustness objectives directly into RLHF-style alignment, treating adversarial jailbreak prompts as negative preference signals -- bridging the gap between traditional adversarial training and LLM safety training.

**DiffuseDef** (ACL 2025) applies diffusion-based input purification to improve NLP model robustness against adversarial text attacks, reconstructing clean inputs from noisy adversarial perturbations without requiring adversarial training. This represents a shift toward purification-based defenses that decouple robustness from the training pipeline, reducing the 3-10x training cost overhead of classical adversarial training.

**AdPO (Adversarial Preference Optimization)** reframes adversarial training as a preference optimization problem for VLMs. Rather than fine-tuning the entire model adversarially, AdPO modifies only the image encoder (e.g., CLIP ViT), training it to prefer normal outputs on clean inputs while rejecting misleading outputs from adversarial examples. This achieves competitive robustness on both clean and adversarial inputs while keeping computational cost manageable -- and training on smaller VLMs transfers effectively to larger ones, a practical advantage for organizations that cannot afford to adversarially fine-tune their largest models.

### Hardening Portfolio for High-Risk Functions

The revised 11.2.3 wording is broader than classical adversarial training. As of May 2026, a credible hardening plan for high-risk functions should read like a portfolio: adversarial training or fine-tuning where model ownership and compute allow it, randomized smoothing or certified training where a bounded perturbation model is defensible, input transformation or purification where latency and clean-accuracy impact are acceptable, and adversarial-robust distillation only when paired with modern attack validation. OWASP AI Exchange maps these controls to evasion-robust model design, adversarial training, input distortion, and adversarial-robust distillation, but it also notes the practical limits: training overhead, poor generalization to new attacks, clean-accuracy trade-offs, and bypasses for brittle preprocessing.

Recent defense surveys make the audit question more concrete. VLM defenses now fall into training-time defenses, test-time adaptation, and training-free input or embedding defenses; broader deep-learning surveys group defenses into detection, purification, and optimization. That taxonomy helps map evidence to 11.2.3: adversarial training and robust distillation are training-time or optimization evidence, randomized smoothing is certification evidence, and input transformation or diffusion purification is runtime evidence. For each category, auditors should ask for the exact threat model, attack suite, defense parameters, clean-task regression, and an adaptive attack result rather than a single benchmark score.

Defensive distillation deserves special scrutiny. Carlini and Wagner showed in 2016 that early defensive distillation was not robust to targeted adversarial examples, and later reviews still treat it as a cautionary example for gradient masking. Modern distillation-based defenses may be useful when combined with adversarial training or robust teacher/student objectives, but a release gate should not accept "distillation" as a control unless the evaluation includes adaptive attacks, transfer attacks, and query-based black-box attacks against the distilled artifact.

### Vision-Language Model Robustness

The attack surface for vision-language models (VLMs) has expanded significantly. **Chain of Attack** (CVPR 2025) demonstrated cross-modal attack surfaces where adversarial perturbations in one modality (e.g., images) cascade to compromise outputs in the other modality (text), confirming that multi-modal systems require both modality-specific and cross-modal adversarial evaluation.

**AnyAttack** (CVPR 2025) introduced a self-supervised adversarial framework pre-trained on LAION-400M that transforms any image into a targeted attack vector without requiring label supervision. The approach transfers successfully to commercial VLM systems (GPT-4, Gemini, Claude, Copilot), demonstrating that adversarial transferability is a systemic concern across deployed models, not just a research curiosity. This has direct implications for requirement 11.2.1 -- evaluations must account for transfer-based attacks originating from open-source surrogate models.

**GLEAM** (ICCV 2025) further advanced transferable adversarial attacks by combining diversified local region transformations with cross-modal feature alignment exploitation, achieving higher transfer rates across VLM architectures. A March 2026 survey of adversarial attacks against modern VLMs confirms that the attack-defense arms race in multi-modal systems is accelerating, with new attack vectors emerging faster than defenses can adapt.

As of April 2026, **Progressive Semantic Infusion (PSI)** shows why transfer testing needs to look beyond visibly noisy perturbations. PSI uses diffusion priors to keep adversarial images aligned with the natural image distribution while progressively steering target semantics, improving black-box transfer and stealth against open-source, adversarially trained, and commercial LVLMs. For requirement 11.2.1, this means visual inspection and simple perturbation detectors are not enough; evaluations should include transfer attacks that preserve image fidelity and test whether defenses depend on obvious artifacts.

**VLM-RobustBench** (ICLR 2026 Workshop) adds a practical benchmark layer for real-world VLM distortions rather than only adversarially optimized perturbations. It covers 49 augmentation types across noise, blur, weather, digital, and geometric transformations, producing 133 corrupted settings across MMBench and MMMU-Pro. The benchmark found that visually mild geometric and resampling transforms can be more damaging than visually severe photometric corruptions, which is useful evidence for requirement 11.2.4 release gates: robustness dashboards should track corruption families and severity, not just a single aggregate score.

### VLM Defense Paradigms: A Maturing Taxonomy

A January 2026 survey (Fu et al.) organizes VLM adversarial defenses into three paradigms that are worth understanding for practical implementation:

1. **Training-time defenses** modify the training or fine-tuning process -- adversarial fine-tuning, preference optimization (AdPO), and adversarial prompt tuning (Adversarial Mixture Prompt Tuning, or AMPT). These are effective but computationally expensive and may not generalize across attack types.
2. **Test-time adaptation defenses** adapt models during inference on unlabeled adversarial examples. These offer flexibility without retraining but increase inference latency and computational overhead.
3. **Training-free defenses** modify inputs or embeddings rather than the model itself. **MirrorCheck** exemplifies this approach: it uses text-to-image models to generate images from captions produced by the target VLM, then compares embedding similarity between the original input and the regenerated image. High divergence flags adversarial inputs. MirrorCheck is model-agnostic, requires no fine-tuning, and shows resilience against adaptive attacks designed to defeat it -- making it relevant for requirement 11.2.2's production detection requirement.

For organizations evaluating which paradigm to adopt, training-free approaches like MirrorCheck have the lowest barrier to deployment (no model retraining), while training-time approaches like AdPO offer deeper robustness at higher cost. Most practical deployments will combine paradigms -- training-time hardening plus runtime detection.

Recent ICLR 2026 work also warns against treating test-time VLM defenses as solved. **Directional Bias-guided Defense (DBD)** reports strong zero-shot robustness for CLIP-style models by reconstructing features along a defense direction estimated from transformed inputs. In contrast, **Adversaries Fight Back** evaluates six VLM test-time defenses under defense-specific adaptive attacks and finds that some reported robustness drops sharply when the attacker optimizes against the defense itself. Auditors should therefore ask for both the non-adaptive benchmark result and the adaptive attack result for any runtime VLM defense claimed under requirements 11.2.2 or 11.2.6.

As of April 2026, **Defense2Attack** adds a useful caution for runtime defense claims: weak defensive patterns can be incorporated into the attack pipeline and turned into stronger VLM jailbreak prompts. The method combines a visual optimizer, defense-styled textual optimizer, and red-team suffix generator, and the Pattern Recognition pre-proof reports single-shot attack success rates up to 80% on open-source VLMs and 50% on commercial VLMs. For requirement 11.2.6, this means adaptive testing should include attacks that explicitly condition on the deployed detector, refusal style, preprocessing rule, or guardrail prompt rather than only attacks that ignore the defense.

### Autonomous Systems and Safety-Critical Deployments

Adversarial attacks on safety-critical vision systems remain a pressing concern. As of 2025, systematic reviews of adversarial attacks on autonomous driving deep learning models document attack success rates of 52--67% against production object detection models (LLaVA-v1.5, Qwen2.5-VL) using gradient-based methods (BIM, PGD, CLIP-based spectral attacks). Physical-world adversarial patches placed on road surfaces have demonstrated the ability to cause lane detection failures, leading to incorrect steering decisions. These findings underscore requirement 11.2.2's emphasis on runtime detection -- adversarial inputs in safety-critical deployments cannot rely solely on pre-deployment hardening.

Clinical VLM deployments now have similar evidence. An April 2026 Scientific Reports study of federated CLIP-style medical VLMs found that client-level FGSM, PGD, BIM, and MI-FGSM perturbations can propagate through federated aggregation and cause severe accuracy degradation, especially under iterative attacks. Test-time defenses such as Test-Time Counter-Attack and CLIPure mitigated some effects, with CLIPure showing more consistent improvements across datasets and attack intensities. For requirement 11.2.1, high-risk medical and federated deployments should test both centralized and client-local adversarial perturbations; for requirement 11.2.2, runtime alerts should be calibrated against site-specific data distributions rather than a single global validation set.

### Certified Robustness Progress

Certified defense methods have advanced beyond Lp-norm bounds for classification. ICLR 2026 work on dissecting adversarial robustness of multi-modal models introduced certified robustness techniques applicable to transformer-based architectures, narrowing the gap between formal verification (requirement 11.2.7) and practical model sizes. However, certified bounds for production-scale LLMs remain out of reach -- current methods scale to models with hundreds of thousands of parameters but not billions.

A NeurIPS 2024 paper ("On the Scalability of Certified Adversarial Robustness with Generated Data") demonstrated that generating additional training data using state-of-the-art diffusion models can substantially improve deterministic certified defenses. The key insight is that synthetic data generated by diffusion models provides a richer perturbation space for certified training methods like Interval Bound Propagation (IBP), and the scaling behavior of certified methods with additional data differs notably from empirical methods. This is a practical path forward for organizations seeking to improve certified bounds without architectural changes -- augment the training set with high-quality synthetic data. The approach is most applicable to smaller safety-critical classification components where certified training is already feasible.

A notable breakthrough is the scaling of **randomized smoothing** to VLMs. Traditional randomized smoothing requires ~10^5 samples per input for certification, making it infeasible for large generative models. As of late 2025, researchers demonstrated that connecting generative outputs to oracle classification tasks (e.g., harmful vs. harmless classification) and applying improved scaling laws reduces the sample requirement by 2--3 orders of magnitude (EMNLP 2025). This makes certified robustness computationally feasible for state-of-the-art VLMs for the first time, though the certification scope is limited to specific output classification properties rather than full generative robustness.

Randomized smoothing has also been applied to **LLM-driven multi-agent systems**, providing statistical robustness certification for agent coordination -- relevant for organizations deploying agentic architectures under requirement 11.2.7's formal verification expectations.

Hybrid defense strategies that combine certified bounds for safety-critical subcomponents with empirical adversarial evaluation for the full system are emerging as the practical approach for organizations implementing both Level 2 and Level 3 requirements.

### Adversarial Robustness Evaluation Tooling

The tooling landscape for adversarial robustness testing has matured considerably:

- **IBM Adversarial Robustness Toolbox (ART) v1.17+** remains the most comprehensive open-source framework, hosted by the Linux Foundation AI & Data Foundation. ART supports evasion, poisoning, extraction, and inference attacks across all major ML frameworks (PyTorch, TensorFlow, scikit-learn, XGBoost) and data types (images, text, audio, tabular). It includes 3 robustness metrics, certification, and verification capabilities. For organizations implementing requirements 11.2.1 and 11.2.4, ART provides both attack simulation and defense evaluation in a single toolkit.

- **AdvERSEM** (StarSEM 2025) provides adversarial robustness testing and training specifically designed for NLP semantic models, complementing TextAttack for text-domain evaluations.

- **Mindgard** has emerged as a commercial automated red-teaming platform (recognized in the Gartner Hype Cycle for Application Security 2025 and winner of the 2025 Cybersecurity Excellence Award). It simulates thousands of adversarial attack scenarios across text, image, audio, and multi-modal models, with CI/CD pipeline integration so adversarial tests run on every commit. The platform aligns its attack library to MITRE ATLAS and OWASP taxonomies, making it useful for organizations that need to demonstrate compliance with requirement 11.2.6's adaptive attack evaluation.

- **Promptfoo** provides MITRE ATLAS-aligned red-teaming for LLM applications, enabling automated adversarial evaluation against the ATLAS technique taxonomy.

- **MITRE ATLAS Arsenal + CALDERA** provides automated adversary emulation for AI systems. Arsenal implements ATLAS techniques as CALDERA Abilities, enabling security teams to simulate adversarial attack chains against ML pipelines using the same emulation framework they use for traditional cyber adversary simulation. As of late 2025, MITRE ATLAS expanded to 66 techniques across 14 tactics, including 14 new techniques focused specifically on AI agents and generative AI systems (developed in collaboration with Zenity Labs). This is particularly useful for organizations implementing requirement 11.2.6's adaptive attack evaluation -- Arsenal can automate attack sequences that test defenses against ATLAS-mapped threat scenarios.

- **HiddenLayer AI Security Platform** offers an integrated approach combining AI discovery, supply chain security, attack simulation, and runtime defense. Its attack simulation module uses MITRE ATLAS tactics and techniques to automate adversarial testing, while the runtime defense module (AIDR) provides real-time adversarial attack detection without requiring access to training data or model internals. HiddenLayer's March 2026 AI Threat Landscape Report found that one in eight reported AI breaches is now linked to agentic systems, highlighting the expanding attack surface beyond traditional adversarial examples.

- **Microsoft Counterfit** remains available as an environment-agnostic and model-agnostic command-line tool for orchestrating adversarial attacks against ML models, interfacing with ART, TextAttack, and AugLy. While not as actively maintained as ART, it provides a useful orchestration layer for teams that need to coordinate multiple attack frameworks.

The growing availability of modality-specific and commercially supported evaluation toolkits reduces the barrier for organizations to implement requirements 11.2.1 and 11.2.4, though expertise in interpreting results and designing adaptive attacks remains a bottleneck.

### Generative AI Red-Teaming Tools

The tooling landscape for adversarial testing of generative AI systems has matured into its own category, distinct from the classical adversarial robustness tools above:

- **Microsoft PyRIT** (Python Risk Identification Tool) is an open-source framework for proactively identifying risks in generative AI systems. PyRIT supports over 20 attack strategies including prompt injection, harmful content generation, misuse enablement, privacy leaks, and robustness failures. It handles both single-turn and multi-turn adversarial interactions and includes a scoring engine for automated evaluation of attack outcomes. PyRIT tests endpoints across providers (OpenAI, Azure, Anthropic, Google, HuggingFace, custom HTTP). Microsoft reports that PyRIT reduced red-teaming cycles from weeks to hours on Copilot systems. As of PyRIT v0.13.0 (April 2026), the framework is moving toward more composable attack declarations through the new AttackTechnique abstraction and stronger target input/output typing. For organizations implementing requirement 11.2.6, PyRIT's adaptive multi-turn attack strategies are particularly relevant -- the tool iterates based on model responses, simulating the adaptive adversary the requirement demands.

- **NVIDIA Garak** (Generative AI Red-teaming & Assessment Kit) is an open-source LLM vulnerability scanner that probes for hallucination, data leakage, prompt injection, misinformation, toxicity generation, and jailbreaks. Garak uses static, dynamic, and adaptive probes with pluggable detectors (string-matching, ML classifiers, LLM-as-judge). It integrates with NVIDIA NeMo Guardrails for end-to-end testing of guardrail configurations. Garak's modular architecture allows custom probe development for organization-specific threat models.

  Garak **v0.15.0** (May 2026) adds agent-focused coverage on top of the April v0.14.1 additions. The new release includes multi-turn GOAT probing, an Agent Breaker probe for systems with tools, system prompt extraction coverage, NeMo Guardrails server support, and reasoning-trace test-generator support. Those changes are relevant to this section because adversarial-example hardening for agentic and multimodal systems now needs streaming, audio-modal, tool-use, and statistically defensible attack-success reporting. Teams using garak evidence should record the probe set, detector versions, confidence interval method, model endpoint, tool inventory, and any guardrail configuration under test.

- **ARMs** (Adaptive Red-Teaming Agent against Multimodal Models) is an emerging multimodal red-teaming framework that optimizes diverse VLM attack strategies through reasoning-enhanced orchestration. The ICLR 2026 poster reports 11 multimodal attack strategies, 17 integrated red-teaming algorithms, and ARMs-Bench with 30,000 red-teaming instances across 51 risk categories. For requirement 11.2.6, ARMs is useful less as a turnkey compliance answer and more as evidence of the bar auditors should expect: adaptive VLM testing should vary visual perturbations, textual wrappers, contextual cloaking, reasoning hijacks, and policy-specific risk objectives rather than running a fixed prompt list.

- **MITRE Armory** is a security-specialized adversarial evaluation framework focused on model poisoning and robustness evaluation during development. While less widely adopted than ART (176 GitHub stars vs. 4,900+), Armory fills a niche for teams that need adversarial evaluation integrated with development-time security testing rather than production monitoring.

- **RobustBench** provides standardized adversarial robustness benchmarks using AutoAttack (an ensemble of white-box and black-box attacks) across CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet. The leaderboard tracks 120+ models and serves as the de facto standard for comparing Lp-norm robustness claims. For requirement 11.2.6, RobustBench's AutoAttack ensemble provides a baseline "minimum bar" for adaptive evaluation in supported vision settings; for requirement 11.2.4, the same results are useful only when recorded as versioned release metrics with the perturbation norm and budget.

The OWASP AI Exchange testing guidance recommends a three-pronged approach to AI security testing: conventional security testing, model performance validation, and AI-specific red teaming. This mirrors the layered evaluation strategy implied by requirements 11.2.1 (known attacks), 11.2.3 (hardening), 11.2.4 (release metrics), and 11.2.6 (adaptive attacks).

### NIST AI 100-2e2025: Expanded Adversarial Taxonomy

NIST published AI 100-2e2025 in March 2025, superseding the e2023 edition. The update significantly broadens the adversarial ML taxonomy to reflect the current threat landscape. Key changes relevant to this section:

- **Generative AI integration:** The taxonomy now explicitly covers attacks against LLMs, RAG systems, and agent-based AI deployments -- categories absent from e2023, which focused primarily on classical ML (evasion, poisoning, privacy attacks). For organizations implementing requirement 11.2.1, the updated taxonomy provides a more complete attack enumeration for evaluation scoping.
- **Misuse violations:** A new top-level attack category captures scenarios where adversaries exploit model capabilities to bypass safeguards, distinct from traditional evasion. This blurs the line between adversarial examples and prompt injection -- a distinction the e2023 edition did not address.
- **AI agent threats:** Dedicated coverage of adversarial attacks against autonomous AI agents, including manipulation of agent decision loops and tool invocation chains. This is especially relevant for organizations deploying agentic architectures that fall under requirement 11.2.1's scope.
- **Supply chain attack guidance:** Expanded sections on adversarial risks in AI supply chains -- poisoned pre-trained models, compromised fine-tuning datasets, and backdoor injection during model distribution.
- **Practical emphasis:** Where e2023 was largely theoretical, e2025 includes real-world deployment scenarios, documented failure cases, and reference architectures for enterprise-grade GenAI security.

For auditors verifying requirement 11.2.1 compliance, NIST AI 100-2e2025 now serves as the primary reference taxonomy for scoping adversarial evaluations across both classical ML and generative AI systems.

### Generative AI Attack Surface: Text-to-Image Diffusion Models

Text-to-image diffusion models (Stable Diffusion, DALL-E, Midjourney) have introduced a distinct adversarial attack surface that extends beyond traditional perturbation-based evasion. As of early 2026, the research landscape shows:

- **Safety bypass attacks:** Adversarial prompts and adversarial perturbations to input embeddings can bypass concept-erasure and safety filtering mechanisms with over 90% success rates in controlled experiments. Existing safety mechanisms (NSFW classifiers, concept removal fine-tuning) are vulnerable to adversarial inputs specifically crafted to reverse the erasure.
- **Unlearning robustness:** Transferable adversarial attacks can probe and defeat "machine unlearning" defenses in diffusion models -- for example, restoring the ability to generate specific celebrity likenesses after the model was fine-tuned to remove them. This raises questions about the durability of content policy enforcement via model modification alone.
- **Denoise-and-classify pipelines:** A promising defense direction treats diffusion models as denoising modules in a classification pipeline, using the model's own generative capabilities for input purification. This approach offers model-agnostic adversarial robustness with provable guarantees for downstream classifiers, without requiring adversarial training. The trade-off is added inference latency from the denoising step.
- **Meta-survey findings (2025):** A comprehensive meta-survey of adversarial attacks across AI algorithms, including diffusion models, found that adversarial vulnerabilities are structural rather than model-specific -- every major generative architecture shares susceptibility to input-space and latent-space perturbations.

For organizations deploying generative AI systems, requirement 11.2.1 evaluations should include generative-model-specific attacks (prompt adversarial manipulation, embedding perturbation, safety bypass) alongside traditional evasion techniques. Requirement 11.2.2's runtime detection is particularly important here, as generative models' safety mechanisms are the primary defense layer and adversarial bypass of those mechanisms can have immediate harmful outputs.

### Physical-World Adversarial Attacks: Ongoing Threat

Physical-world adversarial attacks continue to demonstrate real-world impact beyond laboratory settings, reinforcing the practical urgency of requirements 11.2.1 and 11.2.2:

- **Autonomous vehicle lane manipulation:** Adversarial markings on road surfaces have been shown to mislead Tesla Autopilot's visual system, causing the vehicle to follow fake lanes into oncoming traffic. These are not theoretical -- physical experiments on actual roads demonstrate success with carefully positioned perturbations that are inconspicuous to human drivers.
- **Perception Streaming Attacks (PSA):** A 2025 framework for generating adversarial examples against autonomous driving perception systems can erase most objects that should be perceived by the detection model, effectively blinding the vehicle's environmental awareness. Cross-task attacks that transfer across detection, segmentation, and tracking models simultaneously compound the threat.
- **Adversarial wearables for facial recognition:** Adversarial glasses and accessories remain effective against commercial facial recognition systems, enabling identity misclassification and authentication bypass. The attack translates from digital to physical environments with production-ready form factors.
- **Medical imaging manipulation:** Adversarial perturbations applied to chest X-rays and other diagnostic images can fool healthcare AI diagnostic algorithms, with patient safety implications that make requirement 11.2.2's blocking-response mandate especially critical in clinical deployment contexts.

These physical-world attack vectors demonstrate that adversarial robustness is not purely an academic concern. For safety-critical deployments (autonomous vehicles, medical diagnostics, physical access control), requirements 11.2.1 through 11.2.3 should be treated as safety requirements, not just security requirements.

### Regulatory Momentum: EU AI Act Adversarial Testing

As of May 11, 2026, the EU AI Act has established concrete adversarial testing mandates with the next enforcement date less than three months away. For general-purpose AI (GPAI) models classified as posing systemic risk, providers must perform adversarial testing throughout the model lifecycle, including red-teaming exercises simulating malicious use (GPAI rules effective August 2, 2025; enforcement actions for new models begin August 2, 2026). For high-risk AI systems (full enforcement August 2, 2026), performance standards explicitly require resilience against adversarial manipulation, with testing and validation reports covering stress testing under edge cases and adversarial attack scenarios. Non-compliance penalties reach up to EUR 35 million or 7% of global revenue.

The **EU AI Act General-Purpose AI Code of Practice (Final Version)** now has an endorsed compliance role: the European Commission and AI Board confirmed it as an adequate voluntary tool for GPAI providers to demonstrate AI Act compliance. Its Safety and Security chapter is aimed at systemic-risk GPAI models and centers on a safety/security framework, systemic-risk identification, model evaluations, risk acceptance decisions, mitigations, model reports, and incident reporting. The practical effect for requirements 11.2.4 and 11.2.6 is that generic benchmark evaluation is insufficient in the EU jurisdiction; providers need documented adversarial testing, risk-specific model evaluations, post-market monitoring, and enough evidence to show why residual systemic risk is acceptable.

This regulatory environment directly reinforces the C11.2 control chain -- organizations deploying in the EU will need documented adversarial evaluation (11.2.1), runtime detection (11.2.2), hardening procedures (11.2.3), versioned robustness metrics (11.2.4), and adaptive attack evaluation (11.2.6) as compliance obligations, not just best practices.

### MITRE ATLAS v5.4.0: Agentic AI Adversarial Techniques Formalized

MITRE ATLAS published **v5.4.0 in February 2026**, its second major 2026 update, expanding the framework to **16 tactics, 84 techniques, 56 sub-techniques, 32 mitigations, and 42 real-world case studies** -- up from 15 tactics and 66 techniques in October 2025. The January 12, 2026 update (contributed with Zenity Labs) was particularly relevant to adversarial-example hardening because it formalized several execution-layer agentic AI adversarial techniques that had previously been poorly catalogued:

- **AML.T0096 (AI Service API):** Adversaries exploit AI service APIs (e.g., the OpenAI Assistants API) as covert command-and-control channels, evading conventional network detection because traffic blends into legitimate AI workflow patterns.
- **AML.T0098 (AI Agent Tool Credential Harvesting):** Adversaries retrieve credentials, secrets, and API keys from agent-accessible tools -- a post-exploitation pivot once an agent has been adversarially manipulated.
- **AML.T0099 (AI Agent Tool Data Poisoning):** Bad actors place malicious content where agents will invoke it, enabling hijacking or indirect prompt injection of the agent's reasoning chain.
- **AML.T0100 (AI Agent Clickbait):** Adversaries lure AI-browser agents (e.g., web-browsing VLMs) into unintended actions through manipulated UI elements and hidden instructions embedded in page content.
- **AML.T0101 (Data Destruction via AI Agent Tool Invocation):** Attackers use legitimate tool capabilities the agent holds to destroy data or disrupt systems.
- **SesameOp (AML.CS0042):** A new case study documenting a backdoor that repurposes the OpenAI Assistants API for command and control, illustrating AML.T0096 in the wild.

For requirement 11.2.1, the practical implication is that adversarial evaluation scope for agentic systems should now include these agent-specific techniques alongside traditional evasion attacks. For requirement 11.2.6, adaptive attack methodology should explicitly consider the adversary's ability to chain classical adversarial examples with agent-tool manipulation. Promptfoo's ATLAS-aligned LLM red-teaming and MITRE ATLAS Arsenal (CALDERA plugin) have both been updated to cover the new 2026 technique set.

MITRE's May 2026 Secure AI update adds two operational signals for audit programs: ATLAS is moving toward a monthly release cadence, and the April updates added a Technique Maturity filter so teams can distinguish emerging techniques from mature ones. This matters for requirement 11.2.1 scoping. A defensible adversarial-evaluation plan should document why mature and deployment-relevant ATLAS techniques were selected, and why emerging techniques were deferred or handled through watchlist monitoring.

### New Agent & Multi-Modal Adversarial Attack Classes (2025--2026)

The boundary between adversarial examples and prompt injection has continued to dissolve in 2025--2026 research, with several attack classes that are relevant to requirement 11.2.1's "known adversarial attack techniques" scoping:

- **AdvWeb (AdvAgent):** A black-box adversarial framework that uses Direct Policy Optimization (DPO) to train an adversarial prompter which injects invisible adversarial strings into web page content. Reported approximately **97.5% average success rate** against state-of-the-art GPT-4V-based VLM web agents, with attacks stealthy enough that the page's appearance is unchanged pre- and post-injection. The attack allows substring-level control so operators can re-target an exploit (e.g., from "buy stock X" to "transfer funds to account Y") without retraining. For organizations deploying VLM-driven web agents in finance, trading, or e-commerce, this attack class must be in scope under requirements 11.2.1 and 11.2.4.
- **AdvEDM:** A NeurIPS 2025 fine-grained adversarial attack against VLM-based embodied decision-making agents that disrupts chain-of-thought reasoning by selectively removing or adding the semantics of a few key objects while leaving the rest of the scene intact. Because the attack preserves enough context for the VLM to produce valid but wrong decisions, it is relevant to robotics, autonomous systems, and physical-world agents where global anomaly detectors may miss object-level manipulation.
- **Virtual Scenario Hypnosis (VSH):** A Pattern Recognition 2026 jailbreak framework that combines deceptive narratives, adversarial visual encoding, and encrypted in-context learning to coax VLMs into processing harmful queries, reporting more than **82% overall success** across 500 harmful queries. VSH is an example of a cross-modal adversarial attack that a text-only evaluator would not catch -- relevant for the modality-matching criterion in requirement 11.2.1.
- **Black-box LVLM optimization attacks:** EACL 2026 work on ZO-SPSA showed that gradient-free black-box optimization can craft imperceptible adversarial inputs against LVLMs without model internals or a surrogate model, with reported jailbreak success rates up to **83.0%** on InstructBLIP and cross-model transfer from MiniGPT-4 reaching **64.18%**. This pushes requirement 11.2.1 beyond white-box and surrogate-only testing: high-risk VLM deployments need query-budgeted black-box testing, transfer testing, and rate-limit evidence.
- **AdvWave, AudioJailbreak, AJailBench, audio narrative attacks, and real-world audio-LLM attacks:** EACL 2026 work on audio-based LLMs demonstrated targeted and untargeted manipulation through stealthy background noise, including wake-keyword and harmful-command behaviors. Separate EACL 2026 work on audio narrative attacks used synthetic speech to embed disallowed directives in narrative-style audio and reported **98.26%** success against Gemini 2.0 Flash, showing that safety filters calibrated mainly for text can miss paralinguistic attack structure. AudioJailbreak, last revised in February 2026 and accepted by IEEE TDSC, adds asynchronous, universal, stealthy, over-the-air jailbreak audio for end-to-end audio-language models, including weak-adversary settings where the attacker cannot fully control the user's prompt. AJailBench adds a 1,495-sample benchmark and audio perturbation toolkit for evaluating large audio-language model jailbreak robustness. For organizations deploying voice assistants or audio-modal LLMs, the audio attack surface has crossed from laboratory demonstration to deployable threat and belongs explicitly in requirement 11.2.1 scope.
- **Compositional multi-modal attacks:** Work during 2025 has shown that combining adversarial images with benign text (or vice versa) can induce harmful content generation through vulnerabilities in the cross-modal alignment layer, without requiring white-box access to the language model itself. This means a defender cannot treat modality-specific evaluations as independent -- requirement 11.2.1's modality coverage needs an explicit cross-modal evaluation pass.

### LLM-Specific Robustness Benchmarks

In addition to RobustBench (which focuses on Lp-norm vision robustness), a set of LLM-specific adversarial robustness benchmarks has become the de facto standard for requirement 11.2.1 evaluations in the LLM domain:

- **Microsoft PromptBench** provides a unified evaluation library integrating seven adversarial prompt attack types (TextBugger, TextFooler, BertAttack, DeepWordBug, Checklist, StressTest, and semantic attacks) against tasks drawn from GLUE, MMLU, SQuAD, BIG-Bench Hard, and multimodal benchmarks. Three leaderboards (adversarial prompt attack, prompt engineering, dynamic evaluation) provide standardized comparison points. For LLM robustness claims, PromptBench provides the closest equivalent to RobustBench's role in vision.
- **JailbreakBench** is a standardized evaluation framework for jailbreak attacks with a defined threat model, system prompts, chat templates, and scoring functions. Its public leaderboards track attack and defense performance across frontier LLMs.
- **HarmBench and AdvBench** provide standardized frameworks for automated LLM red-teaming across chemical/biological safety, misinformation, cybercrime, illegal activities, and copyright-related harms. These are frequently cited in EU AI Act compliance discussions as examples of "state-of-the-art adversarial techniques" for GPAI systemic-risk evaluation.
- **IndicJR** (EACL 2026 Industry Track) adds a multilingual robustness angle that English-only jailbreak suites miss. It covers 45,216 prompts across 12 Indic and South Asian languages in JSON and naturalistic tracks, and reports strong English-to-Indic attack transfer with refusal collapse in naturalistic prompts. For global deployments, requirement 11.2.1 evaluations should include languages, scripts, transliteration, and code-switching patterns used by the deployed user population.

For requirement 11.2.1 compliance, LLM deployments should be evaluated against at least one of PromptBench, JailbreakBench, or HarmBench in addition to any vision- or multi-modal-specific adversarial testing, and the choice should be documented alongside the threat model.

### ART Tooling Status as of Early 2026

The IBM Adversarial Robustness Toolbox has continued to release incremental updates. **ART 1.20.x** added the **GREAT Score** for measuring robustness against generative models, added support for YOLO v8+ object detection, and retired TensorFlow v1 and MXNet support. Earlier 1.18.x releases added the Overload Attack against object detectors and fast-accurate loss gradients for all-norm Projected Gradient Descent. 1.19.x introduced the Steal Now Attack Later (SNAL) evasion attack, Rescaling Auto Conjugate Gradient (ReACG), and BEYOND detector support in PyTorch.

Organizations evaluating ART for requirement 11.2.1 should confirm they are on a current 1.20+ line; older 1.17 deployments lack the generative-model robustness metric and recent evasion attack implementations that current adversarial evaluations should include.

### Certified Robustness Operations and Tooling

As of May 2026, certified robustness is becoming more operational but remains narrow. **alpha-beta-CROWN** now provides a Python API and reports VNN-COMP 2025 top ranking across scored benchmarks, making it a practical candidate for CI spot-checks on supported neural-network architectures. **auto_LiRPA** remains useful for deriving CROWN/DeepPoly-style bounds on PyTorch computational graphs, while **Marabou** is better suited to smaller SMT-style verification queries and ONNX/VNNLIB workflows. These tools are relevant to requirements 11.2.4 and 11.2.7, but their outputs should be recorded with the exact model hash, perturbation bound, verifier version, timeout, and unresolved/unknown cases.

For empirical robustness metrics, **RobustBench** continues to provide a standardized AutoAttack-based baseline for Lp-norm vision robustness. It should not be treated as a complete adversarial evaluation program: the benchmark itself warns that adaptive attacks remain necessary where defenses may overfit to AutoAttack. A practical release gate records both empirical metrics (clean accuracy, robust accuracy, attack success rate) and certified metrics (certified radius, verified robust accuracy, abstention/unknown rate) so a later model version can be compared without reinterpreting old reports.

Randomized smoothing is operationally useful only when the certificate is tied to a concrete deployment claim. ART exposes randomized-smoothing estimators that return both the selected class and certified radius, while alpha-beta-CROWN covers supported neural-network verification properties with a Python API. The 2026 General Lipschitz work extends smoothing-style certification toward semantic transformations such as blur, translation, and gamma correction. For 11.2.3 and 11.2.4, that means "smoothing enabled" is not evidence by itself; the release record should state the noise distribution or transformation family, sampling budget, abstention policy, confidence level, and the deployment decision that depends on the certificate.

### Production Detection for Adversarial Prompts

As of March 2026, SENTRY-style predictive-entropy detection gives teams another runtime signal for requirement 11.2.2. The approach measures token-level predictive entropy after a short generation window and uses thresholding to separate benign, malicious, and adversarial prompts across model families. This is attractive operationally because it can run without prior knowledge of the exact attack and does not require full adversarial retraining, but it should be deployed as one signal in a layered detector rather than a standalone block decision. Auditors should ask for threshold calibration data, false-positive review, adaptive bypass testing, and latency impact under production traffic.

### Post-Training Transformations and Adapter Backdoors

Recent work has made requirement 11.2.8 more concrete. **On the Robustness Tradeoff in Fine-Tuning** (ICCV 2025) evaluated six benchmark datasets and seven fine-tuning strategies and found a consistent trade-off between clean accuracy and adversarial robustness. The practical takeaway is that fine-tuning strategy is a security decision, not just a performance optimization; teams should re-measure robustness after BitFit, adapters, attention-layer tuning, or full fine-tuning rather than assuming the upstream model's robustness still applies.

Adapter and model-merging workflows add a separate integrity risk. **LoBAM** (ICLR 2025 Workshop) showed that a malicious LoRA-fine-tuned model can compromise a merged model while remaining difficult to detect. **Causal-Guided Detoxify Backdoor Attack of Open-Weight LoRA Models** (NDSS 2026) goes further: it targets shared LoRA adapters, works without original training data, allows post-training control over attack intensity, and reports substantially reduced false-trigger rates against baseline backdoor attacks. As of May 2026, externally sourced LoRA adapters should be treated as executable supply-chain artifacts that need provenance review, behavioral testing, and post-merge adversarial evaluation.

Quantization also changes the backdoor-detection picture. A 2024 open-access study of backdoor defenses on quantized models found that Neural Cleanse was generally less affected by quantization, while ABS, Fine-Pruning, and Trojan Signature degraded or failed on int8 and especially 1-bit models because activation and weight ranking signals became less informative. This directly supports repeating backdoor and robustness audits after quantization, pruning, or compression rather than relying on full-precision evaluation results.

Early LoRA-specific integrity checks are emerging. A February 2026 preprint on weight-space detection of LoRA backdoors screens adapters by spectral statistics of the low-rank updates rather than by running trigger prompts, reporting high accuracy across Llama, Qwen, and Gemma adapter sets. This is promising for large adapter marketplaces, but it is not yet a substitute for behavioral backdoor tests and post-merge evaluation.

### Notable Incidents and Empirical Failures

- **Defensive distillation bypass (2016):** Carlini and Wagner's targeted attack against defensive distillation remains the canonical reminder that a named hardening method is not the same as evidence of robustness. It is still relevant to 11.2.3 because several modern systems use distillation, compression, or teacher/student transfer during deployment.
- **Defense-equipped VLM transfer failures (AAAI 2026):** Multi-Faceted Attack showed that input- and output-filtered VLMs can still be exposed through lightweight transfer enhancement and repetition strategies. That failure mode is a practical test case for 11.2.3 and 11.2.6: input transformation, filtering, and guardrails need cross-model and defense-aware attacks, not only clean benchmark validation.
- **Physical-world safety failures:** Road-surface lane manipulation, adversarial wearables, and medical-image perturbations continue to show that hardening must be evaluated in the actual sensing and deployment path. Digital-only perturbation budgets are useful for comparability but do not fully represent cameras, microphones, compression, lighting, or human-in-the-loop operations.

### Implementation Maturity

- **Mature for narrow vision classifiers:** ART, AutoAttack, RobustBench, randomized smoothing, and alpha-beta-CROWN make adversarial testing and bounded certificates practical for supported image classifiers and smaller safety-critical components.
- **Developing for LLMs, VLMs, and audio-language models:** PromptBench, JailbreakBench, HarmBench, garak, PyRIT, ARMs, and modality-specific research benchmarks provide useful empirical coverage, but certification and hardening evidence remain property-specific and threat-model-specific.
- **Emerging for agentic systems:** Tool-use, browsing, memory, and multi-agent coordination attacks do not fit neatly into classical adversarial-example assumptions. Hardening evidence should combine model-level tests, guardrail tests, tool permission checks, and incident-response exercises.
- **Weak as standalone controls:** Defensive distillation, feature squeezing, JPEG compression, prompt filters, and detector-only approaches should be treated as supporting controls unless they survive adaptive, transfer, and query-based black-box testing.

## Open Research Questions

- Can adversarial robustness for LLMs be meaningfully quantified, or is it fundamentally different from the Lp-norm robustness studied in vision?
- How should organizations prioritize adversarial hardening when clean-accuracy trade-offs exist -- what is an acceptable accuracy loss for a given robustness gain?
- Will formal verification methods ever scale to production-sized models, or will empirical evaluation remain the practical ceiling?
- How do adversarial examples interact with multi-modal models that accept both text and images -- does one modality create attack surfaces for the other?
- What is the relationship between adversarial robustness and out-of-distribution generalization -- can improving one harm the other?
- How should adversarial preference learning (integrating robustness into RLHF) be evaluated -- do alignment-focused adversarial defenses transfer to traditional evasion robustness?
- Can diffusion-based purification defenses like DiffuseDef scale to real-time inference workloads without unacceptable latency?
- How should organizations defend against self-supervised transfer attacks (e.g., AnyAttack) that require no target-model access and transfer across commercial systems?
- As the EU AI Act mandates adversarial testing, what constitutes a "sufficient" evaluation methodology -- and who certifies the adequacy of red-team exercises?
- Can randomized smoothing's recent scaling advances for VLMs extend to certifying robustness of full generative outputs, or will certification remain limited to classification-like properties?
- How effective are training-free detection methods (MirrorCheck, feature squeezing) against adaptive adversaries who know the defense is deployed -- and what is the ceiling for detection without model modification?
- As MITRE ATLAS expands to 66+ techniques covering agentic AI, how should adversarial evaluation scope be defined -- test all relevant techniques, or prioritize based on deployment context?
- Can synthetic data generation (diffusion models) consistently improve certified robustness bounds across architectures, or is this limited to specific model families and perturbation types?
- How should adversarial evaluation scope expand to cover text-to-image diffusion models -- is safety bypass (prompt adversarial manipulation) fundamentally different from traditional evasion, or can existing frameworks accommodate it?
- As NIST AI 100-2e2025 introduces "misuse violations" as a distinct attack category, how should organizations draw the boundary between adversarial examples and intentional misuse in their evaluation programs?
- What is the minimum viable adversarial testing program for physical-world deployments (autonomous vehicles, medical devices) -- and how should physical-world perturbation budgets be defined when Lp norms are meaningless in 3D environments?
- How should adversarial evaluation scope expand for audio-modal LLMs now that over-the-air acoustic adversarial attacks achieve ~87--88% success rates -- and is there an equivalent to RobustBench for audio robustness claims?
- As agent-specific adversarial techniques (MITRE ATLAS AML.T0096--T0101) become the dominant attack class against deployed AI systems, how should requirement 11.2.1's modality-focused scoping evolve to cover tool-use and multi-step reasoning attack surfaces?
- What constitutes a "state-of-the-art adversarial technique" under the EU AI Act GPAI Code of Practice -- which benchmarks, attack libraries, and methodologies satisfy the bar, and who makes that determination in practice?
- If weak VLM defenses can be converted into stronger jailbreak guidance, how should runtime detectors be evaluated without revealing enough structure to make adaptive attacks easier?
- How can federated medical VLM operators measure site-specific adversarial robustness without sharing protected clinical data or masking robustness failures through global aggregation?
- Can LoRA adapter screening scale to open model marketplaces without unacceptable false positives, and how should weight-space findings be combined with behavioral trigger search?
- Which post-training transformations preserve certified robustness under documented conditions, and when must every transformed artifact be treated as a new model for certification purposes?
