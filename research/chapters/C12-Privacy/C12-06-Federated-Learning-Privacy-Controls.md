# C12.6: Federated Learning with Privacy Controls

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-05-01 (updated with IEEE S&P 2026 ARES, TPAMI 2026 GIA survey, Shadow Defense, and 2026 certified federated unlearning)

## Purpose

Apply differential privacy and poisoning-resistant aggregation to federated learning (FL) to protect individual participant data. FL avoids centralizing raw data, but gradient updates still leak sensitive information. Zhu et al. (2019) demonstrated pixel-accurate reconstruction of training images from shared gradients; Deng et al. (2021) showed text data can be recovered from BERT-style gradients. Research has accelerated since: the 2025 COLING paper "Exposing Text Data through Discrete Optimization" pushed text reconstruction exact-match rates up by 39% on TinyBERT-6, 20% on BERT-base, and 15% on BERT-large over prior baselines, and the ICCV 2025 paper "Geminio: Language-Guided Gradient Inversion Attacks" introduced attacks that use natural-language prompts to target specific content in gradients. MMGIA (IJCAI 2025) exploits intermodal correlations to invert gradients in multimodal FL, widening the attack surface beyond single-modality classifiers. A 2025 medical-imaging study on retinal federated learning reconstructed images well enough to re-identify 92% of participants — a useful reality check for "FL is private by design" claims. The IEEE TPAMI 2026 survey "Exploring the Vulnerabilities of Federated Learning: A Deep Dive into Gradient Inversion Attacks" now organizes the field into optimization-based (OP-GIA), generation-based (GEN-GIA), and analytics-based (ANA-GIA) families and is the most current taxonomy reference. Two 2026 attacks reset the bar: ARES (Gong, Zhang et al., IEEE S&P 2026) recovers training samples from large batches by formulating activation recovery as a noisy sparse-recovery (generalized Lasso) problem, working without architectural modifications and significantly outperforming prior attacks at realistic FL batch sizes; and a verifiable gradient-inversion construction (arXiv 2604.15063, 2026) lets an attacker prove a successful reconstruction to a third party.

Google's Gboard remains the canonical production reference: as of 2024-2025, all production Gboard neural language models trained on user data ship with formal DP guarantees via DP-FTRL and its successor BLT-DP-FTRL, with more than twenty deployed models achieving ρ-zCDP in the range ρ ∈ (0.2, 2) and two additionally using secure aggregation. Apple's Private Cloud Compute (PCC) takes a different path: end-to-end encrypted on-device-to-enclave inference on Apple Silicon with verifiable transparency, no persistent storage, and stateless computation — sidestepping gradient sharing entirely rather than hardening it. NIST SP 800-226 "Guidelines for Evaluating Differential Privacy Guarantees" was finalized in March 2025 and is now the baseline reference for evaluating ε claims, including for privacy-preserving machine learning; it introduces the "DP pyramid" and a catalog of privacy hazards that practitioners commonly miss. Federated Unlearning (FUL) has matured into a recognized subfield (see the 2024-2025 ACM Computing Surveys review and the PoPETs 2025 paper on unlearning clients, features, and samples in vertical FL) but production tooling for certified client removal remains thin.

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| **12.6.1** | **Verify that** client updates apply local differential privacy noise addition before aggregation, or that a documented central differential privacy mechanism is enforced at the aggregator. | 1 | Gradient inversion attacks reconstructing training data from gradient updates. Zhu et al. (2019) demonstrated gradient-inverted image reconstruction; Deng et al. (2021) extended this to text. The 2025 COLING "Discrete Optimization" attack raised text exact-match recovery by 15-39% across BERT variants. ICCV 2025 "Geminio" introduced language-guided gradient inversion that lets an attacker steer reconstruction toward specific content. MMGIA (IJCAI 2025) exploits intermodal correlation in multimodal FL. A 2025 retinal-image FL study re-identified 92% of participants from inverted gradients. ARES (IEEE S&P 2026) scales gradient inversion to realistic large-batch FL settings via Lasso-based activation recovery, undermining the "large batch + DP" defense pattern that many production deployments still rely on. The IEEE TPAMI 2026 survey ("Exploring the Vulnerabilities of Federated Learning") consolidates the OP-GIA, GEN-GIA, and ANA-GIA families. Maps to MITRE ATLAS AML.T0024 (Exfiltration via ML Inference API) and adjacent membership-inference techniques. | Inspect client-side code for DP noise injection (Gaussian or Laplacian) applied to clipped gradients before transmission. Verify noise calibration matches declared epsilon using the privacy-accountant output (RDP, zCDP, or PLD). Capture client updates with and without noise and confirm statistical indistinguishability at the claimed level. Reproduce a public gradient-inversion attack (e.g., DLG, iDLG, ARES, or the 2025 Geminio code) against the noised updates and confirm reconstruction quality is below a documented threshold (PSNR/SSIM/LPIPS for vision, exact-match/ROUGE for text). Reference Google's distributed DP deployment for Gboard language models — all production Gboard NWP and key-decoder models trained on user data now ship under DP-FTRL or BLT-DP-FTRL — as a production benchmark. | Local DP adds more noise per update than central DP, so maintaining utility requires many more participants. Noise calibration depends on the gradient-clipping bound, which trades off privacy and convergence — too-aggressive clipping harms utility, too-loose clipping weakens privacy. Flower's 2026 release (Feb 7, 2026) includes fixed and adaptive clipping DP strategies on both server and client sides; Opacus (Fast Gradient Clipping and Ghost Clipping, Aug 2024; LoRA+peft+DP-SGD tutorial, Dec 2024) reduces DP-SGD memory cost and integrates cleanly with Flower for sample-level DP. TensorFlow Federated, NVIDIA FLARE (differential privacy + homomorphic encryption + PSI; the Flower-on-FLARE bridge lets Flower DP/SecAgg apps run unmodified on the FLARE runtime), and PySyft are the other major frameworks. For sample-targeted noise on medical imaging — where uniform DP destroys diagnostic utility — Shadow Defense (Hu et al., Medical Image Analysis, Oct 2025; arXiv 2506.15711) uses an interpretable shadow model to inject sample-specific noise and reports +3.73 PSNR / +0.2 SSIM headroom on ChestXRay vs. uniform noise baselines. Apple's PCC avoids the gradient-sharing problem entirely via encrypted on-device-to-enclave inference. |
| **12.6.2** | **Verify that** training metrics shared with the aggregator or with clients are differentially private and never reveal single-client loss or single-client gradients. | 2 | Per-client loss and metric leakage enabling membership inference and property inference. Melis et al. (2019) showed unprotected FL metrics reveal property-level information about participants' data; aggregated-but-unnoised metrics still leak information about outlier clients. Operations dashboards, A/B logs, and model-quality telemetry are common leak points overlooked by teams that only protected model parameters. | Review the metric-aggregation pipeline and confirm DP noise is applied before reporting. Attempt to isolate single-client contributions from aggregated metrics using known outlier or canary clients and confirm their influence is masked. Audit metric dashboards and logging systems for per-client loss values, per-client confusion matrices, or per-client sample counts. For stronger protection, verify a secure-aggregation protocol (Bonawitz et al. 2017 SecAgg or a 2024-2025 successor such as the single-setup SecAgg with forward/backward secrecy in IACR ePrint 2024/1655 and arXiv 2502.08989) hides per-client contributions even from the server. | Noisy metrics make debugging harder — teams need to balance observability with privacy, typically by running a trusted eval set in the clear on the server. Secure aggregation protocols add cryptographic overhead and client-dropout handling complexity; the Bonawitz design requires a per-round key-setup phase that dominates cost at scale. Forward-/backward-secret single-setup variants (2025) address this but are not yet default in mainstream FL frameworks. Metric privacy is still widely overlooked in FL deployments. |
| **12.6.3** | **Verify that** federated learning systems implement canary-based privacy auditing to empirically bound privacy leakage, with audit results logged and reviewed per training cycle. | 3 | Vacuous or over-stated theoretical ε guarantees that hide real leakage in production FL. Theoretical DP analysis is conservative and frequently overestimates the privacy that an actual adversary can break — without empirical audit, teams ship "ε = 1" claims that come from loose composition, miss-applied sub-sampling amplification, or accountant bugs. CANIFE (Maddock, Sablayrolles, Stock, ICLR 2023) showed empirical per-round ε in real FL pipelines can be 4-5× lower than the theoretical bound, while related work (Steinke, Nasr, Jagielski — "Privacy Auditing with One (1) Training Run," NeurIPS 2023 outstanding paper) and "Auditing f-Differential Privacy in One Run" (NeurIPS 2024) show the inverse failure mode: cases where empirical ε̂ exceeds claimed ε, exposing real bugs. The 2025 "How Well Can DP Be Audited in One Run?" analysis and "Tight Privacy Audit in One Run" (arXiv 2509.08704, Sep 2025) further demonstrate that earlier one-run audits can fail to give meaningful results under specific (ε, δ) configurations — meaning even a "passing" audit may be uninformative if the test setup is weak. Maps to MITRE ATLAS membership-inference and exfiltration techniques (AML.T0024 family). | Require an audit report per training cycle that estimates ε̂ as an empirical lower bound at a stated confidence level (typically 95% via Clopper-Pearson on the binomial test in canary-detection auditing, or f-DP confidence intervals as in arXiv 2410.22235). Confirm the methodology — random gradient canaries on the unit sphere with cosine-similarity test statistic (Andrew, Kairouz et al., "One-shot Empirical Privacy Estimation for Federated Learning," ICLR 2024 / arXiv 2302.03098), CANIFE-style crafted canaries (https://github.com/facebookresearch/canife), or metagradient-optimized canaries (arXiv 2507.15836, NeurIPS 2025) — and that the number of canaries scales appropriately (k ≈ √d for the one-shot cosine method on d-dimensional models, ~1000 typical). Verify ε̂ is consistent with the declared theoretical ε within documented tolerance and that any ε̂ > ε triggers a privacy incident. Cross-check against NIST SP 800-226 §6 ("How Should I Evaluate the Privacy of a Particular Use of DP?") which now treats empirical audits as a recommended complement to formal accounting. Reproduce the audit run from source if possible and confirm canaries are inserted with realistic per-round participation patterns (not all canaries every round — that inflates leakage). | Empirical ε̂ is a lower bound, not an upper bound — passing an audit does not prove ε ≤ claimed value, only that the specific attack used could not extract more leakage. Audit strength depends heavily on canary quality: random canaries underestimate leakage on low-dimensional models; CANIFE and metagradient canaries (Boglioni-Liu-Ilyas-Wu 2025, >2× improvement over random in some regimes) tighten bounds at the cost of compute. Practical auditing libraries: Meta's open-source canife (https://github.com/facebookresearch/canife) targets DP-FL specifically, JAX-Privacy 1.0 (Google DeepMind, 2026) ships clipping/noise/accounting/auditing primitives in one library, TensorFlow Privacy 0.12.3 splits into `tensorflow-privacy` and `tensorflow-empirical-privacy` for the audit side, and Trail of Bits' PrivacyRaven covers membership inference and model extraction (centralized but reusable patterns). Flower and NVIDIA FLARE provide DP training but no built-in canary auditing as of 2026 — teams currently bolt on Opacus + canife or one-shot canary clients themselves. Sequential auditing (arXiv 2509.07055, 2025) can detect violations within a few hundred gradient steps, useful for fast feedback during development. Honest reporting: many production FL deployments do not audit at all; even "we use DP" announcements rarely include ε̂ numbers. EU AI Act Article 27 FRIA + GDPR Article 35 DPIA (both required for Annex III high-risk systems by August 2, 2026) increasingly expect documented privacy testing, not just paper proofs. |
| **12.6.4** | **Verify that** formal proofs document the overall ε budget and the corresponding utility loss against a declared baseline. | 3 | Excessive utility loss from privacy mechanisms incentivizing teams to weaken or disable them. Without a formal analysis that ties ε to measured utility, DP settings tend to drift toward looser values over time. This is also the requirement that surfaces epsilon-inflation bugs — naive composition, missed sub-sampling amplification, and double-counted queries all produce falsely small reported ε. | Request a formal privacy analysis with a utility-impact assessment. Verify ε is computed using a tight accountant — Rényi DP (RDP), zero-concentrated DP (zCDP), Gaussian DP, or Privacy Loss Distributions (PLD) — not naive composition. Reproduce the accountant run from source if possible. Compare model accuracy (task-specific metrics: F1, AUC, perplexity, BLEU, NDCG) between DP and non-DP runs and confirm utility loss is within the threshold. Walk through NIST SP 800-226 (finalized March 2025) and its accompanying Jupyter notebooks as the structured evaluation baseline. Cross-check against Google's Gboard production numbers (ρ-zCDP with ρ ∈ (0.2, 2), dozens of models in production as of 2024-2025) for expected privacy-utility frontiers on language tasks. | The 5% threshold is application-dependent: safety-critical models may not tolerate 1%, recommender systems can tolerate 10%+. Tight accountants (RDP, Gaussian DP, PLD) can reduce reported ε by an order of magnitude vs. naive composition. DP-FTRL, BLT-DP-FTRL, and DP-Adam optimizers improve the frontier; large-batch training and synthetic-data pretraining (as in Google's Gboard 2024-2025 work) push it further. For vertical FL and feature/sample-level unlearning, PoPETs 2025 (popets-2025-0048) gives evaluation methodology. Honest reporting: many public "ε = 1" claims come from loose composition — require auditability of the accounting, not just the final number. EU AI Act high-risk systems (Annex III, applicable August 2, 2026) trigger both an FRIA (Article 27) and a GDPR DPIA (Article 35) on any personal-data processing in federated training. |

## Tooling

- **FL frameworks:** Flower (flwr, Feb 2026 release; fixed + adaptive DP clipping, message-based FedAvg/FedOpt/FedAdam/FedAdagrad/FedYogi with DP), TensorFlow Federated, NVIDIA FLARE (DP + HE + PSI, 2025 biopharma deployments documented in J. Biopharm Stat.), PySyft (OpenMined), FedML.
- **DP libraries:** Opacus (Meta-PyTorch, Fast Gradient Clipping and Ghost Clipping reduce DP-SGD memory; LoRA+peft+DP-SGD tutorial Dec 2024), TensorFlow Privacy 0.12.3 (per-example gradient clipping in Keras; the library is now split into `tensorflow-privacy` for DP training and `tensorflow-empirical-privacy` for empirical analysis), JAX-Privacy 1.0 (Google DeepMind, 2026 — one library covering clipping, noise addition, batch selection, accounting, and auditing), Google's DP library, OpenMined PyDP.
- **Privacy auditing libraries:** Meta `canife` (https://github.com/facebookresearch/canife — crafted-canary DP-FL auditing, modified FLSim base), Trail of Bits PrivacyRaven (membership inference, label-only black-box model extraction), JAX-Privacy auditing module (one-shot empirical ε̂), `tensorflow-empirical-privacy` (membership-inference attack reports), and reference implementations of one-shot cosine-canary auditing (Andrew, Kairouz et al. 2024) and metagradient canary optimization (NeurIPS 2025).
- **Secure aggregation:** Original Bonawitz SecAgg (2017), single-setup SecAgg with forward/backward secrecy (arXiv 2502.08989, 2025), verifiable SecAgg variants (ScienceDirect 2025).
- **Robust aggregation:** Multi-Krum, Trimmed-Mean, coordinate-wise median, Bulyan, FLTrust, FedGreed (arXiv 2508.18060), GAN-based defenses (arXiv 2503.20884).
- **Privacy-preserving inference alternatives:** Apple Private Cloud Compute (Apple Silicon + Secure Enclave + verifiable transparency + stateless computation), Azure Confidential Computing, AWS Nitro Enclaves, GCP Confidential Space, GPU-accelerated FHE for LLM inference (OpenFHE — as of 2026, practical for medium-sized models or short sequences, still far slower than cleartext).
- **Federated unlearning:** Still mostly research-stage but maturing fast. See ACM Computing Surveys (2024) and PoPETs 2025 for vertical-FL client/feature/sample removal. Certified federated-recommendation unlearning landed in ACM TOIS 2025 (CFRU). 2026 brings two notable additions: certified unlearning in decentralized FL via Newton-style updates with Fisher-information curvature approximations (arXiv 2601.06436), and the 2F2L / FedRemoval PyTorch implementation (https://github.com/Nanboy-Ronan/2F2L-Federated-Unlearning) which linearizes a pretrained network and runs server-only, communication-free certified deletion via a Newton-style correction over server-held linearized statistics. Still no single widely-adopted library — production deployments typically bolt this on themselves.
