# C3.6: Fine-Tuning Pipeline Authorization & Reward Model Integrity

> [Back to C03 Index](C03-Model-Lifecycle-Management.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-15 (refreshed)

## Purpose

Fine-tuning pipelines are high-privilege operations that can alter deployed model behavior at scale. Multi-stage pipelines compound this risk because a compromise at any intermediate stage produces a subtly altered artifact that subsequent stages accept. Reward models used in RLHF are ML artifacts subject to tampering yet often treated as static infrastructure rather than versioned, validated components.

This section addresses the separation of duties for fine-tuning authorization, the integrity of reward models used in RLHF, automated detection of reward hacking and over-optimization, and per-stage integrity verification in multi-stage pipelines. Research has demonstrated that fine-tuning can remove RLHF safety protections, and the Checkpoint-GCG attack (2025) showed that intermediate fine-tuning checkpoints can be exploited as stepping stones for adversarial attacks. Anthropic's natural emergent misalignment research (November 2025) demonstrated that reward hacking during production RL training correlates with sharp increases across all misalignment evaluations, with alignment-faking reasoning appearing in 50% of responses — even though the model was never trained to exhibit those behaviors. Cisco research (2025) found that fine-tuned LLM variants produced harmful outputs at over 22 times the rate of the base model, underscoring that alignment safeguards reduce but do not fundamentally remove harmful patterns. The ProAttack technique (March 2026) demonstrated that as few as six poisoned samples can embed undetectable backdoors during fine-tuning while preserving correct labels and natural text. A complementary harmless-input poisoning framework (Kong et al., 2025) showed that backdoors can be implanted using exclusively benign training data — associating triggers with affirmative prefixes rather than harmful content — achieving 86–100% attack success rates while evading both safety alignment checks and guardrail filtering entirely. The BackdoorLLM benchmark (NeurIPS 2025) systematized this threat landscape with over 200 experiments spanning 8 attack strategies and 6 model architectures, alongside the Backdoor-DefenseBox toolkit integrating 7 defense techniques. The industry's gradual shift from RLHF toward RLVR (Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards), where programmatic verifiers replace human preference models, introduces a new integrity surface: verifier assets themselves become high-value targets requiring the same signing and versioning rigor applied to reward models.

OMS plans to extend to dataset signatures and other ML-related artifacts, which would eventually cover reward training data. The COBRA framework (consensus-based reward, published in Nature Scientific Reports 2025) addresses malicious RLHF feedback by aggregating across annotator pools, but does not replace cryptographic integrity on the reward model artifact itself. Most MLOps platforms (MLflow, W&B) can version reward models, but few organizations explicitly include them in signing and verification workflows. | **3.6.3** | **Verify that** RLHF training stages include automated detection of reward hacking or reward model over-optimization (e.g., held-out human-preference probe sets, divergence thresholds, or KL penalty monitoring), with the run blocked from promotion if detection thresholds are exceeded. | 3 | Reward hacking: the policy model exploits spurious correlations in the reward model (length bias, sycophancy, code gaming) to achieve high reward scores while violating genuine human preferences; Anthropic's emergent misalignment research (November 2025) showed misalignment spikes at the exact point reward hacking is learned — with sabotaged reward-hacking classifiers only 65% as effective as clean ones; over-optimization causing Goodhart's Law failures where the model optimizes the proxy reward at the expense of actual alignment; subtly misaligned models passing superficial evaluations but behaving unsafely in production edge cases. MITRE ATLAS [AML.T0043](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0043) (Craft Adversarial Data). | Inspect the RLHF training pipeline for reward hacking detection mechanisms. Verify at least one detection approach is implemented: held-out human-preference probe sets evaluated periodically during training, KL divergence penalty monitoring against the reference policy, reward score distribution analysis for outlier detection (InfoRM's Mahalanobis Outlier Probability), interpretable reward reconstruction (IR³'s contrastive inverse RL achieves 0.89 ground-truth correlation and 90%+ precision identifying hacking features), or dedicated detection frameworks (ARA Auditor networks, dedicated reward-hacking classifiers). Confirm that exceeding detection thresholds automatically blocks the run from promotion to production. Test by injecting a training run with known reward-hacking characteristics and verifying the detection gate fires. Review historical training logs for detection metric trends. Consider implementing Anthropic's three effective mitigations: preventing reward hacking directly, increasing diversity of RLHF safety training, and inoculation prompting. | Active research area with significant advances through early 2026. IR³ (Beigi et al., February 2026) introduces contrastive inverse reinforcement learning to reverse-engineer, interpret, and surgically repair implicit reward objectives — reconstructing rewards with 0.89 ground-truth correlation, identifying hacking features with over 90% precision, and reducing hacking behaviors while maintaining capabilities within 3% of the original model. Bayesian Non-Negative Reward Modeling (BNRM, Duan et al., February 2026) integrates non-negative factor analysis into the Bradley-Terry preference model, using instance-specific disentanglement and global sparsity constraints to suppress spurious correlations like length bias and stylistic patterns, yielding more interpretable and robust reward decompositions. Liu et al. (ICLR 2026) formalize reward hacking as robust optimization over correlated proxy rewards via a max-min formulation, providing stronger worst-case guarantees than fixed-proxy approaches like ORPO. Adversarial Reward Auditing (ARA, February 2026) formulates reward hacking as a competitive Hacker-vs-Auditor game, achieving the best alignment-utility tradeoff among baselines with cross-domain transfer. SignCert-PO (April 2026) computes certified sign-preservation radii for advantage estimates and downweights non-robust completions during policy gradient updates — lightweight, operating purely at the policy optimization stage. Preference As Reward (PAR) leverages latent preferences embedded within the reward model rather than raw scalar scores, achieving win rates 5+ percentage points higher than competing approaches on AlpacaEval 2.0. InfoRM uses a variational information bottleneck to filter spurious reward signals and introduces Mahalanobis Outlier Probability (MOP) as a quantitative metric for reward hacking severity. Anthropic found that penalizing reward hacking with an HHH preference model reward or a dedicated reward-hacking classifier reduces misaligned generalization by over 75%. Standard KL penalty monitoring (PPO with KL constraint) remains the most widely deployed approach but is insufficient alone. Safety-Aware Probing (SAP, May 2025) offers a complementary defense angle: a bi-level optimization framework that injects hidden-state perturbations during fine-tuning to steer gradient updates away from harmful parameter directions, reducing average harmful scores from 32.5% to 23.1% across Llama-2, Vicuna, and Qwen2.5. The ProAttack backdoor technique (March 2026) demonstrated that as few as 6 poisoned samples can embed undetectable backdoors during fine-tuning by preserving correct labels and natural text; LoRA fine-tuning was identified as a partial defense since restricting parameter updates to low-rank matrices limits trigger-target associations. Level 3 reflects the research maturity gap — detection frameworks exist but few are production-hardened. *(Updated 2026-04)* |
| **3.6.4** | **Verify that** in multi-stage fine-tuning pipelines, each stage's output is integrity-verified before the next stage consumes it, and intermediate checkpoints are registered as distinct artifacts enabling per-stage rollback. | 3 | Compromise at any intermediate fine-tuning stage producing a subtly altered artifact that subsequent stages accept as legitimate; inability to isolate which pipeline stage introduced a safety regression; Checkpoint-GCG attack (2025) demonstrated that intermediate fine-tuning checkpoints can be exploited as stepping stones for adversarial attacks by using GCG optimization with each checkpoint as initialization for the next; cascading corruption across pipeline stages without detection; inability to perform per-stage rollback when the final artifact fails validation; SafeMoE research (2025-2026) showed that MoE LLM safety depends on routing harmful inputs to safety-critical experts — routing drift at any pipeline stage can silently compromise this mechanism. | Inspect multi-stage fine-tuning pipeline for checkpoint handling: verify each stage's output is registered as a distinct artifact in the model registry with a unique version/digest. Confirm integrity verification (checksum or cryptographic signature check via OMS/Sigstore) occurs before a downstream stage consumes the checkpoint. Test by corrupting an intermediate checkpoint and verifying the next stage rejects it. Verify per-stage rollback capability by rolling back to a specific intermediate checkpoint and confirming the pipeline can resume from that point. Review artifact lineage to confirm each stage's input and output are linked. For MoE architectures, verify that routing distributions for safety-critical inputs are monitored across stages to detect drift (SafeMoE-style KL-divergence checks). | DevSecMLOps frameworks (OpenSSF MLSecOps, 2025) recommend integrating security checks directly within ML pipeline stages rather than only at pipeline boundaries. OpenSSF Model Signing (OMS) v1.0 (April 2025) creates detached signature bundles with per-file cryptographic hashes, applicable to intermediate checkpoints as distinct artifacts — though OMS currently focuses on final model artifacts and plans to extend to datasets and other ML-related artifacts. NVIDIA adopted OMS for all NGC Catalog models (March 2025), establishing an industry baseline for model artifact signing. AWS SageMaker and similar platforms support checkpointing with fine-tuning, but checkpoint integrity verification between pipeline stages remains custom logic. The SEAL framework (ICLR 2025) proposes safety-enhanced alignment that optimizes models to occupy flat loss regions resistant to fine-tuning perturbation, offering a complementary defense at the alignment stage. For MoE architectures, SafeMoE demonstrates that routing-aware regularization can preserve safety-critical expert activation across fine-tuning stages with only 2% computational overhead. The "Attesting LLM Pipelines" framework (Tan et al., LLMSC 2026) proposes attestation-aware promotion gates that cryptographically bind training and release claims to artifacts before they enter trusted environments — covering data lineage, build environment provenance, and security scanning results — and optionally incorporating runtime security signals for high-risk artifacts. Chainguard's ML pipeline maturity analysis (2026) highlights that PyTorch's continued default use of pickle serialization creates arbitrary code execution risk at every deserialization point; hardened alternatives like safetensors reduce the attack surface but haven't become the pipeline default. The main challenge remains that multi-stage pipelines (e.g., SFT → reward model training → RLHF → DPO refinement) may span different compute environments and teams, and SLSA-style signing at each handoff point requires organizational maturity that most ML teams lack today. The harmless-input poisoning framework (Kong et al., 2025) compounds this challenge: because backdoors can now be implanted using only benign training data (86–100% ASR while evading guardrails), integrity verification between stages must go beyond simple checksum validation to include behavioral testing — the BackdoorLLM benchmark (NeurIPS 2025) provides a systematic framework with 8 attack strategies and 7 defense techniques for this purpose. Level 3 reflects the tooling and organizational maturity required for consistent cross-stage integrity verification. *(Updated 2026-04)* |
