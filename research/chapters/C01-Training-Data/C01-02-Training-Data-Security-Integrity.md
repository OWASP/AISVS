# C1.2: Training Data Security & Integrity

> [Back to C01 Index](C01-Training-Data.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-05-13

## Purpose

Restricting access to training data, encrypting it at rest and in transit, and validating its integrity are fundamental controls against tampering, theft, and data poisoning. A compromised training dataset can silently degrade model behavior, and unlike a conventional data breach, the damage may persist in model weights long after the poisoned data is removed from storage.

This section covers the full lifecycle of training data security: access controls and audit logging on storage and pipelines, encryption standards, cryptographic integrity verification, automated integrity monitoring, secure purging of data no longer required for the model's stated purpose, and immutable versioning for rollback and forensics. The machine unlearning problem makes prevention and detection controls here especially important: once poisoned or disallowed data trains a model, remediation is usually expensive and only partially verifiable.

As of May 2026, the defensive baseline is becoming clearer. The May 2025 joint CISA/NSA/FBI guidance on AI data security treats the data supply chain, maliciously modified data, and data drift as the three central data-security risk areas, and recommends provenance tracking, cryptographic hashes, quantum-resistant digital signatures for trusted data revisions, zero-trust processing environments, AES-256 encryption, TLS, secure deletion, and continuous risk assessment. MITRE ATLAS 5.6.0 tracks `AML.T0020 Poison Training Data`, `AML.T0019 Publish Poisoned Datasets`, and `AML.T0010.002 AI Supply Chain Compromise: Data` as realized or demonstrated techniques. Recent research also changed the attack math: OATML, Anthropic, the UK AI Security Institute, and the Alan Turing Institute reported in October 2025 that about 250 malicious documents could backdoor models from 600M to 13B parameters, while a January 2025 *Nature Medicine* study found harmful medical behavior after replacing only 0.001% of training tokens with misinformation. EU AI Act Article 10 obligations for high-risk systems enter into force on 2 August 2026, so dataset-security evidence now has a near-term regulatory deadline tied to intended purpose, data origin, preparation, bias controls, and documented deletion of special-category personal data when retention is no longer justified.

## Current Research Notes

* **Integrity controls need to be pipeline controls, not only storage controls.** Cloud object storage can enforce strong encryption, IAM, audit logging, versioning, and WORM retention, but most poisoning paths run through ingestion jobs, feature/embedding pipelines, labeling queues, temporary caches, and training-node scratch space. Evidence should show the same access, logging, and integrity checks at each handoff.
* **Dataset signing is improving, but still lacks one dominant workflow.** OpenSSF's June 2025 OMS specification describes a detached signature format that can bind related AI artifacts, including model weights, configuration files, tokenizers, and datasets, into one verifiable unit. For large or mutable training datasets, auditors should still expect signed manifests, per-shard hashes, Merkle-tree commits, or lakehouse snapshot IDs rather than assuming one ecosystem standard covers every storage pattern.
* **Secure purge should be tested against purpose, not age alone.** The current AISVS wording asks whether training data is still required for the model's stated purpose. That shifts review evidence toward retention schedules, purpose registers, deletion tickets, lineage tombstones, and proof that purge or anonymization reached derived shards, embeddings, feature stores, fine-tuning sets, and backups.
* **Sanitization evidence should use the current NIST baseline.** NIST SP 800-88 Rev. 2 was finalized in September 2025 and supersedes Rev. 1. For reusable drives, removable media, training-node disks, and decommissioned storage appliances, audit evidence should cite Rev. 2 methods such as clear, purge, destroy, and cryptographic erase where they match the media and confidentiality risk.
* **Versioning and immutability are separate claims.** DVC, lakeFS, Delta Lake, and Iceberg can give reproducible version IDs and rollback points. True tamper resistance still needs an append-only or WORM backing store, such as S3 Object Lock Compliance Mode, Azure immutable blob policies, or GCS retention locks, plus logs that capture who read, wrote, deleted, or restored each version.

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| **1.2.1** | **Verify that** access controls protect training data storage and pipelines. | 1 | Unauthorized access leading to data theft, exfiltration of proprietary datasets, or injection of poisoned samples. Map this to MITRE ATLAS `AML.T0020 Poison Training Data`, `AML.T0019 Publish Poisoned Datasets`, and `AML.T0010.002 AI Supply Chain Compromise: Data`; poisoned data may arrive through public datasets, compromised labeling providers, or post-ingest write access to private stores. | Review IAM policies on data storage (S3, GCS, Azure Blob, data lake tables), pipeline orchestrators (Airflow, Dagster, Kubeflow, Metaflow), labeling tools, feature stores, and training-node service accounts. Confirm least privilege, MFA/conditional access for admin roles, separate read/write roles, scoped temporary credentials, and no shared long-lived keys in notebooks or CI. Test whether a pipeline identity can write outside its approved dataset prefix or branch. | Cloud IAM tooling is mature. The gap is pipeline-level access control: ML orchestrators often have broader permissions than storage layers, and Kubeflow or Airflow service accounts are rarely scoped per dataset. LoRA/adapters, embedding stores, and RLHF preference stores are common blind spots because they can materially affect model behavior but are often treated as lower-sensitivity artifacts. |
| **1.2.2** | **Verify that** all access to training data is logged, including user, time, and action. | 1 | Undetected insider activity, slow-burn poisoning, and loss of forensic evidence after a data incident. The May 2025 joint AI data security guidance stresses provenance and immutable records because AI data integrity depends on knowing who changed data, when, and along which path it moved. | Verify object-level audit logging: AWS CloudTrail data events for S3 `GetObject`, `PutObject`, and `DeleteObject`; Google Cloud Storage Data Access audit logs for `DATA_READ` and `DATA_WRITE`; Azure Storage diagnostic logs for blob read/write/delete. Confirm logs are routed to a separate security account or immutable bucket, retained for the model lifetime plus regulatory needs, and correlated with pipeline run IDs, dataset version IDs, and approver identity. Run a sample forensic query: "who read or wrote shard X during training run Y?" | Storage-layer logging is mature but often disabled for high-volume object reads because of cost. Pipeline-internal reads from local caches, Spark executors, GPU nodes, and data loaders are rarely logged with user-level attribution. Distributed training multiplies the event volume; sampling can miss the single write or delete that matters. |
| **1.2.3** | **Verify that** training datasets are encrypted in transit and at rest, using current recommended cryptographic algorithms and key management practices. | 1 | Data theft through storage compromise, snapshot leakage, exposed scratch disks, or network interception; regulatory non-compliance for datasets containing PII, health, financial, or proprietary content. | Verify encryption at rest on object stores, lakehouse tables, temporary volumes, checkpoints, feature/embedding stores, and backups. AWS S3 now applies SSE-S3 with AES-256 by default, Google Cloud Storage encrypts server-side by default, and Azure Storage encrypts all storage accounts with 256-bit AES; regulated workloads should still justify whether customer-managed keys, HSM-backed keys, or FIPS 140-3 validated modules are required. Check TLS 1.2+ everywhere and TLS 1.3 where supported, KMS key rotation, key-separation between raw/processed/sensitive datasets, and encrypted training-node scratch disks. | Provider defaults cover many at-rest cases, but they do not prove that temporary caches, exported parquet shards, model checkpoints, gradients, or local NVMe instance stores are encrypted. Customer-managed keys improve control but add outage and recovery risk if key policies are misconfigured. Post-quantum adoption is early: NIST finalized FIPS 204 (ML-DSA) in August 2024 for digital signatures, but most production dataset-authentication workflows still use RSA/ECDSA or unsigned manifests. |
| **1.2.4** | **Verify that** training data is securely purged or anonymized when it is no longer required for the model's stated purpose. | 1 | Purpose drift, stale PII, revoked licenses, deprecated policy examples, or known poisoned records persisting after the business or safety justification has expired; GDPR/CCPA deletion exposure; inability to demonstrate that remediation reached derived datasets and caches. | Review the model's stated purpose, data inventory, retention schedule, and deletion policy together, then sample actual deletion evidence. Verify lifecycle rules, delete markers, backup expiration, cryptographic erase, NIST SP 800-88 Rev. 2 media sanitization for reusable drives, and tombstone records in lineage systems so downstream reviewers can distinguish "deleted" from "never ingested." For anonymized retention, review the privacy method used (masking, aggregation, differential privacy, k-anonymity where appropriate) and confirm re-identification risk was assessed. Check whether purge jobs reach derived shards, embeddings, feature stores, fine-tuning sets, generated/synthetic derivatives, and replicated regions. | Secure deletion on cloud object storage is generally reliable when versioning, replication, backups, and retention locks are accounted for. The hard gap is model weights: data already incorporated into a model cannot be confidently "deleted" with ordinary storage controls. Machine unlearning remains research-grade for most real systems, so the practical choices are rollback, retraining from a clean snapshot, targeted fine-tuning with documented limitations, or compensating controls that explain why the data is no longer retrievable or influential enough for the stated purpose. |
| **1.2.5** | **Verify that** cryptographic hashes or digital signatures are used to ensure data integrity during training data storage and transfer. | 2 | Silent corruption, malicious transfer modification, compromised mirrors, poisoned public datasets, or unauthorized post-approval edits to training shards. | Check for SHA-256 or stronger hash manifests per shard, validation in ingest and data-loader code, and failure behavior that blocks training rather than warning only. For signed revisions, verify signatures against a pinned trust root and ensure each approved dataset version has a signed manifest tying source URI, content hash, approver, timestamp, and pipeline run. OpenSSF Model Signing v1.0, the OMS signature format, and Sigstore model-transparency are practical references for signed AI artifact bundles; for datasets, also look for signed manifests, in-toto/DSSE attestations, Sigstore bundles, DVC lockfiles, lakeFS commits, Delta Lake transaction versions, or Iceberg snapshot IDs. | Hashing files is straightforward; signing large, dynamic datasets is not. OMS narrows the gap by supporting signatures over related artifact bundles that may include datasets, but multi-TB corpora, streaming ingest, and mutable lakehouse tables still need careful manifest boundaries. Per-shard hashes can miss composition attacks where all shard hashes are valid but the approved dataset mix changed. |
| **1.2.6** | **Verify that** automated integrity monitoring is applied to guard against unauthorized modifications or corruption of training data. | 2 | Slow-burn data poisoning, unauthorized delete/overwrite operations, bit rot, or corruption that is discovered only after retraining. Recent poisoning research shows that low absolute counts can matter: the October 2025 constant-poison study reported successful backdoors with about 250 documents, and the January 2025 medical-LLM study found harmful behavior from 0.001% token replacement while common benchmarks still passed. | Verify scheduled and event-triggered integrity checks on approved dataset locations. Confirm object-store versioning and delete alerts, CloudTrail/Cloud Audit/Azure Monitor alerts for unexpected write/delete operations, Wazuh/OSSEC/Tripwire-style FIM where datasets are staged on file systems, and lakehouse commit monitoring for forced branch resets or unexpected snapshot expiration. For data lakes, alert on changes to manifest files, transaction logs, and partition membership. Test with a controlled unauthorized write or hash mismatch and confirm the run is blocked. | Classical file integrity monitoring was designed for system files, not multi-TB data lakes; it can either miss object-store changes or flood on legitimate ingest. Integrity monitoring catches unauthorized modification and corruption, but detecting semantically poisoned yet syntactically valid records needs statistical and behavioral checks covered more directly in C1.4. |
| **1.2.7** | **Verify that** all training dataset versions are uniquely identified, stored immutably, and auditable to support rollback and forensic analysis. | 3 | Inability to reproduce a training run, roll back to a pre-poisoning dataset, prove which records were used, or preserve evidence for EU AI Act Article 10/12-style audits. | Verify a dataset versioning system is in use: lakeFS (now stewarding DVC after the November 2025 acquisition), DVC with a remote, Delta Lake, Apache Iceberg, or an equivalent content-addressed manifest system. Check that version IDs are tied to content, transformation code, approvals, and training runs. For immutability, verify WORM or retention controls such as S3 Object Lock Compliance Mode, Azure immutable blobs, or GCS Bucket Lock; then test that an administrator cannot overwrite or shorten retention for an approved dataset version. Run a real rollback: restore dataset version N-1, re-run a small training or validation job, and compare outputs within the model's determinism budget. | Versioning tools are mature enough to support audit and rollback, but they are not automatically immutable. Delta Lake and Iceberg snapshots can be expired; DVC remotes can be overwritten by privileged users; lakeFS branches can still need protected-branch controls. True WORM retention adds cost and planning friction, and retrofitting Object Lock may require new buckets or migration. |
