# C12.2: Right-to-be-Forgotten & Deletion Enforcement

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-10 (rev 9)

## Purpose

Ensure data-subject deletion requests propagate across all AI artifacts — raw datasets, feature stores, embeddings, checkpoints, logs, and backups — and that model unlearning is demonstrably verifiable rather than a cosmetic output-suppression exercise. The EDPB formally adopted its 2025 Coordinated Enforcement Framework (CEF) report on Article 17 on 18 February 2026, after examining 764 controllers across 32 DPAs throughout 2025. The report flagged seven systemic gaps: (1) absent internal procedures, (2) insufficient information to data subjects, (3) ineffective anonymization used as a substitute for erasure, (4) inconsistent implementation, (5) unclear retention periods, (6) backup-system erasure failures, and (7) flawed balancing of Article 17(3) exceptions. Nine DPAs opened formal investigations; follow-through supervision in Ireland, France, Portugal, Slovenia, and Germany runs through 2026. CNIL, Portugal's CNPD, and Sweden's IMY have already indicated CEF findings will drive sector-specific inspections this year. The follow-up CEF for 2026, announced 14 October 2025, shifts focus to GDPR Articles 12–14 (transparency and information obligations) — relevant here because controllers must clearly tell data subjects how the right to erasure applies to AI-derived artifacts, what the realistic propagation SLA looks like, and what happens to model weights. Sitting underneath the CEF is **EDPB Opinion 28/2024 (17 December 2024)**, which DPAs are now applying in the field: an AI model trained on personal data is **not automatically anonymous**, anonymity must be assessed case-by-case, and where unlawful processing occurred during development the worst-case remedy is "erasure of the whole dataset used to develop the AI model and/or the AI model itself." That language is the doctrinal hook for every model-deletion order DPAs have issued since.

Enforcement around AI deletion gained two useful 2026 lessons, but the sources need to be read carefully. On **18 March 2026**, the Court of Rome annulled the €15 million fine the Italian Garante had imposed on OpenAI in November 2024 for ChatGPT-related GDPR violations. The full reasoning, published **28 May 2026**, makes the scope clear: the annulment rests entirely on the one-stop-shop jurisdictional point — the Garante lost competence once OpenAI's Irish establishment was recognized on 15 February 2024, months before the final decision issued — and the court never examined the substantive allegations (lawful basis for training, transparency, age verification, breach notification). Those questions now sit, at least in principle, with the Irish DPC as lead supervisory authority. Commentators have called this an "establish, then escape" enforcement gap: a non-EU provider can restart the supervisory clock mid-investigation by standing up an EU main establishment. For deletion-evidence planning the takeaway is procedural, not substantive — no court has blessed any AI-training practice, and Article 17 duties are untouched. On **30 March 2026**, the FTC announced the **OkCupid/Match** settlement after alleging that OkCupid shared nearly three million user photos plus location and other personal information with an unrelated third party, without opt-out and without formal contractual use limits. The stipulated order does not make Clarifai a defendant, but it permanently bars OkCupid and Match from misrepresenting how they collect, maintain, use, disclose, delete, or protect personal information and how their privacy controls work. Subsequent Reuters/TechCrunch reporting says Clarifai certified deletion to the FTC on **7 April 2026** and told Rep. Lori Trahan's office on **16 April 2026** that it deleted the OkCupid photos and any facial-recognition models trained on them. The practical lesson is narrower than a blanket U.S. model-deletion rule, but still important: when unlawfully shared training data produced derived models, deletion of the data alone is no longer persuasive evidence.

The U.S. state-level deletion stack also matured sharply this year. California's **Delete Request and Opt-out Platform (DROP)** opened to consumers on **1 January 2026** under SB 362 (Delete Act). Data brokers must begin processing DROP lists on **1 August 2026**, access the platform at least every 45 days, and continue deleting data on that cadence. CPPA guidance also reflects SB 361's 2025 expansion of data-broker registration disclosures, including whether brokers shared data with **developers of generative AI systems** — a concrete U.S. hook tying broker-to-AI data flows to deletion obligations. The CPPA's Data Broker Strike Force has already issued 2026 enforcement decisions for registration failures, signaling the agency will not wait until August to scrutinize broker compliance. Separately, the amended **COPPA Rule** was published on **22 April 2025**, took effect on **23 June 2025**, and reached its main compliance date on **22 April 2026**. It is not an AI-training-specific deletion law, but it adds stricter notice, security, retention, and third-party disclosure obligations around children's personal information; AI training pipelines that ingest children's data now need a defensible retention/deletion story and separate consent analysis when data is disclosed to model, advertising, analytics, or enrichment vendors. In Europe, the **EU Digital Omnibus** changed again after this page's last pass: on **7 May 2026**, Parliament and Council negotiators reached a provisional political agreement that moves high-risk AI Act obligations for Annex III systems to **2 December 2027** and product-integrated Annex I systems to **2 August 2028**, with formal adoption still required before it enters into law. That does not weaken Article 17 erasure duties, but it changes the planning clock for AI Act Article 10 data-governance evidence; deployers should keep deletion lineage and retention controls moving now because GPAI, transparency, and GDPR timelines continue to run independently of the high-risk deferral. As of early June 2026 the Omnibus has **not** been formally adopted: Parliament and Council are expected to vote by July 2026, and until publication in the Official Journal the original 2 August 2026 high-risk date remains the legally operative deadline. The deal also adds a new prohibition on "nudifier" applications effective 2 December 2026 — relevant to deletion teams because non-consensual intimate imagery and the models that generate it become per-se prohibited artifacts, not just erasure-request subjects.

Machine unlearning itself is in the middle of a credibility reset. The ICLR 2025 paper from Hu et al. (CMU) showed that approximate unlearning methods largely **obfuscate** rather than forget — a "benign relearning attack" using small amounts of loosely related public data can reverse the unlearning effect. The most widely reported example: an LLM that had been "unlearned" of biohazard knowledge regained the ability to produce harmful bioweapon text after a brief fine-tune on publicly available medical articles. CMU's follow-up SaTML '25 position paper ("LLM Unlearning Benchmarks are Weak Measures of Progress") went further: a survey of 72 unlearning papers found that the dominant benchmarks (TOFU, WMDP) test forget and retain queries independently and miss dependencies between them, leaving room for methods to overfit to test queries while leaving the underlying knowledge intact. The team released *swapped WMDP* and *paired TOFU* on Hugging Face as harder evaluation sets. Recent attack literature has compounded the problem along three axes: ("Stealthy MLLM Unlearning Attack", "Step-by-Step Reasoning Attack", and "Unlearning Isn't Invisible") shows that adversaries can recover unlearned content with reasoning-style prompts, detect that unlearning happened from logits or even text outputs, and use those traces as a new reconnaissance signal. The **ROKA paper** (arxiv:2603.00436, 28 February 2026) introduced the *indirect unlearning attack*: an adversary requests deletion of one record specifically to degrade a model's performance on a different security-critical task, exploiting the **knowledge contamination** that conventional unlearning methods inflict on conceptually related neighbors. ROKA itself proposes a "Neural Healing" defense that re-allocates contributions to nullify forgotten data while reinforcing related knowledge — the first method offering theoretical guarantees for knowledge preservation during unlearning. The companion "Attack by Unlearning" paper (arxiv:2603.18570, 19 March 2026) extends this to graph neural networks: an adversary injects carefully crafted nodes into the training graph and later issues legally mandated deletion requests, producing an attack surface that cannot be refused without violating data-subject rights.

A second 2026 privacy thread widens the auditor's lens from the deleted subject to everyone else left in the model. **"Revisiting Privacy Leakage in Machine Unlearning"** (arxiv:2605.01129, accepted Euro S&P 2026) introduces **TC-UMIA**, a tri-class membership-inference attack that uses before/after predictions to distinguish forget, retain, and unseen records and finds that retain-set privacy can worsen after unlearning. Xue et al.'s **DVIA** paper at AAAI 2026 reaches the same operational conclusion in a black-box dual-view setting: if the original and unlearned models remain queryable, an attacker can infer membership for retained data more effectively than by querying either model alone. Cohen et al.'s **"Protecting the Undeleted"** goes further at the definition level, showing that "perfect retraining" style unlearning can leak undeleted data through deletion requests unless the security definition explicitly protects the retain set.

The 2026 evaluation literature has begun to catch up. **"LLM Unlearning Under the Microscope: A Full-Stack View on Methods and Metrics"** (arxiv:2510.07626) gives the first principled taxonomy of twelve stateful unlearning methods across three families — divergence-driven optimization (NPO, SimNPO), representation misalignment (RMU and variants), and rejection-based targeted unlearning (IDK) — and introduces **Open-QA metrics** that better capture generative performance and surface the unlearning-effectiveness vs. utility-retention tradeoff that WMDP and TOFU tend to mask. **"LLM Unlearning with LLM Beliefs"** (arxiv:2510.19422) reframes the objective around the model's own belief distribution rather than a fixed forget set, addressing the case where the forget set is incomplete or noisy. **"LLM Unlearning on Noisy Forget Sets"** (arxiv:2510.09007) studies incomplete, rewritten, and watermarked forget sets and reaches a more nuanced conclusion than "noise breaks unlearning": RMU and NPO remain surprisingly robust when the core semantic signal survives, so auditors should vary semantic content, not just surface wording. ICLR 2026 also delivered **RASLIK** (Randomized Antipodal Search on Linearized Influence Kernel), which reframes unlearning as a **data-selection** problem: hashing-based gradient sketches identify both forget-aligned and retain-aligned samples in sublinear time, shifting the Pareto frontier outward beyond oracle sampling. Representation-level tests also matured: **"Suppression or Deletion"** (arxiv:2602.18505) uses sparse autoencoders and inference-time steering to distinguish output suppression from internal deletion, and **MUNKEY** (arxiv:2603.15033) shows a promising "unlearning by design" direction where instance-specific memorization is externalized behind deletable keys. The combined effect: the field has pushed toward certified unlearning, SAM-based robust unlearning (Fan et al., ICML 2025), Neural-Healing-style contribution re-allocation (Choi et al., 2026), influence-kernel data selection (RASLIK, ICLR 2026), and retraining-style approaches for anything that must stand up to regulator scrutiny — while assuming that any unlearning claim will be tested by benign-relearning, reasoning-chain, stealthy fine-tune, unlearning-trace detection, dual-view / tri-class retained-data inference, representation-restoration probes, and indirect-attack adversaries who weaponize the deletion right itself.

Two May 2026 updates sharpen the evidence bar. **SMI: Statistical Membership Inference for Reliable Unlearned Model Auditing** (revised 7 May 2026) argues that a failed MIA is not proof of forgetting because unlearned samples can occupy feature-space positions distinct from true non-members; it proposes a training-free estimate of the non-member mixture proportion with bootstrap reference ranges. That is useful audit evidence for 12.2.2, but it also means teams should stop treating a single low MIA AUC as a pass/fail deletion certificate. In federated settings, the **Scientific Reports** paper **"Decoupling forgetting and preservation in federated unlearning via knowledge distillation"** (11 May 2026) reports 83.6% forget accuracy drop with 76.43% friend accuracy on Tiny-ImageNet and validates forgetting against retrained gold-standard comparisons. It is promising for C12.6-style deployments, but still benchmark-scale; controllers should not extrapolate it to foundation-model erasure without record-level lineage and retain-set privacy tests.

As of June 2026, the cleanest conceptual frame for all of this is the **removal-vs-suppression** distinction formalized in **"SoK: Machine Unlearning for Large Language Models"** (Ren et al., arxiv:2506.09227): classify every unlearning method by whether it *intends* to remove internal knowledge or merely suppress its behavioral expression, because suppression-intended methods can pass output-level audits while leaving the knowledge recoverable. **"Unlearning Isn't Deletion: Investigating Reversibility of Machine Unlearning in LLMs"** (arxiv:2505.16831, accepted ICML 2026) operationalizes that distinction: it maps unlearning outcomes into four regimes along reversibility and catastrophicity axes, shows that minimal retraining quickly restores "forgotten" behavior in most conventional methods, and ships a representation-level diagnostic toolkit — PCA similarity and shift, centered kernel alignment (CKA), and Fisher information — that auditors can run where task-level accuracy and perplexity metrics mislead. The attack surface keeps widening too: **"Towards Unveiling Vulnerabilities of Large Reasoning Models in Machine Unlearning"** (Chen et al., arxiv:2604.04255, April 2026) demonstrates a bi-level exact-unlearning attack against large reasoning models that forces incorrect final answers while emitting convincing step-by-step rationales — meaning a poisoned or adversarial deletion request against an LRM can corrupt downstream decisions while the reasoning trace *looks* sound. And domain-specific evidence arrived for healthcare: the **AMNESIA** benchmark (Davoudi et al., arxiv:2605.30599, 28 May 2026; 70,560 QA pairs from 8,820 patient notes across 11 disease categories) found that unlearning one patient erodes the model's knowledge of other patients with the same condition — the medical instantiation of retain-forget entanglement — and contributes a terminology-leakage metric for detecting residual PHI.

Three more 2025–2026 papers reset expectations for how regulators should read unlearning evidence. **"Machine Unlearning under Retain-Forget Entanglement"** (Cheng et al., arxiv:2603.26569, ICLR 2026) shows that conventional unlearning collateral-damages retained samples that share pretraining features or semantics with the forget set, and proposes a two-phase optimizer — an augmented Lagrangian step on the forget set followed by a Wasserstein-2-regularized gradient projection on semantically related retained samples — to recover both unlearning effectiveness and retain accuracy. **"Beyond Superficial Forgetting: Thorough Unlearning through Knowledge Density Estimation and Block Re-insertion (KUnBR)"** (Guo et al., arxiv:2511.11667, AAAI 2026) attacks the residual-knowledge problem at the layer level: it quantifies *knowledge density* per layer, extracts the densest layers, and re-inserts them into a copy of the LLM so the unlearning gradients are not blocked by cover layers — useful when post-hoc fine-tuning leaves harmful capabilities just one or two layers below the surface and existing relearning probes recover them. **"Rewind-to-Delete: Certified Machine Unlearning for Nonconvex Functions"** (Mu and Klabjan, arxiv:2409.09778, NeurIPS 2025) gives the first certified (ε, δ) unlearning result for general nonconvex losses by rolling the model back to an earlier training step and retraining on the retained data only — a result that pushes certified unlearning closer to the LLM regime where the loss surface is not convex. Taken together with **per-instance privacy losses for unlearning** (arxiv:2505.18786) and the privacy-amplification work in **"Efficient Unlearning with Privacy Guarantees"** (arxiv:2507.04771), the credible-evidence stack for high-stakes deletion now includes: an entanglement-aware optimizer, a thoroughness check (KUnBR-style layer probe or representation-restoration test), a certified method when the architecture allows it, and a retain-set privacy audit. None of these alone replaces retraining or raw-data deletion, but they let auditors challenge "we ran NPO, MIA dropped, done" claims with structurally different probes.

## Notable Incidents

- **EDPB Article 17 CEF, 18 February 2026:** 32 DPAs examined 764 controllers and found recurring failures around internal procedures, data-subject information, weak anonymization, retention-period decisions, backup erasure, and balancing of Article 17(3) exceptions. Treat this as the live supervisory baseline for deletion evidence.
- **OkCupid / Match / Clarifai, March-April 2026:** the FTC case centered on nearly three million OkCupid photos shared for facial-recognition development without the promised controls. The settlement binds OkCupid and Match, while follow-up reporting says Clarifai certified deletion of the photos and derived facial-recognition models. The practical lesson is that derived-model deletion evidence now belongs in the remediation file when unlawfully shared training data was used.
- **California DROP, 2026 rollout:** DROP launched for California residents on 1 January 2026, covers over 500 registered data brokers, and requires broker processing from 1 August 2026. Brokers must process through the accessible deletion mechanism at least every 45 days, and the consumer-facing CPPA timeline says broker data must be deleted within 90 days after processing begins. Unfulfilled requests carry penalties of $200 per request per day with no cure period, and brokers must maintain a suppression list so deleted subjects' data is not re-collected or resold.
- **Court of Rome annuls Garante's OpenAI fine, 18 March 2026 (reasoning published 28 May 2026):** the €15M ChatGPT fine fell on a pure one-stop-shop jurisdiction point — the Garante lost competence when OpenAI's Irish establishment was recognized in February 2024. No substantive GDPR question (lawful basis, transparency, age verification) was decided; those move to the Irish DPC. Read it as a venue shift, not a merits precedent on AI erasure.

Critically, run the Hu et al. benign-relearning test: fine-tune the "unlearned" model on loosely related public data and measure how much of the forgotten knowledge resurfaces — if relearning trivially recovers the target knowledge, the unlearning is obfuscation, not deletion. Add Step-by-Step/Sleek-style reasoning prompts and an unlearning-trace detector that classifies logits or sampled text from forget-irrelevant prompts; those tests do not prove residual personal data on their own, but they reveal whether the unlearning intervention left a measurable attack surface. Also run the CMU SaTML '25 robustness probes — the *swapped WMDP* and *paired TOFU* datasets — methods that fail these are overfitting to the test set rather than truly forgetting. For multimodal LLMs, run the MLLMU-Bench attack-defense framework (four white-box, three black-box attacks against six defense objectives). Add an **indirect-unlearning probe** following Choi et al. (ROKA, 2026): after each unlearning round, measure accuracy delta on a fixed set of security-critical retain queries — a regression > policy threshold signals knowledge contamination an adversary could weaponize by issuing deletion requests targeting conceptual neighbors. For graph-structured data (recommendation, fraud, knowledge graphs), assume the "Attack by Unlearning" threat model (arxiv:2603.18570, Mar 2026). Add retained-data leakage tests: keep the pre-unlearning model offline where possible, and if auditors can access both versions, run DVIA-style black-box likelihood-ratio probes, TC-UMIA tri-class probes across forget/retain/unseen records, and InfoRMIA-style token-level memorization checks so the evaluation does not protect the forget set by exposing the retain set. Test with **noisy / partial forget sets** (arxiv:2510.09007) to mirror how DSARs actually arrive in production. Add a **retain-forget entanglement check** following Cheng et al. (ICLR 2026, arxiv:2603.26569): measure retain accuracy on a held-out slice of samples that share pretraining features or semantic neighbors with the forget set — if those degrade disproportionately, the unlearning method is hitting conceptually related neighbors and an entanglement-aware optimizer (augmented Lagrangian + Wasserstein-2 projection) is the appropriate countermeasure. For LLM safety-critical deletion (bio, cyber, CSAM proxies, copyrighted text), add a KUnBR-style **knowledge-density probe** (Guo et al., AAAI 2026, arxiv:2511.11667): estimate per-layer knowledge density on the forget topic, then verify that the unlearned model's dense layers no longer contain the targeted concepts — superficial fine-tuning often leaves harmful knowledge intact one or two layers below the output. Static MIA baselines are now an under-estimate of attacker capability: two April 2026 results — **AutoMIA** (Liu et al., NUS, arxiv:2604.01014) and Tran et al.'s **"Automated Membership Inference Attacks: Discovering MIA Signal Computations using LLM Agents"** (Emory/ORNL, arxiv:2603.19375) — independently show that LLM-agent frameworks can self-explore the attack space and produce new logits-level MIA strategies that match or beat hand-crafted baselines, with Tran et al. reporting up to +0.18 absolute AUC over prior art. Auditors should re-run agentic MIA passes against any "we ran NPO/RMU, MIA dropped, done" claim, because the attack frontier moves between audits even if the unlearning method does not. Pair logit-level MIA with the representation-level evaluation in **"Are We Truly Forgetting?"** (Ko et al., arxiv:2503.06991, EAAI 2026): the paper shows state-of-the-art methods often improve logit-based metrics by collapsing or rewriting only the classifier head while leaving forget-class representations intact, and proposes a transfer-learning probe where forget-class features must diverge from the original model to count as genuine forgetting. Before any of these tests, classify the chosen method as removal-intended or suppression-intended using the SoK taxonomy (arxiv:2506.09227) — suppression-intended methods should be presumed reversible until proven otherwise. ## Implementation Maturity

- **Deletion propagation:** Mature in ordinary DSAR systems and primary data stores; uneven across feature stores, training shards, checkpoints, derived caches, event streams, and backups. Evidence quality improves when each model registry entry records a data-version commit, record manifest, and production endpoint mapping so auditors can answer "which models used this subject's data?" without notebook archaeology.
- **Backup and stream erasure:** Mature pattern, uneven adoption. Envelope encryption and crypto-shredding can make WORM backups or Kafka topics unrecoverable for a subject, but the deletion record must show which DEK/EDEK was destroyed, when, by whom, and which downstream consumers were re-keyed or quarantined.
- **Unlearning verification:** Emerging. OpenUnlearning (13 algorithms, 16 evaluations, 450+ checkpoints), MUSE, TOFU, WMDP, AMNESIA (medical), SMI, TC-UMIA, DVIA, retain-forget entanglement checks (Cheng et al. ICLR 2026), KUnBR-style knowledge-density probes (Guo et al. AAAI 2026), reversibility/representation-drift diagnostics (PCA shift, CKA, Fisher information — ICML 2026), and representation-restoration probes make claims more testable, but none is a regulator-approved substitute for raw-data deletion, retraining where feasible, or formal (ε, δ) guarantees. Rewind-to-Delete (Mu and Klabjan, NeurIPS 2025) extends certified unlearning into the nonconvex regime and is the strongest available evidence form for non-LLM neural networks under regulator scrutiny.
- **Federated and unlearning-by-design approaches:** Experimental to early mature. FedRecovery, FedADP, certified VFL, MUNKEY, and knowledge-distillation federated unlearning show useful design patterns, but auditors should require retained-client utility, retain-set privacy, and retrained-gold-standard comparisons before accepting them for high-impact systems.

## Tooling

- **Machine unlearning (LLM-focused):** [OpenUnlearning](https://github.com/locuslab/open-unlearning) (NeurIPS D&B 2025, 13 algorithms across TOFU/MUSE/WMDP, 450+ open-sourced checkpoints on HuggingFace, six integrated MIA variants and a meta-evaluation benchmark for evaluation faithfulness), [SISA reference implementation](https://github.com/cleverhans-lab/machine-unlearning) (Bourtoule et al., 2021), [SAM-based robust unlearning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.05374) (Fan et al., ICML 2025), [ROKA / Neural Healing](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.00436) (Choi et al., Feb 2026 — contribution re-allocation defense against indirect unlearning attacks; first theoretical guarantee for knowledge preservation), [RASLIK](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=Xn6EnJZghu) (ICLR 2026 — randomized antipodal search on linearized influence kernel; reframes unlearning as data selection), [Retain-Forget Entanglement](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.26569) (Cheng et al., ICLR 2026 — augmented Lagrangian + Wasserstein-2 gradient projection to protect semantically related retained samples), [KUnBR / Beyond Superficial Forgetting](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.11667) (Guo et al., AAAI 2026 — knowledge-density estimation + block re-insertion to overcome cover-layer gradient obstruction), EUPG (Universitat Rovira i Virgili, 2025 — hybrid k-anonymity/DP pre-train + fine-tune approach), MMUnlearner (multimodal LLMs, 2025), [awesome-llm-unlearning](https://github.com/chrisliu298/awesome-llm-unlearning) and [awesome-machine-unlearning](https://github.com/tamlhp/awesome-machine-unlearning) for current literature tracking, [Machine-Unlearning-Comparator](https://github.com/gnueaj/Machine-Unlearning-Comparator) (TVCG 2026 visual analytics for unlearning comparison).
- **Unlearning evaluation & realism:** [SoK: Machine Unlearning for Large Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.09227) (Ren et al. — removal-intended vs. suppression-intended taxonomy; the first question an auditor should ask of any method), [Unlearning Isn't Deletion](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.16831) (ICML 2026 — four forgetting regimes along reversibility/catastrophicity;
