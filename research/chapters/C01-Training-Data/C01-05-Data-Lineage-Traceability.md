# C1.5: Data Lineage and Traceability

> [Back to C01 Index](C01-Training-Data.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-10

## Purpose

When a model produces unexpected or harmful outputs, the first question in incident response is "what data trained this model, and where did it come from?" Without complete lineage records — tracking every transformation, augmentation, merge, and synthetic data generation step — that question is unanswerable. Lineage is also a regulatory survival requirement: the FTC's algorithmic disgorgement pattern (enforcement actions against Everalbum in 2021, Weight Watchers in 2022, Ring and Edmodo in 2023, and Rite Aid and Avast in 2024 — all requiring deletion of models trained on improperly collected data) means organizations that cannot reconstruct a dataset's full journey from source to model input face existential regulatory risk. As the FTC has made clear, if you cannot trace where everything came from and what transformations were applied, the enforcement agency is unlikely to be charitable.

This section requires recording the full provenance chain of each dataset, storing lineage records immutably for audit, and extending lineage tracking to cover synthetic data so that it remains clearly distinguishable from real data throughout the pipeline. As organizations increasingly generate synthetic training data from other models, the risk of inheriting and amplifying upstream poisoning makes synthetic data lineage tracking especially urgent. The February 2025 ["Basilisk Venom" proof-of-concept](https://0din.ai/blog/poison-in-the-pipeline-liberating-models-with-basilisk-venom) showed how hidden prompts in public GitHub repositories could later surface as fine-tuning backdoors. The March 2026 LiteLLM/Mercor supply chain breach — where TeamPCP hijacked LiteLLM's PyPI token on March 24, 2026 and published poisoned versions (1.82.7 and 1.82.8) containing credential-harvesting payloads and auto-executing `.pth` persistence — illustrated how a single dependency without provenance attestation can compromise an entire AI supply chain. [TechCrunch reported](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/31/mercor-says-it-was-hit-by-cyberattack-tied-to-compromise-of-open-source-litellm-project/) that Mercor confirmed an incident tied to the LiteLLM compromise; Lapsus$ claimed data theft, while researchers such as [Endor Labs](https://www.endorlabs.com/learn/teampcp-isnt-done) and [Snyk](https://snyk.io/de/blog/poisoned-security-scanner-backdooring-litellm/) documented the malicious package versions and persistence behavior. Mitiga's April 2026 ["Brainjacked" disclosure](https://www.mitiga.io/blog/brainjacked-planting-a-false-reality-in-the-ai-training-pipeline) demonstrated an adjacent vector: a path traversal vulnerability in a file upload application allowed an attacker to write poisoned training data directly into a production S3 bucket feeding an automated SageMaker retraining pipeline, embedding a backdoor that produced attacker-controlled predictions on trigger inputs — all because the web application's IAM role shared permissions with the training bucket and no lineage integrity validation existed at the retraining trigger point.

The [CISA/NSA/FBI Joint Cybersecurity Information Sheet](https://www.cisa.gov/resources-tools/resources/ai-data-security-best-practices-securing-data-used-train-operate-ai-systems) (May 2025) — co-signed by cybersecurity agencies from the UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, South Korea, and Japan — identifies data provenance tracking and immutable logging as foundational controls for AI data security, specifically calling out data supply chain risks, poisoned data, and data drift as the three primary threat categories. The guidance recommends automated metadata tagging and digital signatures throughout the AI data lifecycle, targeting defense industrial bases, federal agencies, and critical infrastructure operators. A [Kiteworks 2026 Forecast Report](https://www.kiteworks.com/regulatory-compliance/state-ai-legislation-2026-compliance-data-governance/) found that 78% of organizations lack pre-training data validation and 77% lack provenance and lineage capabilities — underscoring how far the industry has to go.

Carnegie Mellon SEI's April 27, 2026 [chain-of-custody analysis](https://www.sei.cmu.edu/blog/data-poisoning-in-ai-models-the-case-for-chain-of-custody-controls/) sharpens the operational point: for a 50,000-image training set, changing 50 images (0.1%) can be enough to create a poisoning effect. The recommended control pattern is not just "log the pipeline," but record who or what handled each data item, hash the data and metadata at each lifecycle step, sign dataset manifests, and require the training job to verify the chain of custody before loading the data. That makes lineage a preventive control, not merely a forensic convenience after a model has already been contaminated.

EU AI Act Article 10 (Data and Data Governance) — originally entering enforcement on 2 August 2026, deferred to 2 December 2027 by the May 2026 Digital Omnibus (AI embedded in Annex I regulated products: 2 August 2028) — will make data lineage a hard compliance requirement for all high-risk AI systems. Critically, Article 10 demands *operational evidence* — timestamped, automated records proving that quality and lineage controls were active for a specific dataset at a specific pipeline stage at a specific point in time — not merely governance policies describing intended practices. Even with the extra runway, organizations deploying high-risk AI face fines up to €35 million or 7% of global revenue for non-compliance — making lineage infrastructure an operational priority, not a future aspiration. The European Commission opened two consultations in May 2026 that sharpen what enforcers will look for: the [draft Article 50 transparency guidelines](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/consultations/consultation-draft-guidelines-transparency-obligations-under-ai-act) (published 8 May 2026, consultation closed 3 June 2026) cover AI-generated content disclosure, while the [draft Article 6 high-risk classification guidelines](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/draft-commission-guidelines-classification-high-risk-ai-systems) (published 19 May 2026, 148 pages, consultation closing 23 June 2026) determine which systems must satisfy Article 10's data governance and lineage obligations in the first place — and explicitly warn that the Article 6(3) "filter mechanism" for narrow procedural tasks must be interpreted narrowly. The Digital Omnibus itself reached [trilogue agreement on 7 May 2026](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/legislative-train/package-digital-package/file-digital-omnibus-on-ai) with a plenary vote expected by early July 2026; notably, while high-risk obligations slipped to December 2027, the grace period for marking AI-generated content was *cut* — systems already on the market before 2 August 2026 must comply with machine-readable marking by 2 December 2026, a three-month rather than six-month runway. Separately, the [mandatory GPAI training-content summary template](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/template-general-purpose-ai-model-providers-summarise-their-training-content) under Article 53(1)(d) becomes enforceable on 2 August 2026 (fines up to €15M or 3% of worldwide turnover) and requires providers to disclose data sources by category — with *synthetic data* as an explicit mandatory category — which is only answerable from real lineage records. ISO/IEC 42001 Annex A.7.5 (Data Provenance) independently requires continuous, automated provenance capture — including origin, consent, transformation logs with script versions and operator IDs, and tamper-resistant storage — making it a useful operational checklist even for organizations not yet subject to the EU AI Act. The normative anchor for *how* to record this is firming up: [ISO/IEC 24970 (AI system logging)](https://www.iso.org/standard/88723.html) entered its final FDIS approval ballot on 18 May 2026, defining a common information model for logging AI system events tied to risk management, with parallel CEN/CENELEC adoption that links it directly to EU AI Act conformity. NIST AI 600-1 (GenAI Profile) similarly identifies provenance as a core risk management action mapped across the Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage functions of the AI RMF, and NIST AI 100-4 remains the primary US technical reference for synthetic-content provenance tracking, watermarking, and labeling techniques.

The May 11, 2026 Mini Shai-Hulud npm worm reshaped expectations about what signed provenance proves. TeamPCP — the same group behind the March 2026 LiteLLM compromise — published [84 malicious @tanstack/* package versions in six minutes](https://snyk.io/blog/tanstack-npm-packages-compromised/) by chaining a GitHub Actions `pull_request_target` "Pwn Request," runner-cache poisoning, and OIDC-token extraction from `/proc/<pid>/mem`. Within 48 hours the campaign expanded to 172 packages across 403 malicious versions on npm and PyPI, including `@mistralai/mistralai` and `guardrails-ai` — both directly relevant to AI training and guardrail pipelines. CVE-2026-45321 (CVSS 9.6) was assigned to the TanStack compromise. The watershed detail: this is the [first documented case of a malicious npm package carrying valid SLSA provenance](https://www.wiz.io/blog/mini-shai-hulud-strikes-again-tanstack-more-npm-packages-compromised), because the worm hijacked the legitimate build pipeline and made Sigstore attest to a malicious build. The operational takeaway for AI lineage is that signed attestations verify *how* an artifact was built, not whether the build itself was *authorized* — so dataset, model, and dependency provenance must be paired with install-time behavioral analysis, release-age cooldowns, and OIDC pinning, not consumed as a stand-alone trust signal.

| **1.5.1** | **Verify that** the lineage of each dataset and its components, including all transformations, augmentations, and merges, is recorded and can be reconstructed. | 1 | Inability to trace poisoned data back through the pipeline (MITRE ATLAS [AML.T0020](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0020)); undetectable introduction of malicious transformations; failure to reproduce training conditions for incident investigation. The February 2025 ["Basilisk Venom" proof-of-concept](https://0din.ai/blog/poison-in-the-pipeline-liberating-models-with-basilisk-venom) demonstrated how hidden prompts in GitHub code comments can poison fine-tuned models — without lineage, the contaminated source cannot be identified and rolled back. The March 2026 LiteLLM/Mercor breach showed how a single compromised dependency can cascade across an AI supply chain; [Snyk](https://snyk.io/de/blog/poisoned-security-scanner-backdooring-litellm/) ties the malicious LiteLLM releases to versions 1.82.7 and 1.82.8, TeamPCP, credential harvesting, Kubernetes lateral movement, and `.pth` startup persistence. Verify that each transformation step is logged with input/output dataset references. Test reconstruction of a sample dataset's full history. For high-assurance pipelines, evaluate the [Atlas provenance framework](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.19567) (C2PA + SLSA attestable lineage with TEE support). Confirm lineage covers the full DAG: ingestion, cleaning, augmentation, merge, and feature engineering steps. For datasets sourced from public hubs, check whether [MLCommons Croissant 1.1](https://mlcommons.org/2026/02/croissant-1-1-standard/) metadata (released February 2026) is present and populated — it adopts W3C PROV-O so each dataset, file, and record can be linked to its source data, processing steps, and responsible agents (human or software), explicitly to support chain-of-custody checks; over 700,000 datasets on Hugging Face, Kaggle, and OpenML carry Croissant metadata, and Common Crawl has adopted 1.1. Verify SLSA v1.1+ provenance attestations are generated for model artifacts and training data snapshots — but treat the attestation as evidence of *how* a build ran, not as proof the build was authorized. The Mini Shai-Hulud worm (May 2026) showed that pipeline hijack produces signed provenance for malicious artifacts; pair attestations with install-time behavioral analysis, OIDC pinning to specific workflow/branch (not just repository), and a [7-day release-age cooldown](https://snyk.io/blog/tanstack-npm-packages-compromised/) before promoting new training dependencies. For model artifact integrity, verify [sigstore/model-transparency](https://github.com/sigstore/model-transparency) signing is applied — it provides keyless Sigstore-based signing and verification of ML models with in-toto attestation statements. As of March 2025, [NVIDIA signs all NGC Catalog models](https://developer.nvidia.com/blog/bringing-verifiable-trust-to-ai-models-model-signing-in-ngc) using the OpenSSF Model Signing (OMS) specification, and [Google's Kaggle](https://openssf.org/blog/2025/07/23/case-study-google-secures-machine-learning-models-with-sigstore/) has adopted the same standard — verify that models consumed from these hubs are signature-checked before integration. The `model-signing` Python package (`pip install model-signing`, [v1.1.1](https://pypi.org/project/model-signing/)) can verify OMS signatures against a certificate chain. For third-party transformer checkpoints, run Cisco's [Model Provenance Kit](https://github.com/cisco-ai-defense/model-provenance-kit) in `scan` or `compare` mode to test claimed derivation against metadata, tokenizer, and weight-level fingerprints; treat this as supporting evidence alongside signed manifests, not as a substitute for dataset lineage. For lakehouse architectures, confirm Delta Lake or Apache Iceberg time-travel snapshots are enabled and that "as of" queries can reconstruct the exact dataset state used for any given training run. For runtime lineage from code to cloud, evaluate [Relyance AI Data Journeys](https://www.relyance.ai/solutions/ai-data-lineage-training-runtime) — it maps data flows across training pipelines, APIs, and model inference with real-time policy enforcement and generates EU AI Act / GDPR audit-ready reports. To detect poisoning that lineage should help trace, verify that per-class feature drift monitoring, gradient norm outlier detection during training, and trigger concentration analysis for rare tokens/patterns are in place. | MLflow 3 and DVC track experiment-level lineage; [MLflow 3.10](https://mlflow.org/releases/) (March 2026) extended that to GenAI agent observability, LLM tracing, multi-turn evaluation, and cost tracking, giving downstream agent calls a place to live next to model and dataset versions. Apache Atlas and DataHub provide richer graph-based lineage but note that Apache Atlas v2.3.0 and earlier carry CVE-2024-46910 (XSS); upgrade to v2.4.0+ is required. [DataHub v1.5.0](https://github.com/datahub-project/datahub/releases) (February 2026) links ML Models to training runs and experiments with end-to-end lineage from raw data through training to deployed models, and turns on Vertex AI ML Metadata extraction by default. OpenLineage is the emerging open standard for cross-platform lineage metadata with integrations into Airflow, Spark, Flink, dbt, and Dagster as of 2026; Marquez (LF AI & DATA, v0.50.0) serves as the reference implementation with a Data Observability dashboard. [IBM watsonx.data](https://www.ibm.com/new/announcements/openlineage-for-a-unified-lineage-view-across-structured-and-unstructured-data-to-enable-explainable-ai) (announced March 2026) now emits OpenLineage events from its query and processing engines for structured workloads, and watsonx.data integration emits OpenLineage events from ingestion and transformation pipelines that handle *unstructured* data — closing a longstanding gap for RAG corpora and document pipelines feeding LLMs. Databricks Unity Catalog integrates with MLflow to trace data lineage through model training and inference. The Atlas provenance framework (Feb 2025) demonstrated fully attestable ML pipelines with <8% overhead but remains a research prototype. MANTA provides column-level and code-level lineage for legacy ETL environments. [lakeFS](https://lakefs.io/) adds Git-like branching and versioning on top of object stores, enabling per-experiment lineage tracing back to raw datasets. Model artifact signing has reached production maturity: NVIDIA NGC (March 2025) and Google Kaggle both use the OpenSSF Model Signing (OMS) specification with Sigstore — but the Mini Shai-Hulud SLSA-signed-malware case proves signing covers integrity of *the recorded build*, not authorization of the artifact's contents. Cisco's Model Provenance Kit is useful for weight-family evidence, especially when metadata may be misleading, but it does not prove what data, labels, or augmentation steps produced a checkpoint. Gap: fine-grained per-sample lineage (tracking individual records through transformations) is computationally expensive and rarely implemented — most tools track dataset-level, not record-level lineage. Research prototypes like [yProv4ML](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.01078) (per-sample PROV-JSON capture) and [LIMA](https://mboehm7.github.io/resources/sigmod2021a_lima.pdf) (fine-grained lineage reuse) show promise but are not production-ready as of May 2026. |
| **1.5.2** | **Verify that** lineage records are immutable, securely stored, and accessible for audits. | 2 | Tampering with lineage records to cover tracks after data poisoning; loss of audit trail due to storage failure; disputed provenance during regulatory investigation. FTC algorithmic disgorgement actions (6 cases 2021-2024) have required model deletion when provenance could not be demonstrated — immutable records are the defense against this. The Brainjacked attack (April 2026) succeeded partly because no immutable lineage record existed to detect that a web application had written unauthorized data to the training bucket — S3 Object Lock would have prevented the overwrite entirely. The lineage and tracking platforms themselves are now demonstrated attack surface: MLflow versions up to 3.9.0 carry [CVE-2026-2734](https://dailycve.com/mlflow-missing-authorization-bypass-cve-2026-2734-medium-dc-jun2026-92/) (the `SearchModelVersions` REST endpoint and GraphQL query skip permission validators, letting any authenticated user enumerate model versions, source URIs, and run metadata across tenants; fixed in 3.10.0), and [RAXE Labs documented](https://raxe.ai/labs/advisories/RAXE-2026-030) a chain of CVE-2026-2635 (auth bypass via default credentials, CVSS 9.8) with CVE-2026-2033 (artifact path traversal) yielding RCE on the tracking server — full tampering capability over stored lineage. DataHub below 1.3.1.8 carries [CVE-2026-25644](https://www.sentinelone.com/vulnerability-database/cve-2026-25644/) (LDAP TLS downgrade enabling MITM on ingested metadata, disclosed February 2026). Under the EU AI Act high-risk obligations (now due 2 December 2027 after the May 2026 Digital Omnibus deferral), organizations that cannot produce immutable audit trails face fines up to €35M or 7% of global revenue. | Verify lineage metadata is stored in append-only or immutable storage (S3 Object Lock in compliance mode, Azure Immutable Blobs, GCS retention policies). Check access controls on lineage databases — pipeline operators should not have write or delete access to lineage stores. Test that lineage records cannot be modified or deleted via API or console. Verify backup and retention policies align with EU AI Act Article 10 documentation requirements (enforcement originally August 2026, deferred to 2 December 2027 by the May 2026 Digital Omnibus), the [CISA/NSA/FBI AI Data Security guidance](https://www.cisa.gov/resources-tools/resources/ai-data-security-best-practices-securing-data-used-train-operate-ai-systems) (May 2025, which recommends immutable logging and digital signatures for all AI data lifecycle stages), and [ISO 42001 Annex A.7.5](https://www.isms.online/iso-42001/annex-a-controls/a-7-data-for-ai-systems/a-7-5-data-provenance/) (which requires tamper-resistant storage with point-in-time evidence and exportable audit records). Track [ISO/IEC 24970](https://www.iso.org/standard/88723.html) (AI system logging, FDIS ballot since 18 May 2026) as the forthcoming normative information model for AI event logs — auditors will increasingly expect lineage event records to align with it. Patch the lineage platform itself: verify MLflow ≥3.10.0, DataHub ≥1.3.1.8, and Apache Atlas ≥2.4.0, and confirm default credentials are rotated on tracking servers. Audit log retention should cover the full expected model lifetime plus regulatory holding periods. For cryptographic integrity, verify that lineage events carry SHA-256 content hashes computed over canonical event payloads — this enables post-hoc tamper detection without modifying stored records. Consider an [OpenTelemetry-based immutable audit pipeline](https://oneuptime.com/blog/post/2026-02-06-immutable-audit-log-pipeline-otel/view) pattern: instrument lineage events via OTel SDK, process through OTel Collector with integrity hashing, and dual-export to both immutable storage (S3 Object Lock) and a queryable backend (Elasticsearch) for search. For EU AI Act Article 10 pipeline-level compliance evidence, [Ataccama ONE](https://www.ataccama.com/blog/real-time-data-observability-is-the-missing-layer-in-eu-ai-act-compliance) (announced April 2026) provides continuous pipeline monitoring across dbt, Airflow, Dagster, and AWS Glue with timestamped quality validation at each ETL stage, incident traceability linked to Jira/ServiceNow, and on-demand audit reports — designed specifically for financial services institutions facing the originally scheduled August 2026 enforcement deadline (since deferred to 2 December 2027 by the Digital Omnibus). | Immutable storage backends are mature (S3 Object Lock, Azure Immutable Blobs, WORM-compliant storage). Gap: lineage metadata systems (Atlas, DataHub, Marquez) typically use mutable databases (MySQL, PostgreSQL, Elasticsearch) and do not enforce immutability at the storage layer by default. Achieving true immutability requires architectural layering — for example, writing lineage events to an append-only log (Kafka topic with compaction disabled, or a dedicated ledger) and using the lineage UI as a read-only projection. The OpenTelemetry-based pattern (Feb 2026) provides a practical reference architecture: OTel SDK instrumentation → OTel Collector with SHA-256 hashing → dual export to S3 Object Lock (immutable) + Elasticsearch (queryable), satisfying SOC 2, HIPAA, and EU AI Act audit requirements. Delta Lake and Apache Iceberg provide table-level time-travel and snapshot lineage as a complementary layer — Iceberg's snapshot history is queryable and supports point-in-time audits without redundant data copies. No major open-source lineage tool ships with built-in immutability as of April 2026, so the architectural layering approach remains necessary. |
| **1.5.3** | **Verify that** lineage tracking covers synthetic data generated via augmentation, synthesis, or privacy-preserving techniques. | 2 | Synthetic data contamination obscuring real data quality issues; inability to trace model behaviors to real vs. synthetic training examples; privacy violations if synthetic data retains real-data characteristics without proper tracking. The VIA (Virus Infection Attack) demonstrated how poisoned content propagates through synthetic data pipelines, making lineage of synthetic generation steps critical for containment. Model Autophagy Disorder (MAD) — where models trained recursively on their own synthetic outputs degrade — is another risk that lineage tracking helps detect. LoRA adapter poisoning (emerging 2025-2026) adds a new vector: malicious fine-tuning adapters are small, easy to distribute, and can introduce backdoors indistinguishable from legitimate fine-tuning — lineage must capture adapter provenance alongside data provenance. A February 2026 study ([Detecting Backdoored LoRAs from Weights Alone](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15195)) confirmed that community-contributed adapters on Hugging Face Hub can be backdoored while preserving clean-task accuracy, making provenance plus weight-level screening the practical defense pair. | Review lineage records for synthetic data generation steps. Verify each synthetic dataset records: the generator model/tool identity and version, the seed/source data used, generation parameters and prompts, and a timestamp. Verify synthetic data carries metadata tags distinguishing it from real data. Check that downstream consumers can filter by data origin type. Confirm lineage survives merge operations — when synthetic and real datasets are combined, per-record origin tags should persist. For media-type synthetic data, verify [C2PA Content Credentials](https://c2pa.org/) are attached to generated assets; the current v2.2 [AI/ML guidance](https://spec.c2pa.org/specifications/specifications/2.2/ai-ml/ai_ml.html) defines training dataset credentials, model content credentials, fine-tuning ingredients, and LoRA adapter provenance patterns. For LoRA and adapter artifacts, verify that SLSA provenance attestations record the base model, training data snapshot, and hyperparameters used to produce each adapter; for adapters from untrusted hubs, supplement provenance with weight-only spectral screening of the low-rank update matrices ([arXiv:2602.15195](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15195) demonstrates trigger-agnostic backdoor detection without running the model). Confirm synthetic-data lineage can populate regulatory disclosures: the EU [GPAI training-content summary template](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/faqs/template-general-purpose-ai-model-providers-summarise-their-training-content) (enforceable 2 August 2026) and California AB 2013 (effective 1 January 2026, [upheld against constitutional challenge on 4 March 2026](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en-us/knowledge/publications/c1df8419/california-district-court-upholds-transparency-requirements-for-generative-ai-training-data)) both require disclosing whether and how synthetic data was used — an unanswerable question without generation-step lineage. | Synthetic data generation tools (Gretel, MOSTLY AI, SDV, and LLM-based generators) produce data but rarely integrate with lineage systems. Gap: no widely adopted standard metadata schema for marking data as synthetic exists as of May 2026 — the IPTC Photo Metadata Standard 2025.1 (ratified November 2025) introduced four XMP fields for AI-generated media (`AISystemUsed`, `AISystemVersionUsed`, `AIPromptInformation`, `AIPromptWriterName` in the `Iptc4xmpExt` namespace), but nothing equivalent exists for tabular or text training data. C2PA Content Credentials v2.2 provide cryptographic tamper-evident provenance for media content with explicit [AI/ML guidance](https://spec.c2pa.org/specifications/specifications/2.2/ai-ml/ai_ml.html) covering model asset types, training data partition manifests, and fine-tuning provenance chains (base model + adapter dataset). Adoption in ML training pipelines for tabular and text data is still nascent. When synthetic and real data are merged in dataframes or parquet files, distinguishability is typically lost unless explicit column-level origin tags are maintained. OpenDataDiscovery (ODD) is one of the few platforms that gives ML workflows (models, experiments, feature stores) first-class lineage support, though its community is smaller than OpenLineage's. |
| **1.5.4** | **Verify that** synthetic data is clearly labeled and distinguishable from real data throughout the pipeline. | 2 | Unlabeled synthetic data mixed with real data creates compounding risks: model quality degradation from recursive training on synthetic outputs (Model Autophagy Disorder); inability to isolate contaminated synthetic batches during incident response; regulatory non-compliance when auditors cannot determine what fraction of training data was real vs. generated; and inherited bias amplification when synthetic data silently inherits and magnifies biases from the source model. Synthetic-origin data also complicates poisoning investigations: if generated samples are not tagged back to the generator model, prompt, seed data, and privacy-preserving method, responders cannot determine whether a bad batch came from source data, the generator, or a later merge. Recent modeling work sharpens the stakes: an April 2026 epidemiological analysis of model collapse ([arXiv:2606.05168](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05168)) treats synthetic-text contamination as a cross-population infection and finds that mixing many data sources does *not* protect at low contamination fractions — synthetic-content detection and labeling is the highest-leverage intervention. A companion economics paper ([arXiv:2605.20279](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.20279), May 2026) quantifies the payoff, showing calibrated provenance and watermarking interventions improved tenth-generation model quality by 23.1% in simulation. | Inspect dataset schemas for mandatory origin fields such as `data_origin`, `generator_model`, `generator_version`, `source_dataset_hash`, `generation_prompt_ref`, `privacy_method`, and `created_at`. Use schema and data-quality gates (Great Expectations, Soda, Deequ, dbt tests, or Pandera) to fail any ingest or merge that drops these fields or introduces an unapproved origin value. Sample several synthetic records end-to-end and confirm the label survives dataframe operations, parquet/Delta/Iceberg writes, feature-store publication, training-batch creation, and model-card or dataset-card export. For media assets, verify C2PA Content Credentials and the IPTC Photo Metadata Standard 2025.1 fields (`AI System Used`, `AI System Version Used`, `AI Prompt Information`, `AI Prompt Writer Name`) are preserved through resize, compression, and packaging steps. In MLflow, W&B, DVC, or lakehouse metadata, verify that each training run records the real/synthetic/augmented ratio and can filter out synthetic records before retraining. | The practical gap is schema governance, not just metadata generation. Most synthetic data tools can label their own outputs, but labels are easily lost in notebook exports, ad hoc dataframe joins, feature engineering, and model fine-tuning datasets unless enforced by data contracts. Media provenance is ahead of tabular/text provenance: C2PA and IPTC provide useful fields for images and some AI/ML artifacts, while structured datasets still depend on project-specific columns, sidecar manifests, or catalog tags. Watermarking for synthetic *tabular* data is an active 2026 research wave — [TableMark](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2603.13722) embeds multi-bit payloads that can encode generator and provenance IDs (not just a synthetic/real flag) and [TAB-DRW](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.21600) offers a DFT-based robust scheme — but no production tool ships these yet. Regulatory pressure is arriving faster than the tooling: under the Digital Omnibus agreement, machine-readable marking of AI-generated content applies from 2 August 2026 for new systems and 2 December 2026 even for systems already on the market. Privacy-preserving synthetic data also needs separate risk evidence (membership inference, nearest-neighbor similarity, and attribute disclosure checks); a "synthetic" label is not proof that real personal data cannot be recovered. |

## Notable Incidents

- **Basilisk Venom (February 2025)**: 0DIN documented a fine-tuning poisoning proof-of-concept in which attacker-controlled prompts embedded in public GitHub repositories later appeared as model behavior without live internet access, reinforcing the need to record source repository, commit, collector, and filter decisions for code-training corpora.
- **LiteLLM / Mercor (March 2026)**: TeamPCP's compromise of LiteLLM PyPI releases 1.82.7 and 1.82.8 showed why training and evaluation pipelines need dependency provenance alongside data lineage; Snyk and Endor Labs both documented credential harvesting and Python startup-hook persistence in the malicious packages.
- **Brainjacked (April 2026)**: Mitiga described a path-traversal-to-S3 poisoning chain where unauthorized writes into a SageMaker training bucket triggered retraining on tainted data. A signed dataset manifest and a pre-training lineage integrity gate would have turned the overwrite into a detectable failure instead of a silent model update.
- **SEI chain-of-custody warning (April 2026)**: Carnegie Mellon SEI emphasized that changing 0.1% of a 50,000-image training set can be enough for poisoning, making per-item checksums, signed transfer records, and loader-side manifest verification practical security controls rather than paperwork.
- **Mini Shai-Hulud npm worm (11 May 2026)**: TeamPCP published 84 malicious `@tanstack/*` versions in six minutes via a chained `pull_request_target` "Pwn Request," GitHub Actions cache poisoning, and OIDC-token theft from runner memory. Within 48 hours [the worm spread to 172 packages across 403 versions on npm and PyPI](https://venturebeat.com/security/shai-hulud-worm-172-npm-pypi-packages-valid-provenance-ci-cd-audit), including `@mistralai/mistralai` and `guardrails-ai`. CVE-2026-45321 (CVSS 9.6) marked the [first documented case of a malicious npm package carrying valid SLSA provenance](https://www.wiz.io/blog/mini-shai-hulud-strikes-again-tanstack-more-npm-packages-compromised) — the worm hijacked the legitimate build pipeline, so Sigstore attested truthfully to a malicious build. Snyk's behavioral analysis flagged all 84 artifacts within six minutes, and [OpenAI publicly described its containment response](https://openai.com/index/our-response-to-the-tanstack-npm-supply-chain-attack/). The lineage lesson is that "signed manifest present" is no longer sufficient evidence of safety; AI training pipelines need install-time behavioral checks, OIDC pinning to workflow and branch, and a release-age cooldown before promoting new dependencies into training, eval, or guardrail layers.

### Implementation Maturity

- **Mature**: Dataset-level lineage, table snapshots, immutable object storage, signed model artifacts, and cloud audit logs are production-ready when teams wire them together deliberately.
- **Developing**: Cross-platform lineage standards are improving quickly, especially OpenLineage integrations for dbt, Spark, Flink, Fabric, ClickHouse, and now structured/unstructured pipelines via IBM watsonx.data, but ML-specific semantics still require custom facets or sidecar metadata.
- **Emerging**: Per-sample lineage, synthetic text/tabular provenance, and federated-learning custody records remain research-heavy and expensive to operate at scale. The tabular gap is starting to close on two fronts: Croissant 1.1's PROV-O provenance gives published datasets a machine-actionable lineage vocabulary, and tabular watermarking schemes (TableMark, TAB-DRW) show multi-bit provenance payloads are feasible — though neither has production enforcement tooling yet as of June 2026.
- **Newly contested**: Signed provenance — once treated as terminal evidence — was demoted in May 2026 when the Mini Shai-Hulud worm produced valid SLSA attestations on malicious npm artifacts by hijacking the legitimate build pipeline. Pipelines that previously stopped at "manifest present, signature valid" now need install-time behavioral analysis, OIDC pinning to specific workflow/branch (not just repository), release-age cooldowns (Snyk recommends a seven-day hold), and cache-purge gates on `pull_request_target` workflows.
- **Practical baseline**: For most audits, require signed dataset manifests, immutable lineage event storage, reproducible "as of" snapshots, and training jobs that fail closed when a manifest, checksum, or origin tag is missing — and treat provenance as one of several layered signals, not as a stand-alone trust verdict.

### Immutable Storage Architecture

For organizations that need to satisfy both EU AI Act Article 10 documentation requirements and FTC algorithmic disgorgement defense, the recommended layered approach is:

1. **Event capture**: Instrument pipeline steps with OpenLineage or OpenTelemetry SDK to emit structured lineage events with SHA-256 content hashes
2. **Immutable persistence**: Write events to S3 Object Lock (compliance mode), Azure Immutable Blobs, or GCS with retention policies — pipeline operators must not have write/delete access
3. **Queryable projection**: Dual-export to Elasticsearch or a graph database for search and visualization
4. **Table-level snapshots**: Enable Delta Lake or Iceberg time-travel on all training data tables for point-in-time reconstruction
5. **Attestation**: Generate SLSA v1.1+ provenance attestations for model artifacts and training data snapshots

### Chain-of-Custody Control Pattern

For high-impact models, lineage should be enforced as a chain-of-custody gate before training data is loaded:

- **At creation**: Record source system, collector identity, timestamp, license/consent status, content hash, and metadata hash
- **At transfer**: Sign the transfer record with source, destination, tool identity, and observed checksum
- **At transformation**: Record script or notebook version, operator/service principal, input hashes, output hashes, and validation result
- **At dataset assembly**: Verify every constituent record or file has an unbroken custody chain before signing the dataset manifest
- **At training**: Require the loader to reject any dataset whose manifest, signatures, or hashes fail verification

### Synthetic Data Labeling Strategies

Until a universal standard emerges for non-media synthetic data labeling, practical approaches include:

- **Column-level tagging**: Add a mandatory `data_origin` field (`synthetic | real | augmented`) to every record at creation time, enforced by pipeline validation
- **Croissant 1.1 provenance**: For published or hub-sourced datasets, populate the PROV-O provenance fields so generation steps and generator agents are machine-readable; record DUO/ODRL usage terms alongside
- **Metadata sidecars**: JSON/YAML files that travel with datasets through storage and compute
- **Table properties**: Databricks Unity Catalog tags or Delta Lake table properties for dataset-level markers
- **Media assets**: Apply C2PA v2.2 Content Credentials + IPTC 2025.1 XMP fields at generation time; verify preservation through pipeline
- **Batch-level tracking**: Log synthetic-to-real ratios per training batch in experiment tracking systems (MLflow, W&B)

### Federated and Distributed Training Lineage

As organizations adopt federated learning (FL) and edge training, lineage tracking faces new challenges — data never leaves participant nodes, so traditional centralized lineage systems cannot observe the full pipeline. Emerging approaches include:

- **Model snapshot provenance**: Store snapshots at each FL aggregation round with client-level metadata (data volume, distribution statistics, training hyperparameters) to reconstruct how the global model evolved
- **Cryptographic attestation per node**: Each participant signs a lineage attestation covering their local data characteristics and training parameters before contributing gradients — enables post-hoc audit without centralizing raw data
- **Blockchain-anchored FL lineage**: Research prototypes (as of 2026) use blockchain to record per-round aggregation events immutably, though governance capabilities remain limited and real-time auditability is not yet production-grade
- **EDPS guidance**: The European Data Protection Supervisor's [June 2025 TechDispatch on Federated Learning](https://www.edps.europa.eu/data-protection/our-work/publications/techdispatch/2025-06-10-techdispatch-12025-federated-learning_en) highlights that FL does not eliminate provenance obligations — organizations must still demonstrate data quality and representativeness per Article 10, even when data remains decentralized

Gap: No production-ready open-source tool provides end-to-end lineage for federated learning pipelines as of April 2026. This is an active research area with significant regulatory implications as FL adoption grows.

### Poisoning Detection Techniques Enabled by Lineage

When lineage is comprehensive, incident response teams can apply targeted detection at specific pipeline stages:

- **Pre-training**: Per-class feature drift monitoring, label entropy checks, cosine similarity baselines against known-good data distributions
- **During training**: Gradient norm outlier detection (flags samples exerting outsized influence on weights), per-example influence function estimation, suspicious performance gains on narrow data slices
- **Post-training**: Backdoor activation probes with targeted trigger patterns, red-team prompt variants testing semantic preservation across formatting changes
- **Supply chain**: Transparency log monitoring via sigstore for unauthorized model signing events, signed dataset manifests for immutable dataset versioning
