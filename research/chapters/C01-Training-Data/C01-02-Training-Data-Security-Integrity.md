# C1.2: Training Data Security & Integrity

> [Back to C01 Index](C01-Training-Data.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-11

## Purpose

Training data must be protected against tampering, corruption, and poisoning throughout its lifecycle. The current AISVS source draws a deliberate scope line here: generic data security controls — access control on storage, access logging, and encryption at rest and in transit — are covered by OWASP ASVS v5 (V6, V7, V8) and must be implemented as a baseline before the AI-specific controls in this section mean anything. What remains in C1.2 is the part conventional data security does not cover: cryptographic integrity verification against data poisoning, automated tamper monitoring, immutable dataset versioning for rollback and forensics, and the residual-memorization risk that persists in trained models after training data is retired.

That last point is what makes this section different from a storage-security checklist. A compromised training dataset can silently degrade model behavior, and unlike a conventional data breach, the damage may persist in model weights long after the poisoned or disallowed data is removed from storage. Recent research keeps lowering the bar for attackers: OATML, Anthropic, the UK AI Security Institute, and the Alan Turing Institute reported in October 2025 that about 250 malicious documents could backdoor models from 600M to 13B parameters, and a January 2025 *Nature Medicine* study found harmful medical behavior after replacing only 0.001% of training tokens with misinformation. MITRE ATLAS 5.6.0 tracks `AML.T0020 Poison Training Data`, `AML.T0019 Publish Poisoned Datasets`, and `AML.T0010.002 AI Supply Chain Compromise: Data` as realized or demonstrated techniques.

The regulatory clock still matters, with one nuance worth getting right: the Digital Omnibus deferral of EU AI Act Article 10 obligations (Annex III high-risk from 2 August 2026 to 2 December 2027, Annex I embedded high-risk to 2 August 2028) rests on a 7 May 2026 provisional political agreement — formal adoption and Official Journal publication are still pending as of June 2026, so the new dates are expected but not yet law. Two obligations are not deferred: Article 50 transparency still lands 2 August 2026, and AI Office enforcement of the GPAI training-content summary duty (Article 53(1)(d), template published July 2025, refresh required every six months of further training) also begins 2 August 2026. On the deletion side, EDPB Opinion 28/2024 (December 2024) established that whether a trained model is anonymous requires a case-by-case extraction-risk assessment — if personal data can be extracted from the model with means reasonably likely to be used, GDPR applies to the model itself, which is exactly the residual-memorization question requirement 1.2.1 forces teams to document. The EDPB's coordinated enforcement action on the right to erasure concluded with a final report adopted 10 February 2026; among its findings across 764 controllers, reliance on weak anonymisation as a substitute for actual deletion was flagged as a recurring problem — the same failure mode as an undocumented unlearning claim.

## Current Research Notes

* **Check the ASVS v5 baseline first, then the AI-specific layer.** Since the source section now delegates storage access control, access logging, and encryption to OWASP ASVS v5 (V6 cryptography, V7 logging, V8 data protection), reviewers should confirm that baseline evidence exists — IAM policies, object-level audit logs, encryption configuration — and then spend their AISVS effort on what ASVS cannot answer: are dataset hashes verified before training, would tampering be detected, can a poisoned version be rolled back, and what happens to model weights when data is retired.
* **"Unlearning" claims need adversarial evidence, not query checks.** ICLR 2025 work from CMU showed that benign relearning attacks — fine-tuning on small amounts of loosely related public data — recover supposedly unlearned content from models scrubbed with Gradient Ascent, Gradient Difference, KL minimization, NPO, and SCRUB. A separate 2025 line of work demonstrated prototypical relearning attacks restoring forget-class behavior with a handful of samples. If a 1.2.1 impact assessment selects machine unlearning as the mitigation, the verification evidence should include relearning-resistance and membership-inference testing, not just confirmation that the model no longer answers the deleted-topic queries.
* **Integrity controls need to be pipeline controls, not only storage controls.** Most poisoning paths run through ingestion jobs, feature/embedding pipelines, labeling queues, temporary caches, and training-node scratch space. Hash verification and tamper monitoring evidence should show the same checks at each handoff, not just on the bucket where the canonical copy rests.
* **Provider checksum defaults are not tamper evidence.** Since December 2024, Amazon S3 attaches full-object CRC64NVME checksums to new uploads by default and validates them server-side. That detects corruption, not malice — CRCs are trivially forgeable by anyone who can write the object. Requirement 1.2.2 evidence needs keyed or signed integrity: per-shard SHA-256 manifests, detached signatures, or attestations. Hugging Face `datasets` supports SHA-256 checksum verification on load but it is disabled by default, so auditors should check that pipelines actually enable it.
* **Dataset signing is improving, but still lacks one dominant workflow.** OpenSSF's June 2025 OMS specification describes a detached signature format that can bind related AI artifacts — model weights, configuration files, tokenizers, and datasets — into one verifiable unit, and Sigstore's model-transparency tooling reached v1.0 in 2025. For large or mutable training corpora, auditors should still expect signed manifests, per-shard hashes, Merkle-tree commits, or lakehouse snapshot IDs rather than assuming one ecosystem standard covers every storage pattern.
* **Versioning and immutability are separate claims.** DVC, lakeFS, Delta Lake, and Iceberg can give reproducible version IDs and rollback points. True tamper resistance still needs an append-only or WORM backing store, such as S3 Object Lock Compliance Mode, Azure immutable blob policies, or GCS retention locks, plus logs that capture who created, deleted, or restored each version. Lakehouse time travel is also bounded by retention settings — a `VACUUM` job can quietly destroy the forensic history a 1.2.4 rollback depends on.
* **Management-system standards now name specific data controls.** ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Annex A includes A.7.4 (data quality, including documented poisoning checks), A.7.5 (data provenance and chain of custody), and A.7.6 (documented data-preparation methods). ISO/IEC 5259-5:2025 adds a governance framework for data quality across the ML lifecycle. Auditors increasingly expect dataset-integrity evidence to map back to these controls alongside AISVS requirement IDs.
* **Three more control catalogs are converging on this section.** ISO/IEC 27090 (security threats and compromises to AI systems, with per-threat detection and mitigation guidance including training-data poisoning) reached FDIS in March 2026 with publication expected late 2026. NIST's COSAiS project is drafting SP 800-53 control overlays scoped to protect AI models, training data, weights, and configurations — concept paper August 2025, first annotated outline January 2026, no final overlay yet. CSA's AI Controls Matrix v1.0 (July 2025, 243 control objectives including Data Security & Privacy and Data Lineage domains) now anchors the STAR for AI assurance program, whose Level 2 third-party assessment became available in mid-2026. None of these replaces AISVS verification detail, but mapping 1.2.x evidence to them once is cheaper than re-deriving it per audit.
* **Content addressing and provenance metadata are arriving on the hubs — but neither is signer identity.** Hugging Face's Xet storage content-addresses every dataset chunk and file with BLAKE3 hashes, and MLCommons Croissant 1.1 (February 2026) adds machine-actionable provenance via W3C PROV-O plus structured usage policies, with 700K+ datasets carrying Croissant metadata across Hugging Face, Kaggle, and OpenML. These strengthen 1.2.2 evidence for corruption detection and origin documentation, but a content hash or metadata record still tells you nothing about *who* vouched for the bytes — detached signatures or attestations remain the tamper-evidence layer.
* **Deletion workflows are themselves an attack surface.** February 2026 work ("Protecting the Undeleted in Machine Unlearning") shows that even exact retraining-based unlearning leaks information about the *remaining* dataset: an adversary controlling a handful of data points can reconstruct nearly the entire dataset just by issuing deletion requests and observing model changes. 1.2.1 impact assessments that treat deletion as purely risk-reducing should note this — erasure pipelines need the same abuse monitoring as any other data-mutation path.

| **1.2.1** | **Verify that** when training data is retired or removed, an impact assessment is documented. The assessment covers all models trained on that data, the assessed residual memorization risk, and the selected mitigation (targeted fine-tuning, machine unlearning, model retraining, or documented risk acceptance). | 1 | Residual memorization: retired, unlawful, or poisoned data continuing to influence model behavior after deletion from storage. Memorized content is recoverable via training-data extraction and membership inference (MITRE ATLAS `AML.T0024 Exfiltration via AI Inference API`, `AML.T0057 LLM Data Leakage`), and the bar keeps dropping: SaTML 2026 work integrating membership inference into extraction pipelines pulled 33.5% of phone numbers seen only once during training from Llama-3.2-1B, and over 94% of samples seen five times from Qwen-2.5-1.5B. That creates GDPR Article 17 exposure when the data is personal — EDPB Opinion 28/2024 makes model anonymity a case-by-case extraction-risk assessment, and the EDPB's erasure enforcement sweep (final report February 2026) flagged weak anonymisation passed off as deletion as a recurring controller failure. Check the assessment states a residual-memorization judgment and a selected mitigation with rationale. Where unlearning or targeted fine-tuning was chosen, review the evaluation evidence: TOFU, MUSE, or WMDP-style benchmarks run through a framework such as OpenUnlearning (now the official maintenance point for TOFU and MUSE, with 450+ released unlearning checkpoints), plus adversarial checks — membership inference on the forget set, a relearning-resistance test, and quantization stress tests, since OpenUnlearning's own metric-faithfulness analysis shows ROUGE-L and extraction-strength drops can be gamed. Domain-specific benchmarks are appearing: AMNESIA (May 2026) evaluates patient-level medical unlearning across 70K QA pairs and finds unlearning one patient erodes knowledge of others with the same condition. Confirm the model-to-dataset-version mapping needed to even enumerate affected models exists (see C1.5). | The weakest link is usually the inventory: many programs cannot enumerate which models a given dataset shard influenced, which makes the impact assessment guesswork. Unlearning verification is fragmented — a 2025 survey splits methods into behavioral and parametric verification and finds both underexplored, and benign relearning defeats Gradient Ascent, Gradient Difference, KL minimization, NPO, and SCRUB. For most production systems the defensible mitigations remain retraining from a clean snapshot or documented risk acceptance; treat unlearning claims as research-grade unless adversarially evaluated. February 2026 theory adds a twist: even exact retraining leaks information about the undeleted remainder to an adversary who controls deletion requests, so erasure pipelines need rate limiting and abuse monitoring of their own. |
| **1.2.2** | **Verify that** cryptographic hashes or digital signatures are used to ensure data integrity when training data is stored and transferred. | 2 | Tampering with dataset shards in storage or in transit between ingestion, labeling vendors, feature pipelines, and training clusters — the substitution path for `AML.T0020 Poison Training Data` and `AML.T0010.002 AI Supply Chain Compromise: Data`. The May 2025 joint CISA/NSA/FBI AI data security guidance recommends cryptographic hashes and quantum-resistant digital signatures for trusted data revisions precisely because provenance claims are worthless if the bytes can be silently swapped after approval. Unauthenticated checksums (CRC32, CRC64NVME) do not mitigate this: anyone with write access can recompute them. | Inspect the dataset manifest scheme: per-shard SHA-256 (or stronger) hashes committed at ingestion, and verify the training pipeline actually re-validates hashes before each run and fails closed on mismatch — test with a deliberately altered canary shard. For cross-organization transfer (labeling vendors, public corpora, model hubs), look for detached signatures: Sigstore model-transparency (v1.1 added PKCS#11 hardware signing, private Sigstore instances, and BLAKE3) or OpenSSF OMS bundles binding datasets to signer identity, or in-toto/DSSE attestations — NVIDIA has signed all NGC Catalog models with OMS since March 2025, so adoption evidence exists to point to. For hub-hosted data, note what the platform already gives you: Hugging Face Xet storage content-addresses every chunk and file with BLAKE3, and Croissant 1.1 metadata (Hugging Face, Kaggle, OpenML) carries PROV-O provenance — useful corroboration, but neither binds bytes to a signer. Confirm Hugging Face `datasets` loads run with checksum verification enabled rather than the default `no_checks` behavior. Distinguish transport integrity (S3 default CRC64NVME upload validation, TLS) from end-to-end cryptographic integrity, and check signing keys or Sigstore identities are themselves access-controlled. | No dominant dataset-signing workflow exists yet; OMS and model-transparency adoption is ahead for model weights (NGC, Kaggle) but thin for multi-TB and streaming training corpora, where signed Merkle manifests or lakehouse snapshot hashes are the practical substitute. The in-toto attestation framework still has no dataset-specific predicate type (latest spec release remains v1.2.0, March 2024), so teams reuse SLSA Provenance for dataset attestations. Hash verification is frequently implemented at ingestion but skipped at training time for performance, which is exactly the window an insider would use. Post-quantum signing (FIPS 204 ML-DSA, finalized August 2024) is recommended in the joint guidance but rare in production dataset workflows as of mid-2026. |
| **1.2.3** | **Verify that** automated integrity monitoring is applied to guard against unauthorized modifications or corruption of training data. | 2 | Slow-burn poisoning and insider modification between dataset approval and training execution — small, targeted edits that one-time hash checks miss because they happen after the manifest was cut. The October 2025 constant-poison result (~250 documents backdoor 600M–13B-parameter models) and the *Nature Medicine* 0.001%-token result mean integrity monitoring must catch absolute-count-scale changes, not just bulk corruption; clean-label work published March 2026 (ProAttack, *Expert Systems with Applications*) pushed the bar lower still — near-100% backdoor success with as few as 6 poisoned samples carrying correct labels and natural text, with ONION, SCPD, back-translation, and fine-pruning all failing to reliably remove it. Storage-layer corruption (bit rot, failed replication) is the second, non-adversarial failure mode this requirement covers. | Confirm monitoring is continuous, not point-in-time: scheduled re-hashing of dataset shards against the signed manifest with alerts on drift; object-version and delete-marker alerting on the canonical buckets; file integrity monitoring (Wazuh FIM, CimTrak, Netwrix Change Tracker) on training-node staging areas and NFS/scratch volumes; and data-observability anomaly detection (Monte Carlo, Soda) for record-level distribution shifts that byte hashes cannot see. Verify alerts route to security on-call with runbooks, and run a live test: modify one canary record out-of-band and time the detection. Cross-check that monitoring covers derived copies — feature stores, embedding caches, tokenized shards — not just the source-of-truth bucket. | Classic FIM tooling is filesystem-centric; object-store and lakehouse-native tamper detection is still an emerging category, so most teams assemble it from audit-log alerting plus scheduled re-hash jobs. Cost pressure is the recurring failure: continuous re-hashing of multi-TB corpora gets disabled or sampled, and sampling can miss the single write that matters. Byte-level integrity monitoring also cannot detect semantically poisoned data that was ingested "legitimately" through upstream sources — that requires the statistical and embedding-space checks in C1.4. |
| **1.2.4** | **Verify that** all training dataset versions are uniquely identified, stored immutably, and auditable to support rollback and forensic analysis. | 2 | Inability to recover from discovered poisoning: when a backdoor or bad batch is found post-training, teams without immutable versions cannot determine which runs consumed the poisoned shards, cannot rebuild the pre-poisoning dataset state, and cannot prove to auditors or counsel what the model was actually trained on. Attackers (or embarrassed insiders) rewriting dataset history to cover a poisoning event is the adversarial variant — `AML.T0020` paired with log/version destruction. EU AI Act Annex IV technical documentation and ISO/IEC 42001 A.7.5 chain-of-custody expectations both lean on this evidence. | Verify every training and fine-tuning run records an immutable dataset version ID (DVC/lakeFS commit, Delta Lake or Iceberg snapshot ID) in run metadata (MLflow, W&B, or pipeline logs), so model-to-data mapping is mechanical. Check immutability is real: S3 Object Lock in Compliance Mode, Azure immutable blob policies (version-level WORM must be enabled at storage-account creation and cannot be added later — a provisioning detail that surfaces in audits), or GCS retention locks on the version store — not just bucket versioning, which an admin can purge. Review lakehouse retention settings to confirm time travel covers the model lifetime (no `VACUUM` destroying needed snapshots). Run a rollback drill: pick a six-month-old training run and rebuild its exact dataset from the recorded version ID, timing the exercise. Confirm version creation, restore, and deletion events appear in audit logs with actor identity. | Version control and immutability are routinely conflated: DVC and lakeFS give reproducible IDs but inherit the mutability of their backing store unless WORM controls are layered on. Iceberg manifests carry per-file checksums and Delta's transaction log is append-only, but neither format cryptographically signs its log or metadata as of mid-2026, so lakehouse "tamper evidence" stops at whoever can write the metadata layer. Lakehouse retention defaults (e.g., 7-day time travel) are far shorter than model lifetimes. For GPAI providers, the Article 53(1)(d) training-content summary (refresh required every six months of further training, AI Office enforcement from August 2026) is only mechanically producible if this requirement's version IDs exist. Few organizations rehearse rollback, so the capability exists on paper but fails under incident pressure — the rollback drill is the single most revealing audit step. Storage cost for immutable multi-version corpora is the practical objection; Merkle/dedup-based stores mitigate but complicate the integrity story. |

## Notable Incidents

* **Anthropic Mythos preview, April 2026.** Information about Anthropic's infrastructure practices leaked through a cyberattack on labeling and contractor platform Mercor, itself tied to a compromise of the open-source LiteLLM project. A third-party contractor with legitimate vendor access then provided the initial foothold, and an external group reached the Mythos preview model on the day it was announced. Treat this as the recent reference point for third-party labeling, RLHF, and data-vendor access being part of the training-data attack surface — and for why dataset integrity must be re-verified after vendor handoffs, not assumed from vendor contracts.
* **Constant-poison study, October 2025.** OATML, Anthropic, the UK AI Security Institute, and the Alan Turing Institute reported that around 250 malicious documents could backdoor models with 600M to 13B parameters, regardless of overall dataset size. This shifted the integrity-monitoring story from percentage thresholds toward absolute counts and provenance signals.
* **Benign relearning results, ICLR 2025.** CMU researchers showed that fine-tuning on small amounts of loosely related public data recovers content from models "unlearned" with Gradient Ascent, Gradient Difference, KL minimization, NPO, and SCRUB — including verbatim copyrighted text and hazardous-knowledge benchmarks. The practical consequence for 1.2.1: an impact assessment that selects unlearning without adversarial evaluation documents a mitigation that may not exist.
* **Medical-LLM poisoning, January 2025.** A *Nature Medicine* study showed that replacing about 0.001% of training tokens with deliberate misinformation made medical LLMs produce harmful answers while still passing common benchmarks. The result reinforces why byte-level integrity monitoring alone is insufficient and must be paired with the semantic and behavioral checks described in C1.4.
* **ProAttack clean-label backdoor, March 2026.** NTU researchers published a prompt-based clean-label backdoor in *Expert Systems with Applications*: malicious prompts attached to correctly labeled, natural-looking samples achieved near-100% attack success across five datasets and five models, in some configurations with only six poisoned samples, and survived ONION, SCPD, back-translation, and fine-pruning defenses. Shared public prompt templates and open datasets are the delivery vector.
* **Targeted extraction via membership inference, SaTML 2026.** Work submitted December 2025 showed that integrating membership-inference attacks into extraction pipelines recovers 33.5% of phone numbers seen exactly once in training from Llama-3.2-1B and over 94% of five-occurrence samples from Qwen-2.5-1.5B — concrete numbers for the extraction-risk assessments EDPB Opinion 28/2024 and requirement 1.2.1 call for.

## Implementation Maturity

* **Mature.** The delegated ASVS v5 baseline (cloud IAM, object-level audit logging, encryption at rest and in transit), SHA-256 manifests, S3/Azure/GCS WORM and retention-lock controls, default upload-integrity checksums (S3 CRC64NVME since December 2024), and lakehouse versioning with DVC, lakeFS, Delta Lake, and Iceberg.
* **Improving.** Dataset signing and bundled attestations through OpenSSF Model Signing (model-transparency v1.1 with hardware-key and private-instance support; OMS shipped in production at NVIDIA NGC and Kaggle), Sigstore/Cosign, and in-toto/DSSE under SLSA builders; hub-native content addressing (Hugging Face Xet BLAKE3) and provenance metadata (Croissant 1.1 with PROV-O, 700K+ datasets); CycloneDX ML-BOM 1.7 / SPDX 3.0 AI-BOM documentation; unlearning evaluation frameworks (OpenUnlearning unifying TOFU, MUSE, and WMDP with 13 algorithms and 16 metrics, plus domain benchmarks like AMNESIA). Workflows are consolidating but not yet standard practice for multi-TB and streaming corpora.
* **Emerging.** Object-store and lakehouse-native tamper detection (versus filesystem FIM) — lakehouse formats still lack signed transaction logs; quantum-resistant dataset signatures using FIPS 204 (ML-DSA); residual-memorization impact assessment practice for 1.2.1; control-catalog coverage of this section (ISO/IEC 27090 at FDIS, NIST COSAiS overlays in draft, CSA AICM v1.0 with STAR for AI Level 2 newly available); and machine unlearning itself — relearning attacks, deletion-request leakage of undeleted data, and fragmented verification methods keep it research-grade, so retraining from a clean immutable snapshot remains the realistic remediation for data already incorporated into model weights.
