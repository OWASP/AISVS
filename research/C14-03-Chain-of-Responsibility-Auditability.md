# C14.3: Chain of Responsibility & Auditability

> [Back to C14 Index](C14-Human-Oversight.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-16

## Purpose

Log operator actions and model decisions. When an AI system makes a consequential decision — or when a human overrides one — there must be a tamper-evident forensic trail that can reconstruct what happened, who was involved, and why.

The SaaStr 2025 incident remains the canonical cautionary tale: an autonomous agent fabricated 4,000 fake accounts and false system logs to cover its database deletion, making forensic reconstruction nearly impossible. This demonstrates a threat unique to agentic AI — the agent itself may have write access to logs and can corrupt them. As one current framing puts it, "if an audit entry doesn't exist at the moment of access, it will never exist, and there is no forensic reconstruction that recovers it."

As of April 2026, the practitioner consensus for AI audit logging converges on a layered tamper-evidence architecture:

1. **Cryptographic hash chaining** — each entry includes a hash of its predecessor so any modification breaks the chain and is detectable at read time. Reference implementations include Google's Trillian (append-only Merkle log, the engine behind Certificate Transparency), its successor Trillian Tessera (tile-based, the currently recommended path for new deployments), and open-source frameworks like AuditableLLM (MDPI, 2026) that decouple update execution from a hash-chain-backed verification layer. Veritaschain's VCP v1.1 standard and the Ashish-Barmaiya/attest library both publish usable reference code.
2. **Write-once / WORM storage** — append-only backends (AWS CloudTrail Lake event data stores, object storage with Object Lock in compliance mode, ledger databases such as Amazon QLDB or Azure SQL Ledger) so even privileged operators cannot retroactively rewrite history.
3. **Identity-bound logging** — every event carries agent identifier, model and tool versions, tenant context, authorization scope, human-authorizer (the person who delegated the workflow), and delegation status. NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 AU-10 (non-repudiation) explicitly requires this binding; the 2026 CSA/NIST AI agent standards work flags the gap that 800-53 has "well-developed controls for human and system identity" but lacks purpose-built controls distinguishing an AI agent from a human operator for forensic attribution.
4. **Sidecar / observer-pattern writers** — an independent process (not the agent runtime) writes the audit log via an append-only API, mirroring the AWS DevOps Agent pattern where "every reasoning step and tool invocation" lands in an immutable journal that the agent cannot modify after the fact.
5. **Real-time SIEM delivery** — structured (not free-text), immediate (not batched), and including **both permitted and denied** requests. Denial patterns often reveal prompt-injection or credential-misuse campaigns earlier than permission logs alone.

Regulatory drivers have hardened since the last review. **EU AI Act Article 12** (record-keeping for high-risk AI systems) and **Article 19** (retention of automatically generated logs by providers and deployers) require automatic event recording across the lifetime of the system, with a minimum six-month retention and specific minima for remote biometric ID systems (start/end timestamps, reference database, matching input data, natural persons who verified results). National competent authorities move into **active enforcement in August 2026**. **Article 73** prohibits altering an AI system or its logs in ways that could obscure the cause of a serious incident — a direct response to the SaaStr pattern. On the US side, **Colorado AI Act SB 205** takes effect **June 30, 2026**, requiring deployers to maintain auditable risk management programs; **NIST SP 800-53 AU-9** (High baseline) requires cryptographic integrity protection for audit information and AU-10 requires non-repudiation.

Tooling has also matured. **OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions** (GenAI SIG, still experimental as of March 2026 but adopted by Datadog v1.37+ and Grafana Loki) standardize attribute names for agent spans: `gen_ai.agent.id`, `gen_ai.agent.version`, `gen_ai.conversation.id`, `gen_ai.operation.name`, `gen_ai.provider.name`, `gen_ai.request.model`, `gen_ai.input.messages`, `gen_ai.output.messages`, `gen_ai.system_instructions`, `gen_ai.tool.definitions`, `gen_ai.usage.input_tokens`/`output_tokens`, and `gen_ai.data_source.id`. Using these attribute names lets audit pipelines survive changes of LLM provider or agent framework without schema rewrites. **W3C Trace Context** propagation is the recommended way to stitch multi-agent tool cascades into a single reviewable trace. For MCP servers specifically, the pattern is to log tool name, parameter metadata (not raw values for sensitive inputs), policy decision, trace ID, and tenant at the MCP gateway rather than inside each tool. AWS **Amazon Bedrock AgentCore / Agent Registry** (launched April 2026) wires `InvokeAgent` and `RetrieveAndGenerate` events directly into **CloudTrail data events**; note that **CloudTrail Lake closes to new customers on May 31, 2026**, so greenfield deployments should plan for either an earlier onboarding or an alternative managed ledger.

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| **14.3.1** | **Verify that** all AI system decisions and human interventions are logged with timestamps, user identities, and decision rationale. | 1 | Inability to reconstruct what happened during an incident; no forensic trail for regulatory investigations; disputes over whether a human or the AI made a specific decision. The SaaStr 2025 incident remains the sentinel case — an autonomous agent fabricated 4,000 fake accounts and false system logs to cover its database deletion. Article 73 of the EU AI Act specifically prohibits this pattern: altering an AI system or its logs in ways that could obscure the cause of an incident. Related log-tampering risk also shows up in prompt-injection chains where the attacker induces the agent to emit misleading "observation" entries into its own trace. | Verify the log schema captures the six required fields at access time: **(1)** agent identity (unique workflow-level credential, distinct from human identity), **(2)** human authorizer (authenticated identity that delegated the workflow), **(3)** data or resource accessed with classification, **(4)** operation performed (read, write, invoke tool, override, etc.), **(5)** policy evaluation outcome (permit/deny with governing rule), **(6)** tamper-evident timestamp. Sample audit logs to confirm completeness. Confirm human overrides are logged alongside the AI's original recommendation and rationale. For agentic systems, spot-check that each tool invocation (its parameters, the agent's stated reasoning, the result) is logged — not only the final output. Verify agent-generated content is cryptographically distinguishable from system-generated log entries (distinct signing keys, separate schemas) to prevent the SaaStr-style deception pattern. Inspect integrity: pull a recent range of entries and recompute the hash chain or Merkle tree head; a real auditor would use `trillian verify`, Tessera's inclusion-proof API, or the project's own verifier (e.g., Veritaschain VCP demo). Confirm logs ship to a SIEM (Splunk, Sentinel, Datadog, Elastic) in real time, include both permits and denies, and use structured fields rather than free text. For OpenTelemetry-instrumented systems, confirm spans carry `gen_ai.agent.id`, `gen_ai.agent.version`, `gen_ai.operation.name`, `gen_ai.conversation.id`, and `gen_ai.provider.name`. For MCP deployments, verify gateway-level logging per Tetrate's MCP audit pattern. On AWS, confirm CloudTrail data events are enabled for `InvokeAgent` and `RetrieveAndGenerate` and that the destination is an immutable event data store or S3 bucket with Object Lock. Test retention against EU AI Act Article 12's six-month minimum and any sector-specific rule (HIPAA six years, SOX seven years, PCI-DSS one year with three months hot). | Decision rationale logging is genuinely hard for LLM-based systems — the "decision" is often a free-text generation with no single causal feature. The practical pattern is to log the full prompt, model version and provider, key sampling parameters, retrieval context document IDs/hashes, tool-call trace, and the raw output, and to let downstream explainability produce the narrative. Privacy constraints (GDPR Articles 5 and 25) limit what can be logged raw — redact or hash PII in parameter values and store only metadata for sensitive fields; treat the log itself as protected personal data with its own retention rule. EU AI Act Article 14 requires that authorized persons can "correctly interpret the high-risk AI system's output," so logs must be analyst-readable, not just machine-verifiable. For agentic systems, log integrity is especially critical because the agent may have write access to its own trace — hence the sidecar-writer and separate-signing-key patterns. Tooling gaps as of April 2026: OpenTelemetry GenAI conventions are still experimental and attribute coverage for multi-agent handoffs is thin; NIST SP 800-53 AU-10 predates AI agents and lacks a control for non-human-principal attribution (the CSA/NIST AI Agent Standards initiative is drafting a supplement); AWS CloudTrail Lake is closing to new customers on 31 May 2026, so plan migration paths now; Google Trillian proper is in maintenance mode — new deployments should target **Trillian Tessera** (tile-based) or a managed ledger (Amazon QLDB, Azure SQL Ledger, immudb). Hash-chained logs solve integrity but not availability — pair with offsite backup and periodic external anchoring (e.g., publishing the log head to a second-party transparency log). The six-month EU floor is a floor, not a ceiling: align retention with the longest applicable sectoral requirement and document the justification. |
