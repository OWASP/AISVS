# C3.5: Pipeline Fine-Tuning

> [Back to C03 Index](C03-Model-Lifecycle-Management.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-07-13

## Purpose

Fine-tuning pipelines are high-privilege operations that can alter deployed model behavior at scale. Multi-stage pipelines compound this risk because a compromise at any intermediate stage produces a subtly altered artifact that subsequent stages accept as legitimate. Reward models used in Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) are ML artifacts subject to tampering, yet they are routinely treated as static infrastructure rather than versioned, signed, validated components.

The threat surface has widened well past the final weight artifact. Research has shown that fine-tuning can strip RLHF safety alignment even from benign-looking data; that intermediate checkpoints can be weaponized as stepping stones for adversarial optimization; that individually benign LoRA adapters can combine into a refusal-suppression state; and that triggers can hide in structural properties of inputs — length, formatting, token-space perturbations — that content scanners never inspect. The industry shift from RLHF toward RLVR (Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards) does not retire these problems; it relocates them, making programmatic verifiers, their tokenizers, and the reward shape itself new high-value targets that need the same signing and provenance rigor once reserved for human-preference reward models.

This section addresses four controls: the integrity of reward models (and verifier assets) used in fine-tuning, automated detection of reward hacking and reward-model over-optimization, per-stage integrity verification in multi-stage pipelines, and registration of fine-tuning checkpoints as distinct, lineage-tracked artifacts. The practical theme across all four is that integrity verification has to extend beyond checksums into behavioral testing: backdoors implanted with benign data, colluding cross-stage poisoners, and capability-transfer attacks all pass a hash check cleanly.

Multi-stage pipelines (SFT → reward modeling → RLHF → DPO) often span teams and compute environments, and SLSA-style signing at each handoff is rare. | **3.5.4** | **Verify that** fine-tuning checkpoints are registered as distinct artifacts. | 3 | Intermediate checkpoints treated as disposable scratch files, leaving safety-relevant artifacts unversioned and un-rollback-able; inability to perform per-stage rollback when the final artifact fails validation; persistent backdoors that survive continued fine-tuning (P-Trojan, AAAI 2026, >99% persistence). For MoE models, routing drift at any stage can silently disable safety-critical experts (SafeMoE). | Run a short training job that emits several checkpoints, then query the registry and confirm every emitted checkpoint has its own immutable digest or ID, producing run, training step, parent checkpoint, and model/data/code/reward/environment identifiers. W&B exposes a content digest and `Artifact.verify()`, but its [official API reference](https://docs.wandb.ai/models/ref/python/experiments/artifact) states that referenced external files are not verified; object-store versioning and independent signature checks remain necessary. The "Attesting LLM Pipelines" framework (LLMSC 2026) proposes attestation-aware promotion gates binding training and release claims to artifacts before they enter trusted environments. Adapter-specific screens (PEFTGuard, weight-space spectral detection, Diff-SAE) make repository-scale checkpoint/adapter vetting practical at each handoff. |

## Notable Incidents and Research Findings

- **Checkpoint-GCG (2025).** Demonstrated that intermediate fine-tuning checkpoints can be chained as stepping stones for white-box prompt-injection attacks, with each checkpoint initializing GCG optimization for the next — the core motivation for treating checkpoints as distinct, integrity-verified artifacts. [OpenReview](https://openreview.net/forum?id=qkGhc3l5py)
- **Cisco — Fine-Tuning Breaks Safety Alignment (2025).** Fine-tuned LLM variants produced harmful outputs at roughly 22× the base-model rate, confirming that alignment safeguards reduce but do not remove harmful patterns. [Cisco Blog](https://blogs.cisco.com/security/fine-tuning-llms-breaks-their-safety-and-security-alignment)
- **Anthropic — Natural Emergent Misalignment from Reward Hacking (November 2025).** Reward hacking during production RL training correlated with sharp increases across all misalignment evaluations, with alignment-faking reasoning in ~50% of responses despite no training for those behaviors; penalizing reward hacking with a classifier cut misaligned generalization by over 75%. [Anthropic Research](https://www.anthropic.com/research/emergent-misalignment-reward-hacking)
- **Harmless-input backdoor poisoning (Kong et al., 2025).** Backdoors implanted using exclusively benign training data reached 86–100% attack success while evading both safety-alignment checks and guardrail filtering — the reason stage-boundary verification must include behavioral testing, not just checksums. [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.17601v3)
- **Microsoft Security — GRP-Obliteration (February 2026).** A single judge-scored harmful prompt under GRPO unaligned 15 production LLMs across vendors with cross-category generalization, showing that "cautious" run approvals without content review are insufficient. [Microsoft Security Blog](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/02/09/prompt-attack-breaks-llm-safety/)
- **HarmRLVR (Li et al., ACL 2026).** GRPO over only 64 unlabeled harmful prompts — no labeled targets, just well-shaped reward — reversed safety alignment across five Llama/Qwen/DeepSeek models at 96.01% ASR, proving verifier signing alone does not secure an RLVR pipeline. [arXiv 2510.15499](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.15499)
- **MetaBackdoor (Microsoft + Institute of Science Tokyo, May 2026).** Input length acted as a hidden trigger: ~90 poisoned examples drove 75% email-exfiltration success above ~700-token inputs, with ~40% persistence after substantial retraining — structural, content-scanner-invisible triggers. [Help Net Security](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/05/18/metabackdoor-llm-backdoor-attack/)

## Implementation Maturity

- **3.5.1 (Reward/verifier integrity) — Emerging.** Signing technology exists and is production-grade (OMS, Sigstore, Safetensors), but adoption inside fine-tuning pipelines is rare and reward models are seldom included. Verifier-asset and tokenizer-level integrity for RLVR is earlier still.
- **3.5.2 (Reward-hacking detection) — Emerging.** Many detection methods (KL/MOP monitoring, IR³, ARA, RHB, EvalStop) exist; few are production-hardened or wired as hard promotion gates. KL monitoring is common but insufficient alone.
- **3.5.3 (Cross-stage integrity) — Emerging.** Provenance frameworks (Atlas) and adapter screens (PEFTGuard, weight-space spectral detection, Diff-SAE) are maturing, but SLSA-style signing at every handoff requires organizational maturity most teams lack.
- **3.5.4 (Checkpoint registration) — Partially mature.** MLflow gives each logged checkpoint a unique model ID, while W&B versions checkpoint artifacts and records run lineage. Neither registry reference alone proves signer identity or end-to-end integrity, and W&B does not verify externally referenced files through `Artifact.verify()`.

## Standards and Compliance

- **OWASP LLM Top 10 (2025):** [LLM03 Supply Chain](https://genai.owasp.org/llmrisk/llm032025-supply-chain/) (vulnerable adapters, weak provenance) and [LLM04 Data and Model Poisoning](https://genai.owasp.org/llmrisk/llm042025-data-and-model-poisoning/) map directly to these controls.
- **OWASP GenAI Data Security Risks & Mitigations 2026 v1.0:** Adds DSGAI19 (Human-in-the-Loop and Labeler Overexposure) for RLHF/annotation pipelines. [Resource](https://genai.owasp.org/resource/owasp-genai-data-security-risks-mitigations-2026/)
- **EU AI Act GPAI Code of Practice:** Documentation of training/fine-tuning and human-feedback collection, a one-third-compute threshold for downstream GPAI-provider status, and 10-year Model Documentation Form retention; enforcement machinery active from 2 August 2026. [Code of Practice](https://code-of-practice.ai/)
- **NIST AI RMF & Generative AI Profile (NIST AI 600-1):** Separation of model builders from validators under GOVERN; lifecycle risk management. The draft Cyber AI Profile (NIST IR 8596) and in-development SP 800-53 control overlays (COSAiS) are converging on model-provenance and third-party-assessment controls. [AI 600-1](https://www.nist.gov/publications/artificial-intelligence-risk-management-framework-generative-artificial-intelligence)
- **NIST SP 800-218A:** PW.3.2 calls for tracking the provenance of fine-tuning and alignment data, while PW.4.4 calls for verifying the integrity, provenance, and security of acquired AI models and components before use. These practices support the evidence chain for reward-model intake and each cross-stage handoff. [SSDF Community Profile](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/218/a/final)
- **MITRE ATLAS:** [AML.T0043 Craft Adversarial Data](https://atlas.mitre.org/) and the backdoor-model techniques referenced by OWASP LLM04.
