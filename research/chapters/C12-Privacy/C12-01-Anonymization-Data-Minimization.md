# C12.1: Anonymization & Data Minimization

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-10

## Purpose

Remove or transform personal identifiers before training to prevent re-identification and minimize privacy exposure. AI privacy incidents jumped 56.4% in 2024, with nearly half involving customer PII. As of May 2026, at least 20 AI-app data breaches have been documented since January 2025 — including Chat & Ask AI (300M+ messages), McHire/McDonald's (64M job applicants), OmniGPT (34M conversation lines), Bondu AI Toy (50K children's transcripts), and Sears Home Services (3.7M chat logs with audio recordings spanning 2024-2026). The March-April 2026 **Mercor breach** raised the stakes for upstream training-data intermediaries: a 40-minute window of malicious LiteLLM packages (v1.82.7 and v1.82.8) on PyPI on 24 March 2026 — published by threat actor group TeamPCP after compromising the Trivy scanner used in LiteLLM's CI/CD pipeline — cascaded into the theft of roughly 4 TB from Mercor on 31 March 2026, including 939 GB of platform source, a 211 GB user database covering 40,000+ contractors, and ~3 TB of video interview recordings and identity verification documents. Stolen material reportedly exposed RLHF training strategies, labeling protocols, and data selection criteria used by Meta, OpenAI, and Anthropic; Meta paused contracts and at least seven class actions (lead case Gill v. Mercor.io Corporation, N.D. Cal., filed 1 April 2026) allege failure to implement MFA, encryption, access limits, monitoring, and credential rotation. The EDPB's Opinion 28/2024 (adopted 17 December 2024) set an extremely high bar for anonymization in AI contexts: for a model to be considered anonymous it must be "very unlikely" to directly or indirectly identify individuals in the training set AND "very unlikely" to allow extraction of personal data via queries. The EDPB explicitly warned that "LLMs will not generally be considered anonymous" and anonymity must be assessed on a case-by-case basis; the April 2025 SPE LLM privacy-risks report (Isabel Barbera) and the EDPB's 2025 Annual Report (adopted 9 April 2026) confirmed that the ChatGPT Taskforce has been extended "to cover Generative AI more broadly," with €1.15 billion in combined GDPR fines across member states in 2025. The picture is not unidirectional: on 18 March 2026 the **Court of Rome annulled the Garante's €15M OpenAI decision** of 2 November 2024, leaving the underlying anonymization-and-lawful-basis questions unsettled (a Garante appeal remains likely). Divergence attacks (Carlini et al. / DeepMind / Google research, extended through 2025) showed ChatGPT leaks training data at 150x the normal rate when forced to repeat tokens, and the PII-Scope benchmark (arXiv 2410.06704, updated May 2025) found that 16.9% of generations contained memorized PII with 85.8% of suspected PII confirmed as real — extraction rates up to 5x higher against fine-tuned vs. pretrained models. Model-inversion work against Llama 3.2-1B (arXiv 2507.04478, July 2025) extracted emails, passwords, phone numbers, account fragments, and LinkedIn profiles via simple black-box prompts ("account number:", "my password is:") and confirmed the extracted values were real through Google verification — reinforcing that small-model deployment is not by itself a privacy control. The EU AI Act's high-risk obligations were deferred to 2 December 2027 by the May 2026 Digital Omnibus (Annex I products: 2 August 2028), while Article 50 transparency stays on the 2 August 2026 timeline; the Commission published the mandatory **public summary of training content template** under Article 53(1)(d) on 24 July 2025, requiring GPAI providers to disclose dataset sources, collection methods, content types, and an update at six-month intervals — non-compliance carries fines up to EUR 15M or 3% of global turnover (and up to EUR 35M or 7% for the broader GPAI violations). Models on the market before 2 August 2025 must publish their summary by 2 August 2027.

The 8 May 2026 **California AG / GM/OnStar settlement** ($12.75M — announced by AG Bonta with the Los Angeles, San Francisco, Alameda, and Riverside DAs and the California Privacy Protection Agency, and characterized by IAPP as "the largest CCPA fine to date" and the first CCPA action focused on data minimization and purpose limitation) is the strongest US-side enforcement signal yet that "necessary and proportional" is a litigable standard, not a policy aspiration: GM/OnStar allegedly collected and sold drivers' geolocation, driving-behavior, and PII to LexisNexis Risk Solutions and Verisk Analytics without adequate notice or consent. Beyond the cash penalty, the consent decree bars GM from selling driving data to consumer reporting agencies for five years, requires deletion of retained driving data within 180 days absent affirmative express consumer consent, requires GM to *request deletion* of the already-shared driving data from LexisNexis and Verisk, and obligates GM to build a privacy program with documented assessments and reporting — the deletion-request clause is the practical template controllers should now copy into AI-training data-sharing contracts because it shows a regulator was willing to demand downstream-cleanup as a remediation step, not just a forward-looking restriction. Read alongside the Mercor and OkCupid/Match precedents, the GM settlement is the third 2025-2026 enforcement track demonstrating that *upstream* data-sharing partners (data brokers, AI labelers, third-party AI integrators) are reachable through the regulated controller and that "I sold it to a partner" is not a get-out-of-minimization-jail card. May 2026 brought several practical reminders that minimization has to happen before data ever reaches model-adjacent tools. The Register's 3 March 2026 reporting on Lee Dryburgh's investigation showed how minimization can fail at the *client* boundary: deceptive Chrome/Edge extensions posing as VPNs or ad-blockers overrode `fetch()` and `XMLHttpRequest()` to silently intercept conversations with ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and DeepSeek, then forwarded them to a venture-backed "generative engine optimization" data broker that offered API-based semantic search to authenticated paying customers — ~490 unique prompts across 20 sensitive categories were catalogued, including HIPAA clinical notes, HIV/STD/cancer/fertility lab results, depression and suicide conversations, immigration and asylum PII, domestic-violence narratives, and children's chats; pseudonymous SHA-256 user IDs gave no real protection because real names, DOBs, MRNs, and diagnosis codes lived in the prompt text itself. CB Financial Services disclosed that Community Bank handled customer names, Social Security numbers, and dates of birth through an unauthorized AI-based application; a patient-facing medical RAG case study found full health-chat records retrievable without authentication; and a May 2026 Hacker News-published scan of roughly 1 million internet-exposed AI services found large numbers of unauthenticated chat histories, dataset endpoints, and model artifacts indexable by anyone with a project URL. The 14 May 2026 update to **Couture v. OpenAI** (originally filed in the Northern District of California on 6 May 2026, with a second complaint filed in the Southern District of California on 14 May 2026) extended the minimization problem from training-set construction into the analytics layer: the suit alleges OpenAI's Meta Pixel and Google Analytics integrations on ChatGPT.com disclosed chat-query topics, user IDs, and email addresses to Meta and Google without consent, and seeks $5,000 per violation under ECPA, CIPA, and California constitutional privacy rights — the controller's PII-redaction story does not cover prompts or response metadata that the front-end leaks to third-party tags. Verizon's 2026 DBIR (released 20 May 2026) put numbers behind the shadow-AI exposure: it analyzed 858,440 DLP events tied to uploads into generative-AI tools, ranked source code as the dominant pasted data type, found 67% of users accessing AI services from non-corporate accounts on corporate devices, recorded that shadow AI is now the third most common non-malicious insider action (a fourfold year-over-year increase), reported research/technical documentation in 3.2% of policy violations, and observed that 60% of malicious-insider breaches are now driven by *convenience*. CISA's interim chief Madhu Gottumukkala uploaded "For Official Use Only" contracting documents into public ChatGPT in late January 2026 despite DHS restrictions — a high-profile reminder that minimization controls applied only at the training corpus do nothing about ad-hoc prompts. Check Point Research disclosed (and OpenAI patched, 20 February 2026) a hidden DNS-based outbound channel in the ChatGPT code-execution runtime that allowed sensitive conversation data to be silently exfiltrated despite the runtime's stated no-direct-outbound-network guardrail; treat tool-use/code-execution sandboxes as a minimization egress surface, not just an isolation surface. HHS OCR closed 11 hacking-incident investigations with HIPAA risk-analysis penalties by 31 January 2026, federal civil-penalty caps for repeated violations now exceed US $2M, and industry surveys (Censinet, Kiteworks) report that 65% of organizations cannot prove dataset access controls and 53% have no way to recover or unlearn training data after an incident — "prompt leaking" is now tracked as an emerging HIPAA breach category. The 20 January 2026 **Eightfold AI** putative class action (Kistler & Bhaumik v. Eightfold AI, N.D. Cal.) alleges scraping of personal data on over one billion workers, generation of hidden FCRA/ICRAA-grade "consumer reports" scoring applicants 0-5, and silent removal of low-ranked candidates before human review — read as the HR-tech mirror image of the Mercor/OkCupid pattern: the upstream training/scoring corpus is itself the regulated artifact, and downstream model uses do not cure the upstream minimization failure. On 6 May 2026 the **PIPEDA Findings #2026-002** joint investigation — Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada with Quebec's CAI, British Columbia's OIPC, and Alberta's OIPC — concluded that OpenAI's training of GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 violated PIPEDA s.5(3), PIPA-BC ss.2/11/14/17, PIPA-AB ss.11/16/19, and Quebec's Private Sector Act s.5: scraping from public sources and licensed datasets was "overbroad and therefore not necessary and proportional," no valid consent was obtained for the sensitive information collected (financial, medical, religious/political beliefs, information relating to children), and the company did not screen out children-aimed or vulnerable-group sites. The OPC and Quebec's CAI accepted OpenAI's 3-6 month remediation plan as resolving their complaint, but the BC and Alberta commissioners labelled the complaint "well-founded but unresolved" — their position is that PIPA-BC and PIPA-AB are more specific than PIPEDA on consent for personal-information collection and that consent for already-scraped data cannot be obtained after the fact, so the foundational lawful-basis question remains open in those provinces and may force a different remediation path for any BC/Alberta-resident dataset in future models. OpenAI's remediation set the most concrete production benchmark to date for training-data minimization tooling: a PII detection-and-masking tool reported at 98-99% recall with 3-6% false-positive rates, contextual filtering that distinguishes private individuals from public figures and fictional characters, application of the same masking tool to user-generated fine-tuning data, retirement of GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, and contractual conditioning of licensed-data partnerships on appropriate consents — auditors should now treat ~98% recall / ≤5% FPR as a defensible floor for any in-house or vendor redaction claim covering similar entity classes. The MIA literature has also moved decisively in 2026: AttenMIA (arXiv 2601.18110, 26 Jan 2026) reached 0.996 ROC AUC and 87.9% TPR at 1% FPR on WikiMIA-32 against Llama2-13b by reading self-attention divergence rather than output logits; token-level InfoRMIA (arXiv 2510.05582, Oct 2025) and HT-MIA "What Hard Tokens Reveal" (arXiv 2601.20885, Jan 2026) localize leakage down to the individual token, beating seven SOTA baselines on medical and general-purpose corpora; EL-MIA (arXiv 2511.00192, 31 Oct 2025) reframed the audit at entity granularity — testing whether a specific PII span or credit-card number was in training — and the federated-setting ProjRes attack (arXiv 2604.21197, accepted IEEE S&P 2026) hit ~100% accuracy on four federated LLMs and remained effective under "strong" DP. The synthetic-data threat model widened too: "When Privacy Isn't Synthetic" (arXiv 2512.06062, Dec 2025) showed a model-agnostic, black-box cluster-overlap attack that leaks training-set membership from GANs, VAEs, diffusion models, and LLMs — including DP-trained generators — by exploiting manifold structure rather than memorization, and LevAtt (arXiv 2512.08875, revised 8 May 2026) added a *no-box* MIA against LLM-based tabular data generators that needs only the synthetic output, targets numeric digit sequences, and is reported as a perfect membership classifier on some configurations. The May 2026 ablation study "On the Privacy of LLMs" (arXiv 2605.02255, 4 May 2026) reinforced the multi-attack point: across four attack families (MIA, attribute inference, data extraction, backdoor), mask-based MIAs are the strongest reliable signal but backdoor attacks consistently succeed too — privacy risk is "highly context-dependent and driven by design choices," so single-attack acceptance tests are no longer credible. Treat chat transcripts, support logs, prompt metadata, image datasets, contractor labeling workspaces, and any "anonymized" synthetic release as training-data candidates unless retention, redaction, access controls, **and** a documented multi-attack-panel audit prove otherwise. On the regulatory side, on 7 May 2026 Council/Parliament/Commission negotiators reached a provisional Digital Omnibus on AI agreement — the first amendments to the EU AI Act, mixing timeline relief, simplification, and a few substantive policy changes. The Omnibus delays Annex III high-risk AI compliance from 2 August 2026 to **2 December 2027** and Annex I product-regulated systems to 2 August 2028, and pushes Article 50(2) synthetic-content marking obligations to 2 December 2026 — but the GPAI obligations and the Commission's enforcement powers still apply from 2 August 2026, and the EDPB Work Programme 2026-2027 (adopted 11 February 2026) confirms upcoming guidelines on anonymisation, generative AI, and data scraping that will sit on top of EDPB Opinion 28/2024. The Omnibus also introduces a new **Article 4a** legal basis for processing special-category personal data for bias detection and correction across *all* AI systems (not only high-risk), but conditioned on a strict-necessity test plus mandatory pseudonymization, technical limitations on reuse, access controls, a prohibition on onward sharing, and deletion once correction is complete — read together with EDPS Generative AI Orientations v2 (28 October 2025), which require EU institutions to verify whether the intended purpose can be achieved with synthetic or anonymised data *before* using personal data and set the anonymity bar at an "insignificant" likelihood of identification. EDPB-EDPS Joint Opinion 2/2026 (Feb 2026) flagged residual data-protection concerns with parts of the Omnibus package.

Three late-May / early-June 2026 developments tightened the screws further. On 26 May 2026 the **CNIL fined IQVIA Operations France €5M** over its LRX and EMR health-data warehouses (fed by roughly 14,000 pharmacies and several thousand physicians): IQVIA argued — invoking the CJEU's 4 September 2025 SRB judgment — that the warehouse data was anonymous and therefore outside the GDPR, but the restricted committee held it was merely *pseudonymous* because re-identification remained possible "using reasonable means," citing persistent unique identifiers, data depth, combinability with IQVIA's other holdings and public datasets, absent connection-log analysis, and missing MFA; the decision (published 28 May 2026) gives six months to remediate on pain of €10,000/day. It is the clearest post-SRB signal that a controller's anonymity self-assessment will be stress-tested against the full re-identification toolchain, not the label on the warehouse. On 8 June 2026 Meta disclosed that a bug in **High Touch Support (HTS)** — its AI-assisted Instagram account-recovery chatbot — let attackers simply ask the bot to attach a victim's account to an attacker-controlled email between 17 April and 31 May 2026: 20,225 accounts were hijacked (including the Obama White House account and the U.S. Space Force's Chief Master Sergeant), exposing DMs, contact details, dates of birth, and full account activity; the AI support layer itself is now a PII-disclosure surface that minimization reviews must cover. And the **NYT v. OpenAI** copyright MDL turned conversation logs into a discovery artifact at scale: Judge Sidney Stein affirmed on 5 January 2026 the order compelling OpenAI to hand a 20-million-conversation de-identified log sample to plaintiffs under an attorneys'-eyes-only protective order, after a litigation hold had earlier forced OpenAI to retain consumer ChatGPT and API content past its normal deletion windows (OpenAI has since returned to standard ~30-day deletion for deleted chats). The operational lesson for 12.1.1 is that retention promises and deletion SLAs can be overridden by a single preservation order — the only conversation data that cannot be compelled, breached, or subpoenaed is the data that was minimized before storage. The Spanish AEPD flagged the same month (citing IMDEA Networks research) that popular chatbots embed trackers that expose persistent session links and identity-linkable interaction data to Google, Meta, and TikTok with privacy policies that misdescribe the flows, and Belgium's DPA closed a conversational-AI sweep warning that vendors ship product first and bolt on data-protection obligations later — both reinforcing that the analytics/tracker layer (Couture v. OpenAI) is a standing minimization gap, not a one-off.

| **12.1.1** | **Verify that** direct and quasi-identifiers in training and fine-tuning datasets are removed, hashed, or generalized before the data is used to fit or update a model. | 1 | Direct memorization and verbatim regurgitation of training PII (names, SSNs, addresses, MRNs, account numbers, phone numbers) — observed at scale by the Carlini et al. divergence attack against ChatGPT (150x leakage when prompted to repeat tokens, arXiv 2311.17035) and quantified by PII-Scope (16.9% memorized-PII rate; 85.8% confirmed-real, arXiv 2410.06704). USENIX Security 2025 "Effective PII Extraction from LLMs" demonstrated augmented few-shot prompting that pushed extraction rates 5x higher against fine-tuned checkpoints. Black-box model-inversion against Llama 3.2-1B (arXiv 2507.04478, Jul 2025) showed even small open-weight checkpoints surface real emails, passwords, phone numbers, account details, and LinkedIn profiles when prompted with simple field labels — and Llama 65B's measured 0.789% memorization rate translates to thousands of leakable records at production query volumes. Quasi-identifier reconstruction attacks (date-of-birth + ZIP + gender uniqueness, Sweeney's classic 87% U.S. population result) extend to AI: residual ZIP/age/diagnosis fields in clinical fine-tuning sets enable linkage even after direct identifiers are stripped. The "false sense of privacy" effect (Xin, Mireshghallah, Li, Duan, Kim, Choi, Tsvetkov, Oh & Koh — arXiv 2504.21035, v4 13 Mar 2026) sharpens that finding for free-text training data: removing explicit identifiers leaves *contextual* re-identification channels open — routine social activities, occupation mentions, and lifestyle fragments are sufficient for an LLM-based attacker to infer sensitive attributes like age range or substance-use history from supposedly "sanitized" text, and the authors propose a re-identification-attack framework rather than identifier-counting as the right acceptance test. RAT-Bench (Krčo, Yao, Meeus & de Montjoye — arXiv 2602.12806, 13 Feb 2026) makes this measurable end-to-end: it generates synthetic text seeded with U.S. demographic statistics across domains, languages, and difficulty levels, runs an LLM-based attacker against the anonymized output of NER- and LLM-based tools (Azure Language Studio, Presidio, Scrubadub, GliNER, UniNER, TextWash, GPT-4.1, Gemini 2.5 Flash, Llama 3.1-8B), and reports actual re-identification probability in the U.S. population. Even Azure (29%) and GPT-4 with the Rescriber prompt (27%) leave roughly one-in-four records re-identifiable post-redaction — treat any anonymization claim that publishes only NER F1 / entity recall (and no RAT-style re-identification-rate number) as an unverified marketing claim. **Privacy Ripple Effects** (Borkar et al., ACL Findings 2025, arXiv 2502.15680) added an uncomfortable wrinkle for naive minimization: *assisted memorization* — similar-appearing PII seen later in training elicits memorization of earlier-seen sequences and accounted for roughly one-third of leakage in their experiments — and *onward privacy*, where adding one person's PII can increase memorization of unrelated PII by up to 7.5x and removing a user's data can paradoxically increase regurgitation of someone else's. The 2025-2026 OmniGPT (34M lines), McHire (64M applicants), Sears Home Services (3.7M chat logs), and Mercor (4 TB including 211 GB user DB and ~3 TB video interviews / ID documents, March 2026) breaches all started with un-minimized training/logging corpora — the Mercor case is especially instructive because the upstream RLHF labeling shop had aggregated unredacted contractor PII alongside ID-verification scans and proprietary training methodology in the same accessible environment. A separate fine-tuning-data theft channel emerged in ICLR 2026: "Be Careful When Fine-tuning On Open-Source LLMs" (arXiv 2505.15656, accepted ICLR 2026) showed that a malicious open-source-base-model publisher can plant a simple training-time backdoor that later lets them recover 76.3% of downstream fine-tuning queries from a 5,000-sample dataset under black-box access, rising to 94.9% in ideal settings, against four 3B-32B base models — so "minimized at our perimeter" no longer covers data whose base weights came from an untrusted upstream party. Maps to OWASP LLM02:2025 Sensitive Information Disclosure and MITRE ATLAS AML.T0024 Exfiltration via ML Inference API. | Inventory direct identifiers (name, SSN, email, phone, MRN, account number, government IDs, biometric templates) and quasi-identifiers (DOB, ZIP, gender, ethnicity, occupation, employer, rare diagnoses) per dataset using a documented data-classification policy. Run a centralized PII detection pass before any training/fine-tuning job — Microsoft Presidio (PyPI release 15 Mar 2026 added the Azure Health Data Services Surrogate anonymization operator for realistic PHI surrogates, an improved Indian Aadhaar recognizer with contextual delimiters, and a corrected Italian Driver License regex), **OpenAI Privacy Filter** (released 22 April 2026, Apache 2.0 on Hugging Face — a 1.5B-parameter bidirectional token classifier with ~50M active parameters, 128K context, that runs locally or in-browser so unfiltered data never leaves the client; eight entity classes — account_number, private_address, private_email, private_person, private_phone, private_url, private_date, secret — with reported 97.43% F1 / 96.79% precision / 98.08% recall on a corrected PII-Masking-300k benchmark and 96.0% F1 / 94.04% precision / 98.04% recall on the uncorrected version), Google Cloud DLP, AWS Comprehend PII, Private AI, Nightfall AI, or Skyflow LLM Privacy Vault for tokenized stand-ins that survive train/serve. For clinical/PHI corpora, evaluate purpose-built de-id pipelines such as John Snow Labs Healthcare NLP v6.3.0 (Feb 2026, ~96% F1 PHI detection) or NEJM AI's local LLM-Anonymizer. Where redacted placeholders break downstream utility, evaluate surrogate-substitution instead of deletion: the Anonymous-by-Construction framework (arXiv 2603.17217, Mar 2026) replaces PII with realistic, type-consistent surrogates using fully on-premise local models, benchmarks against Presidio, Google Cloud DLP, and ZSTS, and adds a lifecycle-ready acceptance criterion — fine-tune a compact encoder (BERT+LoRA) on the sanitized text and jointly measure privacy, semantic drift, and trainability rather than entity recall alone. For PRvL-style instruction-tuned redaction (arXiv 2508.05545, Aug 2025), DeepSeek-Q1-tuned models hit SPriV ≈ 0.002 (lowest leakage) — verify reported metrics on a representative sample. Confirm the chosen transformation per field: removal (drop column), one-way hashing with peppered HMAC for joinable-but-unrecoverable IDs, or generalization (year-only birthdate, 3-digit ZIP, age band, broader diagnosis category). Re-run a memorization canary check post-training (Opacus / TensorFlow Privacy canary insertion) and reject the build if known canaries surface. Audit the redaction pipeline itself for failure modes — empty-output, missed-locale, OCR'd PDFs, multi-modal images. Benchmark in-house and vendor redactors against the PIPEDA Findings #2026-002 (6 May 2026) OpenAI remediation reference — 98-99% recall with 3-6% false positives for private-individual entities, contextual filtering that separates private individuals from public figures and fictional characters — and reject vendor claims that publish neither recall nor false-positive rates per entity class. Pair training-corpus redaction with a prompt-side minimization pass on every inference call: the ICLR 2026 **Operationalizing Data Minimization** framework (Zhou, Mireshghallah & Li, arXiv 2510.03662) defines the least privacy-revealing disclosure that preserves task utility, uses a priority-queue tree search over a privacy-ordered transformation space, and benchmarks nine LLMs across ShareGPT, WildChat, CaseHold, and MedQA — GPT-5 tolerated 85.7% redaction with preserved utility versus 19.3% for Qwen2.5-0.5B, and the authors caution that LLMs themselves have a "bias toward abstraction that leads to oversharing" (treat self-generated minimization rewrites as untrusted by default). **Character-Level Differential Privacy for User Prompts** (arXiv 2603.26032, 27 Mar 2026) gives a detector-free alternative for the prompt path: a randomized-response mechanism perturbs each character independently, then a server-side restoration step reconstructs common context words from co-occurrence patterns while leaving rare sensitive tokens unrecoverable; evaluated on i2b2/UTHealth and Enron with Llama-3.1 8B Instruct and GPT-4o mini, sensitive PII reconstruction collapses to near-random while non-sensitive utility holds up. Useful where the prompt source cannot run a reliable NER pass (legacy clients, multi-lingual chat, IoT). The complementary **LLM-Redactor** empirical study (Agyemang, Kponyo, Amponsah, Boakye & Obour Agyekum — arXiv 2604.12064, 13 April 2026) evaluates eight privacy-preserving LLM-request techniques (local-only inference, redaction with placeholder restoration, semantic rephrasing, TEE-hosted inference, split inference, FHE, MPC secret sharing, DP noise) on a ground-truth-labeled leak dataset of 1,300 samples / 4,014 annotations across four workload classes — and reports that the A+B+C combination (route locally when possible, then redact and rephrase the rest) achieves **0.6% combined leak on PII and zero exact PII leaks across 500 samples**, while no single technique dominates and proprietary-code leakage stays at 31.3%. Treat this as quantitative evidence for the layered-defense framing — single-tool redaction claims (Presidio-only, OpenAI Privacy Filter-only) should be rejected without a published combination-and-leak number, and code-bearing prompts need their own minimization budget separate from PII. For browsing-enabled assistants whose minimization burden falls on the *page owner* rather than the model operator, the **PIIGuard** webpage-level defense (Liu, Zha & Chen — arXiv 2605.03129, 4 May 2026) repurposes indirect prompt injection as protection: optimized hidden HTML fragments steer browsing models away from verbatim or reconstructible disclosure of contact PII, with reported defense success rates of ≥97% (often 100%) against GPT-5.4-nano, Claude-haiku-4.5, and DeepSeek-chat v3.2 in direct-HTML evaluation while preserving benign Q&A utility — treat publisher-side egress controls as a complement to corpus-side and prompt-side redaction once browsing/agent traffic dominates the assistant surface. Cross-check against deployment-side exposures rather than only training-set construction: Verizon's 20 May 2026 DBIR documents 858,440 DLP events tied to GenAI uploads, 67% of users on non-corporate accounts, shadow AI as the third-most-common non-malicious insider action with a 4x year-over-year jump, and 60% of malicious-insider breaches now driven by *convenience* — minimization controls must include managed-browser/MDM extension allowlists, CASB inspection of AI-platform endpoints, DLP on paste/upload of regulated identifiers, sanctioned-tooling defaults, and audit logs for every AI-tool interaction (Kiteworks 2026 AI data-governance reporting: 78% of organizations cannot validate data before it enters training pipelines, 77% cannot trace training-data provenance, 65% cannot prove dataset access controls, 62% cannot demonstrate data-minimization practices for AI under GDPR / state privacy laws, 53% have no path to unlearn or recover training data post-incident; PET adoption gap is concrete — 43% have adopted data minimization, only 20% differential privacy, and 17% federated learning — and the 2026 DBIR put 45% of employees as regular corporate-device AI users versus 15% the year before; HHS OCR closed 11 hacking-incident investigations with HIPAA risk-analysis penalties by 31 January 2026 and "prompt leaking" is now an emerging HIPAA breach category). | Naive regex/hash redaction breaks semantic fidelity and inflates token counts (Statsig, Skyflow analyses Feb 2026); LLM-based redactors are strong on common entity types but degrade on rare locales, code-switched text, and structured logs. Per RAT-Bench (arXiv 2602.12806, Feb 2026), even the best mainstream tools — Azure Language Studio (29% residual re-identification) and GPT-4 with the Rescriber prompt (27%) — leave roughly one record in four re-identifiable post-redaction against an LLM-based attacker, and per the False Sense of Privacy framework (arXiv 2504.21035, v4 Mar 2026) explicit-identifier removal still leaks attributes like age range or substance-use history through routine activity and lifestyle fragments — so "X% PII recall" without a published re-identification-rate benchmark should be rejected as evidence of anonymization. Hashing alone is reversible against small-domain values (e.g., ZIP codes — 41,692 possibilities) without a high-entropy pepper, and rainbow-table attacks remain trivial for SSN-shaped fields. Generalization that satisfies GDPR pseudonymization is **not** anonymization under EDPB Opinion 28/2024 — the model still falls under data-protection law, and the EDPB's April 2025 anonymization-stakeholder workshop reaffirmed case-by-case assessment with no safe-harbor list. The CNIL's 26 May 2026 €5M IQVIA decision is the enforcement proof point: IQVIA's claim that its LRX/EMR health-data warehouses were anonymous (leaning on the CJEU SRB judgment) was rejected because re-identification remained possible "using reasonable means" — persistent unique identifiers, data depth, cross-holding combinability, no connection-log review, no MFA — so expect anonymity claims over hashed/generalized training corpora to be tested against the controller's *entire* data estate, not the dataset in isolation. EU AI Act Article 10 + GDPR Art. 5(1)(c) require provable minimization with documented justification per field; the Article 53(1)(d) public training-summary template (Commission, 24 July 2025) now forces GPAI providers to disclose data sources and collection methods, making vendor "PII-safe" claims without published evaluation metrics increasingly indefensible. NVIDIA/Gretel transition: Gretel was acquired March 2025 and is now NVIDIA NeMo Data Designer (Apache 2.0) — re-evaluate licensing and feature parity. Multi-modal coverage is uneven: Presidio image OCR, audio transcription, and document layout PII detection still trail text by 5-10% F1 in independent benchmarks. |
| **12.1.2** | **Verify that** automated audits measure k-anonymity or l-diversity on training datasets and alert when thresholds drop below policy. | 2 | Quasi-identifier-based re-identification of training records via linkage against external auxiliary datasets (voter rolls, LinkedIn scrapes, broker-data, public breach dumps). Sweeney's seminal work showed 87% of U.S. population is uniquely identifiable by {DOB, ZIP, gender}; the same combinatorial uniqueness lurks in clinical, HR, financial, and customer-support training corpora. Without continuous auditing, schema changes (a new "department" or "rare diagnosis" column added by a data-eng team) silently push k below policy and create re-identification exposure that surfaces only after a breach. Healthcare PMC-published privacy-engineering work (PMC11885643, Mar 2025) catalogues contemporary linkage threats against anonymized clinical data and argues for continuous audit, not one-shot anonymization. Combinatorial Refinement Attacks (CRA) on k-anonymity (arXiv 2509.03350, Sept 2025) raised the bar further: by exploiting the utility-optimizing greedy decisions of ARX-style local-recoding algorithms, the authors reduced the set of plausible original records by **77x to 39,000x** on real clinical datasets — without using any auxiliary information. Flat datasets that pass k-anonymity can still leak via attribute disclosure if sensitive values are homogeneous within an equivalence class — l-diversity addresses this; t-closeness addresses skew. | Run k-anonymity and l-diversity computations on every training/fine-tuning snapshot before it enters the training queue, gated by CI policy. ## Recent Research (2025-2026)

- **12.1.1 / 12.1.3:** As of May 2026, PANORAMA (arXiv 2505.12238) gives auditors a synthetic, ethically sourced PII memorization benchmark: 384,789 samples across 9,674 synthetic profiles, with Mistral-7B fine-tunes showing memorization increasing as repeated PII-laced content moves from 1x to 25x replication. Use it as a canary-style evaluation set before accepting claims that redaction or minimization alone prevents memorization.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.3:** ACL Findings 2025 R.R. showed that masked or scrubbed PII can still be reconstructed through a recollection-and-ranking attack, so audits should test whether "removed" identifiers can be inferred from surrounding text rather than only scanning generated output for exact original strings.
- **12.1.3:** The ICLR 2026 OpenReview submission on membership decoding argues that greedy decoding audits miss partially memorized training sequences. Treat it as a signal to include non-greedy extraction and membership-guided decoding in privacy red teams where the model owner can run controlled shadow attacks.
- **12.1.3:** EZ-MIA (arXiv 2601.12104, revised Apr 2026) raises the bar for fine-tune privacy audits: it needs only two forward passes and no attack-model training, yet reports AUC 0.98 on WikiText/GPT-2 and materially higher true-positive rates at low false-positive thresholds. Treat token-probability access as enough for a serious membership audit.
- **12.1.3:** ADRA / ADRA+ (arXiv 2602.19020, Feb 2026) reframes membership inference as active reconstruction: the auditor fine-tunes a policy initialized from the target model with reconstruction rewards, then checks whether candidate records are easier to elicit. This is especially relevant when pre-training, post-training, or distillation datasets are disputed.
- **12.1.3:** SimMIA and WikiMIA-25 (arXiv 2601.11314, Jan 2026) show that text-only black-box audits are improving. When only generated text is available, require sampling-based membership tests rather than accepting "no logits available" as a reason to skip training-data privacy evaluation.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.3:** The cue-controlled memorization framework (arXiv 2601.03791, Jan 2026) is a useful corrective: PII "leakage" tests should control prompt-target lexical overlap, because high-cue prompts can measure pattern completion rather than memorization. Pair exact-PII extraction tests with low-cue and cue-free probes before treating a finding as evidence of training-data memorization.
- **12.1.3:** Causal MIA evaluation (arXiv 2602.02819, Feb 2026) warns that one-run and zero-run audit shortcuts can be biased by interference and non-random membership assignment. Privacy test reports should document how member/non-member sets were sampled and whether the estimator remains valid when full retraining is impractical.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.3:** Large Audio Language Model MIA work (arXiv 2603.28378, Mar 2026) shows that audio privacy audits need blind spectral/prosodic baselines: common speech datasets can be separable without model inference, while true cross-modal memorization arises from binding speaker identity to text. Use distribution-matched controls before claiming audio PII was memorized.
- **12.1.3 / 12.1.4:** Diffusion language model memorization research (arXiv 2603.02333, Mar 2026) found that higher sampling resolution increases exact extraction probability, even though aligned tests showed lower PII leakage than autoregressive models. Do not treat non-autoregressive generation as an anonymization control without model-specific extraction testing.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.3:** Unintended Memorization of Sensitive Information in Fine-Tuned Language Models (arXiv 2601.17480, EACL 2026) showed that PII appearing only in inputs, not labels, can still leak after fine-tuning. Its mitigation comparison is a useful audit checklist: test differential privacy, unlearning, regularization, and preference alignment on the same extraction probes instead of relying on redaction claims alone.
- **12.1.2 / 12.1.4:** Large-scale online deanonymization work (arXiv 2602.16800, revised Feb 2026) showed that unstructured pseudonymous text can be linked across platforms with up to 68% recall at 90% precision. For training corpora, k-anonymity reviews should include free-text stylometry, semantic-profile features, and cross-dataset linkage tests, not only tabular quasi-identifiers.
- **12.1.1:** Tracking Conversations (arXiv 2604.27438, Apr 2026) measured 20 popular chatbots and found 17 sharing some information with third parties, including plaintext prompt or response snippets in three cases and chat URLs or identifiers in 15 cases. Minimize prompt and conversation metadata before analytics, advertising, support widgets, or replay tooling can convert it into another exposed dataset.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.4:** The FTC's March 2026 OkCupid/Match action alleged that nearly three million user photos plus location and other information were shared with a third party without formal use restrictions. Treat contractual purpose limits, deletion rights, and downstream model-disgorgement clauses as part of data minimization evidence, not just legal boilerplate.
- **12.1.1:** Presidio 2.2.361 changed the hash anonymizer to use random per-entity salt by default. That improves brute-force resistance, but verification should explicitly check whether a managed salt is provided when referential integrity across records is required.
- **12.1.2:** sdcMicro's March 2026 AI-assisted anonymization workflow keeps the microdata local and sends only metadata (variable names, types, cardinality, factor levels, and current k violations) for strategy suggestions. That is useful for disclosure-control planning, but the generated recoding/noise/suppression script still needs human approval and independent risk/utility review.
- **12.1.4:** The Commission's March 2026 FAQ for the Article 53(1)(d) training-summary template confirms that GPAI providers must disclose whether training used user-interaction data, publish summaries by model release, update them at six-month intervals when additional training data materially changes the summary, and face AI Office enforcement from 2 August 2026.
- **12.1.3:** AttenMIA (arXiv 2601.18110, 26 Jan 2026) is the first attention-feature MIA to clear the 0.99 AUC bar on WikiMIA-32, with 87.9% TPR at 1% FPR against Llama2-13b and generalization across Pythia and OPT. Privacy panels that only score output logits should add an attention-divergence probe — the signal is in the model internals attention provides, not the output.
- **12.1.3:** Token-level InfoRMIA (arXiv 2510.05582, Tao & Shokri, NUS / Google Research, Oct 2025) shows that a single per-token information-theoretic score can both pinpoint which tokens were memorized and beat sequence-level MIAs at low FPR. Treat it as the new baseline for "where exactly is the leakage" audits, especially before targeted unlearning.
- **12.1.3:** HT-MIA — "What Hard Tokens Reveal" (arXiv 2601.20885, Jan 2026) — exploits low-confidence tokens with a fine-tuned-vs-reference probability gap and outperforms seven SOTA MIA baselines on both medical and general-purpose corpora. The paper's defense evaluation reinforces that DP fine-tuning is still the most reliable mitigation, but only at meaningful ε; high-ε DP buys little.
- **12.1.3:** EL-MIA (arXiv 2511.00192, 31 Oct 2025) shifts the unit of audit from "document" to "sensitive entity," and finds that prior MIA work systematically underestimates PII risk at entity granularity. For PII-bearing fine-tunes, run the EL-MIA benchmark against the actual sensitive-entity inventory (names, account numbers, MRNs, credit-card spans) instead of accepting a passing document-level MIA score.
- **12.1.3 / 12.1.4:** ProjRes (arXiv 2604.21197, accepted IEEE S&P 2026) reports ~100% accuracy on four federated LLMs and remains effective against "strong" DP, beating prior federated MIAs by up to 75.75% with no shadow models or auxiliary classifiers. Federated and split-learning AI privacy claims that lean on DP alone need an explicit ProjRes-style replay before release.
- **12.1.3:** Automated MIA via LLM Agents (arXiv 2603.19375, Mar 2026) shows that agentic systems can discover novel MIA signal computations without human design. Treat the catalogue of agent-discovered signals as part of the audit toolbox even when running a smaller fixed panel by hand.
- **12.1.3:** Targeted Data Extraction MIA (arXiv 2512.13352, Dec 2025) quantifies how well sequence-level MIA actually helps a downstream extraction attacker — a useful framing for risk reporting because boards and regulators care about extraction, not AUC.
- **12.1.4:** "When Privacy Isn't Synthetic" (arXiv 2512.06062, 5 Dec 2025) demonstrates a black-box cluster-overlap MIA that works across GANs, VAEs, diffusion models, and LLMs and survives DP training when manifold alignment is tight. Treat it as the new minimum bar for synthetic-data audits; SBPM scores alone are no longer credible evidence of anonymity.
- **12.1.4:** Local-likelihood synthetic-data attacks (arXiv 2508.21146) give auditors a tractable, model-agnostic likelihood test for membership in released synthetic tabular data — useful when full DP-Sniper-style audits are out of scope.
- **12.1.4:** Authorship-attribution-style re-identification of synthetic social-media data (arXiv 2603.03906, Mar 2026) reports 81% accuracy on real data and 16.5–29.7% on synthetic posts. Privacy/fidelity trade-offs in unstructured-text synthetic releases must therefore be reported as residual risk, not zero.

- **12.1.1:** California Attorney General / GM-OnStar CCPA settlement (8 May 2026, $12.75M) is the first US enforcement action squarely targeting data minimization and purpose limitation in CCPA: AG Bonta plus the LA, SF, Alameda, and Riverside DAs and the California Privacy Protection Agency alleged that GM and OnStar collected and sold drivers' geolocation, driving behavior, and PII to LexisNexis Risk Solutions and Verisk Analytics without adequate notice or consent. The consent decree bars GM from selling driving data to consumer reporting agencies for five years, requires 180-day deletion of retained driving data absent affirmative express consent, and — most useful as a contractual template — obliges GM to *request deletion* of already-shared driving data from LexisNexis and Verisk, plus build and document a privacy program. Treat the deletion-request clause as the precedent to mirror into AI-training data-sharing contracts: "necessary and proportional" is now an enforced standard, and downstream-cleanup is a remediation step regulators have demanded. Cite alongside the Mercor and OkCupid/Match precedents as evidence that upstream data-broker and AI-partner exposure runs through the regulated controller.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.3:** The Instructure / Canvas LMS incident (April–May 2026) reads as the educational-AI training-data analogue of the Mercor case: ShinyHunters exploited an unspecified Free-for-Teacher support-ticket vulnerability around 25 April 2026, Instructure detected the intrusion four days later, and a second wave on 7 May 2026 defaced login portals at ~330 institutions with extortion messages; by 11 May 2026 Instructure publicly acknowledged it had paid ShinyHunters and that the compromised data was "destroyed." Independent reporting (The Hacker News, Help Net Security, Inside Higher Ed, CNN, Malwarebytes, Wikipedia) put the theft at 3.65 TB / ~275M records — usernames, email addresses, course names, enrollment information, and messages — across roughly 8,809 universities, ministries, and institutions worldwide, characterized as the largest educational security breach on record. The takeaway for minimization: any AI tutoring, grading-assistant, or LMS-fine-tuning pipeline that ingested Canvas exports should treat those records as adversary-known training data, re-run an EL-MIA / membership-inference panel against fine-tunes that touched them, and verify that contractual deletion claims by an extortion-paying vendor are not accepted as evidence of corpus minimization (the data is in the threat-actor ecosystem regardless of the vendor's claim). Paying a ransom does not satisfy GDPR Art. 5(1)(c) or CCPA minimization, and "the attacker says they destroyed it" is not auditable evidence — require provenance scans of any newly trained model against known Canvas-derived corpora.
- **12.1.1:** Verizon 2026 DBIR (released 20 May 2026) is the clearest empirical signal yet that minimization controls fail at the deployment boundary: 858,440 DLP events tied to GenAI uploads were analyzed, source code was the dominant pasted data type, 67% of users hit AI services from non-corporate accounts on corporate devices, shadow AI is now the third most common non-malicious insider action with a 4x year-over-year jump, and 60% of malicious-insider breaches are driven by *convenience*. Treat AI prohibition as predicted-to-fail and budget for sanctioned-tooling defaults, managed-browser extension allowlists, CASB inspection of AI endpoints, and DLP on paste/upload of regulated identifiers. The CISA Gottumukkala incident (late Jan 2026 — "For Official Use Only" contracting documents pasted into public ChatGPT despite DHS restrictions) is a concrete worked example.
- **12.1.1:** Check Point Research's early-2026 disclosure of a hidden DNS-based outbound channel in ChatGPT's code-execution runtime (patched by OpenAI 20 February 2026) shows that even when the documented runtime guardrail says "no direct outbound network requests," a covert exfil path through DNS can survive. Treat tool-use and code-execution sandboxes as a minimization egress surface in their own right — instrument outbound DNS, monitor for unexpected lookup volume during model tool calls, and require vendor disclosure of sandbox egress controls before sending sensitive context into tool calls.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.4:** **Eightfold AI** putative class action (Kistler & Bhaumik v. Eightfold AI, N.D. Cal., filed 20 January 2026 by Outten & Golden) alleges scraping of personal data on over one billion workers, generation of hidden FCRA/ICRAA-grade "consumer reports" scoring applicants 0-5, and silent removal of low-ranked candidates before any human review — naming Microsoft, PayPal, Morgan Stanley, Starbucks, Chevron, and Bayer among customers. Read as the HR-tech mirror of the OkCupid/Mercor pattern: upstream training/scoring corpora are themselves the regulated artifact, and downstream model uses do not cure the upstream minimization failure. Reinforce FCRA/ICRAA disclosure-and-dispute obligations in any AI hiring pipeline before training a scoring model on scraped data.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.4:** HHS OCR data (as of 31 January 2026) and 2026 industry surveys (Censinet, Kiteworks): 11 hacking-incident investigations closed with HIPAA risk-analysis penalties; federal civil-penalty caps for repeated violations now exceed US $2M; 65% of organizations cannot prove dataset access controls for AI training data; 53% have no mechanism to recover or unlearn after an incident; "prompt leaking" is becoming a recognized HIPAA breach category. Cite when scoping AI/PHI minimization audits and tying them to Business Associate Agreement obligations.
- **12.1.3 / 12.1.4:** **Federated Fine-Tuning Cross-Domain Benchmark** (Jimenez-Gutierrez, Zuazua, Kellaris, del Rio, Sliusarenko & Uribe-Etxebarria — Sherpa.ai, arXiv 2605.13936, 13 May 2026) evaluates LoRA, QLoRA, and IA3 federated fine-tuning across MedQA, MedMCQA, FPB, and FiQA-SA under non-IID conditions; federated training approaches centralized accuracy and beats isolated single-institution training, with QLoRA and IA3 trading limited accuracy for major efficiency gains. Useful counterpoint for institutions weighing federated vs. centralized strategies for sensitive corpora — but still pair with C12-06 federated-privacy controls and a ProjRes (arXiv 2604.21197) replay before treating federation as the privacy guarantee.

- **12.1.1 / 12.1.2:** CNIL fine against IQVIA Operations France (€5M, decision 26 May 2026, published 28 May 2026): the LRX (≈14,000 pharmacies) and EMR (several thousand physicians) health-data warehouses were held to be *pseudonymous, not anonymous* — IQVIA's SRB-judgment-based anonymity argument failed because re-identification remained possible "using reasonable means" given persistent unique identifiers, data depth, combinability with IQVIA's other holdings and public data, missing connection-log analysis, and missing MFA. Six months to remediate or €10,000/day. This is the post-SRB template for how EU regulators will audit anonymity claims over AI training warehouses: estate-wide linkage analysis plus operational-control review, not dataset-local k metrics.
- **12.1.1:** Meta's High Touch Support (HTS) Instagram breach (first access 17 April 2026, discovered 31 May, disclosed 8 June 2026): the AI-assisted account-recovery chatbot failed to verify that the requester-supplied email matched the account's registered email, so attackers — using VPNs to appear geographically near targets — simply asked the bot to attach victim accounts to their own addresses. 20,225 accounts were hijacked, including the Obama White House account, exposing DMs, contact details, DOBs, and account activity. Treat AI support/recovery chatbots as a first-class PII-disclosure and account-takeover surface: minimization reviews must cover what identity-bearing actions the assistant can take and what verification gates each action, not only what data the model was trained on.
- **12.1.1:** NYT v. OpenAI logs order (affirmed by Judge Sidney Stein, 5 January 2026): OpenAI must produce a 20-million-conversation de-identified ChatGPT log sample to copyright plaintiffs under an attorneys'-eyes-only protective order, and an earlier preservation order forced retention of consumer and API content past normal deletion windows (standard ~30-day deletion has since resumed). Conversation logs are now a litigation-discoverable corpus: deletion SLAs and retention promises yield to a single preservation order, so the defensible minimization posture is to redact or aggregate before storage — data that was never retained cannot be compelled.
- **12.1.1:** Anonymous-by-Construction (Albanese, Ronco & D'Ippolito — arXiv 2603.17217, 17 Mar 2026) replaces PII with realistic, type-consistent surrogates via fully on-premise local LLMs and proposes a *lifecycle-ready* acceptance criterion: fine-tune a compact BERT+LoRA encoder on the sanitized corpus and jointly score privacy, semantic drift, and trainability. Benchmarked against Presidio, Google Cloud DLP, and ZSTS (redaction-only and redaction-plus-substitution). Useful where placeholder-style redaction destroys downstream fine-tuning utility — but still pair with a RAT-Bench-style end-to-end re-identification probe, since surrogate quality does not address contextual re-identification.
- **12.1.1:** Spanish AEPD guidance (May 2026, building on IMDEA Networks research) found popular AI chatbots embed third-party trackers that expose persistent session links and identity-linkable interaction data to Google, Meta, and TikTok, with privacy policies that misdescribe actual flows; Belgium's DPA closed a parallel conversational-AI sweep warning that vendors fulfill formal data-protection obligations "at a later stage" than product development. Together with Couture v. OpenAI, the tracker/analytics layer on AI front-ends is now a recurring enforcement theme — inventory web tags and SDKs on every AI-product domain as part of the 12.1.1 audit.
