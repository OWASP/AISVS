# C1.3: Training Data Quality and Security Assurance

> [Back to C01 Index](C01-Training-Data.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-11

## Purpose

Data quality validation is the last defensive line before poisoned, corrupted, or malformed data enters the training pipeline. This section treats quality checks as a security control, not a hygiene concern: the same statistical and schema checks that catch upstream pipeline bugs are also the cheapest broad-spectrum detector for adversarial contamination.

Several lines of research have reset the threat model since 2024:

- **Absolute-count poisoning.** Anthropic, the UK AI Security Institute, and the Alan Turing Institute published [*Poisoning Attacks on LLMs Require a Near-constant Number of Poison Samples*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07192) in October 2025. Across 72 models from 600M to 13B parameters trained on 6B–260B tokens, **250 crafted documents consistently produced a working backdoor** regardless of clean-data volume. In the 13B regime that is about 0.00016% of the corpus — well below the sensitivity of most percentage-based contamination audits. [Anthropic's research summary](https://www.anthropic.com/research/small-samples-poison) frames the asymmetry bluntly: defenders must detect fixed-count contamination, not dilute it.
- **Clinical and code-gen blast radius.** The [*Nature Medicine* study on medical LLMs (Jan 2025)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-024-03445-1) showed that replacing just **0.001% of training tokens** with targeted misinformation produced measurably more harmful completions while passing standard medical QA benchmarks. [CodeScan (Mar 2026, arXiv:2603.17174)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.17174) then demonstrated that token-level consistency scans are weak for source code; AST normalization and vulnerability-oriented black-box scanning reached 97%+ detection accuracy in the authors' test set, but only for code-generation poisoning.
- **Preference and synthetic-data poisoning.** [PoisonBench (ICML 2025)](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v267/fu25e.html) found that poisoned preference data can affect 22 widely used models across eight scenarios, that larger parameter counts do not consistently improve resilience, and that effects can generalize to triggers not present in the poisoned set. [Virus Infection Attack (NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041) then showed poisoning content propagating through synthetic-data generation even when the downstream synthetic-data queries are clean.
- **Bias as an attack surface.** Bias in security-relevant classifiers is not only a fairness problem — it is an evasion channel. The [CHI 2025 "Lost in Moderation" study (arXiv:2503.01623)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.01623) measured deployed commercial moderation APIs systematically under-moderating codified hate speech while over-moderating reclaimed speech from the targeted communities, and [*Adversarial Bias* (Nov 2025, arXiv:2511.08331)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.08331) showed an attacker can poison a small fraction of training data to deliberately *create* subgroup blind spots while overall accuracy stays intact.

The practical implication for C1.3 is layered detection. Format and distribution validation catches obviously broken or skewed records, statistical/embedding analysis catches clumpy or out-of-distribution poisoning, confidence gates stop auto-labels from laundering upstream model errors into fresh training data, clean-label-specific defenses (purification, k-NN filtering, partition aggregation) cover the poisoning family that label checks structurally cannot see, and security-exploitable-bias probing closes the evaluation loop for abuse-detection and fraud models. As of June 2026, no single tool covers all of this; a research-grade check list means naming specific libraries, versions, thresholds, owners, and what each one does **not** catch.

| **1.4.2** | **Verify that** automatically generated labels are subject to confidence thresholds and consistency checks to detect misleading or low-confidence labels. | 2 | Propagation of model errors into training data (feedback loops); adversarial manipulation of auto-labeling models to inject systematic mislabels; cost-minimized label-flipping against alignment/preference labels ([arXiv:2511.09105, Nov 2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.09105v1)); quality degradation from overreliance on weak supervision; *Virus Infection Attack* showing poisoning propagates through synthetic-data generation and amplifies across generations ([arXiv:2509.23041](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041)); model collapse from recursive training on machine-generated data ([Shumailov et al., *Nature* 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07566-y)). | Review the auto-labeling pipeline for explicit confidence gates. Confirm it uses Confident Learning (Prune-by-Class or Prune-by-Noise-Rate variants — see the [noisy-label-detection benchmark, arXiv:2510.16211](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.16211)) via [Cleanlab](https://github.com/cleanlab/cleanlab) or equivalent statistical screening. For weak-supervision stacks, check [Snorkel](https://github.com/snorkel-team/snorkel)- or [Snuba](https://www.vldb.org/pvldb/vol12/p223-varma.pdf)-style labeling-function aggregation and that conflicting votes are flagged, not silently averaged. For LLM-as-annotator pipelines, look for a published consistency-gating pattern: the [LREC 2026 cognitive-distortion annotation study (arXiv:2511.01482)](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.01482) accepts only labels that recur in at least 4 of 5 sampled runs and routes the rest to explicit ambiguity categories — a concrete, citable threshold recipe. Where label tasks admit multiple valid answers, check the calibration method itself: [Semantic Confidence Aggregation (Jun 2026, arXiv:2602.07842)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.07842) showed standard calibration systematically underestimates confidence on multi-answer questions across 15 methods and 4 model families. Cross-model agreement (ensemble disagreement > X% = quarantine), temporal-stability checks against a held-out canary set, and documented human-review sampling rates should all be evidenced. Confirm synthetic data produced from potentially poisoned upstream models is screened against the 1.4.1 detection stack before re-ingest. | No standard methodology exists for *choosing* the confidence threshold; teams pick it empirically per project, which makes the control easy to satisfy on paper and weak in practice. LLM annotators reproduce majority labels but not human label uncertainty ([arXiv:2604.27345, Apr 2026](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.27345)), so agreement-based gates can look healthy while hiding genuinely ambiguous data. Snorkel's open-source package has been dormant since v0.10.0 (Feb 2024) — plan around the commercial platform or maintain a fork. VIA means synthetic pipelines silently inherit upstream poisoning; screening the *output* of synthetic generation against the 1.4.1 stack is load-bearing. |
| **1.4.3** | **Verify that** models used in security-relevant decisions are evaluated for bias patterns. | 2 | Subgroup evasion: adversaries exploiting systematic per-group blind spots in abuse-detection, fraud, and moderation classifiers (MITRE ATLAS [AML.T0015 Evade AI Model](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0015)). The [CHI 2025 "Lost in Moderation" study (arXiv:2503.01623)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.01623) tested five commercial moderation APIs across ~5M queries and found they lean on identity terms as predictive features — so codified/implicit hate evades detection while legitimate reclaimed speech from targeted communities gets flagged, an asymmetric and exploitable failure. [*Adversarial Bias* (Nov 2025, arXiv:2511.08331)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.08331) goes further: a training-data poisoning attack that deliberately *induces* subgroup blind spots while keeping aggregate accuracy intact, turning bias into an attacker-created evasion channel. Mechanistic analysis ([arXiv:2509.12672, Sep 2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.12672v1)) shows different model circuits carry the adversarial vulnerability for different demographic groups, so robustness gaps are demographic-conditional. | Require disaggregated error metrics, not aggregate accuracy. [Fairlearn 0.14.0 (Jun 2026)](https://github.com/fairlearn/fairlearn/releases) `MetricFrame` computes per-subgroup FPR/FNR — for a security classifier, per-group false-negative rate *is* the evasion exposure. [Giskard 2.18](https://github.com/Giskard-AI/giskard-oss) scans tabular models for performance bias across protected groups and runs LLM stereotype/discrimination detectors with auto-generated adversarial inputs. [IBM AI Fairness 360 0.6.1](https://github.com/Trusted-AI/AIF360) provides 70+ metrics and mitigation algorithms; [Aequitas 1.0](https://pypi.org/project/aequitas/) generates group FPR/FNR disparity audit reports well suited to fraud and abuse decisions; the [Microsoft Responsible AI Toolbox](https://github.com/microsoft/responsible-ai-toolbox/releases) error-analysis view finds cohorts where detector error concentrates. Beyond measurement, require red-team probing: group-conditioned evasion samples (dialect, reclaimed terms, codified phrasing) with per-group attack-success rates recorded before and after mitigation. Normative basis: [EU AI Act Article 10(2)(f)–(g)](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/10/) requires examining datasets for biases likely to affect health, safety, or fundamental rights and taking measures to detect, prevent, and mitigate them; [NIST SP 1270](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/SpecialPublications/NIST.SP.1270.pdf) supplies the systemic/statistical/human-cognitive taxonomy; [ISO/IEC TR 24027:2021](https://www.iso.org/standard/77607.html) catalogs bias sources and assessment metrics across the lifecycle. | Fairness metrics mathematically conflict, so "evaluated for bias" needs a documented choice of which disparities matter for the threat model — no tool makes that call. Weaponization analysis (which blind spot an adversary would actually use) is still human-led; automated scanners measure disparity, not exploitability. For biometric KYC, demographic error disparities are well documented ([arXiv:2502.02309, Feb 2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.02309v1)) but no public incident yet ties a fraud campaign specifically to demographic FRT bias — the exploit link remains inferential. EU AI Act enforcement pressure is real but deferred: Annex III high-risk obligations now apply from 2 December 2027 under the May 2026 Digital Omnibus agreement. |
| **1.4.4** | **Verify that** defenses against clean-label poisoning attacks are implemented. | 3 | Clean-label poisoning: correctly labeled but adversarially manipulated samples that label checks structurally cannot see. Targeted variants ([Bullseye Polytope, arXiv:2005.00191](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.00191), Gradient Matching, Narcissus) implant backdoors or misclassify specific victims; indiscriminate variants degrade availability. Triggerless code-generation poisoning evades spectral signatures, activation clustering, and static analysis ([ReSAISE 2025, arXiv:2508.21636](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.21636)). The [systematic review of LLM poisoning attacks (arXiv:2506.06518)](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.06518v1) confirms poisoned-data filtering works for dirty-label but consistently fails on clean-label attacks — the reason this control exists as its own requirement. | Confirm at least one defense from each family is wired into the pipeline, not just available. **Filtering:** [Deep k-NN defense (arXiv:1909.13374)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.13374) — feature-space neighbors that disagree with a sample's label flag it for quarantine. **Certified aggregation:** [Deep Partition Aggregation (arXiv:2006.14768)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.14768) → [Finite Aggregation (ICML 2022, arXiv:2202.02628)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.02628) → [Run-Off Election (ICML 2023, arXiv:2302.02300)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.02300) provide provable poisoning tolerance; record the certificate parameters. **Purification:** [Friendly Noise (NeurIPS 2022, arXiv:2208.10224)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.10224), [Mendata (arXiv:2312.01281)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.01281), [FLARE (IEEE TIFS 2025, arXiv:2411.19479)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.19479) all-layer activation clustering (ships in BackdoorBox), [PureVQ-GAN (arXiv:2509.25792)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.25792) VQ-bottleneck purification for images (0% poison success vs Gradient Matching/Bullseye Polytope, ~50× diffusion-purifier throughput), [certified diffusion denoising (Hong, Carlini & Kurakin, arXiv:2403.11981)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.11981) (attack success to 0–16% across seven clean-label attacks, with formal guarantees), and [ECLIPSE (ESORICS 2024, arXiv:2406.15093)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.15093) for indiscriminate poisons. **LLM/code-specific:** [MB-Defense (arXiv:2601.04448, updated Apr 2026)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.04448) defensive poisoning for instruction-tuned LLMs requiring no prior trigger knowledge; [Semantic Consensus Decoding (Feb 2026, arXiv:2602.04195)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.04195) inference-time consensus for code generation, cutting attack success from ~89% to under 3% on Verilog backdoors. Audit: run clean-label poison canaries (not just dirty-label ones) through the pipeline; record k-NN/filter thresholds, aggregation certificates, detection precision/recall, and manual review outcomes for quarantined samples. | Certified defenses (aggregation, diffusion denoising) carry real accuracy and compute cost — [arXiv:2306.16415](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.16415) documents the trade-offs at scale — and remain the only options with guarantees. Published evidence is concentrated in vision classification; text and code coverage is thin (Semantic Consensus Decoding is Verilog-specific, MB-Defense's quantitative results are in the paper body only, and PureVQ-GAN has no text/code numbers). No new certified clean-label defense has appeared since the diffusion-denoising work as of June 2026. Quantization after training undermines detection-based defenses ([arXiv:2512.06243](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.06243)). Vendors routinely cite 2020-era aggregation numbers without the later corrections — ask for the lineage. |

## Recent Research and Incident Context (2025-2026)

- **Feb 2023 / 2024 — Carlini et al.** [*Poisoning Web-Scale Training Datasets is Practical* (arXiv:2302.10149)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.10149). Introduced *split-view* and *frontrunning* attacks on URL-based web-scale corpora such as LAION-400M and COYO-700M, showing that an adversary could have poisoned 0.01% of the datasets for roughly $60. Foundational reference for why dataset fingerprints (1.4.1) and origin/lineage controls (C1.1) are now mandatory and not optional.
- **Oct 2024 — Rando & Tramèr (ETH Zurich SPYLab, ICLR 2025).** [*Persistent Pre-Training Poisoning of LLMs* (arXiv:2410.13722)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.13722). 0.1% poison during pre-training survives full SFT + DPO alignment up to 7B parameters across denial-of-service, jailbreak, prompt-stealing, and belief-manipulation objectives; even 0.001% produces measurable belief manipulation. Wikipedia alone can plausibly cover 0.27% of OLMo-style pretraining corpora, well above this threshold. Reinforces the case for treating poisoning as a *pre-training* concern, not just a fine-tuning one.
- **Oct 2025 — Anthropic/UK AISI/Turing.** [*Poisoning Attacks on LLMs Require a Near-constant Number of Poison Samples*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07192). 250-document threshold across 600M–13B parameter models. Resets the defender's math from percentages to absolute counts.
- **Jan 2025 — *Nature Medicine*.** [Medical LLMs vulnerable to data poisoning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-024-03445-1). 0.001% token replacement produces measurably harmful completions while evading standard medical QA benchmarks. A [PMC case study in breast-cancer clinical LLMs](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10984073/) extended the finding to specialty models.
- **Mar 2025 — "Lost in Moderation" (CHI 2025).** [arXiv:2503.01623](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.01623). Roughly 5M queries against five commercial moderation APIs (including OpenAI and Amazon offerings) across four datasets found systematic over-moderation of counter-speech and reclaimed terms from Black, LGBTQIA+, Jewish, and Muslim communities, alongside under-moderation of implicit "codified" hate. Deployed-system evidence that moderation bias is an exploitable evasion surface, not a lab artifact (1.4.3).
- **May 2025 — Trail of Bits.** [Datasig release](https://blog.trailofbits.com/2025/05/02/datasig-fingerprinting-ai/ml-datasets-to-stop-data-borne-attacks/). MinHash-based dataset fingerprinting specifically designed for AIBOM / supply-chain consumption. [Source repo](https://github.com/trailofbits/datasig).
- **ICML 2025 — PoisonBench.** [Poisoned preference data benchmark](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v267/fu25e.html) across 22 models, two attack types, and eight scenarios. Larger models are not consistently more resilient, attack effects scale with poison ratio, and poisoned behavior can generalize to unseen triggers.
- **Aug 2025.** Triggerless code-generation poisoning study — spectral signatures, activation clustering, and static analysis all failed on AST-normalized attacks; [CodeScan (Mar 2026, arXiv:2603.17174)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.17174) reported ~97% detection for this one attack class and is currently the only defense with published numbers.
- **NeurIPS 2025 — Virus Infection Attack.** [arXiv:2509.23041](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23041). Poisoning propagates through synthetic-data generation pipelines; organizations that generate training data from other models inherit and amplify upstream contamination.
- **Nov 2025 — Adversarial Bias.** [arXiv:2511.08331](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.08331). Data-poisoning attack that degrades fairness metrics — deliberately inducing subgroup blind spots — while preserving aggregate accuracy, outperforming prior fairness-poisoning attacks across benchmark datasets. Bridges the poisoning threat model (1.4.1) and the bias-evaluation requirement (1.4.3): an unaudited disparity may be attacker-made.
- **Dec 2025 — Quantization Blindspots.** [arXiv:2512.06243](https://www.arxiv.org/pdf/2512.06243). INT8 quantization drops classic backdoor defenses (Activation Clustering, STRIP, Spectral Signatures, Fine-Pruning) to 0% detection while the backdoor remains fully functional. Only Neural Cleanse survived, and only at full precision. Changes the risk picture for edge deployments that quantize after training.
- **Dec 2025 — SCOUT (IEEE BigData 2025).** [arXiv:2512.10998](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.10998). Token-level saliency defense for fine-tuned language models: measures the effect of token removal on target-label logits rather than relying on representation clustering. Catches BadNet/AddSent/SynBkd/StyleBkd plus three novel "contextually-aware" attacks the paper introduces that hide triggers inside legitimate domain vocabulary.
- **ICLR 2025 — FLORAL.** [Adversarial Training for Defense Against Label Poisoning Attacks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.17121) frames label poisoning as a Stackelberg-style training problem and reports stronger robust accuracy than several baselines under increasing attacker budgets. The work is promising for classical classification settings, but not a drop-in defense for LLM fine-tuning or multi-modal pipelines.
- **Robustness evaluation baseline.** [AutoAttack](https://github.com/fra31/auto-attack) remains a practical empirical check for perturbation robustness, while [RobustBench](https://robustbench.github.io/) gives a maintained benchmark and model zoo. These tools help catch weak or gradient-masking defenses, but they do not certify poisoning resistance.
- **Feb 2026 — Semantic Consensus Decoding.** [arXiv:2602.04195](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.04195). Inference-time backdoor defense for hardware-code generation: consensus-decodes full-spec and functional-only-spec generations, suppressing divergences caused by triggers hidden in non-functional requirements. Attack success ~89% → under 3% across three backdoor attacks with minimal quality loss. Verilog-specific, but the consensus pattern generalizes conceptually.
- **Apr 2026 — MB-Defense.** [arXiv:2601.04448](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.04448) (v4). Two-stage defensive poisoning for instruction-tuned LLMs — merges adversarial and defensive triggers into a unified representation, then neutralizes via additional training. Notably requires no prior knowledge of the attacker's trigger.
- **Apr 2026 — NIST critical-infrastructure profile concept note.** NIST published a [concept note for an AI RMF Profile on Trustworthy AI in Critical Infrastructure](https://www.nist.gov/programs-projects/concept-note-ai-rmf-profile-trustworthy-ai-critical-infrastructure) (dated 7 Apr 2026) and is forming a Community of Interest; still at concept stage as of June 2026, so quality-gate requirements for high-impact pipelines remain in flux. NIST's separate [Cybersecurity Framework Profile for AI preliminary draft (Dec 2025)](https://www.insideprivacy.com/artificial-intelligence/nist-publishes-preliminary-draft-of-cybersecurity-framework-profile-for-artificial-intelligence-for-public-comment/) is also in comment processing.
- **May 2026 — MITRE ATLAS moves to calendar versioning.** After v5.5.0 (Mar 31, 12 new techniques) and v5.6.0 (May 4, deepfake-phishing and agent-configuration techniques), ATLAS shipped [v2026.05 on 27 May 2026](https://github.com/mitre-atlas/atlas-data/releases) with a new calendar versioning scheme, platform tags on every technique (Predictive/Generative/Agentic AI), and a monthly release cadence under the [CTID "Secure AI v2" collaboration](https://ctid.mitre.org/blog/2026/05/06/secure-ai-v2-release). Feb 2026's v5.4.0 had already added [AML.T0104 Publish Poisoned AI Agent Tool](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0104), extending the poisoning delivery model to agent-tool registries; training-data quality programs should track the monthly drops rather than pinning an annual version.
- **AAAI 2026 — ConfGuard.** [arXiv:2508.01365](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.01365). Detects backdoored generations by spotting "sequence lock" — abnormally high and consistent token confidence across a sliding window. Reports near-100% TPR and negligible FPR with almost no inference latency, useful as a runtime tripwire that complements offline data screening.
- **Sep 2025 — PureVQ-GAN.** [arXiv:2509.25792](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.25792). Vector-quantized bottleneck purification for image data. 0% poison success against Gradient Matching and Bullseye Polytope and 1.64% against Narcissus on CIFAR-10, ~91–95% clean accuracy, and ~50× the throughput of diffusion-based purifiers. Vision-only; no text or code numbers yet.
- **Mar 2024 — Hong, Carlini & Kurakin.** [*Certified Robustness to Clean-Label Poisoning Using Diffusion Denoising* (arXiv:2403.11981)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.11981). Off-the-shelf diffusion denoiser used as a randomized-smoothing-style sanitizer. Drops attack success to 0–16% across seven clean-label poisoning attacks with negligible clean-accuracy loss; one of the few defenses with formal certified guarantees rather than empirical-only numbers.
- **ESORICS 2024 — ECLIPSE.** [*Expunging Clean-label Indiscriminate Poisons via Sparse Diffusion Purification* (arXiv:2406.15093)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.15093). Aimed at indiscriminate availability-degrading poisons (the inverse of targeted backdoors). Combines theoretical analysis of Gaussian-noise absorption with sparse diffusion purification and reports ~38% improvement over prior baselines on CIFAR-10/ResNet18.
- **Mar 2026 — LiteLLM supply-chain compromise.** [Trend Micro analysis](https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/26/c/inside-litellm-supply-chain-compromise.html) and [TechCrunch coverage of the Mercor breach](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/31/mercor-says-it-was-hit-by-cyberattack-tied-to-compromise-of-open-source-litellm-project/). Two backdoored PyPI releases of LiteLLM (1.82.7 / 1.82.8) harvested credentials and installed persistent backdoors across thousands of AI stacks. Primarily a supply-chain story (see C06), but a useful reminder that the data ingest and labeling pipelines covered by this section often run on top of compromisable middleware — a poisoned proxy can rewrite labels, drop integrity checks, or quietly mirror datasets to attacker-controlled stores.
- **Jun 2026 — Sequential Data Poisoning in LLM Post-Training.** [arXiv:2606.04929](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04929). Models the realistic case where different adversaries poison different post-training stages. In SFT→DPO pipelines the contributions are additive (splitting a poison budget across stages beats concentrating it); in SFT→PPO they are complementary (neither stage succeeds alone, the combination does). The authors call the resulting blind spot the "single-attacker illusion": evaluating each stage's data screening in isolation systematically underestimates compound vulnerability. Directly relevant to how 1.4.1/1.4.4 audits should be scoped — per-stage gates are necessary but not sufficient.
- **Jun 2026 — Semantic Confidence Aggregation.** [arXiv:2602.07842](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.07842) (v2, Jun 2026). Benchmarked 15 calibration methods across 4 LLM families on 12,000 questions and found systematic confidence underestimation whenever multiple valid answers exist; proposes aggregating confidence over multiple high-probability sampled responses. Directly applicable to confidence-gating machine-generated labels (1.4.2).
- **TIFS 2025 — FLARE.** [*Universal Dataset Purification against Backdoor Attacks* (arXiv:2411.19479)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.19479). Aggregates abnormal activations across *all* hidden layers (not just the final layer, where separability varies by attack) and clusters the dataset, treating the more stable cluster as poisoned. Evaluated against 22 backdoor attack variants including all-to-all, untargeted, and adaptive attacks; implementations ship in the BackdoorBox and backdoor-toolbox repositories.
- **Clean-label defense lineage (for 1.4.4).** [Deep k-NN filtering (arXiv:1909.13374)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.13374) → [Deep Partition Aggregation (arXiv:2006.14768)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.14768) → [Finite Aggregation (ICML 2022, arXiv:2202.02628)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.02628) → [Run-Off Election (ICML 2023, arXiv:2302.02300)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.02300), with [Friendly Noise (NeurIPS 2022, arXiv:2208.10224)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.10224) and [Mendata (arXiv:2312.01281)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.01281) on the purification side and [arXiv:2306.16415](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.16415) documenting the practical cost/accuracy trade-offs of aggregation at scale. Worth knowing the lineage because vendors routinely cite the 2020-era numbers without the later corrections.
- **ReSAISE 2025 — Stealthy Code-Gen Poisoning.** [arXiv:2508.21636](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.21636). Independent evaluation of spectral signatures, activation clustering, and static analysis on CodeBERT, CodeT5+, and AST-T5 found all three classes struggle against triggerless code-generation poisoning. Reinforces the case for CodeScan-style AST-normalized scanning plus end-to-end behavioral testing.
- **EU AI Act high-risk enforcement milestone.** High-risk AI systems must demonstrate documented data governance, bias measurement, traceability, and human oversight under the [EU AI Act high-risk obligations](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai). The Digital Omnibus on AI — provisional trilogue agreement 6–7 May 2026, endorsed by Member States 13 May 2026, formal adoption pending as of June 2026 — moved the Annex III high-risk application date from 2 August 2026 to **2 December 2027** (AI embedded in Annex I regulated products: 2 August 2028), as fixed dates rather than the standards-availability trigger the Commission originally floated ([Gibson Dunn summary, May 2026](https://www.gibsondunn.com/eu-ai-act-omnibus-agreement-postponed-high-risk-deadlines-and-other-key-changes/)). Penalties remain up to €35M or 7% of global turnover for the most serious violations and up to €15M or 3% for non-compliance with high-risk obligations. Article 10's text is unchanged — training-data quality controls are still audit targets, just on a longer fuse. See the [Cloud Security Alliance mapping of ISO/IEC 42001 and NIST AI RMF onto the EU AI Act](https://cloudsecurityalliance.org/blog/2025/01/29/how-can-iso-iec-42001-nist-ai-rmf-help-comply-with-the-eu-ai-act).
- **May 2025 — CISA/NSA/FBI joint guidance.** The [AI Data Security CSI](https://media.defense.gov/2025/May/22/2003720601/-1/-1/0/CSI_AI_DATA_SECURITY.PDF) (rolled into [CISA's resource page](https://www.cisa.gov/resources-tools/resources/ai-data-security-best-practices-securing-data-used-train-operate-ai-systems)) directs operators to track data provenance, verify integrity with checksums and cryptographic hashes during storage and transport, and use quantum-resistant digital signatures to authenticate trusted revisions across training, fine-tuning, and alignment.

## Audit Evidence Checklist

For this section, a paper control is not enough. Ask for artifacts that prove the quality gates run at the right points in the pipeline:

- Ingest DAG screenshots or configuration showing schema, null, type, and label-distribution checks before data lands in the training set.
- Current expectation suites, Pandera schemas, TFX schemas, or equivalent contracts, with owners and review history.
- Alert records showing failed data-quality checks, who approved any override, and whether downstream training was blocked.
- Baseline label distributions and embedding/outlier thresholds stored with dataset versions, not just in a dashboard.
- Canary poisoned samples or synthetic corruption tests that exercise poisoning detectors without entering production data — including clean-label canaries, not just dirty-label ones.
- Auto-labeling confidence thresholds, ensemble-disagreement rules, sampled-run consistency criteria, and human-review sampling rates for machine-generated labels.
- Dataset fingerprints, checksums, or AIBOM entries tying each model version back to the exact dataset snapshot used.
- Robustness evaluation runs showing attack budget, norm, seeds, AutoAttack/Foolbox/ART configuration, clean accuracy, robust accuracy, and attack success rate before and after hardening.
- Clean-label poison canary results showing k-NN/filtering thresholds, DPA certificate or ensemble settings, poison-detection precision/recall, and manual review outcomes for quarantined samples.
- Per-subgroup error metrics (FPR/FNR disaggregated by relevant groups) and subgroup-evasion test reports for security-critical models, including the red-team prompts or samples used to probe bypass patterns.

## Standards Cross-Reference

- **OWASP AISVS** C1.1 (origin and data security), C1.2 (labeling), C6.3 (third-party dataset risk), C11.2 (adversarial-example hardening), C11.6 (inference-time poisoned-data detection).
- **NIST AI RMF** (GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE) — [framework home](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework). Data validation sits squarely in MEASURE + MANAGE, and the 2026 critical-infrastructure profile work makes operational monitoring more important for high-impact deployments.
- **NIST AI 100-2e2025** — [Adversarial Machine Learning: A Taxonomy and Terminology of Attacks and Mitigations](https://www.nist.gov/publications/adversarial-machine-learning-taxonomy-and-terminology-attacks-and-mitigations-0) gives the current NIST taxonomy for poisoning, evasion, privacy, and mitigation language; useful for writing a defensible threat model before choosing a robustness defense.
- **NIST SP 1270** — [Towards a Standard for Identifying and Managing Bias in AI](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/SpecialPublications/NIST.SP.1270.pdf). The systemic / statistical-computational / human-cognitive bias taxonomy and lifecycle approach underpinning 1.4.3 evaluations.
- **EU AI Act Article 10** — [Data & Data Governance](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/10/). Mandates data governance for design choices, source/origin, preparation operations, bias examination (10(2)(f)–(g)), representativeness, and data gaps for high-risk systems; Article 10(3) requires training, validation, and test data to be relevant, sufficiently representative, and to the best extent possible free of errors and complete. Annex III application deferred to 2 December 2027 by the May 2026 Digital Omnibus agreement.
- **ISO/IEC 42001** — [AI management system](https://www.iso.org/standard/42001). Data quality belongs inside the management system: scoped ownership, risk treatment, monitoring, internal audit, and continual improvement. The 2025 companions [ISO/IEC 42005 (AI impact assessment)](https://www.iso.org/standard/42005) and [ISO/IEC 42006 (audit-body requirements)](https://www.iso.org/standard/42006) round out the certification ecosystem.
- **ISO/IEC 5259 series (2024–2026)** — data quality for analytics and ML, now complete in six parts: [5259-1 overview/terminology](https://www.iso.org/standard/81088.html), [5259-2 quality measures](https://www.iso.org/standard/81860.html) (quantitative metrics including label accuracy — 1.4.2), [5259-3 quality management requirements](https://www.iso.org/standard/81092.html) (the auditable "requirements" part — 1.4.1), [5259-4 process framework](https://www.iso.org/standard/81093.html) (covers annotation processes — 1.4.2), [5259-5 governance framework](https://www.iso.org/standard/84150.html), and [TR 5259-6 visualization framework (2026)](https://www.iso.org/standard/86532.html).
- **ISO/IEC TR 24027:2021** — [Bias in AI systems and AI-aided decision making](https://www.iso.org/standard/77607.html). Bias sources (human-cognitive, data, engineering-decision) plus assessment metrics and treatment methods across the lifecycle; the ISO-side basis for 1.4.3.
- **CSA AI Controls Matrix** — [AICM](https://cloudsecurityalliance.org/artifacts/ai-controls-matrix) maps 243 AI control objectives to frameworks including ISO 42001 and NIST AI RMF. Useful for turning this page's checks into governance evidence.
- **MITRE ATLAS** (v2026.05, May 2026; now on monthly calendar releases) — [AML.T0020 Poison Training Data](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0020), [AML.T0010 AI Supply Chain Compromise](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0010), [AML.T0018 Backdoor ML Model](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0018), [AML.T0015 Evade AI Model](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0015), [AML.T0042 Craft Adversarial Data](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0042), [AML.T0104 Publish Poisoned AI Agent Tool](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0104).
- **NIST AI 600-1** — [Generative AI Profile](https://airc.nist.gov/docs/NIST.AI.600-1.GenAI-Profile.ipd.pdf) lists data poisoning, information integrity, and data privacy as named GenAI risks under the AI RMF. Useful when justifying why training-data quality is a Govern/Measure/Manage concern, not just a development hygiene task.
- **CISA / NSA / FBI and partners** — [AI Data Security: Best Practices for Securing Data Used to Train & Operate AI Systems](https://www.cisa.gov/resources-tools/resources/ai-data-security-best-practices-securing-data-used-train-operate-ai-systems) and the [underlying CSI PDF](https://media.defense.gov/2025/May/22/2003720601/-1/-1/0/CSI_AI_DATA_SECURITY.PDF) call out provenance tracking, checksums and cryptographic hashes during storage and transport, and quantum-resistant signatures across training, fine-tuning, and alignment.
