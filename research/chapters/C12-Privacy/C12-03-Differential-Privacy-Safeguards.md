# C12.3: Differential-Privacy Safeguards

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-05-19 (25th pass)

## Purpose

Track and enforce privacy budgets to provide formal guarantees against individual data leakage. The Fundamental Law of Information Recovery (Dwork & Roth, 2014) shows that too many adaptive queries destroy privacy — without explicit budget tracking, the cumulative epsilon can grow without limit, degrading privacy guarantees to meaninglessness. The tooling ecosystem continues to consolidate: on October 2, 2025, OpenDP formally absorbed Tumult Analytics and Tumult Core, with former Tumult Labs staff joining the OpenDP community and publishing the first post-merger release from the new home — a meaningful development for practitioners evaluating which DP stack to standardize on, because the U.S. Census Bureau, Wikimedia Foundation, and IRS deployments that previously tracked Tumult are now anchored inside OpenDP governance. DP Wizard v0.8 (February 10, 2026) landed shortly after, with Dataverse-driven usability improvements and a fresh FAQ embedded inside the application — a signal that the DP authoring tools are being shaped by data curators rather than DP researchers alone. On March 30, 2026, Harvard's John A. Paulson School of Engineering and Applied Sciences highlighted the Differential Privacy Deployments Registry as a flagship Salil Vadhan initiative, signaling growing institutional support for cross-organization deployment transparency — a credibility boost worth citing when defending epsilon choices in a regulated environment. As of 2025-2026, the DP tooling ecosystem has matured significantly: Google's VaultGemma (September 2025) demonstrated production-grade DP-SGD for LLMs at 1B parameters with epsilon <= 2.0, proving formal DP guarantees are achievable at LLM scale. DynamoEnhance extends DP-SGD to >7B-parameter models via multi-GPU DeepSpeed integration. Google's user-level DP fine-tuning research (ELS/ULS) shows orders-of-magnitude noise reduction versus prior approaches. PrivCode (NDSS 2026) is the first DP synthesizer for code datasets. The threat surface has continued to widen. PETAL (USENIX Security 2025) demonstrated that label-only access (no logits) suffices for membership inference: per-token semantic similarity approximates output probabilities well enough (Pearson r = 0.765) to achieve AUC 0.64 on Pythia-6.9B, and common defenses like paraphrasing and deduplication provide negligible protection. Tong et al. (USENIX Security 2026, arXiv 2510.05699, latest revision March 8, 2026) extended this further by showing that the *tokenizer itself* is an attack vector: because tokenizers are trained on data representative of pre-training corpora and can be retrained from scratch, five distinct MIA methods can infer dataset membership at the tokenizer layer alone — bypassing the mislabeled-sample, distribution-shift, and model-size confounders that have undermined model-level MIA studies. Their adaptive defense is the first dedicated tokenizer privacy mechanism, and audit programs that only test the trained model will miss this leakage class entirely. Khalil et al. (November 2025) showed that MIA vulnerability extends beyond overfitting — outlier samples (noisy, hard-to-classify records near class boundaries) leak membership even in well-generalizing models, which means DP must protect per-sample privacy regardless of aggregate model accuracy. Implementation-side risks remain underappreciated: Ratliff et al. (CCS 2024, arXiv 2409.05623) introduced a formal framework for differential privacy against timing attacks, defining a "timing privacy" notion that captures programs which remain DP to an adversary observing both runtime and output, and showing that timing-stable component programs followed by a random delay can be chained into a timing-private DP system. They demonstrate end-to-end timing-attack success rates up to 99.65% against unprotected discrete Laplace and 92.56% against floating-point Gaussian implementations, and provide a natural extension to the OpenDP Programming Framework — meaning DP-SGD pipelines now have a concrete, auditable mitigation path against side-channel epsilon erosion. A separate privacy-hallucination tradeoff has been quantified: DP fine-tuning at ε = 8 reduces factual text generation by 17-24% compared to non-private models, and DP pre-training causes a 43% factuality drop — a critical consideration for high-stakes domains like healthcare. A June 2025 systematic review published in npj Digital Medicine (Nature, January 2026 issue) covering medical DP deployments found that DP-SGD can maintain clinically acceptable performance at moderate budgets (ε ≈ 10), particularly for imaging tasks using compact CNN architectures (ResNet-9/18) with group normalization to avoid batch-statistics leakage; strict privacy (ε ≈ 1) typically causes substantial accuracy loss, with degradation amplified on smaller or heterogeneous datasets. The same review reinforced that subgroup fairness reporting must be standard at multiple privacy budgets, because aggregate accuracy can mask DP-induced disparities. On the efficiency front, FlashDP (NeurIPS 2025) introduced a cache-friendly per-layer DP-SGD that reaches 90% of non-private throughput on Llama-13B pre-training on four A100s, while TTLoRA-DP (January 2026) applies tensor train decomposition to shrink the trainable parameter space by 7.6x versus standard LoRA with lower membership leakage even without DP noise. For inference-time privacy, Yang & Zhu (March 2026) formalized token-level and message-level differential privacy for AI agents accessing enterprise databases, deriving privacy bounds as a function of generation temperature and message length — a complementary approach when training-time DP is unavailable. DP-Fusion (ICLR 2026, latest revision February 2026) offers a complementary token-level DPI mechanism that provably bounds the influence sensitive context tokens can have on LLM outputs, achieving lower perplexity at lower epsilon than prior inference-time methods by blending baseline and sensitive-token output distributions. NIST finalized SP 800-226 on March 6, 2025, providing the "DP pyramid" evaluation framework — privacy parameters (ε, δ) at the top, trust models and security at the middle, data collection practices at the base — requiring evaluation of every layer for meaningful guarantees. The OpenDP Differential Privacy Deployments Registry (launched November 2025) now catalogs 21 real-world DP deployments as structured "deployment cards," helping practitioners benchmark epsilon choices against comparable use cases — accompanying research accepted at IEEE S&P 2026 shows practitioners use the registry to compare deployments by dataset size and domain. The communication side of DP is also moving toward standardization: Dibia et al. ("We Need a Standard," PoPETs 2026, arXiv 2507.15997) synthesized 12 expert interviews into an essential parameter list for an expert-informed DP "privacy label," addressing the persistent problem that real-world deployments often fail to disclose ε, δ, the trust model, the unit of privacy, or the composition assumptions — a gap that audit teams should now treat as a finding in itself. The EU AI Act now needs more precise timing: transparency rules apply in August 2026, GPAI obligations have applied since August 2025, and the Commission's May 2026 simplification update extends high-risk rules for AI embedded in regulated products to August 2028 while transparency guidance remains due in Q2 2026. Domestically, the California Privacy Protection Agency's cybersecurity-audit rules took effect on January 1, 2026 under Article 9 of the CCPA regulations, but first audit reports and certifications phase in on April 1 of 2028, 2029, or 2030 depending on revenue band; DP deployments handling California consumer data should retain budget logs, accountant outputs, and evidence of independent review now. Importantly, Google Research found that the common practice of shuffling training data into fixed-size mini-batches can yield weaker privacy protection than DP-SGD theory assumes (which relies on Poisson subsampling); their scalable Map-Reduce Poisson subsampling implementation closes this gap and delivers better model performance for the same privacy guarantee. The Balls-and-Bins Sampling work (Chua et al., AISTATS 2025, arXiv 2412.16802) goes further: it provides a sampler whose implementation looks like shuffling but whose privacy amplification matches or beats Poisson subsampling, giving practitioners a "best-of-both" option when Poisson subsampling is operationally inconvenient. On the auditing side, ContextLeak (December 2025) introduced the first black-box auditing framework for private in-context learning (ICL), estimating empirical epsilon lower bounds for ICL methods — revealing that many existing private ICL techniques strike poor privacy-utility tradeoffs, either leaking sensitive information or severely degrading output quality. PrivLLM-Guard (Scientific Reports, 2026) demonstrated an adaptive DP framework for clinical LLMs combining context-aware Gaussian noise calibration (dynamically adjusted based on input sensitivity and real-time privacy risk scores) with hierarchical budget allocation across medical data categories — a domain-specific approach that could inform health-sector AISVS implementations.

## May 2026 Update

Two audit details deserve attention in May 2026. ContextLeak's April 20, 2026 revision frames private in-context learning as a measurable DP audit target: canary-based tests now give auditors a way to check whether prompt exemplars leak despite formal or heuristic privacy defenses, and leakage rises with the stated privacy budget. Google Research's NeurIPS 2025 sequential DP audit work is also useful for assurance programs because it processes output streams with anytime-valid inference and can expose DP-SGD violations before a full training run completes. USENIX Security 2026 adds another practical MIA check for fine-tuned LLMs: Window-Based Comparison aggregates local token- and phrase-level membership signals instead of averaging loss over the whole sequence, producing 2-3x better low-false-positive detection in the authors' experiments. Bhuekar's February 2026 hidden-state DP-SGD audit is a useful companion check for model-release programs because it shows final-weight-only releases do not automatically gain privacy amplification when a point participates in every optimization step; auditors should document whether the hidden-state assumption is actually part of the declared threat model. For compliance evidence, the OpenDP/NIST registry work is becoming more concrete: NIST IR 8588's comment period closed on December 5, 2025, while the live registry's Tier 2 and Tier 3 disclosures now give auditors a practical checklist for privacy unit, epsilon/delta, trust model, composition, post-processing, and implementation-security rationale. California timing also needs precision: the CPPA regulations are effective January 1, 2026, but cybersecurity-audit certifications phase in on April 1 of 2028, 2029, or 2030 depending on business revenue; DP budget logs should be retained now, but the first certifications are not due in 2026.

Two additional signals tighten the audit scope. ProjRes (April 23, 2026) shows that federated LLM fine-tuning can still leak membership through projection residuals in shared-gradient space, with the authors reporting near-100% accuracy across four benchmarks and continued effectiveness under strong DP defenses; federated DP reviews should therefore test the gradient-sharing protocol, not just the nominal client-level epsilon. DP-MTV (March 2026) moves private multimodal in-context learning from token-level protection into activation-space task vectors, giving a formal route for many-shot VLM adaptation at ε = 1.0 in the paper's VizWiz experiment, but only when auditors can verify disjoint partitioning, per-layer clipping, calibrated noise, and the claim that later inference is post-processing.

Three more 2026 papers are useful for deciding what "audit the DP implementation" means in practice. Re:cord-play (PoPETs 2026) instruments DP libraries on neighboring datasets with identical randomness and found 13 privacy violations across 12 open-source libraries, including SmartNoise SDK, Opacus, and Diffprivlib; this is a strong argument for grey-box library tests before accepting an accountant output as evidence. G-Drift MIA (April 2026, accepted for ICPR 2026) shows that white-box auditors can use a single gradient-ascent perturbation and internal representation drift to separate members from non-members more reliably than confidence, perplexity, or reference-model baselines. Fine-tuned diffusion language models need their own MIA coverage too: SAMA (ICLR 2026) exploits many maskable token configurations and reports 30% relative AUC improvement over the best baseline, with up to 8x improvement at low false-positive rates. Systems that expose counterfactual explanations should be reviewed separately, because Ezzeddine et al. (February 2026) found that explanation APIs can strengthen shadow-model MIAs unless privacy, utility, and explanation quality are tuned together.

## May 18, 2026 Update

Seven developments since May 13 deserve attention. First, on May 8, 2026 the European Commission published draft guidelines for AI Act Article 50 transparency obligations alongside a Code of Practice on Transparency of AI-Generated Content, with a targeted consultation open through June 3, 2026 ahead of the August 2, 2026 effective date — DP teams should track this because the draft pushes providers toward machine-readable disclosure of synthetic content and AI interaction, which interacts directly with how DP-trained generative pipelines document training-data provenance, noise mechanisms, and downstream synthetic-data labels. A May 12, 2026 analysis of the draft (Inside Global Tech, Covington) emphasized that the Commission explicitly accepts no single marking technique currently satisfies the Article 50(2) bar of effectiveness, interoperability, robustness, and reliability simultaneously, so providers should expect to combine watermarking, fingerprinting, cryptographic signatures, and metadata labels — DP audit packages should therefore document which of these signals the pipeline produces and whether the DP noise mechanism interferes with any of them. Second, SnapAudit (Xia, Liu & Xiong, arXiv 2511.13502, v3 May 7, 2026) is now the most efficient DP-ICL auditor — it decomposes the pipeline into a deterministic clean-inference stage and a stochastic DP-noise stage, achieves 80-200x speedup over prior passive auditors like ContextLeak, and in its own runs flagged two real implementation bugs: classical Gaussian noise underestimating leakage at large privacy budgets, and an embedding-aggregation mechanism with faulty sensitivity at partition count = 1 that produced an outright DP violation. Third, EZ-MIA (Ilić, Stanojević & Cvejoski, arXiv 2601.12104, January 2026) introduces a training-free MIA using an "Error Zone" score over two forward passes, achieving 66.3% TPR at 1% FPR on WikiText with GPT-2 (3.8x the prior state of the art) and 46.7% TPR at 1% FPR on AG News with Llama-2-7B — because the attack is so cheap, audit programs can now run continuous MIA regression tests against DP-fine-tuned LLMs without rebuilding a shadow-model pipeline. Fourth, BlackSpectrum (Lu et al., arXiv 2601.13607, January 2026) initiates membership inference against black-box large reasoning models (Gemini 2.5, Claude Sonnet, and similar systems that expose intermediate reasoning traces through APIs) by projecting trace representations onto a recall-versus-inference axis — meaning DP guarantees on the trained model do not by themselves protect against trace-based leakage when chain-of-thought is exposed.

The adversary side is also automating. AutoMIA (Liu et al., "Automated Membership Inference Attacks," arXiv 2603.19375, March 2026) and the follow-on AutoMIA self-exploration framework (arXiv 2604.01014, April 1, 2026) show that LLM agents can discover new MIA signal computations with up to 0.18 absolute AUC improvement over hand-crafted attacks, and consistently match or beat state-of-the-art baselines without manual feature engineering. The practical implication for audit programs is a cost asymmetry: a defender can no longer assume that "no published MIA beats our DP bound today" will hold for long, because the attack-design loop itself is becoming agentic. On the defensive side, SOFT (Zhang et al., USENIX Security 2025, arXiv 2506.10424, code at https://github.com/KaiyuanZh/SOFT) demonstrates that selective data obfuscation — paraphrasing the most influential fine-tuning samples below an MIA-loss threshold — preserves utility across six domains while measurably reducing MIA TPR, giving teams a non-DP defense to layer with DP-SGD when strict ε budgets would erode task accuracy beyond what the deployment can tolerate.

Three additional papers tighten 2026 audit baselines. AttenMIA (Zaree et al., arXiv 2601.18110, January 26, 2026) shifts MIA signals from output confidence into self-attention patterns inside the transformer, combining attention-head features with perturbation-based divergence metrics to reach 0.996 ROC AUC and 87.9% TPR at 1% FPR on WikiMIA-32 against Llama-2-13b — a clear indicator that white-box-friendly deployments now need attention-channel audits beyond the usual loss/perplexity tests. HT-MIA (Jawad et al., "What Hard Tokens Reveal," arXiv 2601.20885, January 27, 2026) goes the opposite direction: it concentrates on low-confidence "hard" tokens where membership signals are strongest, comparing fine-tuned versus pre-trained behavior at those tokens to outperform seven SOTA baselines on both medical and general-purpose datasets while explicitly confirming DP training as an effective defense. EL-MIA (Satvaty et al., arXiv 2511.00192, October 31, 2025) reframes the audit unit from the document to the sensitive entity (names, account numbers, credit-card fragments), publishes a benchmark, and shows that entity-level leakage can be surfaced by relatively simple attacks even when document-level audits look clean — meaning PII-driven deployments must add entity-granular MIA tests to their assurance pipelines.

Two systematization references make the auditor's job easier. "To Shuffle or not to Shuffle: Auditing DP-SGD with Shuffling" (Annamalai, Balle, Hayes & De Cristofaro, NDSS 2026, arXiv 2411.10614, latest revision December 11, 2025) presents custom shuffling-aware auditors and reports that shuffled DP-SGD overestimates its privacy guarantee by up to 4x in standard configurations and up to 10x for two common shuffling variants — strong empirical backup for the Poisson-vs-shuffle gap that prior work flagged theoretically. The Hitchhiker's Guide to DP Auditing (Annamalai, Balle, Hayes, Kaissis & De Cristofaro, arXiv 2506.16666, final revision December 12, 2025) is a SoK paper that organizes the auditing field around three goals — efficiency, end-to-end coverage, and tightness — and is useful as a reference when defending audit-design choices in a third-party review.

## May 18, 2026 Update (Late)

Three more developments arrived later on May 18, 2026 and deserve their own block because each one opens a new audit dimension. First, ICP-MIA (Lu, Chi, Baracaldo, Kadhe, Jeon & Yu, "In-Context Probing for Membership Inference in Fine-Tuned Language Models," arXiv 2512.16292, accepted at NDSS 2026, code at https://github.com/RPI-DSPlab/ICP-MIA) formalizes the "Optimization Gap" as a fundamental black-box membership signal: at convergence, member samples retain little loss-reduction potential while non-members still have room to improve, and In-Context Probing approximates that gap from inference-only queries by constructing strategically chosen contexts (reference data or self-perturbation). The authors report consistent gains over confidence- and likelihood-based MIAs against fine-tuned LLMs at low false-positive rates, which is the operating region that matters for production audits. Because ICP-MIA is training-free and needs no shadow models, audit programs can fold it directly into a regression suite for DP-fine-tuned LLMs without rebuilding pipelines.

Second, "Dependency-Aware Privacy for Multi-turn Agents" (Anshumaan, Choudhary, Palumbo & Jha, arXiv 2605.03188, May 4, 2026) shows that metric-DP prompt sanitizers that noise each release independently leak privacy across multi-turn agent conversations: when a sensitive attribute is the root of a computation graph, independently noising a derived value amplifies the root's distinguishability by up to the deriving function's Lipschitz constant L, which for nonlinear medical and financial workflows can dwarf the nominal ε. Their RootGuard mechanism sanitizes root values once and computes subsequent releases deterministically from the noised roots, so by the post-processing theorem the cumulative privacy cost stays bounded by the initial sanitization regardless of how many turns the adversary collects — the authors report 2.3-3.0x lower target error than independent noising on medical diagnostic templates. AISVS-aligned audits of agentic deployments should now treat "we apply ε-DP to each prompt" as a finding unless dependencies between turns are explicitly modelled.

Third, δ-STEAL (Phan et al., arXiv 2510.21946) sharpens the threat picture for LDP defenses by demonstrating that an adversary can inject LDP-preserving noise into the token embeddings of their distilled model during fine-tuning, bypass the provider's watermark detector at success rates up to 96.95%, and still preserve utility. The implication for DP audit programs is that LDP cannot simultaneously serve as a privacy guarantee and as evidence that downstream models are not stolen — watermark verification must be treated as a separate control with its own threat model.

Two additional items landed in the second half of May 2026 and deserve a brief mention. CorrDP (Sun et al., "Integrating Feature Correlation in Differential Privacy with Applications in DP-ERM," arXiv 2605.03945, AISTATS 2026, submitted May 5, 2026) introduces a relaxed DP framework that quantifies correlations between sensitive and insensitive features using total variation distance and applies distance-dependent gradient noise to DP-ERM and DP-SGD — useful when a deployment has clearly separated sensitive vs ancillary features and can justify weaker protection on the latter, but auditors should treat the relaxation as a finding-class disclosure (which features are deemed insensitive, how correlations were estimated, and whether the estimation itself consumed privacy budget). The TPDP 2026 program (June 1-2, 2026, Northeastern University Curry Student Center, Boston) has also published its accepted-paper list, and several items are worth tracking ahead of the workshop because each one directly reshapes audit baselines: "Extracting Training Data from Differentially Private Pre-trained LLM" (Diwan et al.) extends data-extraction attacks to DP-pretrained models, "Efficient DP-SGD for LLMs with Randomized Clipping" (Ullah et al.) and SPARTA (DP sparse fine-tuning) are the most likely follow-on candidates to Opacus v1.6.0 and DP-LAC, and "Privacy in Theory, Bugs in Practice: Grey-Box Auditing of Differential Privacy Libraries" (Cebere et al.) lines up with the Re:cord-play library-bug findings already in scope; audit teams should plan to refresh their MIA and DP-SGD baselines after June 2 once the workshop materials are public. The European Commission timeline is also slightly more precise than last week's summary: the Code of Practice on Transparency of AI-Generated Content is still drafted by independent experts and is now expected to be finalized at the beginning of June 2026 (digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu page on the Code of Practice plus the Commission's consultation pages), in time for the August 2, 2026 effective date of Article 50 obligations. The first draft (December 17, 2025) and the second draft (March 2026) both confirm that no single marking technique meets the Article 50(2) effectiveness/interoperability/robustness/reliability bar today, which keeps the combined-marking guidance from the May 8 draft transparency guidelines in force. CCPA timing is also worth restating in plain terms because the May 2026 trade press has been blurring it: cybersecurity-audit certifications must be filed with the CPPA by April 1 each year, with the first cycle staggered as April 1, 2028 for businesses over $100M revenue, April 1, 2029 for $50-100M, and April 1, 2030 below $50M — so 2026 is the year DP teams should be assembling evidence, not the year the first certifications are due.

Five more items landed between May 5 and May 11, 2026 and should be incorporated directly into audit baselines rather than treated as "watch list." First, Opacus v1.6.0 (Meta, May 5, 2026, https://github.com/meta-pytorch/opacus/releases) is the first release that combines Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) training, 1D tensor parallelism for fast gradient clipping (beta), and mixed/low-precision DP training in a single PyTorch library, plus a non-wrapping mode (`wrap_model=False`) that finally restores compatibility with HuggingFace Transformers and Accelerate without breaking module hierarchy assumptions — practically this is the version that lets a typical fine-tuning stack add DP-SGD without forking the trainer. The same release also ships epsilon/noise-accounting bug fixes for adaptive gradient clipping, a corrected fast-gradient-clipping path for `ignore_index` masking, empty-batch handling fixes, and a public hook for registering custom noise accountants, which is why audit teams should treat any "Opacus pre-1.6.0 with adaptive clipping" pipeline as a candidate for re-accounting before signing off on declared ε. Second, DP-LAC (Mehmood, Xu, Saravanan, Van Dalen & Ozay, "Lightweight Adaptive Clipping for Differentially Private Federated Fine-tuning of Language Models," arXiv 2605.10272, May 11, 2026, accepted at ICASSP 2026) bootstraps the initial clipping threshold from a private histogram estimate and then keeps adapting during training without consuming additional privacy budget or introducing new hyperparameters; the authors report 6.6% average accuracy gain over state-of-the-art adaptive clipping and vanilla DP-SGD across federated LLM fine-tuning benchmarks, which is the strongest evidence to date that adaptive clipping no longer needs to spend budget to be useful. Third, PRIVX (arXiv 2605.03388, May 5, 2026, "Graph Reconstruction from Differentially Private GNN Explanations") frames the Gaussian DP mechanism on a GNN explanation as a single DDPM forward step and inverts it with reverse diffusion to reconstruct hidden graph structure at AUC > 0.7 on five of seven datasets at ε = 5 — and finds that explainer choice changes the leakage ordering (neighbourhood-aggregating explainers such as GraphLIME and GNNExplainer leak more on homophilic graphs, per-node gradient explainers leak more on strongly heterophilic graphs). For any AISVS-conformant pipeline that releases DP-perturbed explanations to satisfy GDPR Article 22 or sectoral right-to-explanation rules, this is now a finding-class concern, not a research curiosity, and the accompanying PRIVF diagnostic gives auditors a way to decompose leakage into explainer-induced versus intrinsic graph-distribution components. Fourth, the InvisibleInk framework (Vinod, Pillutla & Thakurta, arXiv 2507.02974 v3, May 5, 2026, NeurIPS 2025, pip package `invink`) reformulates token sampling through the exponential mechanism on language-model logits with selective clipping and cost-free sampling from an expanded token subset, achieving 8x or better compute savings at equivalent utility versus prior DP long-form text generation baselines and bringing private generation cost within 4-8x of non-private generation — meaning teams who previously accepted "no DP for long-form private text generation" because of compute cost should re-evaluate as of May 2026. Fifth, "Differentially Private Model Merging" (Yin, Zaheer & Li, arXiv 2604.20985, April 22, 2026) provides a post-processing route to any target ε from existing DP-trained models on the same dataset using random selection or linear combination, with the authors proving linear combination dominates random selection under Rényi DP and privacy-loss-distribution accounting; this is directly useful for deployments that need to ship different ε tiers (research, internal, public) from one training pipeline without paying full retraining cost, but auditors should require explicit privacy accounting of the merge operation itself as part of the formal-proof scope.

The biggest open EU question from last week — whether the AI Act high-risk and transparency deadlines would actually slip — was answered on May 7, 2026, when the Council and Parliament reached a political agreement on the AI Omnibus. The deal rewrites the timeline that DP-trained high-risk pipelines have been tracking: Annex III standalone high-risk systems are postponed from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027 (a 16-month slip), Annex I high-risk AI embedded in regulated products moves from August 2, 2027 to August 2, 2028, and Member State regulatory sandboxes shift from August 2, 2026 to August 2, 2027. Article 50 transparency obligations — including the machine-readable marking requirement for AI-generated and manipulated content — slip four months from August 2 to December 2, 2026, which is the operative date for DP-trained generative pipelines that must label synthetic outputs. The agreement also adds a new prohibition (effective December 2, 2026) on non-consensual intimate imagery and CSAM, and introduces Article 4a permitting limited processing of special-category personal data strictly for bias correction in high-risk systems when no alternative exists, subject to mandatory technical controls and data-deletion requirements — a provision DP teams should track because it directly affects how sensitive training subsets are sourced, retained, and deleted. Formal adoption is still expected before the original August 2, 2026 cliff and still requires legal-linguistic revision plus Official Journal publication, so DP audit programs should not yet lock in the new dates as final. Audit programs that were planning to certify DP-trained high-risk systems for the August 2026 deadline now have measurable additional lead time, but the transparency-marking December 2, 2026 cliff remains firm and is the date most synthetic-content disclosure work should be aligned to.

## May 18, 2026 Update (Late, Part 2)

The CPPA enforcement picture also tightened in a way that directly affects DP-trained pipelines processing California consumer data, and the dates matter because they make audit-readiness an immediate obligation rather than a 2028 deliverable. The CPPA's new Audits Division is now formally operational under Sabrina Boyson Ross — named inaugural Chief Privacy Auditor in February 2026 (https://privacy.ca.gov/2026/02/california-privacy-protection-agency-names-sabrina-boyson-ross-as-chief-auditor/) after most recently serving as Privacy & AI Policy Director at Meta — and the Division has explicit authority to audit any CCPA-covered business at any time based on sector risk, regulatory priority, or independent research, which is distinct from the complaint-driven Enforcement Division that previously handled CCPA actions. The implication for DP teams is that the April 1, 2028 / 2029 / 2030 phased cybersecurity-audit certification timeline (https://www.hunton.com/privacy-and-cybersecurity-law-blog/calprivacy-director-expects-ccpa-compliance-audits-in-2026) is no longer the only relevant date — the Audits Division can request DP epsilon/delta accountant outputs, budget-consumption logs, privacy-label disclosures, and empirical-epsilon audit reports under proactive sector sweeps now, regardless of where a business falls in the phased certification calendar. CPPA Executive Director Tom Kemp's April 2026 IAPP interview (https://iapp.org/news/a/california-privacy-enforcement-in-2026-a-discussion-with-calprivacy-s-tom-kemp) confirmed both announced and unannounced audits are on the 2026 docket, with findings referrable to the Enforcement Division. Recent CCPA enforcement actions outside the DP scope still set the relevant fine-scale precedent for what audit findings can cost: Ford settled on March 5, 2026 for $375,703 over opt-out-friction violations from the connected-vehicle sweep, and Honda's prior settlement reached approximately $632,500 with mandated practice reforms. Separately, the CPPA's data-broker-audit preliminary comment period closed on May 7, 2026, which means the agency is actively shaping audit procedures rather than only enforcing existing ones — DP teams sharing training data with brokers should track this rulemaking because the audit scope is likely to extend through downstream data-sharing relationships.

The practical action for AISVS-conformant DP deployments handling California consumer data is to treat the privacy-label disclosure (Dibia et al., PoPETs 2026), budget-consumption log, accountant output, empirical-epsilon audit, and per-subgroup utility report as a single audit-ready bundle rather than five separate artifacts produced near the certification deadline — the Audits Division can request the bundle at short notice, and a missing or stale element is itself a finding.

## May 19, 2026 Update

Five further developments arrived between May 13 and May 18, 2026 and tighten the audit baseline. First, "A Systematic Exploration of Text Decomposition and Budget Distribution in Differentially Private Text Obfuscation" (Meisenbacher, Kleinert & Matthes, arXiv 2605.01065, PrivateNLP 2026, May 1, 2026) shows that for DP text obfuscation, the choice of decomposition strategy (sentence-level, span-level, token-level) and budget-distribution rule (uniform versus weighted versus learned allocation) can produce substantially different empirical privacy-utility tradeoffs at the same nominal ε; auditors of inference-time text DP should now treat decomposition strategy and per-chunk ε allocation as disclosure-required parameters rather than implementation details, and should reject pipelines that omit either from the privacy label. Second, "Privacy Preserving Machine Learning Workflow: from Anonymization to Personalized Differential Privacy Budgets in Federated Learning" (Sáinz-Pardo Díaz & López García, arXiv 2605.02372, FLICS2026, May 4, 2026) formalizes assigning per-client ε budgets in federated learning based on a re-identification risk metric and demonstrates better model utility on tabular medical data than uniform global-ε settings; the workflow also introduces a client-drift definition with detection methodology aimed at poisoning resistance, so federated DP audits should now check whether per-client ε allocation has a documented re-identification-risk basis and whether client-drift detection is part of the protocol rather than an after-the-fact correction. Third, "Safer Reasoning Traces: Measuring and Mitigating Chain-of-Thought Leakage in LLMs" (Ahrend, Eder, Yang, Pan & Groh, arXiv 2603.05618, March 5, 2026) provides a model-agnostic framework that traces PII leakage curves as a function of allowed CoT budget across 11 PII types, benchmarks four inference-time gatekeepers (rule-based, TF-IDF + logistic regression, GLiNER NER, LLM-as-judge), and reports that no single gatekeeper dominates across models or CoT budgets — meaning DP audits of reasoning systems should pair the BlackSpectrum trace-MIA with a hybrid gatekeeper policy that adapts to the CoT budget rather than relying on a single detector. For federated fine-tuning, check DP-FedLoRA implementations that combine DP-SGD with LoRA in federated settings. Verify that user-level DP guarantees use Example-Level Sampling (ELS) or User-Level Sampling (ULS) where applicable — Google's 2025-2026 research demonstrates orders-of-magnitude noise reduction versus naive conversions from example-level to user-level DP. Pay special attention to alignment method choice: DPO-aligned models are theoretically more vulnerable to MIA than PPO-aligned models, so formal proofs should account for the privacy implications of the alignment technique used. Require disclosure in the Dibia et al. PoPETs 2026 privacy-label format — ε, δ, unit of privacy, trust model, noise mechanism, and composition assumption — so each pipeline stage's contribution to the cumulative budget is explicit and machine-checkable. | Most DP tooling focuses on training-time guarantees. Fine-tuning with DP (DP-LoRA, DP-FedLoRA, DP-adapter methods) is maturing — DP-SFT (Zheng et al., arXiv 2601.11113, January 16, 2026) projects gradients onto a task-specific low-dimensional subspace, adds DP noise selectively, then maps back to full parameter space; the authors report accelerated convergence and substantial accuracy gains over DP fine-tuning baselines under strict ε. TTLoRA-DP (January 2026) achieves 7.6x parameter reduction over standard LoRA with improved privacy-utility tradeoffs on GPT-2, and a 2025 ACM workshop paper evaluated DP-enhanced parameter-efficient fine-tuning for LLMs and found it effective but with utility trade-offs scaling with model size. The privacy-hallucination tradeoff (quantified September 2025) means that formal proofs must balance epsilon budgets against factuality requirements — in healthcare and legal domains, a 17-24% factuality drop may be unacceptable, forcing practitioners toward less aggressive DP budgets or inference-time DP approaches (Private Evolution, DP prediction) that avoid training-time noise entirely. Yang & Zhu (March 2026, arXiv 2603.17902) formalized token-level and message-level DP for AI agents accessing enterprise databases, deriving privacy bounds tied to generation temperature and message length — this extends the scope of formal proofs to cover agentic inference pipelines, not just training. DP-Fusion (Thareja et al., ICLR 2026, arXiv 2507.04531) provides a token-level DPI mechanism that provably bounds the influence sensitive context tokens can have on LLM outputs by labeling sensitive tokens, computing a baseline distribution without them, and blending the sensitive-tokens distribution to remain within a bounded distance — formal proofs for inference-time pipelines should now cite either Yang & Zhu or DP-Fusion (or both) when sensitive tokens flow through context windows. For inference-time text DP, "A Systematic Exploration of Text Decomposition and Budget Distribution in Differentially Private Text Obfuscation" (Meisenbacher, Kleinert & Matthes, arXiv 2605.01065, PrivateNLP 2026, May 1, 2026) shows that decomposition granularity and per-chunk ε allocation are first-class parameters: significantly different empirical results arise at the same nominal budget depending on whether chunks are sentence-, span-, or token-level and whether ε is split uniformly, weighted, or learned — formal proofs that quote only a total ε without disclosing these choices are incomplete. For federated DP fine-tuning, Sáinz-Pardo Díaz & López García (arXiv 2605.02372, FLICS2026, May 4, 2026) extend formal-proof scope to personalized per-client ε budgets grounded in a re-identification risk metric and add a client-drift detection step that interacts with the budget allocation — federated DP audits should require that the chosen per-client ε mapping is auditable from the underlying risk metric, not asserted as a hyperparameter. Anshumaan et al. (arXiv 2605.03188, May 4, 2026, "Dependency-Aware Privacy for Multi-turn Agents") extend the inference-time picture to multi-turn agent workflows: independent metric-DP sanitization of each release leaks privacy as turns accumulate because derived values amplify root distinguishability by the deriving function's Lipschitz constant, so formal proofs for agentic systems must either model dependencies between releases or adopt the RootGuard pattern of sanitizing root attributes once and computing downstream releases as post-processing — the authors demonstrate 2.3-3.0x lower target error than naive independent noising on medical diagnostic templates. Embedding extraction is rarely covered by formal analysis. Tokenizer training is essentially never covered today, despite the Tong et al. results — a recognized gap that AISVS-conformant pipelines should explicitly address by including tokenizer-stage epsilon in the composition or by using DP-trained tokenizers once tooling matures. Reasoning-trace emission is also a new frontier: no current accountant treats chain-of-thought exposure as a budget-consuming release, even though BlackSpectrum demonstrates measurable leakage from those traces. Post-deployment inference queries also consume budget under some threat models but are typically excluded from analysis. The adversary-side cost curve is shifting too — AutoMIA's agentic attack-design loop (arXiv 2603.19375 and arXiv 2604.01014) means audit programs cannot pin acceptance criteria to a snapshot of MIA techniques; the formal-proof scope should commit to periodic re-evaluation as the LLM-discovered attack surface evolves. NIST SP 800-226 emphasizes evaluating the full composition stack but does not prescribe how to handle post-training budget accounting specifically. |

## Tooling

- **Adaptive optimizer correctness under DP:** **FIBER** (Dm, Do, Hoang, Tran, Kim & Nguyen, "FIBER: A Differentially Private Optimizer with Filter-Aware Innovation Bias Correction," arXiv 2605.03425, May 5, 2026) is the first DP-SGD-compatible AdamW variant that explicitly fixes the interaction between temporally filtered privatized gradients and AdamW's second-moment accumulator: when prior DP methods filter privatized gradients to reduce variance, the noise statistics seen by AdamW change, and standard bias corrections such as subtracting σ²_w become miscalibrated. FIBER moves denoising into innovation space through filtered residual streams, decouples observation geometry from innovation gain so each can be tuned independently, and applies a closed-form attenuated noise correction factor A(ω)σ²_w that accounts for the filter's effect on the noise spectrum. The authors report SOTA results on both vision and language benchmarks under equivalent privacy constraints — meaning DP fine-tuning stacks that previously combined Adam-family optimizers with temporally filtered DP-SGD should treat their second-moment accounting as a finding-class concern until either FIBER or a documented filter-aware equivalent is in use. This is also a concrete audit hook: ask the implementation team whether their adaptive optimizer applies an unfiltered or a filtered bias correction and require an explanation if the choice does not match the gradient pipeline.
- **Differential privacy:** Opacus (PyTorch — **v1.6.0 released May 5, 2026** introduces Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) training, 1D tensor parallelism for fast gradient clipping (beta), mixed/low-precision DP training, a non-wrapping mode (`wrap_model=False`) compatible with HuggingFace Transformers and Accelerate, custom noise-accountant registration, and corrects epsilon/noise accounting for adaptive gradient clipping — release notes at https://github.com/meta-pytorch/opacus/releases), TensorFlow Privacy (TF), **JAX-Privacy 1.0** (February 2026, Google — production-ready DP training for JAX with Keras API integration; powers VaultGemma; supports CNNs, State Space models, and Transformers with far lower overhead than TF Privacy; scalable Poisson subsampling and correlated noise generation in distributed environments; the matching industry-track walkthrough is Amer Sinha's "Private Tuning of LLMs in Practice: From VaultGemma to Custom Fine-Tuning" at USENIX PEPR 2026 — Santa Clara, June 1-2, 2026 — which lays out the precise extraction-attack threat model JAX-Privacy is built to defend, useful as a deployment template when an enterprise needs to argue an internal fine-tune actually inherits VaultGemma-class guarantees rather than only borrowing the brand), Google DP Library (C++/Java/Go with Python bindings), OpenDP/SmartNoise (Rust core with Python API, Harvard/Microsoft), PipelineDP4j (JVM — Google's newest release for parallelizable DP computations), Diffprivlib (IBM), DynamoEnhance (multi-GPU DP training for >7B-parameter LLMs built on DeepSpeed, supports LoRA + BitsAndBytes + flash attention). FlashDP (NeurIPS 2025, open-sourced at https://github.com/kaustpradalab/flashdp) provides cache-friendly per-layer DP-SGD achieving 90% non-private throughput on Llama-13B with 50% less memory movement than GhostClip. TTLoRA-DP (January 2026) applies tensor train decomposition to LoRA for 7.6x parameter reduction with improved privacy-utility tradeoffs. **DP-SFT** (Zheng et al., arXiv 2601.11113, January 16, 2026) introduces differentially private subspace fine-tuning: identify the task-specific low-dimensional subspace, project gradients into it before adding DP noise, then map back — reportedly substantial gains over DP-SGD baselines and accelerated convergence under strict ε. Google's VaultGemma (September 2025) demonstrated production-grade DP-SGD for LLMs at 1B parameters with epsilon <= 2.0 — weights available on Hugging Face/Kaggle. Google's user-level DP fine-tuning research (ELS/ULS) shows orders-of-magnitude noise reduction versus prior approaches. PrivCode (NDSS 2026) is the first DP synthesizer specifically for code datasets, using a two-stage DP-SGD + utility-boosting pipeline. AWS fast-differential-privacy library provides mixed ghost clipping with 1-10% memory overhead and <2x slowdown versus non-private training.
- **Budget tracking & dashboards:** Tumult Analytics (PureDPBudget/RhoZCDPBudget session tracking with `remaining_privacy_budget` API and hard-stop enforcement — as of October 2, 2025 now maintained under the OpenDP umbrella at https://github.com/opendp/tumult-analytics following the Tumult Labs → OpenDP consolidation), AWS Clean Rooms Differential Privacy (managed "remaining queries" gauge with epsilon budget policy and CloudWatch integration), Google dp-accounting library (PLD/RDP/GDP accountants with `DpEvent` composition — Connect-the-Dots PLD is 5x faster than PRV Accountant), OpenDP/SmartNoise SQL (odometer-based cumulative epsilon tracking across query workloads), **DP Wizard v0.8** (OpenDP, February 10, 2026 — Docker- or pip-installable authoring UI for non-experts making DP releases through the OpenDP Library, with Dataverse-driven usability fixes and an embedded FAQ; useful for teams that need analyst-facing budget workflows without building a custom dashboard)
- **Subsampling correctness:** Balls-and-Bins Sampling for DP-SGD (Chua et al., AISTATS 2025, arXiv 2412.16802) — best-of-both sampler with shuffle-like implementation and Poisson-equivalent privacy amplification, recommended when Poisson subsampling is hard to integrate into existing data loaders; Google Research's scalable Map-Reduce Poisson subsampler closes the shuffle-vs-Poisson gap for distributed pipelines
- **Adaptive clipping for federated LLM fine-tuning:** **DP-LAC** (Mehmood, Xu, Saravanan, Van Dalen & Ozay, arXiv 2605.10272, May 11, 2026, ICASSP 2026) — bootstraps the initial clipping threshold from a private histogram estimate and adapts during training without consuming extra privacy budget or introducing new hyperparameters; reports 6.6% average accuracy gain over prior adaptive clipping and vanilla DP-SGD on federated LLM fine-tuning, which makes it the first adaptive-clipping method that is privacy-free at runtime in the federated LLM setting
- **DP composition at deployment:** **Differentially Private Model Merging** (Yin, Zaheer & Li, arXiv 2604.20985, April 22, 2026) — random-selection and linear-combination post-processing techniques that produce a model at any target ε from previously trained DP models on the same dataset; the authors prove linear combination dominates random selection under Rényi DP and privacy-loss-distribution accounting and validate on private mean estimation, useful when a deployment needs distinct ε tiers without retraining from scratch
- **GNN explanation leakage:** **PRIVX** (arXiv 2605.03388, May 5, 2026 — "Graph Reconstruction from Differentially Private GNN Explanations") frames Gaussian-DP-perturbed GNN explanations as a single DDPM forward step and inverts via reverse diffusion to reconstruct hidden graph structure at AUC > 0.7 on 5/7 datasets at ε = 5; the companion **PRIVF** diagnostic separates explainer-induced from intrinsic graph-distribution leakage. Audit hook for any pipeline that emits DP-perturbed GNN explanations under GDPR Article 22 or sectoral right-to-explanation rules — explainer choice (neighbourhood-aggregating vs per-node gradient) materially affects leakage and must be reviewed against graph homophily
- **Inference-only DP approaches:** Private Evolution (PE) — generates DP synthetic data by querying inference APIs without fine-tuning, compatible with foundation models accessible only through APIs; DP prediction — releases only model outputs with DP guarantees, avoiding training-time noise entirely. **DP-Fusion** (ICLR 2026, arXiv 2507.04531, code at https://github.com/MBZUAI-Trustworthy-ML/DP-Fusion-DPI) provides token-level differentially private inference that provably bounds sensitive-token influence on outputs — useful when sensitive context cannot be removed but its influence must be capped. **InvisibleInk** (Vinod, Pillutla & Thakurta, arXiv 2507.02974 v3, May 5, 2026, NeurIPS 2025, pip package `invink`) reformulates DP long-form text generation through the exponential mechanism on language-model logits with selective clipping and cost-free expanded-subset sampling, achieving 8x compute reduction over prior DP text generators at equivalent utility and bringing private generation cost to 4-8x of non-private generation — the most practical inference-time DP option for long-form text as of May 2026. These approaches sidestep the privacy-hallucination tradeoff inherent in DP-SGD training.
- **Multimodal and in-context DP:** DP-MTV (March 2026, arXiv 2603.04894) enables many-shot multimodal in-context learning by aggregating private image-text demonstrations into noisy activation-space task vectors; useful audit hooks include disjoint data partitioning, per-layer clipping, epsilon/delta accounting for the offline vector construction, and verification that downstream queries only post-process the privatized vector. ContextLeak and DP-ICL auditors remain necessary for prompt-exemplar leakage where teams use text-only private voting or heuristic prompt defenses.
- **LLM-assisted DP review calibration:** **DPrivBench** (Wang, Huang, Chien, Thakkar, Chaudhuri, Wang & Wu, arXiv 2604.15851, latest revision v3 May 18, 2026) — first benchmark targeting LLM reasoning over differential-privacy proofs and algorithm-level guarantees; the authors find that the strongest models handle textbook mechanisms but all models struggle on advanced algorithms, with a failure-mode taxonomy useful for designing regression tests. Audit hook for any program that uses an LLM to summarize or pre-verify a DP proof — record the model version and its DPrivBench-style accuracy on problem types closest to the deployment, and treat LLM-assisted DP attestations without that record as a finding.
- **Privacy auditing:** **Google DP-Auditorium** (open-source at https://github.com/google/differential-privacy/tree/main/python/dp_auditorium — black-box DP auditing with property testers and dataset finders, divergence optimization over function spaces for Rényi DP, pure DP, and approximate DP; consistently outperforms prior black-box testers and is the most general-purpose DP tester now in production), DP-Sniper (black-box DP auditing), **PrivacyGuard** (Melis, Grange, Kalemaj, Chadha, Hu, Kashtelyan & Bullock, arXiv 2510.23427, October 27, 2025, https://github.com/facebookresearch/PrivacyGuard — modular PyTorch framework for empirical DP analysis with first-class support for membership inference, training-data extraction, and reconstruction attacks against both supervised classifiers and LLMs; the modular architecture lets audit teams plug in newly published attacks and privacy metrics without re-implementing the harness, so a deployment can keep its audit baseline current with the AutoMIA / EZ-MIA / ICP-MIA stream of 2026 attacks rather than freezing on a paper-era benchmark), membership inference attack toolkits (ML-Doctor, privacy meter), canary-based memorization testing (ICLR 2025 methodology for LLM-specific auditing; extended by new-token canary design achieving empirical ε≈1 for analytical ε=4 in black-box LLM settings — arXiv 2503.06808), PETAL label-only MIA (USENIX Security 2025 — per-token semantic similarity, no logit access needed), **Window-Based Comparison** (USENIX Security 2026 — sliding-window MIA for fine-tuned LLMs that captures local memorization signals and improves low-false-positive detection), **Tokenizer MIA harness** (Tong et al., USENIX Security 2026, arXiv 2510.05699 — five MIA methods that target the tokenizer rather than the trained model, plus an adaptive defense; the first practical audit class for tokenizer-stage leakage), **Hidden-state DP-SGD auditing** (Scientific Reports 2026 — gradient-crafting checks for final-weight-only releases, especially when repeated data use eliminates claimed amplification), **ContextLeak** (December 2025 — first black-box auditing framework for private in-context learning, estimates empirical epsilon lower bounds for ICL methods), **SnapAudit** (arXiv 2511.13502, v3 May 7, 2026 — tight active auditing for DP-ICL with 80-200x speedup over passive auditors; decomposes a DP-ICL pipeline into a deterministic clean-inference stage and a stochastic DP-noise stage, supports black-box and white-box threat models, and in the authors' evaluation flagged a Gaussian-noise mechanism that underestimated leakage at large ε plus an embedding-aggregation mechanism whose sensitivity analysis produced an outright DP violation when partition count = 1), **EZ-MIA** (arXiv 2601.12104, January 2026 — training-free MIA using Error Zone scores with two forward passes per query, 66.3% TPR at 1% FPR on WikiText/GPT-2 and 46.7% TPR at 1% FPR on AG News/Llama-2-7B), **BlackSpectrum** (arXiv 2601.13607, January 2026 — first MIA against black-box large reasoning models such as Gemini 2.5 and Claude Sonnet, projecting exposed chain-of-thought traces onto a recall-vs-inference axis), **AutoMIA** (arXiv 2603.19375, March 2026; first audit baseline at PII granularity rather than document granularity), **ICP-MIA** (Lu, Chi, Baracaldo, Kadhe, Jeon & Yu, arXiv 2512.16292, NDSS 2026, code at https://github.com/RPI-DSPlab/ICP-MIA — training-free black-box MIA tailored to fine-tuned LLMs that estimates the Optimization Gap via In-Context Probing; uses reference data or self-perturbation rather than shadow models and is reported to beat confidence/likelihood baselines at low FPR), **Shuffle DP-SGD Audit** (Annamalai, Balle, Hayes & De Cristofaro, NDSS 2026, arXiv 2411.10614 — custom auditors quantifying up to 4x privacy overestimation in standard shuffled DP-SGD and up to 10x for common shuffling variants), **Hitchhiker's Guide to DP Auditing** (Annamalai, Balle, Hayes, Kaissis & De Cristofaro, arXiv 2506.16666, final revision December 12, 2025 — SoK organizing the field around efficiency, end-to-end coverage, and tightness, useful when defending audit-design choices in a third-party review), ensemble privacy defense (arXiv 2512.03100, December 2025)
- **Non-DP fine-tuning defenses:** **SOFT** (Zhang et al., USENIX Security 2025, arXiv 2506.10424, code at https://github.com/KaiyuanZh/SOFT) — Selective data Obfuscation in LLM Fine-Tuning identifies influential samples by MIA loss and paraphrases them, reducing MIA TPR across six domains while preserving downstream utility; useful when strict ε budgets would erode task accuracy beyond what the deployment can tolerate, or as a layered defense alongside DP-SGD. **SCP-Δᵣ** (Segal, Shabtai & Elovici, arXiv 2602.00688, January 31, 2026) — Near-Access-Freeness-based extraction defense that selectively smooths low-impact token-level deviations against a base model while preserving the small subset of influential changes; reports orders-of-magnitude better theoretical extraction bounds than prior NAF methods at minimal utility cost, useful as a layered defense alongside DP-SGD when extraction is the primary threat rather than membership inference.
- **Chain-of-thought leakage measurement:** **Safer Reasoning Traces** (Ahrend, Eder, Yang, Pan & Groh, arXiv 2603.05618, March 5, 2026) — model-agnostic framework that traces PII-leakage curves as a function of allowed CoT budget across 11 PII categories and benchmarks four inference-time gatekeepers (rule-based, TF-IDF + logistic regression, GLiNER, LLM-as-judge); the headline finding is that no single gatekeeper dominates across models or CoT budgets, so reasoning-model audits should record the gatekeeper combination, the CoT budget, and the measured leakage at that budget rather than borrowing a number from a different configuration.
- **Inference-time text DP allocation:** **DP Text Decomposition & Budget Distribution** (Meisenbacher, Kleinert & Matthes, arXiv 2605.01065, PrivateNLP 2026, May 1, 2026) — systematic study showing that decomposition granularity (sentence/span/token) and per-chunk ε allocation rule (uniform/weighted/learned) materially change the empirical privacy-utility tradeoff at the same nominal ε; audit hook for any DP text obfuscation pipeline to disclose both choices in the privacy label.
- **Personalized federated DP:** **Privacy Preserving Federated ML Workflow** (Sáinz-Pardo Díaz & López García, arXiv 2605.02372, FLICS2026, May 4, 2026) — assigns per-client ε budgets from a re-identification-risk metric on tabular data, integrates client-drift detection as a poisoning-resistance step, and reports better utility on medical records than uniform global-ε federated training; useful as a reference architecture when federated DP must justify non-uniform ε across clients.
- **Implementation-level DP testing:** Re:cord-play / Re:cord-play-sample (PoPETs 2026, arXiv 2602.17454; USENIX PEPR 2026 talk) inspects internal DP algorithm state under shared randomness to catch data-dependent control flow, sensitivity mismatches, and component-level violations that black-box auditors may miss; it is especially relevant when an organization wraps Opacus, SmartNoise, Diffprivlib, or custom SQL/analytics mechanisms and needs CI evidence that declared sensitivity and privacy-budget accounting match the executed code.
- **Sequential privacy auditing:** Google Research's NeurIPS 2025 sequential DP audit test processes output streams with anytime-valid inference, controls Type I error, and can identify DP-SGD privacy violations before a full training run completes; use it when batch auditors are too slow or too expensive for continuous training pipelines.
- **Side-channel hardening:** Ratliff et al. timing-privacy framework (CCS 2024, arXiv 2409.05623) — formal "timing privacy" notion plus a timing-stable + random-delay construction realized as an extension of the OpenDP Programming Framework; counters the 92.56% (DP-SGD floating-point Gaussian) and 99.65% (private sum, discrete Laplace) timing-leak attacks documented in the same line of research
- **Inference-time agent DP:** Yang & Zhu (March 2026, arXiv 2603.17902) — token-level and message-level DP framework for AI agents accessing enterprise databases, with privacy bounds tied to generation temperature and message length. **Dependency-Aware Privacy / RootGuard** (Anshumaan, Choudhary, Palumbo & Jha, arXiv 2605.03188, May 4, 2026) — extends agent privacy across multi-turn workflows by formalizing the Lipschitz amplification problem with independent metric-DP sanitization and providing the RootGuard pattern (sanitize roots once, derive downstream releases deterministically); reports 2.3-3.0x lower target error than naive independent noising on medical diagnostic templates and is the first practical answer to "what privacy guarantee does my LLM agent actually carry after N turns?"
- **Domain-specific DP:** PrivLLM-Guard (Scientific Reports, 2026) — adaptive DP framework for clinical LLMs combining context-aware Gaussian noise calibration with hierarchical budget allocation across medical data categories (patient identifiers, diagnoses, treatments); demonstrates domain-tuned DP for healthcare AI deployments. The npj Digital Medicine 2026 review of medical DP (arXiv 2506.00660) gives concrete deployment guidance: ResNet-9/18 with group normalization at ε ≈ 10 for imaging, mandatory subgroup fairness reporting across multiple budgets, and treating the privacy budget as a depletable resource with authenticated access, per-user query limits, logging, and audit trails after deployment.
