# C4.3: Edge & Distributed AI Security

[Back to C04 Index](C04-Infrastructure.md)

## Purpose

Secure distributed AI deployments including edge computing, federated learning, and multi-site architectures. Edge and mobile AI deployments operate in fundamentally hostile environments -- devices are physically accessible to attackers, network connectivity is intermittent, compute resources are constrained, and models deployed to devices can be extracted, reverse-engineered, or tampered with. This section addresses the unique security challenges of running AI inference on devices outside the controlled data center perimeter.

---

## Threat Landscape (2024--2026)

The edge AI attack surface has expanded dramatically as on-device inference moves from simple classifiers to full LLMs running locally on phones, vehicles, robots, and industrial controllers. As of early 2026, an estimated 80% of AI inference now happens locally on devices rather than in cloud data centers, and the security implications are profound. Nearly 3 billion malicious sessions targeted edge infrastructure in H2 2025 alone (averaging 212 malicious sessions per second across 80+ countries), with VPN appliances, consumer routers, and remote access services absorbing sustained exploitation from both criminal and nation-state actors. VulnCheck's 2026 State of Exploitation report quantified this: 884 newly exploited vulnerabilities (KEVs) were documented in 2025 -- a 15% year-over-year increase -- with network edge devices (firewalls, VPNs, proxies) the most frequently targeted technology category. The follow-on network edge report analyzed 181 exploited edge-device vulnerabilities from 2025, and found that only 23.7% were represented in CISA KEV. Nearly 29% of KEVs were exploited on or before their disclosure day (up from 23.6% in 2024), and 73% of actively exploited vulnerabilities mapped to the "Exploit Public-Facing Application" technique, with edge gateways representing roughly 17% of the total. The median time to remediate exposed vulnerabilities ranges from 270 days (utilities) to 577 days (education), creating a fundamental mismatch with exploitation timelines that operate in hours. CISA responded with Binding Operational Directive 26-02 (February 2026), mandating that federal agencies inventory and remove all unsupported edge devices within 12--18 months -- a recognition that unpatched edge infrastructure has become the primary initial-access vector for persistent threat actors. SentinelOne's "Edge Decay" analysis (April 2026) reinforces this pattern: edge appliances that cannot run EDR agents are now the preferred entry point for lateral movement and identity compromise, with campaigns like ArcaneDoor deploying firmware-level bootkits on legacy Cisco ASA devices and attackers using compromised F5 BIG-IP instances to pivot directly into VMware vSphere environments. VulnCheck data shows 42.5% of vulnerabilities exploited in 2025 affected end-of-life devices, with consumer routers and globally distributed networking products accounting for 56% of exploited edge device vulnerabilities. The Trend Micro TrendAI State of AI Security Report quantifies the broader AI vulnerability trend: 4.42% of all CVEs issued in 2025 were AI-related (2,130 total), a 34.6% year-over-year increase from 1,583 in 2024, with cumulative AI CVEs now exceeding 6,000. Of the 3,257 scored AI vulnerabilities, 26.2% carry high or critical CVSS scores, and the AI supply chain category shows the highest severity concentration at 46.5%. Three categories -- GPU/AI hardware (3,127), ML frameworks (1,624), and LLM ecosystem (1,243) -- dominate the total, and Trend Micro projects 2,800--3,600 AI CVEs for 2026, a 31--69% increase driven by agentic system adoption and expanding LLM ecosystems. Key developments include:

### Model Extraction Is Now Practical at Scale

Researchers have demonstrated practical model extraction from production mobile applications. In documented attacks against apps using TensorFlow Lite, attackers used dynamic instrumentation (Frida) to intercept decrypted TFLite models at runtime, bypassing static encryption protections entirely. Studies show most mobile apps today leave their on-device models completely unprotected. A connected-vehicle platform suffered an API abuse attack that exfiltrated core autonomous-driving model parameters, resulting in direct economic losses of $12.7 million due to intellectual-property compromise.

### Side-Channel Attacks on Edge AI Hardware -- and Now Network Traffic

CVE-2025-23270 (CVSS 7.1) demonstrated that neural network weights can be extracted from NVIDIA Jetson chips by measuring electromagnetic radiation during inference. This class of side-channel attack -- using power consumption, EM emissions, or timing analysis -- is particularly dangerous for edge devices that lack the physical security controls of data centers. The industry-standard Test Vector Leakage Assessment (TVLA) method uses Welch's t-test to detect statistical leakage in power traces, but most edge deployments do not implement such testing. A newer class of side-channel threat targets network traffic rather than hardware: Whisper Leak (Microsoft Research, November 2025) demonstrated that encrypted LLM traffic can be analyzed to infer user prompt topics by exploiting packet size and timing patterns in streaming responses. The attack achieved near-perfect classification (>98% AUPRC) across 28 popular LLMs from major providers, with 100% precision in identifying sensitive topics like financial crime discussions. Because TLS preserves the size relationship between plaintext and ciphertext, token-by-token streaming leaks statistical patterns that reveal conversation topics even through encryption. Proposed mitigations (random padding, token batching, packet injection) reduce but do not eliminate the attack's effectiveness. For edge-to-cloud LLM communication, this means that encryption alone is insufficient to protect prompt confidentiality.

### TEE-Based Model Protection Has Fundamental Weaknesses

Research published in early 2026 revealed a systemic vulnerability in partial TEE-shielded LLM inference systems that rely on precomputed noise vectors for performance optimization. These systems precompute a small set of K secret basis vectors at startup and combine them via random coefficients during inference -- confining all "random" noise to a predictable, low-dimensional subspace. Attackers can collect K+delta query outputs with zero-vector inputs, reconstruct the noise subspace via Gram-Schmidt orthonormalization, and then recover secret permutation matrices to extract full model weights. For LLaMA-3 8B, a single layer's secrets can be recovered in approximately 6.2 minutes; the full 405B model requires under two hours per layer. The attack achieves perfect model replicas with identical accuracy. Proposed countermeasures (random subset sampling, increasing K) are ineffective -- this is a fundamental cryptographic flaw rooted in key material reuse, not an implementation bug. This finding challenges the viability of any partial TEE approach that uses static precomputation for performance.

### Supply Chain Compromise: Factory-Infected Devices at Scale

The BadBox 2.0 botnet (disrupted mid-2025, Google lawsuit filed July 2025) demonstrated that supply chain compromise of edge devices can operate at massive scale. Over 10 million budget Android-based IoT devices -- smart TVs, streaming boxes, tablets, and digital projectors -- were shipped with malware pre-installed in firmware before leaving factories, primarily in Asia. Infected devices were enrolled into a residential proxy botnet spanning 222 countries, used for ad fraud, credential theft, and DDoS operations. The Aisuru/TurboMirai botnet variant achieved 20+ Tbps DDoS capability from recruited IoT endpoints. BadBox 2.0 specifically targeted uncertified Android Open Source Project (AOSP) devices rather than Play Protect-certified hardware, highlighting that device certification and hardware attestation are not optional for any edge AI deployment handling sensitive inference workloads.

As of May 2026, the botnet pattern has expanded from factory-compromised devices into active abuse of weakly managed edge fleets and residential proxy services. Kimwolf, an Android variant linked to Aisuru, has infected more than 2 million Android devices and commonly enters through exposed Android Debug Bridge services; researchers found that 67% of connected devices in the botnet were unauthenticated with ADB enabled by default. KadNap, disclosed by Lumen's Black Lotus Labs in March 2026, compromised more than 14,000 ASUS edge devices and used a custom Kademlia DHT command-and-control layer to blend peer discovery with ordinary BitTorrent-like traffic before enrolling devices into the Doppelganger criminal proxy service. RondoDox's January 2026 exploitation of HPE OneView is another warning sign: enterprise management planes for servers, storage, and networking are now attractive botnet targets because a single compromise can reach many downstream edge systems.

### Edge Device Patching Crisis

As of early 2026, the edge device ecosystem faces a systemic patching crisis. Many edge AI systems run proprietary Board Support Packages with custom kernels that become unmaintainable when original build environments disappear. Industry analysis identifies "frozen devices" -- systems where legacy kernel patches no longer exist -- as a growing fleet-wide vulnerability. The EU Cyber Resilience Act will classify inability to patch as a compliance violation, and its Article 14 reporting obligations begin on September 11, 2026: manufacturers must file an early warning within 24 hours, a full notification within 72 hours, and a final vulnerability report no later than 14 days after a corrective measure is available. The practical challenge remains: AI workloads at the edge cannot tolerate traditional patch-and-reboot cycles, with even 10-minute reboots creating dangerous operational gaps for autonomous systems. Organizations are moving toward containerized deployments enabling hot-swap model updates and A/B redundancy (blue-green deployment) to enable zero-downtime patching. A separate concern is security testing parity -- teams commonly disable Secure Boot, disk encryption, and SELinux in CI/CD testing to simplify setup, creating dangerous divergence from production security posture.

NIST IR 8259r1 (April 2026) sharpens the manufacturer side of this problem by treating IoT cybersecurity as a full product-lifecycle obligation, not a one-time hardening exercise. For edge AI devices, auditors should expect documented support lifetimes, customer-facing end-of-life notices, vulnerability disclosure processes, software update mechanisms, product retirement options, and clear statements of product composition and capabilities. These expectations map directly to AI edge fleets because model runtimes, accelerators, companion apps, cloud control planes, and update services all contribute to whether a device remains securable after deployment.

### AI Framework and Orchestration Vulnerabilities Are Now Critical-Severity

As of April 2026, the AI software stack itself has become a high-value attack surface. CVE-2025-59528 (CVSS 10.0) in Flowise AI -- an open-source LLM workflow and agent builder -- allows unauthenticated remote code execution through the CustomMCP node by injecting malicious JavaScript into the mcpServerConfig parameter. The vulnerability runs with full Node.js runtime privileges, granting access to child_process (command execution) and fs (file system), enabling extraction of API keys, credentials, and configuration secrets from any exposed instance. Over 12,000 internet-facing Flowise instances were identified at time of disclosure (April 2026), with active exploitation observed from scanning infrastructure. Separately, CVE-2026-26030 (CVSS 9.8) in Microsoft Semantic Kernel's Python SDK exposed a code injection flaw in the InMemoryVectorStore filter functionality, affecting AI applications using vector search for retrieval-augmented generation -- patched in version 1.39.4. These vulnerabilities demonstrate that AI orchestration layers (agent builders, RAG pipelines, tool-use frameworks) carry the same critical-severity risk profile as traditional web applications, but are often deployed with less security scrutiny.

### MDM and Endpoint Management Platforms as Edge Attack Vectors

CVE-2026-1340 and CVE-2026-1281 (both CVSS 9.8) in Ivanti Endpoint Manager Mobile (EPMM) -- a widely deployed mobile device management platform -- allowed unauthenticated remote code execution through code injection flaws in core authentication and request-handling logic. CISA added CVE-2026-1340 to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on April 8, 2026, confirming active exploitation in the wild. Successful compromise of an EPMM appliance gives attackers administrative control over all managed mobile and edge endpoints, enabling malicious policy deployment, device reconfiguration, and lateral movement into internal infrastructure. This attack pattern -- compromising the management plane to gain fleet-wide control -- is particularly dangerous for edge AI deployments where hundreds or thousands of inference devices report to a central MDM/EMM, because a single management plane compromise can push poisoned model updates or reconfigure security policies across the entire fleet simultaneously.

### Agentic Edge AI Introduces New Attack Layers

The convergence of autonomous AI agents with edge computing (in robots, autonomous vehicles, drones, and industrial systems) has introduced multi-layer attack surfaces spanning perception (sensor spoofing, camera blinding, ultrasonic audio injection), cognition (adversarial inputs, model poisoning via OTA updates), and actuation (command injection, control-channel hijacking). Adversarial perturbations added to smart-home camera input frames can elevate a detector's false-negative rate from 12% to over 60%. By mid-2026, automated AI vulnerability research tools can analyze firmware binaries and generate working exploits against IoT edge devices in hours rather than the weeks previously required by human researchers, dramatically compressing the window between device deployment and exploitation. AI-native exploit kits are shrinking the entire attack lifecycle -- reconnaissance, exploitation, and execution -- into a single autonomous process requiring no human input, with tools like ExploitGen using LLMs to generate working exploit code directly from vulnerability descriptions and Mayhem using genetic algorithms to discover buffer overflows 80% faster than traditional fuzzing. Agentic edge AI also introduces memory poisoning as a new vector -- corrupting an agent's historical data and learned patterns to degrade decision-making accuracy over time, particularly dangerous in autonomous systems where decisions have physical consequences.

### Ransomware Targeting Connected Vehicles and Smart Mobility

Ransomware attacks on automotive and smart mobility systems doubled in 2025, with 44% of the 494 publicly reported automotive cybersecurity incidents involving ransomware -- more than double the 2024 volume. Notably, 92% of automotive cyber attacks were conducted remotely with no physical proximity required, and 67% of incidents involved telematics and cloud-connected systems with APIs as the primary attack vector. In a mid-2025 incident, attackers accessed vehicle command systems through a companion app, locked owners out, took remote control of ignition and door locks, and demanded ransom. A major European OEM attack paralyzed production for several weeks, impacting suppliers and regional GDP. The broader IoT/edge ransomware landscape is projected to cause $57 billion in damages globally in 2026.

### Federated Learning Remains Vulnerable to Sophisticated Poisoning

Recent research (CVPR 2025, NDSS 2025) demonstrates that attacks like PoisonedFL can defeat eight state-of-the-art FL defenses simultaneously, without requiring knowledge of the defense mechanism or access to genuine training data. The attack maintains consistent poisoning direction across rounds, whereas prior attacks' effects self-cancel. New defenses such as FedMP (multi-pronged anomaly detection on update magnitude and direction) and neuron-centric approaches (NC-FLD) show improved resilience even when 70% of participants are malicious, but the arms race continues. As of early 2026, DAARA (Divergence-Aware Attention for Robust Aggregation) shifts the defensive perspective from global anomaly detection to client-level divergence analysis, adaptively assigning attention weights based on statistical divergence and class-wise consistency -- outperforming Krum, Trimmed Mean, FoolsGold, and RFed by up to 40% in benchmarks on cybersecurity datasets (NSL-KDD, UNSW-NB15). FedVoD introduces a dual-voting mechanism for selecting trustworthy updates through similarity-based voting across training rounds, representing the trend toward multi-signal trust assessment rather than single-metric filtering. FedRDF (IEEE TETC, 2025) takes a fundamentally different approach by projecting client-generated weights into the frequency domain using Fourier Transform, selecting updates exhibiting the highest frequency component -- this spectral analysis can detect attack patterns that spatial statistical methods (median, trimmed mean, Krum) miss entirely, and crucially requires no prior knowledge of the number of attackers. A dual-defense line of work combining FHE-based secure aggregation with encrypted model similarity detection addresses the tension between privacy-preserving aggregation and poisoning defense, since standard secure aggregation obscures the very update patterns that anomaly detection needs to see. An ACM Computing Surveys systematic literature review (2026) using the PRISMA framework comprehensively taxonomizes the FL robustness landscape, cataloging adversarial data attacks, model poisoning, noisy updates, and label-flipping as the four primary threat categories, and concluding that static single-metric defenses are increasingly insufficient against adaptive adversaries who adjust strategy based on observed aggregation behavior -- multi-signal, multi-phase defense pipelines are now the consensus direction. A post-quantum secure FL framework for critical IoT infrastructure (January 2026) combines CRYSTALS-Kyber key encapsulation with homomorphic encryption during parameter updates and a reputation-based client selection algorithm that dynamically excludes Byzantine participants while preserving differential privacy -- achieving 96.8% threat detection accuracy, tolerating up to 40% Byzantine attackers with only 18% computational overhead and sub-second aggregation latency at 256-bit post-quantum security. FedGreed (2025) takes a different approach with loss-based sequential aggregation that evaluates client updates by their empirical loss contribution, enabling principled aggregation decisions without requiring prior knowledge of adversary counts -- critically, it has been validated under adversarial ratios as high as 80% with strongly non-IID data partitions, making it one of the most adversary-tolerant aggregation methods currently published. As of April 2026, XFED represents a concerning new threat class: the first aggregation-agnostic, non-collusive model poisoning attack, where compromised clients independently pursue a shared malicious goal without coordinating, exchanging benign models, or knowing what server-side defense is deployed. Evaluated across six benchmark datasets, XFED bypasses eight state-of-the-art defenses and outperforms six existing model poisoning attacks, leading the authors to conclude that FL systems are substantially less secure than previously believed when non-collusive threat models are considered. On April 28, 2026, Scientific Reports published FLAURA, an adaptive trust and representation-space aggregation defense that uses geometric-median penultimate-layer representations, MMD-based trust boundaries, and hybrid hard filtering/soft weighting to separate malicious clusters from benign non-IID client diversity.

### On-Device LLMs Expand the Attack Surface

On-device LLMs (running on mobile SoCs with 8--16 GB RAM) expose significantly more intellectual property than traditional edge classifiers. As of March 2026, the hardware landscape has matured considerably: Apple's A19 Pro Neural Engine delivers 35 TOPS, Qualcomm's Snapdragon 8 Elite provides 60 TOPS with a dedicated Secure Processing Unit (SPU) and Microsoft Pluton integration for hardware-rooted AI workload security, and MediaTek's Dimensity 9400+ reaches 50 TOPS. However, memory bandwidth remains the decisive constraint -- mobile devices achieve 50--90 GB/s versus data centers' 2--3 TB/s, a 30--50x gap that makes quantization and efficient inference strategies more security-critical than raw compute speed. The Qualcomm SPU embeds cryptographic operations, secure key management, and tamper-resistant key storage within a hardware enclave isolated from the main processor, providing a hardware anchor for model decryption and device attestation on Android devices. As of April 2026, on-device LLMs are no longer experimental -- they are embedded in core mobile applications with access to personal data (messages, photos, health records, location), and security scanning has identified over 175,000 exposed Ollama LLM inference servers globally, illustrating the scale of the exposed attack surface. Google's LiteRT-LM (released April 2026) adds another production-grade framework to the landscape, delivering cross-platform edge LLM inference across Android, iOS, Web, Desktop, and IoT (including Raspberry Pi) with GPU and NPU acceleration, sub-1.5GB memory usage, and support for Gemma, Llama, Phi-4, and Qwen models -- benchmarks show 52--160 tokens/second decode throughput depending on device class. ARM's Cortex-A320 and Ethos-U85 NPU (the first Armv9 edge AI platform, available early 2026) bring 10x ML performance uplift over predecessors and enable on-device models over one billion parameters on IoT-class hardware, with integrated SBOM capabilities addressing CRA compliance requirements. TZ-LLM is the first system enabling secure on-device LLM inference using ARM TrustZone, reducing time-to-first-token by up to 90.9% compared to naive TEE approaches, but TrustZone was never designed for multi-gigabyte ML models, creating fundamental architectural tension between security and capability. As of March 2026, FlexServe offers a concurrent approach that eliminates TrustZone's contiguous memory allocation requirement entirely, achieving only 4.41% overhead versus unprotected inference and a 24.3x speedup for multi-model agentic workflows -- a meaningful step toward practical secure on-device LLM serving. ARM's Confidential Compute Architecture (CCA), available on Armv9-A processors, introduces Realms as a more flexible alternative to TrustZone, using page-granular MMU-based isolation rather than coarse-grained bus-level protection, though CCA-capable edge silicon is still in early adoption. LLMs embedded in mobile apps risk exposing sensitive user data (messages, photos, health records, location) through model interactions. LoRO (NeurIPS 2025) offers a different strategy: rather than isolating the entire model inside a TEE, it uses low-rank factors to generate dense masks that completely obfuscate model parameters in the untrusted environment, reducing complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2) and achieving only 1.49x latency overhead compared to 112x for prior secure inference approaches -- it requires no model retraining or modification, downgrading extractable models to black-box level (0.94x membership inference accuracy). However, the precomputed-noise vulnerability described above applies to any system that caches secret basis vectors for performance, so implementers must carefully evaluate whether their TEE strategy relies on static precomputation patterns.

### Verifiable AI Inference Is Approaching Practical Feasibility

A persistent gap in edge AI security has been the inability to verify that a model actually executed the claimed computation -- essential for auditing, regulatory compliance, and detecting model substitution attacks. Full cryptographic proof systems (zkSNARKs) impose prohibitive prover overhead of hundreds of seconds per query for billion-parameter models, making them impractical for production inference. Research accepted at IEEE SaTML 2026 introduces a sampling-based verification protocol that commits to an inference execution trace via Merkle-tree-based vector commitments, then reveals only selected entries along randomly sampled paths from output to input. This reduces proving time by several orders of magnitude -- from minutes to milliseconds -- validated on architectures up to Llama-2-7B. The approach trades absolute soundness for computational efficiency, making it suitable for deployment settings where repeated queries amplify detection probability and economic incentives deter cheating. Separately, an IACR 2026 survey of privacy-preserving LLM inference characterizes a practical deployment trajectory: TEE-based solutions enable large-scale confidential inference today (4--7% throughput reduction); crypto-augmented designs (e.g., SIGMA achieving 12--19x speedup over prior GPU-based secure computation, CipherGPT achieving 6.2x speedup and 4.1x bandwidth savings for two-party inference) reduce reliance on hardware trust; and FHE represents the long-term endpoint for non-interactive confidentiality. For edge deployments, TEE-based inference remains the only currently practical option, while crypto-augmented designs may become viable as edge NPU performance continues to scale.

### Model Compression Introduces Security Brittleness

The push toward smaller on-device models through quantization (INT4, INT2/INT3), pruning, and knowledge distillation enables deployment on constrained hardware but introduces security trade-offs that are often overlooked. Compressed models can become more brittle, with reduced tolerance for noisy or adversarial inputs. As of March 2026, sub-1B parameter models (125M--1.7B range) are viable for practical tasks on mobile devices, and flagship efficient models like Llama 3.2 (1B/3B), Gemma 3 (270M--27B), and Phi-4 (3.8B--14B) are widely deployed. Frameworks like Meta's ExecuTorch 1.0 (50KB footprint, 12+ hardware backends), llama.cpp (GGUF format for CPU inference), Apple's MLX, and MLC-LLM enable cross-platform deployment. However, aggressive quantization using techniques like AWQ/GPTQ at 4-bit or emerging 2-bit approaches (BitNet) reduces the representational capacity that models need to resist adversarial perturbations. Outlier-handling methods (SmoothQuant, SpinQuant) partially address precision-related robustness loss but add complexity. Organizations deploying compressed models at the edge should evaluate adversarial robustness before and after compression, as a model that resists adversarial examples at FP16 may fail at INT4.

### RISC-V TEEs Emerge as Open Alternative for Edge AI Security

The RISC-V ecosystem is gaining a confidential computing capability relevant to edge AI. GATOR-V (2025) is the first production-grade, upstream-ready TEE for RISC-V, providing hardware-enforced isolation with a standards-compliant trusted OS architecture. Released as open source, GATOR-V demonstrated strong security guarantees with minimal performance overhead across multiple RISC-V platforms. AP-TEE provides a RISC-V counterpart to AMD SEV, Intel TDX, and ARM CCA for confidential VMs. For edge AI, this means that RISC-V-based inference platforms (increasingly common in cost-sensitive IoT deployments) are no longer limited to software-only isolation. By 2026, the IoT semiconductor landscape is shifting toward RISC-V and chiplet-based designs, with security-by-design and hardware roots of trust determining which silicon vendors make procurement shortlists. Penglai has been adopted as the default RISC-V TEE for OS distributions including openEuler and OpenHarmony, and deployed on commercial hardware from Andes and Nuclei -- its Penglai-mini variant targets IoT devices without an MMU, achieving scalable isolation with no additional overhead. ACE (2025) takes a different approach, providing open-source, royalty-free confidential computing for embedded RISC-V with formal verification as a first-class design principle (using Rust and the RefinedRust/Rocq prover), VM-based isolation with a minimal three-call ABI (promote, run, destroy), static PMP-based memory partitioning, local attestation for disconnected systems, and post-quantum cryptography (ML-KEM, SHA-384, AES-GCM-256) -- though network I/O workloads incur up to 50% overhead from bounce buffers. Meanwhile, ARM CCA Realms continue to mature: the CAEC system (accepted at EuroS&P 2026) extends ARM CCA firmware to support Confidential Shared Memory (CSM) between confidential VMs, achieving up to 209x reduction in CPU cycles for inter-realm communication compared to encryption-based mechanisms -- a critical capability for multi-model agentic workflows where isolated models need to exchange inference results securely.

### Shadow AI: On-Device Inference as a CISO Blind Spot

As of April 2026, a growing enterprise security concern is developers and employees running local AI inference on laptops and workstations without organizational oversight. Modern hardware (MacBook Pro with 64GB unified memory, high-end PCs with dedicated NPUs) can run quantized 70B-class models at usable speeds, enabling shadow AI usage that bypasses procurement, legal review, and security controls. Detection signals include .gguf files larger than 2GB, processes like llama.cpp or Ollama, port 11434 activity, and GPU/NPU utilization spikes while offline. Older PyTorch Pickle-based model formats can execute arbitrary code when loaded, and most organizations lack model SBOMs to track which models generated production code. The security gap is compounded by the absence of audit trails when offline inference influences code commits or business decisions. Vitalik Buterin's April 2026 "self-sovereign LLM" architecture illustrates both the capability and the risk: running Qwen3.5 35B locally on an NVIDIA 5090 at 90 tokens/second with bubblewrap sandboxes restricting each agent to whitelisted file paths, and a 2-of-2 human+LLM confirmation rule for all outbound communications -- but Buterin himself cautioned that roughly 15% of community-built skills for popular AI agent repositories contained malicious instructions, underscoring that even security-conscious local deployments face supply chain risks from untrusted agent plugins. Recommended controls include curated internal model hubs with approved versions and pinned hashes, endpoint governance via EDR policies, acceptable use policies that explicitly cover model artifacts and provenance, and sandboxing requirements (bubblewrap, Firejail, or container-based isolation) for any local inference workload.

### Regulatory Pressure Is Increasing

The EU AI Act now mandates adversarial-robustness certification for high-risk edge AI applications (e.g., energy distribution, autonomous vehicles), with full compliance for high-risk systems required by August 2027 and most organizational obligations active by August 2026. The EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) applies to edge AI hardware and software; from 11 September 2026, manufacturers will be legally required to report actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe cybersecurity incidents affecting products with digital elements -- this is the first operational CRA milestone. The CRA reporting obligations are operationally demanding: an early warning must be filed within 24 hours, a full notification with technical details within 72 hours, and a final vulnerability report within 14 days of a corrective measure becoming available (or within one month for severe incidents). These timelines apply to any product with digital elements already on the EU market, and the Single Reporting Platform will be operational by September 2026. Full CRA compliance (secure-by-design, SBOMs, vulnerability management, CE marking, minimum 5-year support periods) becomes mandatory for new products placed on the EU market from 11 December 2027. The CRA mandates security-by-design, software integrity protection, vulnerability disclosure procedures, and security update provision throughout the product lifecycle, making the edge patching crisis described above a direct compliance liability. IEEE P2938 defines an incremental learning protocol with embedded differential-privacy mechanisms, achieving over 70% reduction in gradient-parameter transmission during edge-model updates, with large-scale pilots confirming scalability across millions of endpoints. Kudelski Labs presented at Embedded World 2026 on post-quantum cryptography integration for edge AI device lifecycle security, reflecting growing industry awareness that current cryptographic protections for edge model distribution may not survive quantum computing advances.

## Open Research Questions

- [ ] How effective are current anti-tampering techniques (obfuscation, integrity checks) against determined attackers with physical access to edge AI devices? (Frida-based runtime extraction from production apps demonstrates that static encryption alone is insufficient -- what combination of runtime protections raises the bar meaningfully?)
- [ ] What are the practical tradeoffs between model protection strength and inference latency on resource-constrained edge devices? (TZ-LLM and FlexServe demonstrate that TEE-based LLM inference is feasible with 4--25% overhead, but multi-model agentic workflows and CCA Realm transitions add complexity that is not yet well characterized.)
- [ ] How can federated learning aggregation be made robust against adaptive adversaries that modify their strategy based on observed aggregation behavior? (PoisonedFL defeats eight defenses; newer approaches like DAARA and FedVoD shift to client-level divergence analysis and dual-voting, but the arms race continues with adversaries adapting to each new defense signal.)
- [ ] What is the minimum hardware security capability (TPM, TEE, secure enclave) needed to meaningfully protect model weights on edge devices? (ARM CCA Realms offer a promising standardization path on Armv9-A, but adoption across edge silicon vendors is uncertain, and the TrustZone/Secure Enclave/SGX/Nitro fragmentation persists for current-generation devices.)
- [ ] How should model update delivery be secured for edge devices with intermittent connectivity and limited bandwidth? (IEEE P2938 addresses bandwidth reduction; CRA compliance adds integrity requirements.)
- [ ] Can EM side-channel attacks (CVE-2025-23270 class) be mitigated on commodity edge AI hardware without prohibitive cost or performance overhead? What role does TVLA testing play in edge AI security validation?
- [ ] How should on-device LLM security differ from traditional edge classifier security, given the dramatically larger model sizes, higher IP value, and richer interaction surfaces (prompt injection, data exfiltration through model interactions)?
- [ ] What security architecture is appropriate for agentic edge AI systems (autonomous vehicles, robots) where perception-layer attacks (sensor spoofing, adversarial physical objects) can cause physical harm?
- [ ] How effective are model watermarking and fingerprinting techniques for detecting unauthorized deployment of extracted edge AI models, and can they survive common post-extraction transformations (pruning, quantization, fine-tuning)? (The University of Edinburgh's largest independent evaluation of AI fingerprinting -- 12 generators, 14 methods, presented at IEEE SaTML 2026 -- found that fingerprints can be removed in over 80% of cases with full-access attacks and over 50% with zero-knowledge attacks, with no visible trace. Fingerprints can also be forged to falsely implicate other models. The researchers recommend combining fingerprinting with watermarking, but acknowledge the two techniques address fundamentally different problems.)
- [ ] As automated AI vulnerability research tools compress the exploit development timeline from weeks to hours for edge devices, how should patch delivery and secure OTA cadences adapt for devices with intermittent connectivity?
- [ ] Given the demonstrated fundamental vulnerability in partial TEE-shielded inference using precomputed noise bases, can any performance-optimized TEE strategy provide meaningful model confidentiality, or is full model encapsulation within the TEE the only viable path despite its performance costs?
- [ ] How should the tension between secure aggregation (hiding updates for privacy) and poisoning defense (inspecting updates for integrity) be resolved in federated learning systems? Do FHE-based dual-defense approaches scale to production deployments?
- [ ] What post-quantum cryptographic primitives are practical for edge AI model distribution and device authentication, given the constrained compute and power budgets of edge hardware? (Corvex's use of ML-KEM/Kyber-768 for GPU-level key exchange demonstrates feasibility at the data center edge, but mobile and IoT endpoints face tighter constraints.)
- [ ] How should prompt injection defenses for on-device LLMs differ from cloud-hosted LLMs, given that local models may have direct access to device sensors, contacts, location, and local files without the network-level monitoring available in cloud deployments?
- [ ] As ransomware operators increasingly target connected vehicles and smart mobility systems (44% of automotive cyber incidents in 2025), what security architectures can ensure that edge AI inference continues safely during an active ransomware attack on telematics infrastructure?
- [ ] How does aggressive model compression (INT4/INT2 quantization, structured pruning) affect adversarial robustness of edge-deployed models, and should security testing be mandatory on the compressed artifact rather than the original model?
- [ ] Can RISC-V TEE implementations (GATOR-V, AP-TEE) achieve security parity with ARM TrustZone/CCA for edge AI workloads, and what are the implications of open-source TEE designs for auditability versus proprietary attack surface reduction?
- [ ] How should edge AI deployments defend against traffic analysis side channels (Whisper Leak class) when models must communicate with cloud infrastructure, given that proposed mitigations (padding, batching, injection) individually provide incomplete protection?
- [ ] Can sampling-based verifiable inference protocols (SaTML 2026) provide meaningful integrity guarantees for edge AI deployments in regulated industries, or do high-risk applications (medical devices, autonomous vehicles) require full cryptographic proof systems despite their prohibitive overhead?
- [ ] Given that 29% of edge device vulnerabilities are now exploited on or before disclosure day and median remediation takes 270+ days, should edge AI security architectures fundamentally shift from patch-based defense to runtime constraint models that contain exploitation regardless of patching status?
- [ ] How should edge AI fleets detect and disrupt P2P/DHT command-and-control channels like KadNap when malicious peer discovery is intentionally blended with legitimate decentralized traffic and devices cannot run full endpoint agents?
- [ ] How should enterprises manage shadow AI risk from developers running local LLM inference on workstations (quantized 70B-class models on commodity hardware) without organizational oversight, given that Pickle-format models can execute arbitrary code on load and no audit trail exists for locally generated outputs?
- [ ] Can Zero-Trust Foundation Model (ZTFM) architectures -- embedding continuous verification, least-privilege access, and behavioral analytics into the model lifecycle -- scale to resource-constrained edge IoT devices while maintaining sub-second inference latency?
- [ ] As AI orchestration frameworks (agent builders, RAG pipelines, tool-use coordinators) become as attack-critical as traditional web servers -- with CVE-2025-59528 (Flowise, CVSS 10.0) and CVE-2026-26030 (Semantic Kernel, CVSS 9.8) demonstrating maximum-severity RCE in production -- should edge-deployed AI agent orchestrators require the same hardening cadence and vulnerability management as public-facing web applications?
