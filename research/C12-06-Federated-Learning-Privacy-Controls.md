# C12.6: Federated Learning with Privacy Controls

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-16

## Purpose

Apply differential privacy and poisoning-resistant aggregation to federated learning (FL) to protect individual participant data. FL avoids centralizing raw data, but gradient updates still leak sensitive information. Zhu et al. (2019) demonstrated pixel-accurate reconstruction of training images from shared gradients; Deng et al. (2021) showed text data can be recovered from BERT-style gradients. Research has accelerated since: the 2025 COLING paper "Exposing Text Data through Discrete Optimization" pushed text reconstruction exact-match rates up by 39% on TinyBERT-6, 20% on BERT-base, and 15% on BERT-large over prior baselines, and the ICCV 2025 paper "Geminio: Language-Guided Gradient Inversion Attacks" introduced attacks that use natural-language prompts to target specific content in gradients. MMGIA (IJCAI 2025) exploits intermodal correlations to invert gradients in multimodal FL, widening the attack surface beyond single-modality classifiers. A 2025 medical-imaging study on retinal federated learning reconstructed images well enough to re-identify 92% of participants — a useful reality check for "FL is private by design" claims.

Google's Gboard remains the canonical production reference: as of 2024-2025, all production Gboard neural language models trained on user data ship with formal DP guarantees via DP-FTRL and its successor BLT-DP-FTRL, with more than twenty deployed models achieving ρ-zCDP in the range ρ ∈ (0.2, 2) and two additionally using secure aggregation. Apple's Private Cloud Compute (PCC) takes a different path: end-to-end encrypted on-device-to-enclave inference on Apple Silicon with verifiable transparency, no persistent storage, and stateless computation — sidestepping gradient sharing entirely rather than hardening it. NIST SP 800-226 "Guidelines for Evaluating Differential Privacy Guarantees" was finalized in March 2025 and is now the baseline reference for evaluating ε claims, including for privacy-preserving machine learning; it introduces the "DP pyramid" and a catalog of privacy hazards that practitioners commonly miss. Federated Unlearning (FUL) has matured into a recognized subfield (see the 2024-2025 ACM Computing Surveys review and the PoPETs 2025 paper on unlearning clients, features, and samples in vertical FL) but production tooling for certified client removal remains thin.

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| **12.6.1** | **Verify that** client updates employ local differential privacy noise addition before aggregation. | 1 | Gradient inversion attacks reconstructing training data from gradient updates. Zhu et al. (2019) demonstrated gradient-inverted image reconstruction; Deng et al. (2021) extended this to text. The 2025 COLING "Discrete Optimization" attack raised text exact-match recovery by 15-39% across BERT variants. ICCV 2025 "Geminio" introduced language-guided gradient inversion that lets an attacker steer reconstruction toward specific content. MMGIA (IJCAI 2025) exploits intermodal correlation in multimodal FL. A 2025 retinal-image FL study re-identified 92% of participants from inverted gradients. Maps to MITRE ATLAS AML.T0024 (Exfiltration via ML Inference API) and adjacent membership-inference techniques. | Inspect client-side code for DP noise injection (Gaussian or Laplacian) applied to clipped gradients before transmission. Verify noise calibration matches declared epsilon using the privacy-accountant output (RDP, zCDP, or PLD). Capture client updates with and without noise and confirm statistical indistinguishability at the claimed level. Reproduce a public gradient-inversion attack (e.g., DLG, iDLG, or the 2025 Geminio code) against the noised updates and confirm reconstruction quality is below a documented threshold. Reference Google's distributed DP deployment for Gboard language models as a production benchmark. | Local DP adds more noise per update than central DP, so maintaining utility requires many more participants. Noise calibration depends on the gradient-clipping bound, which trades off privacy and convergence — too-aggressive clipping harms utility, too-loose clipping weakens privacy. Flower's 2026 release (Feb 7, 2026) includes fixed and adaptive clipping DP strategies on both server and client sides; Opacus (Fast Gradient Clipping and Ghost Clipping, Aug 2024; LoRA+peft+DP-SGD tutorial, Dec 2024) reduces DP-SGD memory cost and integrates cleanly with Flower for sample-level DP. TensorFlow Federated, NVIDIA FLARE (differential privacy + homomorphic encryption + PSI), and PySyft are the other major frameworks. Apple's PCC avoids the gradient-sharing problem entirely via encrypted on-device-to-enclave inference. |
| **12.6.2** | **Verify that** training metrics are differentially private and never reveal single-client loss. | 2 | Per-client loss and metric leakage enabling membership inference and property inference. Melis et al. (2019) showed unprotected FL metrics reveal property-level information about participants' data; aggregated-but-unnoised metrics still leak information about outlier clients. Operations dashboards, A/B logs, and model-quality telemetry are common leak points overlooked by teams that only protected model parameters. | Review the metric-aggregation pipeline and confirm DP noise is applied before reporting. Attempt to isolate single-client contributions from aggregated metrics using known outlier or canary clients and confirm their influence is masked. Audit metric dashboards and logging systems for per-client loss values, per-client confusion matrices, or per-client sample counts. For stronger protection, verify a secure-aggregation protocol (Bonawitz et al. 2017 SecAgg or a 2024-2025 successor such as the single-setup SecAgg with forward/backward secrecy in IACR ePrint 2024/1655 and arXiv 2502.08989) hides per-client contributions even from the server. | Noisy metrics make debugging harder — teams need to balance observability with privacy, typically by running a trusted eval set in the clear on the server. Secure aggregation protocols add cryptographic overhead and client-dropout handling complexity; the Bonawitz design requires a per-round key-setup phase that dominates cost at scale. Forward-/backward-secret single-setup variants (2025) address this but are not yet default in mainstream FL frameworks. Metric privacy is still widely overlooked in FL deployments. |
| **12.6.3** | **Verify that** poisoning-resistant aggregation (e.g., Krum/Trimmed-Mean) is enabled. | 2 | Byzantine and targeted poisoning by malicious clients. Blanchard et al. (2017) showed a single malicious participant can hijack FedAvg. Fang et al. (USENIX Security 2020) demonstrated local model-poisoning attacks that bypass Krum, Trimmed-Mean, Median, and Bulyan — the Krum-attack is now a standard adversarial baseline. NDSS 2025 "Do We Really Need to Design New Byzantine-Robust Aggregation Rules?" argues many new defenses do not outperform well-tuned classical ones. Adaptive adversaries (IACR ePrint 2025/510 survey) remain an open problem. Maps to MITRE ATLAS AML.T0020 (Poison Training Data) and backdoor-implantation techniques. | Review the aggregation configuration for robust methods (Multi-Krum, Trimmed-Mean, coordinate-wise median, Bulyan, FLTrust, or newer loss-based methods such as FedGreed, arXiv 2508.18060). Run a poisoning test harness: simulate 10-30% Byzantine clients submitting adversarially-crafted updates and confirm the aggregator resists accuracy degradation and backdoor implantation. Measure main-task and backdoor-task accuracy with and without defense. Reference benchmark studies such as the autonomous-vehicle FL poisoning benchmark (ScienceDirect 2024) for expected numbers. | No single robust rule defends against all attack types. Krum is effective against Byzantine failures but converges slowly and is vulnerable to the Krum-attack. Trimmed-Mean assumes a known bad-client fraction. GAN-based defenses (arXiv 2503.20884) retain accuracy under heavy adversarial presence where statistical defenses collapse. FedGreed uses a server-side trusted eval set — useful when a clean probe is available. Defense choice must be informed by the threat model: honest-but-curious vs. actively malicious, static vs. adaptive, collusion scale. Adaptive attackers remain an open problem. |
| **12.6.4** | **Verify that** formal proofs demonstrate overall ε budget with less than 5% utility loss. | 3 | Excessive utility loss from privacy mechanisms incentivizing teams to weaken or disable them. Without a formal analysis that ties ε to measured utility, DP settings tend to drift toward looser values over time. This is also the requirement that surfaces epsilon-inflation bugs — naive composition, missed sub-sampling amplification, and double-counted queries all produce falsely small reported ε. | Request a formal privacy analysis with a utility-impact assessment. Verify ε is computed using a tight accountant — Rényi DP (RDP), zero-concentrated DP (zCDP), Gaussian DP, or Privacy Loss Distributions (PLD) — not naive composition. Reproduce the accountant run from source if possible. Compare model accuracy (task-specific metrics: F1, AUC, perplexity, BLEU, NDCG) between DP and non-DP runs and confirm utility loss is within the threshold. Walk through NIST SP 800-226 (finalized March 2025) and its accompanying Jupyter notebooks as the structured evaluation baseline. Cross-check against Google's Gboard production numbers (ρ-zCDP with ρ ∈ (0.2, 2), dozens of models in production as of 2024-2025) for expected privacy-utility frontiers on language tasks. | The 5% threshold is application-dependent: safety-critical models may not tolerate 1%, recommender systems can tolerate 10%+. Tight accountants (RDP, Gaussian DP, PLD) can reduce reported ε by an order of magnitude vs. naive composition. DP-FTRL, BLT-DP-FTRL, and DP-Adam optimizers improve the frontier; large-batch training and synthetic-data pretraining (as in Google's Gboard 2024-2025 work) push it further. For vertical FL and feature/sample-level unlearning, PoPETs 2025 (popets-2025-0048) gives evaluation methodology. Honest reporting: many public "ε = 1" claims come from loose composition — require auditability of the accounting, not just the final number. EU AI Act high-risk systems (Annex III, applicable August 2, 2026) trigger both an FRIA (Article 27) and a GDPR DPIA (Article 35) on any personal-data processing in federated training. |

## Tooling

- **FL frameworks:** Flower (flwr, Feb 2026 release; fixed + adaptive DP clipping, message-based FedAvg/FedOpt/FedAdam/FedAdagrad/FedYogi with DP), TensorFlow Federated, NVIDIA FLARE (DP + HE + PSI, 2025 biopharma deployments documented in J. Biopharm Stat.), PySyft (OpenMined), FedML.
- **DP libraries:** Opacus (Meta-PyTorch, Fast Gradient Clipping and Ghost Clipping reduce DP-SGD memory; LoRA+peft+DP-SGD tutorial Dec 2024), TensorFlow Privacy 0.12.3 (per-example gradient clipping in Keras), Google's DP library, OpenMined PyDP.
- **Secure aggregation:** Original Bonawitz SecAgg (2017), single-setup SecAgg with forward/backward secrecy (arXiv 2502.08989, 2025), verifiable SecAgg variants (ScienceDirect 2025).
- **Robust aggregation:** Multi-Krum, Trimmed-Mean, coordinate-wise median, Bulyan, FLTrust, FedGreed (arXiv 2508.18060), GAN-based defenses (arXiv 2503.20884).
- **Privacy-preserving inference alternatives:** Apple Private Cloud Compute (Apple Silicon + Secure Enclave + verifiable transparency + stateless computation), Azure Confidential Computing, AWS Nitro Enclaves, GCP Confidential Space, GPU-accelerated FHE for LLM inference (OpenFHE — as of 2026, practical for medium-sized models or short sequences, still far slower than cleartext).
- **Federated unlearning:** Still mostly research-stage — see ACM Computing Surveys (2024) and PoPETs 2025 for vertical-FL client/feature/sample removal. Certified unlearning for federated recommendation available (ACM TOIS 2025), but no widely-adopted production library yet.
