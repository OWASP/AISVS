# C3.2: Model Validation & Testing

> [Back to C03 Index](C03-Model-Lifecycle-Management.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-15 (refreshed)

## Purpose

Models must pass defined security and safety validations before deployment. This section covers the pre-deployment testing gates, audit logging of model changes, override processes, and the re-evaluation requirements for models that undergo post-training compression (quantization, pruning, distillation).

Pre-deployment testing is the primary defense against deploying models vulnerable to prompt injection, jailbreaks, or models that produce unsafe outputs. Despite this, Cleanlab's 2025 production survey found only 5% of AI agents in production have mature monitoring -- underscoring the critical need for automated testing gates before models ever reach production. Post-training compression techniques, increasingly common for edge deployment and cost reduction, can silently degrade safety alignment and adversarial robustness, requiring distinct re-evaluation of compressed artifacts. As of April 2026, the tooling ecosystem has matured considerably -- Palo Alto Networks' Prisma AIRS, Giskard v3, and Deepchecks all offer CI/CD-integrated model scanning, and the OpenSSF MLSecOps whitepaper (August 2025) provides a practical framework for building robust validation pipelines. The EU AI Act's conformity assessment provisions (effective August 2025) now compel organizations deploying high-risk systems to demonstrate pre-deployment safety evaluations with documented evidence -- with full compliance for high-risk AI systems required by August 2, 2026, carrying penalties up to 35 million EUR or 7% of global annual turnover.

The Continuous Automated Red Teaming (CART) approach has gained traction in 2026, with platforms like Mindgard (recognized in the OWASP LLM Security Solutions Landscape Guide and winner of the 2025 Cybersecurity Excellence Award) integrating directly into CI/CD pipelines via GitHub Actions or CLI, running adversarial tests on every commit. Open-source alternatives have also matured -- Garak (prompt injection, data leakage, adversarial prompt testing), PyRIT (Microsoft's Python Risk Identification Tool), and Promptfoo (used by 30,000+ developers for LLM security testing) all support pipeline integration. IBM's 2025 Cost of a Data Breach Report found 63% of breached organizations lacked AI governance policies at the time of their breach, and shadow AI usage (unauthorized tools deployed by ~20% of employees) cost organizations an average of $670,000 more per breach than other incidents.

| **3.2.5** | **Verify that** all model changes (deployment, configuration, retirement) generate immutable audit records including a timestamp, an authenticated actor identity, a change type, and before/after states, with trace metadata (environment and consuming services/agents) and a model identifier (version/digest/signature). | 2 | Unauthorized model changes going undetected; inability to reconstruct incident timelines; compliance failures under EU AI Act conformity assessment; repudiation of destructive actions (e.g., unauthorized model retirement). Verify logs contain all required fields (timestamp, actor, change type, before/after states, environment, model version/digest) by inspecting sample entries. Test immutability by attempting to modify or delete a log entry. Confirm logs capture all change types: deployment, configuration change, retirement, and rollback. Tools: AWS CloudTrail, GCP Cloud Audit Logs, Azure Monitor, MLflow Model Registry (webhook-based event logging with Unity Catalog for centralized governance and cross-workspace lineage tracking), or Databricks Model Registry. | **3.2.6** | **Verify that** validation failures automatically block model deployment unless an explicit override approval from pre-designated authorized personnel with documented business justifications. | 3 | Deploying models that failed safety or security validation; normalization of overriding gates without accountability; audit trail gaps for exception handling; compliance violations when bypassed deployments cause incidents. Confirm override approvers cannot self-approve. Tools: GitHub branch protection with required reviewers, DataRobot MLOps approval workflows, Azure DevOps pipeline approvals, OPA Gatekeeper admission controllers for Kubernetes-based model serving, or MLflow Model Registry webhook-triggered approval gates. Inspect override audit logs for completeness. Verify that pre-production policy gates define acceptable thresholds for hallucination rates, model drift, and compliance requirements as part of the governance framework. | Requires tight CI/CD integration with approval workflows. DataRobot and Vertex AI Model Registry both support configurable approval workflows with audit logging. The OpenSSF MLSecOps whitepaper recommends a maturity ladder from manual checklists to policy-as-code enforcement. Most organizations still lack formal override processes for ML pipelines specifically -- traditional change management (ServiceNow, PagerDuty) can be adapted but requires ML-specific policy configuration. The EU AI Act full compliance deadline of August 2, 2026 adds urgency -- penalties for non-compliance with high-risk system requirements can reach 35 million EUR or 7% of global annual turnover. *(Updated 2026-04)* |
| **3.2.7** | **Verify that** models subjected to post-training quantization, pruning, or distillation are re-evaluated against the same safety and alignment test suite on the compressed artifact before deployment, and that evaluation results are retained as distinct records linked to the compressed artifact's version or digest. | 2 | Safety alignment degradation after compression; adversarial robustness loss in quantized/pruned models; deploying compressed models that no longer satisfy safety thresholds established for the uncompressed version. MITRE ATLAS [AML.T0043](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0043) (Craft Adversarial Data) -- compressed models may be more susceptible to adversarial examples. Research from January 2026 ([arXiv:2601.12033](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.12033)) demonstrates that non-English safety is particularly vulnerable to quantization, with Korean and Arabic showing substantial safety declines. | Run the full safety/alignment test suite against the compressed artifact and compare results to the uncompressed baseline. Specifically evaluate with SafetyBench (multiple-choice safety questions), TruthfulQA, Do-Not-Answer, HEx-PHI (attack success rates), MultiJail (multilingual safety across English, Korean, Arabic), and OpenSafetyMini (1,067-question benchmark for distinguishing quantization-method safety impacts). For fairness evaluation, include StereoSet, CrowS-Pair, Jigsaw, and MBBQ (4-language bias benchmark). Verify that evaluation records are stored with a distinct version/digest identifier for the compressed model. Confirm CI/CD gates enforce re-evaluation before compressed models reach production. Check that hybrid compression pipelines (prune-then-quantize) trigger re-evaluation at each compression stage. | Research from February 2025 (arXiv:2502.15799) shows 4-bit quantization generally maintains safety, but 2-bit precision introduces pronounced safety declines -- particularly for certain architectures. A Nature study (2026) on phase transitions in LLM compression found that performance degradation follows a pattern of gradual decline followed by abrupt catastrophic collapse once a critical compression threshold is reached -- 4-bit achieves 43.1% size reduction with only 0.51% perplexity loss, but 2-bit causes a steep 7.94% accuracy drop. Dynamic quantization methods (FP8, LLM.int8()) demonstrate the best safety/fairness preservation, while static methods (GPTQ, AWQ) lack consistency. Critical Weight Protection ([arXiv:2601.12033](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.12033)) is a promising technique that identifies safety-critical weights via sensitivity scoring and preserves them in FP16 while quantizing the remainder to INT4 -- avoiding expensive retraining. Larger models (70B+) exhibit more consistent behavior across quantization methods; smaller models show greater divergence in safety performance. Vector quantization methods (AQLM) demonstrate superior stability compared to scalar approaches at extreme compression. The NSA/CISA advisory (March 2026) recommends regular testing as a key mitigation. Edge-specific and modality-aware safety benchmarks remain gaps -- most benchmarks focus on text-only LLMs. *(Updated 2026-04)* |
