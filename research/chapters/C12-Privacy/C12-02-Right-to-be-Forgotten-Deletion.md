# C12.2: Right-to-be-Forgotten & Deletion Enforcement

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-05-01 (rev 3)

## Purpose

Ensure data-subject deletion requests propagate across all AI artifacts — raw datasets, feature stores, embeddings, checkpoints, logs, and backups — and that model unlearning is demonstrably verifiable rather than a cosmetic output-suppression exercise. The EDPB formally adopted its 2025 Coordinated Enforcement Framework (CEF) report on Article 17 on 18 February 2026, after examining 764 controllers across 32 DPAs throughout 2025. The report flagged seven systemic gaps: (1) absent internal procedures, (2) insufficient information to data subjects, (3) ineffective anonymization used as a substitute for erasure, (4) inconsistent implementation, (5) unclear retention periods, (6) backup-system erasure failures, and (7) flawed balancing of Article 17(3) exceptions. Nine DPAs opened formal investigations; follow-through supervision in Ireland, France, Portugal, Slovenia, and Germany runs through 2026. CNIL, Portugal's CNPD, and Sweden's IMY have already indicated CEF findings will drive sector-specific inspections this year. The follow-up CEF for 2026, announced 14 October 2025, shifts focus to GDPR Articles 12–14 (transparency and information obligations) — relevant here because controllers must clearly tell data subjects how the right to erasure applies to AI-derived artifacts, what the realistic propagation SLA looks like, and what happens to model weights. Sitting underneath the CEF is **EDPB Opinion 28/2024 (17 December 2024)**, which DPAs are now applying in the field: an AI model trained on personal data is **not automatically anonymous**, anonymity must be assessed case-by-case, and where unlawful processing occurred during development the worst-case remedy is "erasure of the whole dataset used to develop the AI model and/or the AI model itself." That language is the doctrinal hook for every model-deletion order DPAs have issued since.

Enforcement around AI deletion gained three notable case studies in the spring of 2026. On **11 March 2026**, the FTC published its AI Policy Statement — the first federal AI enforcement framework — interpreting Section 5 of the FTC Act as applying across the AI lifecycle and naming model deletion as the flagship remedy. The statement enters a 2026 warning phase (consent orders, guidance letters) and turns into full enforcement in 2027 at $53,088 per violation; for an AI agent making thousands of automated decisions per day, every output is potentially a separate violation. On **18 March 2026**, the Court of Rome annulled the €15 million fine the Italian Garante had imposed on OpenAI in November 2024 for ChatGPT-related GDPR violations — a setback for direct erasure enforcement against foundation-model providers, though the full reasoning has not yet been published and the Garante is weighing an appeal. Three weeks later, the **FTC's OkCupid–Clarifai consent order** (announced 30 March 2026; Clarifai certified deletion to the FTC on 7 April 2026 and notified Rep. Lori Trahan's office on 16 April) required Clarifai to delete the three million OkCupid user photos plus *any models trained on them*. This is the practical playbook for **algorithmic disgorgement**: deletion of data plus deletion of derived models is now a regulator-backed remedy in the United States, even where Section 5 of the FTC Act provides no fining authority — and after the March policy statement, that posture is no longer case-by-case but written into FTC enforcement doctrine. The 20-year prohibition on misrepresenting data practices that came with the consent order signals that "we couldn't possibly delete the model" arguments are losing rhetorical ground.

The U.S. state-level deletion stack also matured sharply this year. California's **Delete Request and Opt-out Platform (DROP)** opened to consumers on **1 January 2026** under SB 362 (Delete Act); the CPPA reported **18,000 requests in the first 48 hours** and more than **242,000 requests** by April. Data brokers must access DROP at least every 45 days starting **1 August 2026** and process verified deletion requests on the same 45-day cycle, *including newly collected data about consumers who have already submitted requests*. Penalties are **$200 per unfulfilled request per day** with **no cure period**, and DSAR logs must be retained for at least 24 months as objective audit evidence. The Delete Act also requires brokers to report whether they sold or shared data with **generative AI developers** in the prior year — the first U.S. statutory hook tying broker-to-AI data flows to deletion obligations. The CPPA's new Data Broker Strike Force has already issued 2026 enforcement decisions ($45,000 fine against Rickenbacher Data, $62,600 against S&P Global) for registration failures, signaling the agency will not wait until August to start moving on the Delete Act. Separately, on **22 April 2026**, amended **COPPA** rules took effect requiring separate parental consent before children's data can be used for AI training — and when consent is revoked, deletion must propagate through backups, cached versions, and any derived datasets. In Europe, the **EU Digital Omnibus** trilogue intended to defer the 2 August 2026 high-risk AI Act compliance deadline to 2 December 2027 collapsed on **28 April 2026** without agreement; a follow-up trilogue under the Cypriot Presidency is scheduled for around **13 May 2026**. Until that resolves, controllers should plan as if Articles 9–17 (provider obligations, including data governance under Article 10) and Article 26 (deployer obligations) bind on 2 August 2026 — adding a second clock to the GDPR Article 17 timeline that already sits on top of every AI deployment.

Machine unlearning itself is in the middle of a credibility reset. The ICLR 2025 paper from Hu et al. (CMU) showed that approximate unlearning methods largely **obfuscate** rather than forget — a "benign relearning attack" using small amounts of loosely related public data can reverse the unlearning effect. The most widely reported example: an LLM that had been "unlearned" of biohazard knowledge regained the ability to produce harmful bioweapon text after a brief fine-tune on publicly available medical articles. CMU's follow-up SaTML '25 position paper ("LLM Unlearning Benchmarks are Weak Measures of Progress") went further: a survey of 72 unlearning papers found that the dominant benchmarks (TOFU, WMDP) test forget and retain queries independently and miss dependencies between them, leaving room for methods to overfit to test queries while leaving the underlying knowledge intact. The team released *swapped WMDP* and *paired TOFU* on Hugging Face as harder evaluation sets. Newer 2026 attack literature has compounded the problem along three axes: ("Stealthy MLLM Unlearning Attack", "Step-by-Step Reasoning Attack", "Detecting Unlearning Traces in LLMs from Model Outputs") shows that adversaries can both recover unlearned content via chain-of-thought prompting and detect that unlearning happened — useful for membership-inference-style audits. The **ROKA paper** (Choi et al., arxiv:2603.00436, 28 February 2026) introduced the *indirect unlearning attack*: an adversary requests deletion of one record specifically to degrade a model's performance on a different security-critical task, exploiting the **knowledge contamination** that conventional unlearning methods inflict on conceptually related neighbors. ROKA itself proposes a "Neural Healing" defense that re-allocates contributions to nullify forgotten data while reinforcing related knowledge — the first method offering theoretical guarantees for knowledge preservation during unlearning. The companion "Attack by Unlearning" paper (arxiv:2603.18570, 19 March 2026) extends this to graph neural networks: an adversary injects carefully crafted nodes into the training graph and later issues legally mandated deletion requests, producing an attack surface that cannot be refused without violating data-subject rights.

The 2026 evaluation literature has begun to catch up. **"LLM Unlearning Under the Microscope: A Full-Stack View on Methods and Metrics"** (arxiv:2510.07626) gives the first principled taxonomy of twelve stateful unlearning methods across three families — divergence-driven optimization (NPO, SimNPO), representation misalignment (RMU and variants), and rejection-based targeted unlearning (IDK) — and introduces **Open-QA metrics** that better capture generative performance and surface the unlearning-effectiveness vs. utility-retention tradeoff that WMDP and TOFU tend to mask. **"LLM Unlearning with LLM Beliefs"** (arxiv:2510.19422) reframes the objective around the model's own belief distribution rather than a fixed forget set, addressing the case where the forget set is incomplete or noisy. **"LLM Unlearning on Noisy Forget Sets"** (arxiv:2510.09007) studies the realistic case where the deletion request itself is incomplete, rewritten, or watermarked. ICLR 2026 also delivered **RASLIK** (Randomized Antipodal Search on Linearized Influence Kernel), which reframes unlearning as a **data-selection** problem: hashing-based gradient sketches identify both forget-aligned and retain-aligned samples in sublinear time, shifting the Pareto frontier outward beyond oracle sampling. The combined effect: the field has pushed toward certified unlearning, SAM-based robust unlearning (Fan et al., ICML 2025), Neural-Healing-style contribution re-allocation (Choi et al., 2026), influence-kernel data selection (RASLIK, ICLR 2026), and retraining-style approaches for anything that must stand up to regulator scrutiny — while assuming that any unlearning claim will be tested by benign-relearning, reasoning-chain, stealthy fine-tune, **and** indirect-attack adversaries who weaponize the deletion right itself.

| **12.2.1** | **Verify that** data-subject deletion requests propagate to AI-derived artifacts including training and fine-tuning datasets, model checkpoints, evaluation sets, derived caches, and feature stores within a service level agreement of less than 30 days. Embedding and RAG index propagation is governed by C8.3. | 1 | Incomplete propagation: a deletion request is honored in the primary database but the same subject's data still lives in pre-tokenized training shards, fine-tuning corpora, model checkpoints used for ablations, evaluation/probe sets, derived caches (logits caches, KV-caches, distillation outputs), and the feature store. The EDPB CEF report (18 Feb 2026, 764 controllers, 32 DPAs) named **backup erasure failures** and **anonymization-as-substitute** as two of the top-seven systemic gaps in Article 17 implementation, and **EDPB Opinion 28/2024** (17 Dec 2024) explicitly contemplates erasure of the trained model itself as a remedy where development was unlawful. The FTC's OkCupid–Clarifai consent order (March–April 2026) made the algorithmic-disgorgement variant explicit: regulators can require deletion of derived models, not just the source data. The **FTC AI Policy Statement of 11 March 2026** wrote that posture into doctrine, naming model deletion as the flagship Section 5 remedy across the AI lifecycle (warning phase 2026, fines of $53,088 per violation from 2027 — and "every automated decision is potentially a separate violation" for high-throughput agents). U.S. state-level deletion clocks are tightening in parallel: **California's Delete Act / DROP** (consumer launch 1 Jan 2026; broker enforcement 1 Aug 2026) imposes a 45-day processing cycle, **$200/day per unfulfilled request, no cure period**, and a statutory hook requiring brokers to declare data flows to **generative AI developers** — and the **amended COPPA** rules (effective 22 Apr 2026) require deletion to propagate through backups, cached versions, and derived datasets when a parent revokes consent for a child's data used in AI training. Failure mode aligns with OWASP LLM02:2025 (Sensitive Information Disclosure) when residual training fragments resurface in generated text. | Auditor walks the data-flow diagram: every system that ingests subject data should appear in a deletion DAG. Trigger a synthetic deletion request and trace it through (a) primary OLTP, (b) data lake / Parquet shards, (c) feature store (Tecton, Feast, Databricks Feature Store), (d) training-data versioning system (DVC, LakeFS, Pachyderm), (e) checkpoint registry (MLflow, Weights & Biases, Vertex AI Model Registry), (f) evaluation sets, (g) derived caches and logs, (h) backups, (i) Kafka / event-stream topics where subject events were published. Confirm completion within the 30-day SLA via tamper-evident audit logs (CloudTrail + Object Lock, Sigstore/Rekor, Azure immutable storage). For backup-resident data, confirm the team uses crypto-shredding — per-subject DEK wrapped by KEK in AWS KMS / GCP KMS / Azure Key Vault so destroying the key renders the subject's bytes unrecoverable even under WORM (the 2026 OneUptime/Conduktor patterns extend the same envelope-encryption model into Kafka topics, Cloud Storage, and AI training shards, which is what regulators are starting to expect for backup parity). Cross-check the deletion DAG against the controller's Article 30 record of processing — gaps there are the most common failure mode the EDPB flagged. For California-regulated brokers, also confirm the DSAR pipeline retains evidence logs for **at least 24 months**, integrates with **DROP** on the 45-day cycle, and includes the **post-deletion re-collection sweep** (newly collected data about a previously-deleted consumer must be deleted on the same cadence). | Vector-store and RAG-index propagation is intentionally scoped to C8.3 — auditors should expect to look there for embedding deletion. The 30-day SLA aligns with GDPR's "without undue delay" reading the EDPB has consistently used; California's Delete Act sets a tighter 45-day cycle but tied to broker re-polling rather than the original request, so the SLAs are not directly comparable and controllers running under both regimes need to track them separately. Deletion orchestration tooling (BigID, OneTrust, Securiti, DataGrail, Collibra) covers people-data inventories well but coverage of feature stores, checkpoint registries, and ML training shards is uneven — most controllers still rely on bespoke scripts. Honest gap: there is no industry-standard automated test for "did this deletion actually clear the model checkpoint that was used to fine-tune the production model three releases ago?" — most teams document the chain manually and rely on lineage tooling (MLflow lineage, lakeFS, DVC, Pachyderm, the AWS SageMaker MLflow + DVC reference architecture published April 2026) for evidence. The **EU Digital Omnibus trilogue collapsed on 28 April 2026** without agreement on deferring the AI Act high-risk deadline; absent a deal at the ~13 May 2026 follow-up, controllers should plan as if Article 10 (data governance) and the rest of the high-risk obligations bind on **2 August 2026**, layered on top of the GDPR Article 17 timeline. |
| **12.2.2** | **Verify that** shadow-model or membership-inference evaluation demonstrates that forgotten records influence less than a documented policy threshold of model outputs after unlearning. | 2 | Residual memorization of deleted data in model weights. Even after the raw records are gone, a model can continue producing outputs shaped by the removed data, violating the substance (not just the form) of the erasure right. Hu et al. (ICLR 2025, "Unlearning or Obfuscating? Jogging the Memory of Unlearned LLMs via Benign Relearning") established this as a systemic vulnerability: fine-tuning an unlearned model on small amounts of loosely related public data reliably recovers the "forgotten" knowledge. Their case studies included restoring bioweapon knowledge from unlearned LLMs via public medical articles and recovering verbatim Harry Potter passages via Wikipedia summaries. Membership-inference attacks (Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022) further show that weak unlearning leaves a detectable signal that a target record was in the training set. The 2026 attack literature ("Stealthy MLLM Unlearning Attack", "Step-by-Step Reasoning Attack: Revealing 'Erased' Knowledge in Large Language Models", "Detecting Unlearning Traces in LLMs from Model Outputs", and **"Keeping an Eye on LLM Unlearning: The Hidden Risk and Remedy"** arxiv:2506.00359 which introduces a Stealthy Attack against fine-tuning-based unlearning) shows that chain-of-thought prompting can recover unlearned content even when single-shot probes fail, that the very fact that unlearning happened is detectable from output distributions, and that the dominant fine-tuning-based unlearning category has critical vulnerabilities the headline benchmarks miss. Choi et al.'s ROKA paper (arxiv:2603.00436, Feb 2026) introduced a structurally different threat — the **indirect unlearning attack** — where an adversary chains legitimate deletion requests to corrupt unrelated security-critical predictions through *knowledge contamination*, exploiting the fact that GDPR Article 17 and CPRA give attackers a legally mandated channel they cannot be refused. The realism gap also widened: **"LLM Unlearning on Noisy Forget Sets"** (arxiv:2510.09007) shows that incomplete, rewritten, or watermarked deletion requests — the realistic case under DSAR workflows — degrade unlearning effectiveness in ways that pristine TOFU/WMDP forget sets do not surface. | Review which unlearning technique the team has chosen and match the rigor to the threat model. For Level-2 production use, prefer: SISA (Bourtoule et al., 2021 — shard/slice/isolate/aggregate so deletion retrains only a fraction of constituent models), EUPG (Universitat Rovira i Virgili, 2025 — pre-train on k-anonymized/DP-transformed data, fine-tune on raw data, revert to the pre-train checkpoint on deletion), SAM-based robust unlearning (Fan et al., ICML 2025, arxiv:2502.05374 — sharpness-aware minimization makes unlearning resilient to relearning attacks), ROKA's Neural Healing approach (Choi et al., arxiv:2603.00436, Feb 2026 — contribution re-allocation that nullifies forgotten data while reinforcing conceptually related neighbors, with the first theoretical guarantee for knowledge preservation during unlearning), or **RASLIK** (ICLR 2026 — Randomized Antipodal Search on Linearized Influence Kernel; reframes unlearning as data selection, uses hashing-based gradient sketches to identify forget-aligned and retain-aligned samples in sublinear time and shifts the Pareto frontier outward beyond oracle sampling). Evaluate with OpenUnlearning (NeurIPS D&B 2025, arxiv:2506.12618) which integrates 12+ algorithms (GradAscent, GradDiff, NPO, SimNPO, DPO, RMU, UNDIAL, AltPO, SatImp, WGA, CE-U, PDU) across TOFU, MUSE, and WMDP benchmarks, along with six membership-inference attacks. Cross-check method choice against the **"LLM Unlearning Under the Microscope"** taxonomy (arxiv:2510.07626) which groups stateful methods into divergence-driven (NPO, SimNPO), representation-misalignment (RMU), and rejection-based (IDK) families and surfaces the unlearning-effectiveness vs. utility-retention tradeoff via Open-QA metrics — production teams should report Open-QA, not just multiple-choice WMDP, because generative leakage is what regulators see. Critically, run the Hu et al. benign-relearning test: fine-tune the "unlearned" model on loosely related public data and measure how much of the forgotten knowledge resurfaces — if relearning trivially recovers the target knowledge, the unlearning is obfuscation, not deletion. Also run the CMU SaTML '25 robustness probes — the *swapped WMDP* and *paired TOFU* datasets — methods that fail these are overfitting to the test set rather than truly forgetting. For multimodal LLMs, run the MLLMU-Bench attack-defense framework (four white-box, three black-box attacks against six defense objectives). Add an **indirect-unlearning probe** following Choi et al. (ROKA, 2026): after each unlearning round, measure accuracy delta on a fixed set of security-critical retain queries — a regression > policy threshold signals knowledge contamination an adversary could weaponize by issuing deletion requests targeting conceptual neighbors. For graph-structured data (recommendation, fraud, knowledge graphs), assume the "Attack by Unlearning" threat model (arxiv:2603.18570, Mar 2026). Finally, test with **noisy / partial forget sets** (arxiv:2510.09007) to mirror how DSARs actually arrive in production. | The 2025–2026 consensus splits unlearning into three tiers: (1) **exact** (full retrain or SISA) — reliable, expensive; (2) **approximate** (gradient ascent, Fisher forgetting, influence functions) — fast but post-Hu-et-al. considered cosmetic unless hardened with SAM or equivalent; (3) **certified** (provable (ε,δ)-removal) — emerging (Chourasia et al., 2025; Liu et al., "Forgetting Any Data at Any Time" arxiv:2502.17081 for vertical FL; "Beyond Uniform Deletion" arxiv:2511.06794 for data-value-weighted certified unlearning; "Certified Unlearning without Source Data Access" OpenReview 2025 for black-box settings) but not yet scalable to foundation-model sizes. Multimodal coverage is expanding: MMUnlearner (2025), MUV Workshop at CVPR 2026 (Denver, June 2026 — first major CV venue dedicated to unlearning), WIPE-OUT Workshop at ECML-PKDD (Naples, September 2026). Regulators have not yet blessed any specific technique — the Article 29 WP / EDPB treat "unlearning" as inadequate on its own unless paired with raw-data deletion, a posture consistent with **EDPB Opinion 28/2024**'s case-by-case anonymity assessment (a model is anonymous only if it is *very unlikely* to identify training-set members or to leak training data through queries — neither property is established by unlearning alone). Full retraining remains the regulatory gold standard but is prohibitive above a few billion parameters. The 2026 evaluation literature has also exposed that the **forget set itself is rarely clean** — production DSARs arrive incomplete, rewritten, or watermarked, and few published unlearning methods report performance on noisy forget sets. |
| **12.2.3** | **Verify that** machine-unlearning routines, when claimed, either physically retrain the affected model on the retained data or apply a certified unlearning algorithm with documented (ε, δ) guarantees. | 3 | Incomplete unlearning that leaves residual influence in the weights — detectable via membership-inference (Shokri et al., 2017; LiRA, Carlini et al., 2022) or extraction attacks. For LLMs specifically, the MUSE benchmark (Shi et al., 2024) measures verbatim reproduction, knowledge Q&A accuracy, and privacy leakage via MIA; the TOFU benchmark (Maini et al., 2024) uses 200 synthetic author profiles with forget/retain splits and has become the de facto reporting standard for unlearning papers in 2025–2026. An unlearned model that still leaks the forget set at > 1% distinguishability fails these benchmarks. | Train shadow models on datasets with and without the forget set. Compare: (a) membership-inference attack AUC — should be ≤ 0.55 (near random); (b) output distribution KL divergence between unlearned and from-scratch-without-forget-set models; (c) per-sample loss distributions; (d) verbatim reproduction rate on the forget set under greedy and sampled decoding. Use OpenUnlearning's PrivLeak metric to bound privacy leakage, and run its six integrated MIA variants. For LLMs, also run canary-based memorization tests (Carlini et al., 2019) and check if the model can complete prompts that reference forget-set content. Statistically validate the < 1% influence claim with a two-sample test of sufficient power (n ≥ 500 probe queries is typical). | Shadow-model evaluation is compute-heavy: training even one reference LLM shadow model at 7B parameters is costly, and MIA research typically calls for 64–256 shadows for high-confidence attacks (LiRA). Lighter "reference-model-free" MIA (Carlini et al., 2022) reduces the cost but has weaker guarantees. For generative models the "influence on outputs" metric is ill-defined in the open-ended case — most practitioners fall back to MUSE/TOFU/WMDP numbers plus extraction attacks. The 1% threshold is conventional, not regulatory — regulators care about demonstrable non-reconstructability, which is why pairing shadow-model evals with formal DP-bound training remains the strongest posture. Shadow-model frameworks worth naming: ML-Doctor (ACM CCS 2022), Privacy Meter (NUS/NIST), and the membership-inference toolkit inside OpenUnlearning. |

## Tooling

- **Machine unlearning (LLM-focused):** [OpenUnlearning](https://github.com/locuslab/open-unlearning) (NeurIPS D&B 2025, 12+ algorithms across TOFU/MUSE/WMDP, 450+ open-sourced checkpoints on HuggingFace, six integrated MIA variants), [SISA reference implementation](https://github.com/cleverhans-lab/machine-unlearning) (Bourtoule et al., 2021), [SAM-based robust unlearning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.05374) (Fan et al., ICML 2025), [ROKA / Neural Healing](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.00436) (Choi et al., Feb 2026 — contribution re-allocation defense against indirect unlearning attacks; first theoretical guarantee for knowledge preservation), [RASLIK](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=Xn6EnJZghu) (ICLR 2026 — randomized antipodal search on linearized influence kernel; reframes unlearning as data selection), EUPG (Universitat Rovira i Virgili, 2025 — hybrid k-anonymity/DP pre-train + fine-tune approach), MMUnlearner (multimodal LLMs, 2025), [awesome-llm-unlearning](https://github.com/chrisliu298/awesome-llm-unlearning) and [awesome-machine-unlearning](https://github.com/tamlhp/awesome-machine-unlearning) for current literature tracking, [Machine-Unlearning-Comparator](https://github.com/gnueaj/Machine-Unlearning-Comparator) (TVCG 2026 visual analytics for unlearning comparison).
- **Unlearning evaluation & realism:** [LLM Unlearning Under the Microscope](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07626) (taxonomy of 12 stateful methods + Open-QA generative metrics), [LLM Unlearning with LLM Beliefs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.19422) (objective reframed around belief distribution rather than fixed forget set), [LLM Unlearning on Noisy Forget Sets](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.09007) (incomplete / rewritten / watermarked forget sets — closer to real DSAR workflows), [Keeping an Eye on LLM Unlearning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.00359) (Stealthy Attack against fine-tuning-based unlearning).
- **Federated unlearning:** FedRecovery (IEEE TIFS 2023 — weighted gradient residual removal with calibrated Gaussian noise for DP), FedADP (arxiv:2411.11044, 2025 — adaptive DP mechanism balancing privacy and unlearning utility), "Forgetting Any Data at Any Time" certified VFL framework (arxiv:2502.17081, 2025), "Certified Unlearning in Decentralized Federated Learning" (arxiv:2601.06436).
- **Privacy auditing & MIA:** [Privacy Meter](https://github.com/privacytrustlab/ml_privacy_meter) (NUS/NIST), ML-Doctor (ACM CCS 2022), DP-Sniper (black-box DP auditing — Bichsel et al.), canary-based memorization testing (Carlini et al., 2019), [Opacus](https://github.com/pytorch/opacus) for DP-SGD training that provides formal guarantees deletable models inherit.
- **Deletion orchestration:** BigID (unified deletion across people data and AI artifacts, 2026 release), OneTrust Consent & Preference Management, Securiti DataGrail with vector-store connectors, Collibra Data Governance with feature-store lineage, DSAR/SRR automation tooling that integrates with Snowflake, Databricks, Pinecone, and Weaviate. For California's [DROP](https://privacy.ca.gov/drop/about-drop-and-the-delete-act/) (consumer launch 1 Jan 2026; broker-side enforcement from 1 Aug 2026 with $200/day per unfulfilled request), use orchestration that re-polls DROP every 45 days and supports the post-deletion re-collection sweep — most major DSAR platforms (DataGrail, Securiti, OneTrust) shipped DROP integrations through Q1 2026.
- **Immutable audit logs:** AWS CloudTrail + Object Lock compliance mode, Azure Monitor with immutable storage + legal holds, GCP Cloud Audit Logs + bucket lock, [Sigstore / Rekor](https://www.sigstore.dev/) transparency logs for public-verifiable trails, IBM Cloud Object Storage with retention policies.
- **Backup-safe erasure (crypto-shredding):** per-subject envelope encryption with AWS KMS, Google Cloud KMS, or Azure Key Vault, so destroying the per-subject key renders backups of that subject's data unrecoverable even under WORM retention. The pattern now extends to streaming systems — see [Conduktor's Crypto Shredding for Kafka](https://www.conduktor.io/glossary/crypto-shredding-for-kafka) for a Kafka-side reference and [OneUptime's GCP KMS recipe](https://oneuptime.com/blog/post/2026-02-17-how-to-set-up-crypto-shredding-for-gdpr-right-to-erasure-compliance-in-google-cloud/view) (Feb 2026) for envelope encryption tied to per-subject DEK destruction in Google Cloud.
- **Lineage for deletion evidence:** [DVC + SageMaker MLflow end-to-end lineage](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/machine-learning/end-to-end-lineage-with-dvc-and-amazon-sagemaker-ai-mlflow-apps/) (April 2026 reference architecture tracing every model back to its exact training data — useful as the audit substrate when a regulator asks which checkpoints touched a deleted record), [Pachyderm](https://github.com/pachyderm/pachyderm) for data-versioned pipelines on Kubernetes, lakeFS for data lake snapshotting, MLflow lineage for experiment-level traces.
