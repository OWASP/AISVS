# C7.5: Generative Media Safeguards

[Back to C07 Index](C07-Model-Behavior.md)

## Purpose

Generative media safeguards address the unique risks of AI systems that produce images, audio, video, or other non-text media. These systems can create non-consensual intimate imagery, generate deepfakes of real people, produce copyright-infringing content, and manufacture disinformation at scale. Unlike text-based safety filtering, media safeguards must operate across modalities — analyzing both the text prompt that drives generation and the resulting media artifact. This section covers input filtering, consent verification, copyright checking, bypass detection, and provenance marking.

### 2024-2026 Landscape: Deepfakes, Watermarking, and Regulatory Mandates

The generative media threat landscape has shifted dramatically. By 2025, deepfake-as-a-service platforms have proliferated, enabling real-time deepfake injection directly into virtual camera feeds during identity verification sessions. Detection systems have responded: Incode's Deepsight multi-modal AI (analyzing video, motion, and depth data simultaneously) is deployed at scale with TikTok, Scotiabank, and Nubank, protecting millions of users across over six million live identity sessions. Academic detection has also advanced -- a multi-model framework achieved 97% detection accuracy and 92% F1 in the UK Home Office Deepfake Detection Challenge 2024, and parameter-efficient adaptation methods like LNCLIP-DF (fine-tuning only LayerNorm parameters of a CLIP vision encoder) have demonstrated strong cross-benchmark generalization.

On the provenance side, three dominant watermarking approaches have emerged for AI-generated media:

- **C2PA 2.4 Content Credentials** -- The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity has matured its open standard significantly. C2PA 2.4 is now the current specification line, with explicit terminology for Durable Content Credentials: a Content Credential discoverable through one or more soft bindings such as an embedded watermark or perceptual fingerprint in a manifest repository. This moves C2PA from removable metadata toward recoverable provenance, but it still depends on platform preservation, verifier availability, and the quality of the underlying soft-binding signal.
- **Google SynthID** -- Embeds imperceptible watermarks directly into media pixels or token choices, depending on modality. Google states that SynthID is designed to survive common transformations such as cropping, compression, frame-rate changes, audio compression, speed changes, and text generation variation, but 2025-2026 attack work shows that auditors should treat it as a strong signal rather than a forensic guarantee.
- **Meta Video Seal** -- Open-sourced in December 2024, providing an alternative watermarking approach for video content with a focus on open research and interoperability.

Regulatory pressure is now a primary driver. The EU AI Act (Article 50, effective August 2, 2026) mandates machine-readable labeling of AI-generated content, with penalties up to EUR 15 million or 3% of global turnover for violations. To operationalize Article 50, the European Commission published the second draft of its Code of Practice on Marking and Labelling of AI-Generated Content on March 3, 2026, and published the final Code on June 10, 2026 following the closing plenary; the final Code is now undergoing adequacy assessment by the Commission and the AI Board. The Code promotes a two-layer marking strategy: secured metadata and watermarking, with optional fingerprinting and logging mechanisms as fallbacks for short or heavily transformed outputs. On May 8, 2026, the Commission published a 40-page draft of its Article 50 transparency guidelines issued by the AI Office, with a stakeholder feedback deadline of June 3, 2026 and an applicability date that still tracks August 2, 2026. The draft confirms that AI systems released under free and open-source licenses remain fully subject to Article 50, narrows the Article 2(10) "purely personal non-professional activity" exception when an output materially affects public discourse, and applies the same statutory quality criteria (effective, interoperable, robust, reliable) to every in-scope provider regardless of whether they sign the Code. Crucially, companies that sign the final Code and implement its measures will enjoy a presumption of conformity with Article 50 obligations — a significant compliance incentive. Modality-specific disclosure requirements include persistent icons for images and real-time video, opening disclaimers plus end credits for non-real-time video, and repeated audible disclaimers for audio deepfakes. California SB 942 took effect January 2026. Multiple additional US states have enacted digital labeling laws for synthetic media. In January 2025, NSA and CISA jointly published "Strengthening Multimedia Integrity in the Generative AI Era," endorsing C2PA content credentials as a recommended countermeasure for government agencies and critical infrastructure operators. The guidance recommends a multi-faceted approach combining provenance, education, policy, and detection.

As of May 2026, the real-world adoption picture is more nuanced than the regulatory ambition suggests. A study of 50 generative AI systems found that only 38% implement adequate machine-readable marking, and just 10% use invisible watermarks despite their technical feasibility. End-to-end providers (like OpenAI and Google) lead at 80% adoption, while API-based services and open-source tools lag far behind. Social media platforms routinely strip C2PA metadata during upload processing, undermining the provenance chain — though LinkedIn, TikTok, and Cloudflare now support or preserve content credentials at scale. On the hardware side, Samsung Galaxy S25 and Google Pixel 10 now sign photos natively with C2PA credentials, Sony's PXW-Z300 integrates Content Credentials into professional video capture, and Sony's March 2026 Camera Authenticity Solution update adds video verification through C2PA signatures, server-time checks, and 3D depth metadata to distinguish real-world capture from filming a screen. The Content Authenticity Initiative has grown to over 6,000 members, and the C2PA Conformance Program plus official C2PA Trust List launched in mid-2025 to ensure consistent implementation across products (tracked via the C2PA Conformance Explorer); as of January 1, 2026, the older Interim Trust List is frozen. The CAWG 1.2 specification was released alongside, reflecting real-world usage demands. The community's response to the metadata-stripping problem is **Durable Content Credentials** — CAI's open-source framework that explicitly combines three layers: the C2PA manifest, an imperceptible mode-specific watermark embedded directly into pixels/audio frames, and a perceptual fingerprint that allows credential recovery from a cloud repository even when the manifest is stripped. Documentation and reference tooling are published at `opensource.contentauthenticity.org/docs/durable-cr/`.

Real-world implementation is uneven. OpenAI's Sora now layers a visible "Sora" corner watermark with an invisible C2PA manifest, but independent testing at the Effective Altruism Forum and LessWrong found that C2PA metadata only ships on videos downloaded **without** the visible watermark — meaning Pro downloads that strip the visible mark preserve provenance, while standard downloads that carry the visible watermark do not carry machine-readable C2PA. This kind of selective marking undermines the theory of multi-layered durability: if one layer is absent whenever the other is present, the combined system degrades to a single point of failure. The Sora story took a sharp turn in April 2026: OpenAI shut down the standalone Sora app on April 26, 2026, with the public API scheduled for discontinuation on September 24, 2026. By then, third-party tools like BasedLabs AI's watermark remover were already stripping the visible "Sora" watermark from generated videos in roughly four minutes — and because OpenAI shipped C2PA only on the unwatermarked download path, the videos that survived in circulation generally had neither layer of provenance. A late-2025 NewsGuard study reinforced the misuse concern: Sora 2 produced realistic videos advancing provably false claims in 16 out of 20 prompts (an 80% success rate, with 55% succeeding on the first attempt), including five claims tracked to Russian disinformation operations. NewsGuard validated BasedLabs's roughly four-minute watermark-removal time on a Sora upload, and Public Citizen formally petitioned OpenAI to pull Sora 2 from public release based on the same evidence.

### April 2026: SynthID Reverse-Engineered

The most significant April 2026 watermarking development is the public reverse-engineering work against Google's SynthID-Image watermark. A researcher publishing under the handle Aloshdenny released `reverse-SynthID`, an open-source toolkit targeting SynthID patterns in images generated by Gemini 3 (`gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview`) and Nano Banana Pro (`nano-banana-pro-preview`) without access to Google's encoder or decoder. The methodology relies on classical signal processing: the toolkit profiles resolution-dependent carrier frequencies in the image's frequency domain from a small corpus of Gemini outputs, runs 2D Fourier transforms and phase-coherence analysis, and then attacks the carriers directly. The repository reports a 75.8% drop in carrier energy, a 91.4% reduction in phase coherence on top carriers, and Round 06 Gemini-app detector bypasses after chaining VAE round-trip, elastic deformation, geometric warping, resize-squeeze, color shifts, FFT subtraction, and JPEG re-encoding. Google disputed the claim that the tool can systematically remove SynthID marks, and The Verge's April 14 coverage framed the result as decoder confusion rather than complete removal. That nuance matters: for verification, the practical lesson is not that SynthID is useless, but that provenance cannot rely on one private watermark detector when adversaries can test against public-facing verification paths.

The reverse-SynthID release does not stand alone. At IEEE S&P 2025, Andre Kassis and Urs Hengartner of the University of Waterloo presented **UnMarker** (arXiv 2405.08363), the first practical universal attack against defensive image watermarking. UnMarker requires no detector feedback, no knowledge of the scheme, no surrogate model, and no training data; it uses two adversarial optimizations that target the spectral properties shared across robust watermarking architectures. Reported results include a 79% attack success rate against SynthID (detection collapsing from approximately 100% to roughly 21%) and a reduction of the best-performing semantic watermark to a 43% detection rate. Diffusion-based regeneration attacks have produced similarly destructive results: the February 2026 **Vanishing Watermarks** preprint reports near-zero watermark recovery after diffusion edits against StegaStamp, TrustMark, and VINE-style schemes while preserving visual fidelity.

The attack surface now includes forgery as well as removal. **WmForger** (NeurIPS 2025 spotlight; arXiv 2510.20468) shows that post-hoc image watermarks can be forged or removed from a single watermarked example through a preference-model-guided black-box attack, without access to the watermarking model. Conversely, the April 28, 2026 **Forensic Cost of Watermark Removal** preprint argues that current removal pipelines leave statistical artifacts detectable at very low false-positive rates, introducing a fourth evaluation axis: attack success, perceptual quality, provenance verification, and forensic detectability. Together, these results reframe watermarking as a probabilistic signal that buys time and triages bulk content, not as a standalone guarantee that survives a determined attacker.

On the consent and governance front, 61 data protection authorities issued a joint statement in February 2026 calling for robust safeguards against non-consensual AI imagery, meaningful transparency about system capabilities, and effective rapid-removal mechanisms — with enhanced protections for children. While the statement sets political expectations, it lacks prescriptive technical requirements or binding deadlines.

By May 2026, non-consensual imagery abuse had also become more operationally concrete. The Internet Watch Foundation reported a UK school blackmail attempt in which attackers turned public student photos into AI-generated CSAM, produced 150 images classed as CSAM under UK law, and used hashes to help platforms block re-upload. Days earlier, Italian prime minister Giorgia Meloni publicly challenged viral AI-generated sexualized images of her after a broader Italian scandal involving doctored images of women public figures distributed to a site with more than 700,000 subscribers. These cases point to a control gap beyond output watermarking: systems handling identifiable media need source-image minimization, consent hygiene, takedown workflows, and perceptual-hash response paths, not only marks on newly generated outputs.

Despite these advances, the arms race continues. Advanced actors now chain multiple AI agents to simulate entire identity lifecycles — from document creation to live customer interaction — turning deepfake fraud into a fully automated pipeline. Identity verification providers are responding: PXL Vision integrated deepfake detection techniques from Idiap Research Institute to spot manipulated faces in identity documents (February 2026), and Proof launched its "Verify" platform combining live human-assisted identity verification with real-time deepfake detection. The World Economic Forum published "Unmasking Cybercrime: Strengthening Digital Identity Verification against Deepfakes" in 2026, calling for infrastructure-level protections as the primary line of defense. The meaningful shift is from post-hoc detection to cryptographic provenance at capture time.

Enterprise deepfake defense is also moving from point products into communication infrastructure. On April 1, 2026, **Orange Business** launched a Reality Defender-powered multimodal deepfake detection service embedded directly into its collaboration, voice, and contact-center portfolio — a single API endpoint covering audio, image, video, and document analysis, deployed in production without requiring a human in the loop, and available across 7,000+ enterprise customers and roughly 100,000 customer locations. Two weeks earlier, on March 16, 2026, **Zoom** expanded its Pindrop integration from Zoom Meetings into **Zoom Contact Center**, bringing Pindrop Passport (voice authentication), Pindrop Protect (risk analysis), and Pindrop Pulse for Meetings (audio deepfake detection) into customer service flows. Pindrop reported a 1,200% surge in AI-driven voice fraud during 2025, driven by dramatically lower speech-synthesis latency. Resemble AI's Detect-2B reached ~94% accuracy against unknown-source deepfakes in 2026, and its Detect Bot now plugs into Zoom and Microsoft Teams for meeting-time screening. Pindrop also expanded into HIPAA-regulated healthcare environments in February 2026.

The detection layer is also being augmented by an identity-binding layer. On April 17, 2026, **Zoom and Tools for Humanity** announced an integration that places World ID Deep Face verification inside Zoom meetings via Zoom's Realtime Media Streams (RTMS). The verification combines three signals on-device — an Orb-issued World ID enrollment image, a real-time video frame from the Zoom call, and a fresh on-device face authentication selfie — and surfaces a "Verified Human" badge on participants' video tiles, with an optional Deep Face Waiting Room that gates entry to the meeting. Zoom's announcement explicitly cites the Arup deepfake fraud and Deloitte's $40B-by-2027 projection for AI-enabled fraud losses in the US as the motivating threat model. The integration is in beta, scheduled to ship through the Zoom App Marketplace later in 2026, with an initial focus on financial services, healthcare, and executive communications across the network's roughly 18 million verified users in 160 countries. Spain's data protection authority issued a formal GDPR warning against the underlying Worldcoin biometric collection in February 2026, illustrating that biometric-binding solutions for deepfake risk now sit at the intersection of fraud prevention, biometric privacy, and cross-border data protection law.

On the financial-services side, a **US industry coalition** — the American Bankers Association, the Better Identity Coalition, and the Financial Services Sector Coordinating Council — released a coordinated AI-identity-fraud defense plan around April 1, 2026. Recommendations include a Treasury-led task force on identity proofing, broad adoption of mobile driver's licenses backed by asymmetric cryptography, expanded access to the Social Security Administration's eCBSV consent-based verification service, updated NIST guidance on biometric liveness detection, and a regulator-driven push toward FIDO security keys and passkeys for phishing-resistant authentication. The plan cites Deloitte's projection that US AI-enabled fraud losses will reach roughly **$40 billion by 2027** (up from $12.3 billion in 2023) and frames the response on a 2–3 year implementation horizon. Treasury's FinCEN remains the anchor regulator: its 2024 alert (FIN-2024-DEEPFAKEFRAUD) enumerates nine red-flag indicators for suspicious activity tied to GenAI — inconsistencies in identity documents, declined multifactor authentication, third-party webcam plugins used during live verification, reverse-image matches to AI-generated face galleries — and directs filers to include the alert's ID in SAR narratives so schemes can be tracked across institutions.

### May-June 2026: Provenance Goes Mainstream While the Spec Comes Under Fire

The weeks after mid-May 2026 produced the most consequential adoption news since C2PA launched. On May 19, 2026, OpenAI announced it is becoming C2PA-conformant, joining the C2PA steering committee, and — notably — embedding Google DeepMind's SynthID invisible watermark alongside C2PA Content Credentials in images generated by ChatGPT, the OpenAI API, and Codex. OpenAI simultaneously released **Verify**, a public research-preview tool that checks an uploaded image for both C2PA metadata and SynthID watermarks (initially covering OpenAI imagery, with broader coverage planned). The framing is exactly the layered argument auditors have been making: C2PA carries rich signed context but gets stripped by platforms; SynthID preserves a signal when metadata does not survive. Around Google I/O 2026, Google previewed a **SynthID Content Detection API** on the Gemini Enterprise platform — a REST endpoint analyzing JPEG/PNG/WebP for AI-generation artifacts, with preview partners including Shutterstock, Snap, and Canva — and named Nvidia, OpenAI, Kakao, and ElevenLabs as new SynthID adopters. Google also extended C2PA Content Credentials support back to Pixel 8 and 9 devices and reports SynthID now covers over 100 billion images and videos. For the first time, an auditor verifying requirement-7.5.1-style controls against a major commercial generator can exercise a vendor-operated verification endpoint and a programmatic detection API rather than a manual portal.

At the same time, the C2PA specification itself came under formal attack. An April 27, 2026 paper, "Verifying Provenance of Digital Media: Why the C2PA Specifications Fall Short" (arXiv 2604.24890, with Neal Krawetz among the authors), presents the first independent security evaluation of C2PA including formal-methods analysis and concludes the current specifications fail to achieve their claimed security goals for high-stakes uses such as journalism and legal evidence. Krawetz's earlier "authenticated disinformation" demonstration showed a forged manifest produced with the standard c2patool carrying a real vendor's identity and validating cleanly — C2PA constrains how claims are signed, not who may sign what content with what metadata. Complementing this, the **Integrity Clash** work (arXiv 2603.02378, accepted to a CVPR 2026 workshop) demonstrates "authenticated contradictions": a single asset carrying a valid C2PA manifest asserting human authorship *and* an AI-generation watermark, both passing their independent checks, constructed through strategic metadata omission with no cryptography broken. The practical lesson for verification is that provenance layers must be cross-validated against each other — a manifest that contradicts a watermark verdict is a finding, not a pass.

The attack literature also widened beyond image watermark removal. **Chainwash** (arXiv 2605.05503, May 6, 2026) shows that chaining five small open-weight LLM rewriters collapses a diffusion-language-model text watermark from 87.9% to under 5% detection without any knowledge of the watermark key. **MarkSweep** (arXiv 2602.15364, accepted ICASSP 2026) is a no-box image attack — edge-aware noise intensification followed by frequency-aware denoising — that drives bit accuracy for HiDDeN and Stable Signature watermarks below the usable threshold with no decoder access. **DITTO** (arXiv 2510.10987, accepted EACL 2026) inverts the threat: knowledge distillation from a watermarked teacher makes an attacker's model emit text carrying the victim's authentic watermark, turning provenance marks into a false-attribution and framing vector. Watermark stripping is also commoditizing — public GitHub utilities now claim one-command removal of SynthID, C2PA, and EXIF/XMP "Made with AI" markers from images. And on the detection side, a May 27, 2026 independent Podonos benchmark of eight audio deepfake detectors found commercial APIs ranging from 71% to 98% accuracy while open-source models (Wav2Vec2, RawNet2, LCNN, AASIST) scored only 48-63% — near chance — meaning audio verification cannot lean on free detectors alone (caveat: the benchmark write-up is hosted by Resemble AI, the top scorer).

Enforcement and legislation accelerated in parallel. China became the first jurisdiction with sustained national enforcement of mandatory AI-content labeling: a February 13, 2026 CAC campaign penalized 13,421 accounts and removed over 543,000 non-compliant items, and on April 29, 2026 the CAC formally penalized CapCut, Maoxiang (Cat Box), and Dreamina AI for failing to label AI-generated content under the labeling Provisions and GB 45438-2025, using regulatory interviews, rectification orders, and personnel-accountability measures. In the UK, Section 138 of the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 came into force on February 6, 2026, criminalizing both creating and *requesting* a non-consensual intimate AI image (the request alone suffices); Ofcom opened a formal Online Safety Act investigation into X over NCII failures, and a government amendment to the Crime and Policing Bill would require platforms and chatbots to remove reported NCII within 48 hours, making NCII an OSA priority offence. In the US, the NO FAKES Act was reintroduced on May 20, 2026 (S.4591 / H.R.8915) with a new DMCA-style counter-notification process for contested digital-replica takedowns and carve-outs for libraries and educational institutions; EFF launched an opposition campaign in June 2026. The FBI issued a fresh warning in early June 2026 about AI voice-cloning "virtual kidnapping" and family-distress scams, where seconds of social-media audio suffice to clone a voice. And MITRE ATLAS added an iProov red-team case study (December 2025) documenting a face-swap application plus virtual camera defeating a production remote-KYC liveness check, pointing implementers to the CEN 18099 biometric liveness standard — useful as the canonical ATLAS reference for unmarked synthetic media defeating identity verification. Denmark's deepfake copyright bill (consent right over realistic digital imitations of likeness and voice, 50 years post-mortem) remained pending as of early June 2026 after a snap election disrupted its timeline.

### May-June 2026 Verification Notes

C2PA 2.4 changes the evidence an auditor should expect from a serious provenance implementation. Beyond finding a Content Credential in the file, verify that the claim signature chains to the C2PA Trust List or TSA Trust List, that the generator or validator appears in the official Conformance Explorer, and that older Interim Trust List credentials are surfaced differently from conforming 2.x credentials. For systems using a manifest repository, check for the `c2pa.repository-receipt` assertion and prove that a stripped asset can be re-linked to its manifest through a soft binding or fingerprint.

The new `c2pa.ai-disclosure` assertion also gives providers a machine-readable place to carry AI transparency data, which matters for Article 50 workflows. Test this alongside the human-facing disclosure: generate image, audio, video, and mixed-media outputs; confirm the machine-readable marker survives expected transformations; then validate that the deployer-facing label, icon, disclaimer, or audible disclosure is present where the EU draft transparency guidelines and second draft Code of Practice require it. A system that signs media correctly but fails to present the disclosure clearly is still incomplete for August 2, 2026 compliance.

Live video now needs its own provenance test path. C2PA 2.4 defines live-video support, so validators should test per-segment manifests or verifiable segment information, sequence continuity, time-stamp validation, signing-key rollover, dropped or reordered segments, and whether a downstream meeting or streaming platform preserves enough evidence for later review. This matters for real-time deepfake injection because a valid static file manifest says little about whether the capture pipeline, stream transport, and viewer-side verification were intact during the live session.

Watermark negative testing should cover four cases: removal, forgery, cross-layer desynchronization, and removal-forensic detection. Run transformations that target the watermark directly, then try to transplant or forge a mark onto unrelated media and confirm the verifier does not treat that signal as equivalent to a trusted C2PA signature or repository receipt. The Integrity Clash result makes desynchronization a standard test: construct (or request from the red team) an asset whose C2PA manifest claims human authorship while a watermark detector reports AI generation, and confirm the validation pipeline surfaces the contradiction instead of reporting two independent passes. Where a vendor claims removal detection, test whether the classifier flags state-of-the-art removal pipelines without treating ordinary compression, screenshots, or platform re-encodes as hostile edits.

Vendor verification endpoints became real audit surfaces in May 2026. For OpenAI-generated imagery, run the asset through OpenAI's Verify research preview and record both the C2PA and SynthID verdicts. For programmatic checks at scale, evaluate Google's SynthID Content Detection API (preview on Gemini Enterprise; JPEG/PNG/WebP at launch) — noting that a vendor detection API is a detector, not a trust chain, and its verdict should be recorded alongside, not instead of, C2PA validation output. Keep local tooling current: c2patool 0.26.65 (June 9, 2026) fixes a DLL-hijacking vulnerability (CAI-8608), the c2pa Rust SDK 0.85.2 hardens the JUMBF parser against integer underflow, and the May 27 c2patool release adds a `--create <source-type>` flag that signs assets with an explicit `digitalSourceType` such as `trainedAlgorithmicMedia` — the one-flag way to assert AI generation in a manifest, and a quick way for auditors to generate known-good test fixtures.

Takedown workflows are now part of the same verification surface for platforms that host user-generated media. The FTC's May 2026 TIDA guidance makes the control auditable: covered platforms need clear intake, support for people without accounts, a request identifier, 48-hour removal of the reported item and known identical copies, status feedback, and hashing paths to NCMEC Take It Down or StopNCII. For generative-media systems that can create or host intimate imagery, verify that provenance checks, abuse intake, and perceptual-hash blocking share incident IDs instead of operating as separate queues.

As of May 18, 2026, auditors should treat the FTC's platform letters and the May 19 enforcement start date as evidence that TIDA is now in active enforcement, with civil penalties of up to $53,088 per violation calibrated to make repeated 48-hour failures genuinely expensive. A useful test is to submit a synthetic but authorized report in a staging environment, confirm that the platform records the source asset hash, derivative hashes, reporter contact path, decision rationale, timestamps, and cross-platform hash-sharing eligibility, then verify that appeals and lawful-content safeguards do not block the 48-hour duplicate-removal duty. Where the platform also hosts user-generated provenance signals, validate that a removal action cleanly unlinks any associated C2PA manifest, repository receipt, or perceptual-fingerprint lookup so the takedown does not leave a viable provenance pathway to the removed asset.

For provenance implementations, collect verifier output rather than screenshots alone. Evidence should include the raw C2PA validation report or crJSON view, signing certificate chain, trust-list status, timestamp validation, repository receipt if a manifest repository is used, soft-binding or fingerprint lookup result, detector version, model or encoder version, and transformation test matrix. This is the minimum evidence needed to distinguish "the media has a detectable mark" from "the media carries a trusted, recoverable, and policy-compliant provenance record."

Recommended evidence bundle:

- Original prompt, generated asset, and any edited or transformed derivative used in the test.
- Raw C2PA validator output, including validation status codes, claim signature, certificate chain, timestamp, trust-list decision, and active manifest identifier.
- Repository receipt and recovery proof when a manifest repository, Durable Content Credential, soft binding, or fingerprint lookup is claimed.
- Watermark detector output with detector version, confidence score, threshold, media modality, and encoder or generation model version.
- Negative-test results for compression, crop, screenshot, platform upload/download, diffusion regeneration, spectral removal, and one-shot forgery.
- Human-facing disclosure evidence, including label placement, icon behavior, audio disclaimer cadence, and any jurisdiction-specific deployer notice.
- TIDA or NCII intake evidence for hosted content: request ID, reporting path for non-account holders, duplicate-search result, decision time, and appeal path.
- Hash-sharing or blocklist decision record for NCMEC Take It Down, StopNCII, or an internal perceptual-hash service.

## Open Research Questions

- How robust are current watermarking schemes to adversarial removal and forgery? The April 2026 reverse-SynthID release shows that the SynthID-Image signal in Gemini 3 and Nano Banana Pro outputs can be profiled through frequency-domain analysis and, in the author's tests, pushed into Gemini-app detector failures; Google disputes that this is systematic removal. UnMarker (IEEE S&P 2025) establishes the broader risk for defensive image watermarking, WmForger adds one-shot forgery/removal for post-hoc marks, and diffusion-regeneration work shows near-zero recovery for several robust invisible watermarking schemes after generative edits. The next research questions are: can watermarks resist frequency-domain and diffusion attacks without sacrificing perceptual quality? Can verifiers distinguish a valid mark from a forged or transplanted one? Will C2PA's perceptual-fingerprint cloud-recovery layer hold up when the embedded watermark is stripped? And can removal-forensic classifiers detect watermark tampering without creating false alarms on ordinary platform compression?
- How should consent verification work at scale for voice cloning and face generation? Is a consent database technically feasible, and how do existing consent mechanisms (which are often trivially bypassable) need to evolve?
- What similarity threshold constitutes copyright infringement in generated visual and audio content?
- As deepfakes become capable of real-time interaction and injection into live video feeds, how should detection shift from post-hoc analysis to infrastructure-level prevention (e.g., cryptographic provenance at capture time)?
- With C2PA, SynthID, and Video Seal emerging as competing/complementary standards, what interoperability mechanisms are needed to ensure a unified provenance ecosystem?
- How should detection systems handle scale-dependent behavior, given that deepfake detection models exhibit different accuracy patterns at different model scales (e.g., LNCLIP-DF generalization characteristics)?
- Should AI-generated media provenance be mandatory for all generative systems, or only for systems above a certain capability threshold? The EU AI Act and US state laws are converging on broad mandates, but enforcement mechanisms remain unclear.
- How should validators weigh C2PA assurance levels, repository receipts, AI disclosure assertions, and soft-binding recovery results when they conflict? C2PA 2.4 gives implementers richer evidence, but verifiers still need policy rules for cases where a manifest is valid, a watermark is missing, and a repository fingerprint match is probabilistic. The Integrity Clash result sharpens this: a manifest and a watermark can each verify while asserting contradictory provenance, and the April 2026 formal critique of the C2PA specs argues the trust model does not constrain who may sign what. What should a conforming validator output when authenticated layers disagree — and can cross-layer consistency checking be standardized before adversaries operationalize desynchronization at scale?
- How should copyright detection handle hybrid content (partially AI-generated, partially human-created)? Current tools cannot identify which sections of a work are AI-generated, and the legal framework for "substantial similarity" in hybrid works is undefined.
- As image-based prompt injection matures (typographic, steganographic, adversarial perturbation), how should media generation systems defend against attacks that embed malicious instructions within visual inputs to multimodal models? No single defense currently neutralizes all variants.
- Intent laundering demonstrates that safety alignment fundamentally overrelies on detecting triggering language rather than evaluating semantic intent. Can semantic intent classifiers be built that are robust to neutral rephrasing? What training data and architectural changes are needed to move from keyword-triggered to intent-aware safety filtering?
- As platforms diverge on AI content policies (Spotify removing 75M+ tracks vs. others allowing AI content with disclosure), what standardized disclosure framework should emerge? The DDEX AI metadata standard adopted by Spotify is a step, but interoperability across platforms remains unsolved.
- The EU Parliament's March 2026 copyright & GenAI resolution proposes a rebuttable presumption of infringement for providers that fail transparency obligations and extraterritorial application to all GenAI systems placed on the EU market regardless of training jurisdiction. How will this interact with US fair use doctrine and the emerging US licensing market? Will the EUIPO-managed opt-out registry and itemized training data requirements become a de facto global standard, or create jurisdictional fragmentation?
- As deepfake fraud shifts from isolated incidents to coordinated multi-step schemes (combining synthetic identities, deepfake video, and telemetry tampering — Sumsub reports 180% increase in advanced fraud), how should identity verification architectures evolve beyond liveness detection? YouTube's Content ID-like approach for faces suggests platform-level detection may be more scalable than per-organization consent verification, but how should this extend to voice and other biometric modalities?
