# C1.1: Training Data Origin & Traceability

> [Back to C01 Index](C01-Training-Data.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-10

## Purpose

Maintaining a verifiable inventory of every dataset, accepting only trusted sources, and logging every change for auditability are the load-bearing foundations of training-data security. Without knowing where data came from, who is responsible for it, and what happened to it over time, an organization cannot detect supply-chain compromise, trace poisoned samples back to their origin during incident response, or satisfy the regulatory pressure that has intensified sharply since 2024.

The threat landscape is no longer theoretical. LAION's August 2024 Re-LAION-5B release removed 2,236 links to suspected CSAM from a web-scale training-data index after external safety review, a blunt reminder that "public web data" still needs source review, hash matching, and remediation records. In October 2025, Anthropic, the UK AI Security Institute, the Alan Turing Institute, and academic partners showed that as few as 250 malicious documents could backdoor LLMs across tested model sizes. In May 2026, ReversingLabs and other defenders traced a fake `openai/whisper`-style repository on Hugging Face that distributed infostealer malware via SEO-poisoned trending lists, while a separate masquerading OpenAI release reached roughly 244,000 downloads before takedown. A March 2026 ACM Internetware study sampled 1,020,755 models and 219,812 datasets on Hugging Face and flagged 1,574 squatting models (10.4% with malicious traits) and 625 dataset typosquats (42.2% showing intentional impersonation). The platform-trust problem kept escalating through May 2026: aggregate scanning results published that month counted roughly 352,000 unsafe or suspicious findings across 51,700 Hugging Face models out of more than 4 million scanned, and a North Korea-linked npm supply-chain campaign was caught using Hugging Face itself as both a second-stage malware host and an exfiltration backend — stolen developer data was uploaded into private Hugging Face datasets, where it blended into ordinary AI research traffic. These are the failure modes C1.1 is trying to prevent: unknown data sources, weak source accountability, and no durable record of what was accepted into training.

Regulatory traction has caught up with the threat model. California AB 2013 took effect January 1, 2026 and requires developers of publicly available generative AI systems to publish high-level training-data summaries covering sources and ownership, data characteristics and volume, collection and processing methods, IP status, personal-information status, collection timelines, and synthetic-data use. On March 4, 2026, the US District Court for the Central District of California denied X.AI's motion for a preliminary injunction against AB 2013, leaving the statute fully in force and enforceable through California's Unfair Competition Law; OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google have already published disclosures at varying levels of granularity. The EU AI Act timeline shifted in May 2026: the Digital Omnibus on AI, provisionally agreed May 6, 2026 and confirmed by the Council on May 13, defers the high-risk obligations that carry Article 10's data-governance duties — to December 2, 2027 for stand-alone Annex III systems and August 2, 2028 for AI embedded in Annex I regulated products — with formal adoption expected before the original August 2, 2026 date. Article 10 still demands data governance over collection origin, preparation operations, assumptions, availability, suitability, limitations, and representativeness, with fines reaching €35M or 6% of global turnover; the deferral buys time but does not change what evidence the inventory must eventually produce, and Article 50 transparency obligations stay on the August 2026 timeline. On March 17, 2026, NSA, CISA, FBI, and allied agencies from seven partner nations published joint guidance treating AI/ML supply chain security as a distinct discipline from software supply chain security, naming training data as one of six core components and recommending AI BOMs, cryptographic integrity, and end-to-end threat modeling. Neither AB 2013 nor Article 10 is satisfiable by a spreadsheet maintained by goodwill; both presuppose the kind of machine-readable inventory and change-log discipline this section describes.

Two developments in early June 2026 sharpen both sides of the picture. On the threat side, CVE-2026-4372 (disclosed June 4, 2026) showed that a single unvalidated key in a pulled model's `config.json` could yield remote code execution through Hugging Face Transformers 4.56.0–5.2.x despite `trust_remote_code=False` — exactly the unfiltered-field ingestion failure mode that 1.1.2's allow-listing discipline addresses. On the compliance side, the EU AI Act's Article 53(1)(d) training-content summary template becomes enforceable by the AI Office on August 2, 2026 (fines up to 3% of global turnover or €15M), and US state law is diversifying beyond California: Texas's TRAIGA has let the state AG compel training-data descriptions through civil investigative demands since January 1, 2026, while Colorado's heavily amended AI act (SB 26-189, signed May 14, 2026) still requires developers to disclose "categories of training data" to deployers when it takes effect January 1, 2027.

This section covers provenance metadata (origin, responsible party, license, collection method, intended-use constraints, processing history), purpose-bound field allow-listing, logged approval gates on dataset changes, and fingerprinting or watermarking datasets for downstream attribution and unauthorized-use detection.

## Requirements

| **1.1.1** | **Verify that** an up-to-date inventory is kept of every training-data source, including its origin, responsible party, license, collection method, intended use constraints, and processing history. | 1 | Supply-chain compromise via unknown or unvetted data sources (MITRE ATLAS AML.T0010.002 ML Supply Chain Compromise: Data; AML.T0019 Publish Poisoned Datasets); inability to trace poisoned samples or unsafe source material back to origin during incident response; weak evidence for California AB 2013 (effective Jan 1, 2026) and EU AI Act Article 10 (deferred by the May 2026 Digital Omnibus to Dec 2, 2027 for stand-alone high-risk systems), both of which require dataset-source and preparation records. The March 2026 NSA/CISA/FBI joint guidance on AI/ML supply chain risks names "web-scale datasets" as a high-risk component, and Anthropic's Oct 2025 poisoning study confirms that small, targeted poison sets can matter even in large corpora — so source accountability remains load-bearing. State-level exposure is also widening: Texas's TRAIGA (effective Jan 1, 2026) lets the AG compel "training data" descriptions through civil investigative demands without a prior violation notice, and Colorado's amended act (SB 26-189) retains a developer duty to disclose categories of training data from Jan 1, 2027. | Review the data catalog or asset inventory system. Practical options include DataHub, OpenMetadata (1.13.0 GA shipped June 8, 2026 with an MCP server exposing the catalog as LLM-callable tools, governance approval tasks rendering a reviewable "Proposed Changes" view, and language-aware auto-classification of data-lake assets), Marquez/OpenLineage, MLflow's `mlflow.data` tracking for run-linked source and digest metadata, and Unity Catalog with end-to-end column-level lineage and "bring your own data lineage" APIs (public preview April 2026) when the stack is Databricks-centered. Confirm each dataset entry includes all six required metadata fields: origin, responsible party, license, collection method, intended-use constraints, and processing history. Spot-check source links, owners, licenses, and collection dates against upstream evidence. For AB 2013, confirm the public disclosure can be regenerated when a covered model is substantially modified. For EU AI Act Article 10, confirm the inventory emits timestamped evidence per pipeline run. Compare exported manifests against CycloneDX ML-BOM (1.7 added explicit data provenance and citation fields), SPDX 3.0 AI/Dataset profiles, or the OWASP AIBOM Generator output for Hugging Face-hosted models. Check whether dataset entries carry Croissant 1.1 metadata (released Feb 12, 2026): it embeds machine-actionable W3C PROV-O provenance chains linking each dataset, file, and record to its sources, processing steps, and responsible agents, plus DUO/ODRL usage policies, and is already attached to roughly 700,000 datasets across Hugging Face, Kaggle, and OpenML. The EU's Article 53(1)(d) training-content summary template also doubles as an evidence schema — it forces source typing (public/scraped/licensed/user/synthetic) and listing of the top 10% of scraped domains, and AI Office enforcement starts Aug 2, 2026 for new GPAI models (legacy models must publish by Aug 2, 2027). Where media datasets carry C2PA Content Credentials (training-data disclosure assertions landed in v2.1; v2.2 is the current stable release), verify ingest preserves and records the manifests rather than stripping them during preprocessing. | Catalogs capture metadata but do not prove it is complete or true. Required fields can still be blank, owners can be stale, and lineage can break when ingestion jobs miss file-based or ad hoc transformations. Manual sampling remains necessary. MLflow is useful for experiment-linked dataset digests but is not a full governance catalog. OpenLineage now exposes richer dataset facets (DatasetQualityMetricsDatasetFacet, subset facets, severity flags) but usually needs a catalog for ownership, glossary, policy, and disclosure workflows. AB 2013 sets no specific format for the public summary, so consistency across vendors is uneven, and trade-secret pressure pushes some disclosures toward minimums — the first major filings in January 2026 stayed at the level of "publicly available information" and unnamed third-party partners, so the public floor is thin and internal inventories must exceed it to be audit-useful. The EU timeline is also in flux until the Digital Omnibus is published in the Official Journal — treat the December 2027 date as the working assumption but expect audit programs to keep the original evidence expectations. |
| **1.1.2** | **Verify that** training data processes include only features, attributes, and fields required for the model's stated purpose, excluding all others (e.g., unused metadata, sensitive PII, leaked test data). | 1 | Purpose creep, benchmark contamination, and PII or secret retention in training sets; exposure through extraction or membership-inference attacks (MITRE ATLAS AML.T0044 Full ML Model Access, AML.T0057 LLM Data Leakage); larger GDPR/CCPA/HIPAA blast radius from keeping fields the model does not need. LAION's 2024 remediation of unsafe links and the May 2026 Hugging Face infostealer campaign that abused fake OpenAI repositories both show why large scraped or trending-driven corpora need repeatable exclusion and re-scan processes, not one-time review. CVE-2026-4372 (June 2026) is the sharpest recent example of unfiltered-field ingestion: an unvalidated `_attn_implementation_internal` key in a pulled `config.json` gave RCE through Transformers 4.56.0–5.2.x even with `trust_remote_code=False`. The May 2026 TrapDoor campaign hid zero-width Unicode instructions (U+200B–U+200D, U+FEFF) inside `.cursorrules` and `CLAUDE.md` files across 34 npm/PyPI/Crates.io packages — invisible to reviewers and missed by standard scanners. | Review pipeline code, data contracts, schema registries, and feature-store definitions for a positive allow-list that maps every retained field to the model's stated purpose. Confirm sensitive-data scanning is wired into ingest and significant transformations using tools such as Microsoft Presidio, AWS Macie for S3, Google Cloud Sensitive Data Protection, or an equivalent scanner. Verify test/validation/holdout separation at ingest time and use hashes or split manifests to catch contamination. Inspect per-run PII, secret, copyright, and safety scan reports, then spot-check by rerunning a scanner over sampled records and diffing against the stored report. Confirm records that fail sensitive-data, source-policy, or holdout checks are rejected or quarantined before training and cannot remain in cached intermediate artifacts. For Hugging Face-sourced datasets, cross-check repository names, owner organizations, and download-spike anomalies against the typosquatting patterns reported in the March 2026 ACM Internetware study (e.g., near-miss names like `bert-base-uncasedd`). Treat configuration and context files pulled alongside data as in-scope fields: validate config keys against an expected schema before deserialization, and screen ingested text for zero-width and bidirectional Unicode. Where datasets carry Croissant 1.1 metadata, confirm ingest actually evaluates its ODRL/DUO use-policy assertions rather than discarding them. | Automated PII and safety classifiers have false negatives, especially for non-English data, domain-specific identifiers, images, audio, and context-dependent personal information. They also do not decide which business features are necessary. Treat this as a field-justification control, not only a scanner control: high-risk datasets still need human review, documented field-level rationale, and linkage to C12.1 for privacy-specific minimization and anonymization checks. Squatting detection is still mostly manual and depends on registry hygiene from the hosting platform. |
| **1.1.3** | **Verify that** all dataset changes are subject to a logged approval workflow. | 1 | Unauthorized data modification or injection (MITRE ATLAS AML.T0020 Poison Training Data and AML.T0020.000 tainted data from acquisition); insider insertion of poisoned records; untracked composition drift that hides slow-burn poisoning; loss of operational evidence for EU AI Act Article 10 and ISO/IEC 42001 Annex A.7.4/A.7.5, which expect a documented chain of custody covering creation, updates, transfers, and transformations. | Inspect dataset-level version control. lakeFS supports pull requests over data branches so reviewers can inspect diffs and run validation before merge; DVC (now stewarded by lakeFS after the November 2025 acquisition) supports data registries backed by Git history, pull requests, and CI/CD around `.dvc` and lockfile changes. Confirm the production branch is protected, merges require review, and checks for schema, PII, license, source policy, poisoning heuristics, and holdout leakage must pass. Verify audit logs capture approver identity, timestamp, justification, changed source set, and validation result. The CMU SEI chain-of-custody work (April 2026) is a useful audit framing: checksums and signatures on every artifact, metadata and access documentation at each handoff, and chain-of-custody-aware data loaders that refuse to feed training jobs from artifacts whose custody record fails verification — check whether the pipeline enforces anything equivalent before the training step. OpenMetadata 1.13.0's governance approval tasks now render a reviewable "Proposed Changes" diff with clickable entity links, making catalog-level change approval auditable without leaving the catalog. ISO/IEC 24970 (AI system logging) reached FDIS in May 2026 and defines an information model for logging events across models, datasets, requests, and outcomes — it is a harmonized-standard candidate under the EU AI Act and the likely future normative target for what an approval log must capture. Check integration with GitHub/GitLab, Jira, MLflow, Airflow, or the MLOps platform so approval evidence survives incident response and compliance review. Where Databricks is in scope, confirm Unity Catalog access-control changes, table-version history, and AI BOM exports also pass through the same review gate. | lakeFS and DVC provide workflow primitives, but approval policy usually lives in the Git provider, CI system, or platform configuration. Plain S3 versioning, Git-LFS, or notebook-driven uploads may record object history without any review gate. For regulated or high-impact systems, require immutable logs and a break-glass procedure that records who bypassed the normal workflow and why. ISO/IEC 42001 auditors flag missing data-provenance documentation as a common nonconformity, so the approval log should bind to a catalog entry rather than living only in the version-control tool. Texas AG civil investigative demands under TRAIGA can compel training-data descriptions and post-deployment monitoring records with no prior notice of violation, so a missing or unreconstructable approval log is now litigation-readiness risk, not just an audit finding. |
| **1.1.4** | **Verify that** datasets or subsets are watermarked or fingerprinted so their later use can be attributed and any unauthorized use detected. | 3 | Unauthorized redistribution of proprietary datasets (MITRE ATLAS AML.T0010.002 ML Supply Chain Compromise: Data); inability to determine whether a suspect model or derivative dataset used licensed, embargoed, stolen, or poisoned data; weak copyright/license evidence for AB 2013 disclosures and AI-BOM attestations. | Check whether high-value datasets have a stored fingerprint or watermark before release, sharing, or training. [Datasig](https://github.com/trailofbits/datasig) produces compact MinHash-based fingerprints over a canonicalized dataset and is designed to feed AIBOM entries and incident triage without exchanging raw data. For text corpora, monitor TRACE-style black-box detection of copyrighted dataset usage via distortion-free watermarked rewrites (arXiv:2510.02962, Oct 2025) and SPECTRA-style paraphrase-guided training-data watermarking (preprint arXiv:2512.17075, accepted to AAAI 2026), which reported detection below 0.001% of the training corpus. For image-generation pipelines, track radioactive watermarking work such as HMARK for diffusion models. Confirm fingerprints, watermark keys, salts, canonicalization rules, and detection thresholds are stored beside catalog entries, tied to dataset versions, exported in CycloneDX 1.7 ML-BOM provenance/citation fields when relevant, and exercised in an annual unauthorized-use detection test. | As of May 2026, dataset fingerprinting and radioactive data watermarks remain maturing controls rather than universal proof. Datasig is promising but prototype-oriented; MinHash similarity estimates are approximate and depend heavily on canonicalization choices. Text watermarks can be weakened by paraphrasing, deduplication, filtering, or format conversion, and image watermarks vary by model family and fine-tuning method. Unforgeability and recoverability guarantees from recent academic work are not yet standard in production toolchains. The field is consolidating, though: an IJCAI-ECAI 2026 survey (arXiv:2605.29245, May 2026) finally separates fingerprinting (non-intrusive, similarity-based attribution) from watermarking (deliberately embedded, keyed verification) across datasets, models, and generated content, and proposes evaluating any scheme on identifiability, robustness, and deployability — a sensible checklist when vendors claim "watermarked datasets." Zero-knowledge training proofs bound to data provenance (arXiv:2510.16830) are an emerging complement but remain research-stage. Regulatory pull is also arriving slower here than elsewhere: the May 2026 Digital Omnibus agreement adds grace periods for the AI Act's watermarking and marking obligations. And note what still does not exist — OpenSSF Model Signing and Sigstore's model-transparency project remain model-focused as of June 2026 with dataset signing listed as future work, so there is no widely deployed signing or watermark-verification layer for datasets on the major hubs, a gap the May 2026 Hive Security analysis of Hugging Face abuse calls out explicitly. Treat this whole layer as attribution, deterrence, and triage that complements provenance and approval controls, not as the primary integrity control. |

## Notable Incidents

* **2026-06 — CVE-2026-4372: Transformers RCE via poisoned model config.** A malicious `_attn_implementation_internal` key in a model's `config.json` is applied through unfiltered `setattr` parsing in Hugging Face Transformers 4.56.0–5.2.x, silently executing attacker code from a remote repository even with `trust_remote_code=False` when the kernels package is present. Vulnerable versions were still seeing roughly 7–8 million weekly downloads at disclosure — a direct demonstration that unvalidated fields in pulled artifacts are an ingestion attack surface, not a hardening nice-to-have. [CSO Online coverage](https://www.csoonline.com/article/4181094/hugging-face-transformers-rce-flaw-enables-stealthy-compromise-via-ai-model-configs.html)
* **2026-05 — Hive Security maps Hugging Face as a malware distribution platform; InstructLab CVE-2026-6859.** A May 29 analysis documented six additional malicious repositories from a single actor (all uploaded April 24, 2026), CVE-2026-6859 — InstructLab hardcoding `trust_remote_code=True` in `linux_train.py`, enabling RCE via malicious Hugging Face models during `ilab train/download/generate` (CVSS 8.8) — and the ClawHavoc campaign of 341 malicious agent skills. The analysis explicitly notes the ecosystem still has no provenance or watermark verification layer as a defense, only post-incident detection. [Hive Security analysis](https://hivesecurity.gitlab.io/blog/huggingface-ai-supply-chain-attacks-2026/)
* **2026-05 — TrapDoor campaign poisons AI assistant context files across three package ecosystems.** Active since May 19, 2026: 34 malicious packages across npm, PyPI, and Crates.io (384 versions) stole SSH keys, cloud credentials, and tokens — and hid instructions for AI coding assistants in zero-width Unicode (U+200B/200C/200D/FEFF) inside `.cursorrules` and `CLAUDE.md` files. Standard scanners returned zero findings, and no CVEs were assigned. Context and configuration files are an unvetted data-ingest channel that field allow-listing and character screening need to cover. [Phoenix Security writeup](https://phoenix.security/trapdoor-supply-chain-ai-poisoning-npm-pypi-crates/)
* **2026-05 — DPRK npm campaign uses Hugging Face as malware host and exfiltration backend.** A North Korea-linked group behind the `terminal-logger-utils` npm packages hosted second-stage Node.js binaries in a Hugging Face repository (`Lordplay/system-releases`) and exfiltrated stolen developer credentials into private Hugging Face datasets, where the traffic blended into normal AI research activity. Dataset platforms are now part of the attacker's infrastructure, not just a poisoning target — provenance checks need to cover where data goes as well as where it came from. [Cyber Security News writeup](https://cybersecuritynews.com/hackers-use-hugging-face/)
* **2026-05 — Scale of unsafe artifacts on Hugging Face quantified.** Industry scans published in early May 2026 reported roughly 352,000 unsafe or suspicious findings across 51,700 models out of more than 4 million scanned on Hugging Face, plus 100+ models capable of arbitrary code execution — many abusing pickle deserialization tricks (the "nullifAI" technique) that evade the platform's PickleScan. Source allow-listing and pre-import scanning remain load-bearing because platform-side moderation demonstrably does not catch everything. [TNW coverage](https://thenextweb.com/news/hugging-face-clawhub-malware-ai-supply-chain)
* **2026-05 — Fake OpenAI repository on Hugging Face distributes infostealer.** A repository impersonating an OpenAI project on Hugging Face delivered infostealer malware through trending-list and SEO poisoning, targeting developers and data scientists pulling AI assets. Defenders highlighted the need to validate publisher identity, download anomalies, and dataset/model attestations against AIBOM entries before allowing them into pipelines. [Rescana writeup](https://www.rescana.com/post/supply-chain-attack-fake-openai-repository-on-hugging-face-distributes-infostealer-malware-targeting-developers-and-ai-t/)
* **2026-03 — Hugging Face typosquatting study.** Researchers analyzed 1,020,755 models and 219,812 datasets and identified 1,574 squatting models (10.4% with malicious traits) and 625 dataset typosquats (42.2% intentional impersonation). The paper, presented at the 16th Internetware conference, recommended platform-side detection plus consumer-side allow-listing. [ACM Internetware 2026 paper](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3755881.3755921)
* **2026-03 — NSA/CISA/FBI eight-nation AI/ML supply chain guidance.** Joint guidance treats training data as one of six AI supply chain components and calls for AI BOMs, cryptographic integrity, and threat modeling across acquisition, fine-tuning, and deployment. [CSA-hosted PDF](https://labs.cloudsecurityalliance.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/CSA_research_note_nsa_allied_ai_supply_chain_security_guidance_20260317-csa-styled.pdf)

[Norton Rose Fulbright analysis](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en-us/knowledge/publications/c1df8419/california-district-court-upholds-transparency-requirements-for-generative-ai-training-data)
* **2026-01 — Masquerading "OpenAI" model on Hugging Face reaches ~244K downloads.** A malicious model file posing as an OpenAI release accumulated roughly 244,000 downloads before being flagged, underscoring how trending lists and weak source attestation can spread untrusted artifacts deep into downstream training and fine-tuning workflows. ## Implementation Maturity

* **Source inventory & catalogs (1.1.1):** Mature for technical metadata and column-level lineage (DataHub, OpenMetadata, Marquez/OpenLineage, Unity Catalog). Emerging for AI-specific provenance attestation — CycloneDX 1.7 and the OWASP AIBOM Generator are pulling field standards forward, and Croissant 1.1's PROV-O provenance chains (Feb 2026, ~700K datasets across Hugging Face, Kaggle, and OpenML, adopted by Common Crawl) give the first web-scale machine-actionable origin metadata — but completeness scoring and audit-ready exports are still uneven across stacks. A peer-reviewed CycloneDX-extension schema for trusted research environments (Frontiers in Computer Science, Jan 2026) adds dataset-steward, data-use-agreement linkage, and preprocessing-tracking fields worth borrowing, and Hugging Face's OIDC workflow-identity publishing (June 8, 2026) finally gives CI-published hub artifacts verifiable publisher identity.
* **Field allow-listing & sensitive-data exclusion (1.1.2):** Mature for English-language PII scanning (Presidio, Macie, Google Cloud SDP). Emerging for multilingual, multimodal, and context-dependent identifiers, for benchmark/holdout contamination detection at web-scale corpora, and for invisible-Unicode and config-field screening — the TrapDoor and CVE-2026-4372 cases (May–June 2026) show standard scanners miss both.
* **Logged approval workflows (1.1.3):** Mature when teams adopt lakeFS or DVC with protected branches and CI policy gates; OpenMetadata 1.13.0 now brings reviewable change-approval diffs into the catalog itself. Emerging when changes flow through plain object stores, notebook uploads, or hosted-platform UIs without forced review; ISO/IEC 42001 audits frequently catch this gap, and ISO/IEC 24970 (FDIS, May 2026) is on track to standardize what AI-system event logs must capture.
* **Fingerprinting & watermarking (1.1.4):** Emerging. Datasig fingerprints and TRACE/SPECTRA/HMARK-style watermarks are credible attribution layers but not yet drop-in production controls; the May 2026 IJCAI-ECAI survey gives the field shared terminology and evaluation criteria (identifiability, robustness, deployability), which should make vendor claims easier to compare through 2026–2027. Dataset signing remains absent from the OpenSSF Model Signing and Sigstore model-transparency scope as of June 2026.
