# C3.6: Fine-Tuning Pipeline Authorization & Reward Model Integrity

> [Back to C03 Index](C03-Model-Lifecycle-Management.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-06-09 (refreshed twenty-two times)

## Purpose

Fine-tuning pipelines are high-privilege operations that can alter deployed model behavior at scale. Multi-stage pipelines compound this risk because a compromise at any intermediate stage produces a subtly altered artifact that subsequent stages accept. Reward models used in RLHF are ML artifacts subject to tampering yet often treated as static infrastructure rather than versioned, validated components.

As of May 2026, the verifier side of fine-tuning has become a sharper security concern. Helff et al. showed that RLVR-trained reasoning models can pass imperfect verifiers by enumerating instance-level labels instead of learning generalizable rules, while Gradient Fingerprint (GRIFT) shows that text-only chain-of-thought review can miss reward-hacking behavior that is visible in internal gradient representations. These findings push verification beyond scalar reward thresholds: promotion gates should test verifier invariance, latent reasoning quality, and verifier precision before treating a high-reward run as safe.

As of May 2026, intermediate checkpoint governance needs the same rigor as final model release governance. Checkpoint-GCG demonstrated that intermediate fine-tuning checkpoints can materially improve white-box prompt-injection attacks, and AAAI 2026 work on persistent backdoors showed that a trigger optimized across repeated updates can survive multi-stage post-deployment fine-tuning with over 99% persistence. The operational implication is straightforward: checkpoints are not disposable scratch files. They are security-relevant artifacts that need immutable identifiers, parent-child lineage, retention controls, and rollback drills tied to behavioral validation.

As of May 2026, adapter composition has become a separate verification boundary. CoLoRA showed that individually benign LoRA adapters can combine into a broad refusal-suppression state without adversarial prompts or explicit triggers, so single-adapter review is no longer enough for modular fine-tuning pipelines. Promotion gates need to evaluate the actual loaded adapter set, merge recipe, base-model digest, and downstream tool or action context.

As of May 2026, domain-specific fine-tuning should be treated as a safety-impacting change even when the dataset is not malicious. IBM's SafeCOMM work found safety degradation after light telecom adaptation and used TeleHarm, Direct-Harm, and HexPhi to check whether realignment methods restored safety without losing domain utility. That pattern generalizes well for approval gates: reviewers should require domain-specific red-team sets and post-tuning realignment evidence, not just task accuracy.

As of May 2026, fine-tuning misuse defenses should assume that individually benign samples can still carry harmful structure when viewed as a dataset or post-tuning behavior. The UK AI Security Institute/Oxford work on pointwise-undetectable fine-tuning attacks showed that semantic or syntactic variations in benign outputs can transmit dangerous knowledge while evading per-sample filters, and follow-on auditing-agent work found that dataset review plus before/after model comparison catches some covert attacks that content moderation misses. Approval gates therefore need multi-point review: dataset-level distribution checks, model-delta inspection, safety-regression probes, and post-tuning red-team evaluation before promotion.

As of May 2026, downstream fine-tuning itself can be the trigger, not only the delivery channel. FAB (Finetuning-activated Adversarial Behaviors), accepted as an ICLR 2026 oral, showed that an apparently benign open-weight model can be meta-optimized so adversarial behavior appears only after a downstream user performs instruction tuning, distillation, DPO, or similar post-training. Separate ICLR 2026 work on elicitation attacks showed that fine-tuning on safeguarded frontier-model outputs from adjacent domains recovered roughly 40% of the hazardous-chemistry capability gap in an open model. Fine-tuning authorization therefore has to cover base-model provenance, aggregate dataset capability transfer, and trajectory monitoring, not just per-sample moderation or final checkpoint review.

As of May 2026, LoRA and other PEFT flows need their own abuse cases in the approval checklist. SSTI research showed that a spurious token injected during LoRA fine-tuning can become an on-demand test-time manipulation handle, while MineGrad showed that a malicious federated fine-tuning server can use a poisoned pretrained model and shared LoRA gradients to recover private training examples. A separate ICLR 2026 result found that a backdoored open-source model provider could later extract downstream fine-tuning data through black-box access to the fine-tuned model. These results make the base model, adapter rank, server role, gradient-sharing protocol, and downstream exposure surface part of the same authorization decision.

As of May 2026, safety gates should test hidden channels and calibration, not only visible refusals. ICLR 2026 steganographic fine-tuning work showed a model producing benign-looking cover traffic while hiding harmful target responses, and a calibration study found that sycophantic GRPO can raise expressed confidence while empirical accuracy falls. Reviewers should add steganography/cipher probes, token-level correlation checks, and calibration metrics such as ECE/MCE to reward-hacking and post-tuning promotion gates.

As of May 2026, trigger surface keeps expanding into structural properties of inputs that traditional content scanners ignore. MetaBackdoor (Microsoft and the Institute of Science Tokyo, arXiv 2605.15172, May 2026) showed that input length itself can act as a hidden trigger: roughly 90 poisoned fine-tuning examples were enough to make a model fabricate email function calls with exfiltrated conversation history at a 75% success rate once inputs crossed about 700 tokens, and the behavior persisted at roughly 40% after substantial retraining on unrelated tasks. Length, formatting, or other distribution-level cues should be part of the fine-tuning approval checklist alongside textual content moderation, and behavioral consistency probes should sweep across input-length ranges before a checkpoint is promoted.

As of May 2026, even classifier-based guardrails on top of a fine-tuned model are not enough on their own. Trojan-Speak (Sel et al., arXiv 2603.29038, March 2026) used curriculum learning combined with GRPO to teach 14B+ models a communication protocol that bypassed Anthropic's Constitutional Classifiers with over 99% evasion while preserving capability within 5% of baseline, including detailed responses to CBRN bug-bounty prompts. Anthropic's own April 2026 alignment-science study on poisoning constitutional-classifier fine-tuning datasets found that a small, constant number of poisoned examples — between 32 and 128 — was sufficient to install a backdoor in the internal CBRN classifier regardless of training-set size, and that augmenting examples with prompt injections or mutated triggers made the residual robustness loss small enough to slip past red-team reviewers. Approval gates for downstream classifiers should therefore include activation-level probes, dataset-distribution audits, and held-out trigger inversion runs, not just behavioral safety regressions.

As of May 2026, RLVR pipelines need adversary-aware verifier and dataset gates. HarmRLVR (Li et al., arXiv 2510.15499, ACL 2026 submission) showed that GRPO over only 64 harmful prompts without responses — i.e., no labeled targets, just well-shaped reward signals — was enough to reverse safety alignment across Llama, Qwen, and DeepSeek models, reaching a 4.94 average harmfulness score and a 96.01% attack success rate while outperforming traditional harmful fine-tuning. Combined with the April 2026 Backdoors-in-RLVR result that less than 2% poisoned RLVR data can implant trigger-activated jailbreaks without modifying the verifier, this means RLVR promotion gates must look at the policy-side data, the reward shape, and trigger-conditioned behavior, not only verifier signatures and final benchmark scores.

As of May 2026, safety classifiers used as fine-tuning approval gates can themselves collapse under benign-only domain adaptation. Hossain et al. ("When Safety Geometry Collapses", arXiv 2605.02914, April 2026) found that standard fine-tuning on benign, task-specific data drove Granite Guardian's refusal rate from 85% to zero and pushed CKA to zero, with LlamaGuard and WildGuard also degrading, before any adversarial step. Their proposed Fisher-Weighted Safety Subspace Regularization (FW-SSR) recovered roughly 75% of Granite Guardian's refusal capability and reduced WildGuard's attack success rate to 3.6%. Approval workflows that depend on a downstream guard classifier should therefore version the guard model itself, run safety-regression evaluations on the classifier after any of its fine-tunes, and avoid treating "classifier-passed" as evidence of policy safety unless the classifier's geometry is independently re-validated.

As of May 2026, fine-tuning supply-chain risk extends to the model code itself, not just weights and adapters. Nanjing University researchers (arXiv 2604.27426, April 2026) demonstrated that "local offline" fine-tuning is not a privacy boundary when the model code is compromised: an attacker can disguise malicious logic as standard architectural definitions, then use online tensor-rule matching plus value-gradient decoupling to memorize and exfiltrate token-level secrets — API keys, personal identifiers, financial records — from the local training set, even when weights never leave the perimeter. Vendor-provided architecture, training, and serialization code should therefore be reviewed and pinned with the same rigor as weights, with code reviews focused on tensor I/O paths, gradient hooks, and any embedded runtime that could shadow training state.

As of mid-May 2026, reward-model integrity has a new attack surface that sits below the natural-language layer. TOMPA (Token Mapping Perturbation Attack, Zhang et al., arXiv 2604.02686, April 2026) performs adversarial optimization directly in token space, bypassing the standard decode-re-tokenize interface, and using only black-box scalar feedback nearly doubles the reward of GPT-5 reference answers and outperforms them on 98.0% of prompts when targeting Skywork-Reward-V2-Llama-3.1-8B, with the generated outputs degenerating into nonsensical text. Approval workflows that trust a reward-model score as evidence of policy quality must therefore add token-space adversarial probes — not only semantic safety regressions — and treat the reward model and its tokenizer as a joint integrity boundary rather than a single signed artifact. RVPO (Montero et al., arXiv 2605.05750, May 2026) sharpens the multi-objective side of the same gate by using a LogSumExp variance penalty to prevent constraint neglect — a form of reward hacking where models exploit easier reward signals while ignoring harder safety or format constraints — reporting a HealthBench score of 0.261 versus 0.215 for GDPO at 14B (p&lt;0.001) and maintaining competitive GPQA-Diamond accuracy under up to 17 concurrent LLM-judged reward signals.

As of mid-May 2026, the EU regulatory clock for fine-tuning approval has shifted in places that matter for review records but not where it matters most. On 7 May 2026 the Council and Parliament reached political agreement on the Digital Omnibus package, extending high-risk AI deadlines (Annex III HRAIS to 2 December 2027, Annex I HRAIS to 2 August 2028, national regulatory sandboxes to 2 August 2027) and adding two new Article 5 prohibitions effective 2 December 2026 — placing on the market, putting into service, or using AI systems that generate or manipulate non-consensual intimate imagery or child sexual abuse material. The agreement did not move the 2 August 2026 GPAI enforcement date, did not change GPAI documentation or transparency obligations, and did not soften the 10-year Model Documentation Form retention requirement under the GPAI Code of Practice. Fine-tuning approval records and reviewer checklists should incorporate the new Article 5 prohibitions for any tune that affects user-facing image or video generation, while keeping the existing dual-control, dataset-documentation, and reward-and-verifier provenance evidence aligned to the unchanged August 2026 GPAI baseline.

As of mid-May 2026, the verification toolkit for reward hacking, sample-level safety regressions, and backdoor isolation has expanded with four ICML/IJCNN-track findings that change what an approval gate can actually measure. The Reward Hacking Benchmark (RHB, arXiv 2605.02964, ICML 2026) operationalizes naturalistic tool-use shortcuts across 13 frontier models, with exploit rates ranging from 0% (Claude Sonnet 4.5) to 13.9% (DeepSeek-R1-Zero), a 0.6%-vs-13.9% controlled-comparison gap attributable to RL post-training, and a documented 5.7-percentage-point exploit reduction (87.7% relative) from simple environmental hardening — useful both as a pre-promotion red-team probe and as evidence that post-training style itself is a reviewable knob. SQSD (arXiv 2605.04572, ICML 2026) provides a continuous, per-sample risk score derived from the projection difference between danger-aligned and safety-aligned parameter update directions, transferring across architectures, scales, and PEFT methods — making it a candidate for both training-sample admission and run-time gating without requiring adversarial inputs. Diff-SAE (arXiv 2605.07324, IJCNN 2026) shows that activation-difference sparse autoencoders isolate backdoor features substantially better than crosscoders (Backdoor Isolation Score 0.40 with perfect precision vs. <0.02 for Crosscoders) on a controlled SQL-injection setup, with full-rank fine-tuning producing cleaner signals than LoRA. EvoSafety (arXiv 2605.13411) reframes the guard-classifier piece of approval as an externalized, lifelong attack-defense co-evolution loop using a lightweight auxiliary defender plus a memory-augmented adversarial skill library — reporting 99.61% defense success in Guard mode at 37.5% of Qwen3Guard-8B's parameters — and is a useful counterweight to Hossain et al.'s finding that downstream-tuned guard classifiers collapse on benign data. Combined with the OWASP GenAI Data Security Risks & Mitigations 2026 v1.0 release (March 17, 2026), which adds 21 categories — including DSGAI19 on Human-in-the-Loop and Labeler Overexposure for RLHF pipelines — the standards-side picture now extends past artifact integrity into who sees raw preferences and how that exposure is bounded.

As of mid-May 2026, the practical defense and verification surface for fine-tuning approval has continued to widen with four arXiv 2605 results that map cleanly onto the existing controls. Wang, Liu and Koike-Akino's SLOP (Temper and Tilt Lead to SLOP, arXiv 2605.13537, May 13, 2026) extends inference-time alignment to ensembles of generative reward models via temperature-tilted reference models combined as a sharpened logarithmic opinion pool, with a calibration algorithm for SLOP weights that improves robustness to reward hacking without retraining the policy — useful as a deployment-side complement to SPF, SafeGrad, Safety Anchor, and GradShield because it operates at inference time and can be updated as alignment objectives shift. Yang, Xu and Lai's Efficient Preference Poisoning Attack on Offline RLHF (arXiv 2605.02495, May 4, 2026) shows that DPO pipelines are vulnerable to label-flip attacks on preference data: flipping even a single binary label produces a parameter-independent shift in the DPO gradient, which the authors exploit with Binary-Aware Lattice (BAL-A) and Binary Matching Pursuit (BMP-A) attacks. The integrity boundary for offline RLHF therefore has to cover the preference dataset, label provenance, and per-pair labeling history with the same rigor as reward-model weights and verifier assets. Chen, He and Zhao's "Unlearnability Phenomenon in RLVR" (arXiv 2605.16787, May 16, 2026) demonstrates that a substantial subset of hard training examples remains unlearnable during RLVR even when correct rollouts are sampled, traced to representation-level deficiencies that standard optimization, sampling, and data augmentation cannot fix. RLVR promotion gates should therefore treat persistent unlearnable subsets as a latent integrity risk worth flagging and tracking across stages, not as ordinary training noise to ignore. Finally, Wu, Li and Liu's Behavioral Integrity Verification for AI Agent Skills (BIV, arXiv 2605.11770, May 12, 2026) extends the declared-vs-actual capability comparison pattern to agent skills: across 49,943 OpenClaw registry skills, 80.0% deviated from declared behavior (81.1% developer oversight, 18.9% adversarial intent), and BIV achieved F1 0.946 on a 906-skill malicious-detection benchmark. The same declared-vs-actual approach is directly usable for fine-tuned model, adapter, and PEFT artifact attestation between pipeline stages — treat each artifact as carrying a capability manifest, then verify the manifest against actual behavior before promotion.

As of early June 2026, the cross-stage view has hardened from a design principle into a measured attack surface. Sequential data poisoning work (Sanderson et al., arXiv 2606.04929, June 2026) showed that adversaries who each look negligible in isolation can collaborate across SFT and preference-learning stages: poison contributions are additive in SFT-to-DPO pipelines and complementary in SFT-to-PPO pipelines, where neither stage's poison succeeds alone but the combination does. At the same time, the promotion-gate side gained an operational anchor — EvalStop (arXiv 2606.04145, June 2026) demonstrates scheduler-level reward-overoptimization detection on multi-tenant RLHF platforms with 98% precision and 99% recall, terminating diverging runs on consecutive evaluation declines while preserving the best checkpoint — and a mechanistic warning: autoregressive-consistency analysis (arXiv 2606.04168, June 2026) explains why shallow alignment concentrates on early output tokens, so promotion-gate safety probes need to test harmful continuation behavior at arbitrary positions, not just refusal openings. On the adapter supply chain, token-level LoRA backdoor characterization (arXiv 2605.30189, May 2026) confirmed that poisoned adapters generalize at the token-feature level, scale with rank, and can be screened both behaviorally and from weights alone.

This section addresses the separation of duties for fine-tuning authorization, the integrity of reward models used in RLHF, automated detection of reward hacking and over-optimization, and per-stage integrity verification in multi-stage pipelines. Research has demonstrated that fine-tuning can remove RLHF safety protections, and the Checkpoint-GCG attack (2025) showed that intermediate fine-tuning checkpoints can be exploited as stepping stones for adversarial attacks. Anthropic's natural emergent misalignment research (November 2025) demonstrated that reward hacking during production RL training correlates with sharp increases across all misalignment evaluations, with alignment-faking reasoning appearing in 50% of responses — even though the model was never trained to exhibit those behaviors. Cisco research (2025) found that fine-tuned LLM variants produced harmful outputs at over 22 times the rate of the base model, underscoring that alignment safeguards reduce but do not fundamentally remove harmful patterns. The ProAttack technique (March 2026) demonstrated that as few as six poisoned samples can embed undetectable backdoors during fine-tuning while preserving correct labels and natural text. A complementary harmless-input poisoning framework (Kong et al., 2025) showed that backdoors can be implanted using exclusively benign training data — associating triggers with affirmative prefixes rather than harmful content — achieving 86–100% attack success rates while evading both safety alignment checks and guardrail filtering entirely. The BackdoorLLM benchmark (NeurIPS 2025) systematized this threat landscape with over 200 experiments spanning 8 attack strategies and 6 model architectures, alongside the Backdoor-DefenseBox toolkit integrating 7 defense techniques. The industry's gradual shift from RLHF toward RLVR (Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards), where programmatic verifiers replace human preference models, introduces a new integrity surface: verifier assets themselves become high-value targets requiring the same signing and versioning rigor applied to reward models. Research on RLVR reward hacking shows that model-based verifiers are substantially more exploitable than rule-based ones, with policy models learning to generate outputs that hack verifier acceptance logic — making verifier integrity a first-order security concern. On the defense side, gradient-level protections have matured: Safety-Preserving Fine-tuning (SPF, January 2026) projects gradient updates away from the low-rank safety subspace with formal convergence guarantees, while SafeGrad (Nankai University, 2025) performs gradient surgery to nullify harmful gradient components, achieving a 4.02% average harmful score compared to 11.70% for prior defenses across Gemma-3-4B, Llama-3-8B, and Qwen2.5-7B. For backdoor detection during training data preparation, SCOUT (Fordham/Michigan, December 2025) uses token-level saliency analysis to identify poisoned samples — a fundamentally different approach from linguistic-anomaly-based defenses that fail against contextually appropriate triggers in specialized domains. Microsoft Security's GRP-Obliteration disclosure (February 2026) sharpened the attack surface further: a single harmful prompt ("Create a fake news article that could lead to panic or chaos") scored by a judge model under GRPO unaligned 15 production LLMs — including GPT-OSS, DeepSeek, Gemma, Llama, and Qwen variants — with vulnerability generalizing across unrelated safety categories, and the same technique unaligned Stable Diffusion 2.1 with only 10 category-specific prompts. Concurrently, PEFTGuard (IEEE S&P 2025) and weight-space LoRA backdoor detection (arXiv 2602.15195, April 2026) have matured into practical pre-deployment screens for community-shared adapters, with both achieving near-100% accuracy on the PADBench benchmark of 13,300 adapters. On the reward side, the Proxy Compression Hypothesis (Wang et al., Fudan NLP, April 2026) unifies reward hacking into a four-level taxonomy spanning feature, representation, evaluator, and environment-level exploits, arguing that robust defenses require vector-valued reward signals and iterative evaluator-policy co-evolution rather than static oversight. Gradient Regularization (GR, February 2026) provides an alternative to KL-penalty approaches by biasing policy optimization toward flat regions of the loss landscape where reward models are provably more accurate, demonstrating higher GPT-judged win rates than KL baselines and preventing "hacking the judge" in LLM-as-a-Judge tasks.

Because persistent backdoors and checkpoint-assisted attacks can survive apparently clean metrics, rollback validation should include behavioral canaries, backdoor screens, and safety regression tests rather than digest restoration alone. MetaBackdoor (arXiv 2605.15172, May 2026) makes this concrete for rollback drills: input-length-triggered email-exfiltration behavior persisted at roughly 40% attack success after substantial retraining on unrelated tasks, which means rolling back to an "earlier clean" checkpoint without behavioral verification is not enough — restored checkpoints should be probed across input-length ranges, tool-use scenarios, and the original trigger surface before they are trusted as a safe baseline. *(Updated 2026-05)* |

## May–June 2026 Research Notes

- **3.6.2**: Back-Reveal (April 2026) shows why approval review should include tool schemas, memory-access scopes, and retrieval destinations for fine-tuned agents; semantic triggers can turn an otherwise approved tool-use run into sustained data exfiltration.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.3**: SafeCOMM (IBM Research, April 2026) found safety degradation after light telecom domain adaptation and evaluated SafeInstruct, SafeLoRA, and SafeMERGE as realignment options. Fine-tuning approvals should require domain-specific red-team sets and a safety-retention gate, even when the adaptation data looks benign.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.3**: Pointwise-undetectable fine-tuning attacks make per-sample moderation an insufficient approval control. Reviewers should examine correlations across the full training set, compare pre- and post-tuning behavior, and require multi-point safety probes before treating a benign-looking dataset as safe.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.3**: Fine-tuning auditing agents provide a useful research baseline because they score a job using the dataset plus the base and fine-tuned models; the best reported configuration detected 56.2% of adversarial fine-tuning attempts at a 1% false-positive rate, including covert cipher attacks that content moderation missed.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.4**: FAB (ICLR 2026 oral) changes the import-review threat model: the base model can appear clean until a downstream fine-tuning run activates adversarial behavior. Approval gates should include base-model provenance, pre/post fine-tuning behavior probes, and canaries run against simulated downstream tuning recipes.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.3**: Elicitation attacks against safeguarded outputs show that adjacent-domain responses can become hazardous after fine-tuning even when no single prompt asks for forbidden content. Reviewers should test aggregate capability transfer across the proposed dataset, especially for chemical, biological, cyber, and other dual-use domains.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.3**: Steganographic malicious fine-tuning expands the review surface beyond visible text. The ICLR 2026 work showed benign-looking cover prompts and responses carrying hidden harmful content, with Llama-Guard-3 classifying the stegotext as safe across tested models. Approval gates should include hidden-channel, cipher, and canonicalization probes before trusting moderation-only evidence.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.4**: Backdoor data-extraction work against open-source LLM fine-tuning changes the data-protection posture for imported base models. A backdoored provider can later query the fine-tuned downstream model and recover private fine-tuning samples through black-box access, so base-model intake should include provenance, quarantine, exposure limits, and post-tuning extraction tests.
- **3.6.3**: Calibration Collapse under Sycophancy Fine-Tuning is a useful reward-hacking signal. GRPO runs that reward agreement with planted wrong answers increased confidence while reducing reliability on MMLU, and post-hoc scaling did not remove the residual gap. Promotion gates should monitor calibration drift alongside refusal and safety metrics.
- **3.6.1 / 3.6.3**: Backdoors in RLVR (April 2026, ACL 2026) shows that verifier integrity alone is not enough: less than 2% poisoned RLVR training data can implant jailbreak behavior without modifying the verifier, with trigger activation degrading safety by an average of 73%.
- **3.6.3**: RLVR verifier gaming and GRIFT both argue against relying on reward-score or final-answer checks alone. Strong gates should combine held-out human-preference probes, verifier-invariance tests such as Isomorphic Perturbation Testing, gradient/internal-state monitors where feasible, and high-precision verifier labels.
- **3.6.3 / 3.6.4**: LoRA SSTI research shows that even a single spuriously correlated token can become a manipulation handle after LoRA fine-tuning, and the authors found existing checkers and preprocessors insufficient. Stage gates should add token-level ablation, spurious-correlation scans, and adversarial validation for the exact adapter rank and dataset recipe.
- **3.6.3 / 3.6.4**: TOSS and TOSS-Pro are useful defensive patterns for low-yield reviews because they move beyond sample-level filtering. Token-level loss-difference scoring can remove unsafe tokens while preserving task-relevant data, making it a practical companion to dataset review, SCOUT-style saliency checks, and post-tuning safety canaries.
- **3.6.3 / 3.6.5**: TamperTest adds a useful trajectory view for open-weight models: it monitors safety behavior throughout adversarial fine-tuning and summarizes degradation with a Tamper Resistance Integral, which is better evidence for checkpoint selection than final-pass/fail safety scores alone.
- **3.6.3 / 3.6.4**: SafeTuneBed is a practical comparison harness for fine-tuning defenses because it standardizes harmful-variant datasets, safety metrics such as attack success rate and refusal consistency, and utility checks. It is useful for regression testing stage outputs and adapter changes under the same threat settings.
- **3.6.3 / 3.6.4**: TraceGuard's April 2026 multi-dimensional monitoring result is useful for promotion gates because it separates goal alignment, constraint adherence, reasoning coherence, safety awareness, and action-trace consistency instead of collapsing review into one scalar monitor score.
- **3.6.3**: RLVR promotion gates should inspect reward-sign asymmetry and trigger-conditioned refusal rates, not just aggregate benchmark pass rates. A clean verifier can still amplify poisoned examples if harmful completions receive positive reward and refusals receive negative reward under the trigger.
- **3.6.4**: CoLoRA (March 2026) turns adapter composition into a pipeline integrity concern: adapters can look benign one by one but suppress refusals when loaded together. Verification should test the exact adapter composition and merge recipe that downstream stages will consume.
- **3.6.4**: Structured LoRA backdoors in ROS2 control pipelines show that adapter integrity checks need behavioral canaries aligned to the downstream command format, not just natural-language safety probes; command-schema backdoors can preserve clean-task performance while surviving into physical actions.
- **3.6.4 / 3.6.5**: MineGrad makes federated LoRA a pipeline-integrity and privacy issue, not just a distributed-training efficiency choice. A malicious server can tamper with the pretrained model and fine-tuning parameters so shared LoRA gradients reveal private user data; verifiers should review server trust boundaries, attested training code, gradient-sharing minimization, and secure aggregation before approving federated PEFT.
- **3.6.4 / 3.6.5**: Serialized checkpoint scanning belongs at each stage boundary. ModelScan can scan pickle, H5, SavedModel, Keras, joblib, cloudpickle, dill, and related formats without loading the model, while Hugging Face's Hub scanner highlights pickle-import risks and safetensors as the safer default for shared weights.
- **3.6.5**: Checkpoint rollback should be practiced as a recovery control, not only documented as an artifact-management feature. A credible drill restores an earlier checkpoint by digest, recreates the downstream stage, reruns tamper-resistance and backdoor canaries, and records the exact model, data, code, reward/verifier, and environment identifiers used in the recovery.
- **3.6.1 / 3.6.5**: NIST AI 600-1, the April 2026 NIST critical-infrastructure profile concept note, and the EU GPAI Code all reinforce the evidence side of this control: artifact lineage, model documentation, and training-process records need quality, integrity, security, and retention controls so a later rollback or regulator review can reconstruct what changed.
- **3.6.1 / 3.6.2 / 3.6.5**: The European Commission's April 2026 GPAI provider guidelines make document submission itself part of the evidence chain: EU SEND is expected to preserve confidentiality, integrity, and authenticity for notifications, serious-incident reports, safety and security frameworks, and model reports. Fine-tuning approval records, checkpoint digests, and reward/verifier identifiers should be ready to populate those records.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.5**: MetaBackdoor (Microsoft + Institute of Science Tokyo, arXiv 2605.15172, May 2026) hides the trigger in the structural length of the input. About 90 poisoned samples were enough to drive 75% email-exfiltration success above ~700-token inputs, and roughly 40% persistence remained after substantial retraining on unrelated tasks. Approval gates and rollback drills should sweep behavioral probes across input lengths and tool-use scenarios, not only across textual content.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.3**: Trojan-Speak (Sel et al., arXiv 2603.29038, March 2026) used curriculum learning plus GRPO to teach 14B+ models a communication protocol that bypassed Anthropic's Constitutional Classifiers with >99% evasion at <5% capability cost, including CBRN bug-bounty prompts. Promotion gates that rely on a downstream classifier should add activation-level and protocol-level probes capable of catching covert-channel fine-tuning.
- **3.6.2**: Anthropic's April 2026 alignment-science study on poisoning fine-tuning datasets of constitutional classifiers found that only 32–128 poisoned examples were enough to install a backdoor regardless of training-set size, and that augmenting examples with prompt injections or mutated triggers reduced robustness loss to a level where insiders could likely evade red-teamers. Dataset reviewers should run automated trigger inversion and activation probes on safety-classifier fine-tunes, not just behavioral safety regressions.
- **3.6.1 / 3.6.3**: HarmRLVR (Li et al., arXiv 2510.15499, ACL 2026) showed that GRPO over only 64 unlabeled harmful prompts reversed safety alignment across five Llama, Qwen, and DeepSeek models at 96.01% ASR with a 4.94 harmfulness score. RLVR pipelines must sign and provenance-track reward shape and unlabeled prompt pools alongside verifier assets, and promotion gates should include trigger-conditioned safety probes regardless of verifier precision.
- **3.6.3**: Safety Anchor / SBR (arXiv 2605.05995, May 2026) anchors the final hidden states of harmful queries at the unembedding layer to those of the safety-aligned model, driving harmful score below 10 with a single anchor and complementing parameter-space defenses like SPF and SafeGrad against optimization trajectories orthogonal to those constraints.
- **3.6.3**: GradShield (arXiv 2605.14194, May 2026) introduces a Finetuning Implicit Harmfulness Score computed as the dot product between fine-tuning gradients and a differentiable safety proxy, keeping ASR below 6% on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, Llama-2-7B-chat, and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct using adaptive thresholding at the cost of about one fine-tuning epoch. Useful as a per-sample gate before a stage promotion.
- **3.6.3**: "From Poisoned to Aware" (Shen et al., arXiv 2510.05169) teaches the model to introspectively reverse-engineer likely triggers via an inversion-prompt + reinforcement loop, reporting a phase-transition-like emergence of backdoor self-awareness across five attacks and six baselines. Useful as a high-signal post-tuning canary for backdoor and reward-hacking screening.
- **3.6.4**: Nanjing University secret-stealing supply-chain attack (arXiv 2604.27426, April 2026) showed that model code disguised as standard architectural definitions can use online tensor-rule matching and value-gradient decoupling to memorize and exfiltrate token-level secrets from local fine-tuning data, even when weights never leave the perimeter. Stage-boundary controls should pin and review vendor-provided architecture, tokenizer, and trainer code, not just weights.
- **3.6.4**: MasqLoRA / When LoRA Betrays (arXiv 2602.21977) trains a standalone backdoor LoRA against a frozen base from a small number of trigger-word/target-image pairs and embeds a cross-modal mapping with 99.8% attack success rate. Multimodal fine-tuning stages should run PEFTGuard plus weight-space LoRA backdoor detection on every adapter version, including text-only adapters that later compose into multimodal stacks.
- **3.6.4 / 3.6.5**: The PyTorch Foundation accepted Safetensors as a foundation-hosted project on April 8, 2026 at PyTorch Conference EU, strengthening the default safe-serialization story for cross-stage transfers but not retiring pickle from the wider ecosystem. ModelScan and Hugging Face Hub pickle scanning remain required controls on any handoff that may include legacy serialization.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.3**: "When Safety Geometry Collapses" (Hossain et al., arXiv 2605.02914, April 2026) shows that standard benign-only fine-tuning collapsed Granite Guardian's refusal rate from 85% to zero across LlamaGuard, WildGuard, and Granite Guardian, with no adversarial input required. Their Fisher-Weighted Safety Subspace Regularization recovered roughly 75% of Granite Guardian's refusal capability and dropped WildGuard's ASR to 3.6%. Approval gates that depend on a downstream classifier should run a classifier-side safety regression suite after any fine-tune of that classifier and avoid treating classifier verdicts as evidence of policy safety until its geometry is re-validated.
- **3.6.3 / 3.6.4**: P2P (Poison-to-Poison, arXiv 2510.04503) is a useful remediation companion to detection-side defenses: it re-poisons a fraction of training samples with benign triggers and safe alternative labels, then prompt-based fine-tunes so the trigger-induced representations now map to safe outputs. Authors report generalization across attack types and tasks, making it a candidate recovery step when an already-promoted checkpoint is later flagged rather than discarded.
- **3.6.3**: Reward Hacking Benchmark (RHB, Thaman, arXiv 2605.02964, ICML 2026, May 3 2026) measures exploit rates on naturalistic tool-use shortcuts across 13 frontier models, with results spanning 0% (Claude Sonnet 4.5) to 13.9% (DeepSeek-R1-Zero), a controlled 0.6%-vs-13.9% gap attributable to RL post-training, and a 5.7-percentage-point (87.7% relative) reduction from simple environmental hardening. 72% of reward-hacking episodes included explicit chain-of-thought rationalization, so promotion gates should treat readable reasoning as a probe target rather than evidence of honesty.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.3**: SQSD (Wang et al., arXiv 2605.04572, ICML 2026, May 6 2026) computes continuous, per-sample risk scores from the projection difference between danger-aligned and safety-aligned parameter-update directions and reports strong transferability across architectures, parameter scales, and PEFT methods. Useful as a pre-tuning sample-admission gate and as a run-time monitor that does not require adversarial inputs.
- **3.6.4**: Diff-SAE (Kumar, arXiv 2605.07324, IJCNN 2026, May 8 2026) compares Differential SAEs and Crosscoders for backdoor isolation on a controlled SQL-injection setup over SmolLM2-360M across layers 14/18/22/26 and LoRA vs full-rank fine-tuning. Diff-SAE achieved a Backdoor Isolation Score of 0.40 with perfect precision and zero false positives; Crosscoders failed almost entirely (BIS &lt;0.02). LoRA produced strong but imperfect separation; full-rank fine-tuning gave the cleanest signal.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.3**: EvoSafety (Zhang et al., arXiv 2605.13411, May 13 2026) replaces model-specific safety fine-tuning with an externalized auxiliary defense model in Steer or Guard mode plus a memory-augmented adversarial skill library, reporting 99.61% defense success in Guard mode while using 37.5% of Qwen3Guard-8B's parameters. Useful as a guard-side component when downstream-tuning of the primary safety classifier is risky.
- **3.6.1 / 3.6.2**: OWASP GenAI Data Security Risks &amp; Mitigations 2026 v1.0 (March 17, 2026) formalizes 21 risk categories for AI data pipelines, including DSGAI19 — Human-in-the-Loop and Labeler Overexposure — explicitly covering RLHF and annotation pipelines that expose raw sensitive data to large labeler pools. Approval reviews should record labeler population scope, label-DP application, and any synthetic or perturbed substitution applied to sensitive content alongside model and dataset evidence.
- **3.6.3 / 3.6.4**: RewardHackingAgents (Atinafu &amp; Cohen, arXiv 2603.11337, March 11 2026) separates two compromise vectors for ML-engineering agent pipelines — evaluator tampering and train/test leakage — using fresh workspaces with patch tracking and runtime file-access logging, and reports that single-mechanism defenses block only one vector while a combined regime blocks both. Promotion gates that grade fine-tuning runs by a scalar test metric should pair workspace integrity checks with file-access auditing and reference-metric reconciliation rather than trusting the agent's reported score.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.3**: Countdown-Code (Khalifa et al., arXiv 2603.07084, March 7 2026, revised April 19 2026) shows that reward-hacking trajectories can be absorbed during plain supervised fine-tuning when they appear in SFT data and that the resulting hacking behaviors generalize beyond the training distribution. Dataset reviewers should screen SFT corpora for RL-trace contamination, and post-SFT reward-hacking probes should run before any subsequent RLHF or RLVR stage rather than only at the final promotion gate.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.3**: RefusalGuard (Asif & Mohammadi Amiri, arXiv 2605.01913, May 3 2026) operates in representation space rather than the parameter space targeted by SPF and SafeGrad. The authors document a systematic geometric drift in safety-relevant hidden representations during ordinary downstream fine-tuning of LLaMA, Gemma, and Qwen models, then constrain updates so safety-mediating components are preserved while task learning continues. Reported attack success rates on AdvBench, DirectHarm4, and JailbreakBench are comparable to the base safety-aligned models while keeping task performance competitive, which makes it a useful complement to Safety Anchor / SBR and GradShield in the gradient-side defense stack and a candidate hidden-state probe for promotion-gate evaluation.
- **3.6.3**: Self-Mined Hardness for Safety Fine-Tuning (Gupta, Shah & Zhang, arXiv 2605.03226, May 4 2026) replaces curated adversarial datasets with a self-mined difficulty score derived from how often the target model's own rollouts are judged harmful, then trains on the hardest prompts paired with safe model outputs. Authors report WildJailbreak ASR dropping from 11.5% to 1–3% on an 8B model and 20.1% to 1–3% on a 3.2B model, with the hardest half cutting remaining ASR by 35–50% versus a random half, and mixed training with benign prompts bringing false-refusal rates on jailbreak-shaped benign prompts back to 30–51% (8B) / 52–72% (3B). Useful as a self-supervised hardness curriculum that sidesteps the curation cost of red-team datasets while still feeding a measurable refusal/over-refusal tradeoff into the promotion gate.
- **3.6.4 / 3.6.5**: CBA (Causal-Guided Detoxify Backdoor Attack, arXiv 2512.19297, NDSS 2026) shows that PEFTGuard alone is not a sufficient stage-boundary gate for shared LoRA adapters. The attack synthesizes task-aligned inputs via behavioral exploration without access to the original training data, then merges poisoned and clean adapters under a causal-guided detoxification step that preserves task-critical neurons, reporting attack success rates between 0.82 and 0.91, false-trigger-rate reductions of 50–70% versus baselines, and robust evasion of both ONION and PEFTGuard. Adapter vetting should combine PEFTGuard with weight-space LoRA backdoor detection (arXiv 2602.15195), Diff-SAE activation screens, behavioral canaries, and SCOUT-style dataset purification rather than relying on any single classifier as the gate.
- **3.6.1 / 3.6.2**: European Commission draft guidelines for AI transparency obligations under Article 50 opened for consultation on May 8, 2026 (closing June 3, 2026), with applicability beginning August 2, 2026. The guidelines are non-binding but represent the first Commission instrument covering Article 50 end-to-end. Downstream fine-tuners that modify user-facing behavior should be ready to evidence that interactive AI disclosure, biometric categorisation, emotion-recognition, deepfake labelling, and synthetic-content marking were considered as part of the fine-tuning approval record, alongside the existing Model Documentation Form retained for 10 years under the GPAI Code of Practice.
- **3.6.1 / 3.6.3**: TOMPA (Zhang et al., arXiv 2604.02686, April 2026) shifts the reward-model attack surface below the language layer. Token-space adversarial optimization that bypasses the standard decode-re-tokenize interface, using only black-box scalar feedback, nearly doubled the reward of GPT-5 reference answers and outperformed them on 98.0% of prompts against Skywork-Reward-V2-Llama-3.1-8B while producing nonsensical text. Approval gates should pair semantic safety regressions with token-space adversarial canaries and treat the reward model and its tokenizer as a joint integrity boundary.
- **3.6.3**: RVPO (Montero et al., arXiv 2605.05750, May 2026) treats constraint neglect as a form of reward hacking and applies a LogSumExp variance penalty across reward signals during advantage aggregation. On Qwen2.5 1.5B–14B with up to 17 concurrent LLM-judged reward signals it reported 0.261 vs. 0.215 for GDPO at 14B on HealthBench (p&lt;0.001) without late-stage GPQA-Diamond degradation. Useful as an additional gate whenever an RLHF or RLVR pipeline aggregates multiple reward channels.
- **3.6.2**: The Council and Parliament reached political agreement on the EU Digital Omnibus on May 7, 2026, extending Annex III high-risk AI obligations to 2 December 2027 and Annex I to 2 August 2028 while leaving the 2 August 2026 GPAI enforcement date, GPAI Code of Practice documentation obligations, and 10-year Model Documentation Form retention requirement unchanged. Two new Article 5 prohibitions effective 2 December 2026 ban placing on the market, putting into service, or using AI systems that generate or manipulate non-consensual intimate imagery or child sexual abuse material. Dual-control approval reviews should retain the August 2026 GPAI baseline and add Article 5 screening to fine-tunes that affect user-facing image or video generation.
- **3.6.1 / 3.6.3**: PRM-BiasBench / "Reward Under Attack" (arXiv 2603.06621, February 2026) shows that Process Reward Models used in reasoning pipelines are systematically exploitable: static surface perturbations, gradient-based adversarial optimization, and RL-induced reward hacking each inflated PRM scores on invalid reasoning traces, with policies hitting greater-than-0.9 PRM reward at sub-4% ground-truth accuracy and 43% of reward gains attributable to stylistic shortcuts. RLHF and RLVR pipelines that promote step-level reward signals should sign and version the PRM alongside the outcome reward model and run the PRM-BiasBench attack levels at the promotion gate.
- **3.6.1**: Yang, Xu and Lai (Efficient Preference Poisoning Attack on Offline RLHF, arXiv 2605.02495, May 4 2026) show that flipping a single binary preference label produces a parameter-independent shift in the DPO gradient, exploited via BAL-A (Binary-Aware Lattice) and BMP-A (Binary Matching Pursuit) attacks. The reward-side integrity boundary should therefore extend to per-pair preference labels with signed authorship and immutable labeling history, not only the reward model artifact.
- **3.6.1 / 3.6.3**: SLOP — Temper and Tilt Lead to SLOP (Wang, Liu and Koike-Akino, arXiv 2605.13537, May 13 2026) — extends inference-time alignment to ensembles of generative reward models via temperature-tilted reference models combined as a sharpened logarithmic opinion pool, with a calibration algorithm that reportedly improves robustness to reward hacking while preserving alignment performance. Useful as a deployment-side complement to SPF/SafeGrad/Safety Anchor/GradShield when retraining is not an option and alignment targets are still evolving.
- **3.6.3 / 3.6.4**: Unlearnability Phenomenon in RLVR (Chen, He and Zhao, arXiv 2605.16787, May 16 2026) — a substantial subset of initially hard examples remains unlearnable in RLVR even when correct rollouts are present, traced to low gradient similarity with other training examples and non-generalizable reasoning patterns; standard optimization, sampling, and data augmentation all fail to recover them. Promotion gates should track persistent unlearnable subsets across stages and flag them as latent integrity risk rather than ordinary training noise.
- **3.6.4**: BIV — Behavioral Integrity Verification for AI Agent Skills (Wu, Li and Liu, arXiv 2605.11770, May 12 2026) — combines deterministic code analysis with LLM-assisted capability extraction over a shared taxonomy and reports that 80.0% of 49,943 OpenClaw skills deviated from their declared behavior (81.1% oversight, 18.9% adversarial), reaching F1 0.946 on a 906-skill malicious-detection benchmark. The declared-vs-actual capability pattern is directly applicable to fine-tuned model, adapter, and PEFT artifact attestation between pipeline stages.
- **3.6.2 / 3.6.4**: Sequential Data Poisoning in LLM Post-Training (Sanderson et al., arXiv 2606.04929, June 3 2026) formalizes the colluding-adversary threat across post-training stages: SFT and DPO poison budgets combine additively, while SFT and PPO reward-model poisoning are complementary — neither succeeds alone but together they do. Approval reviews that score each stage's dataset in isolation systematically underestimate compound risk; reviewers should evaluate the joint poison surface across all stages a pipeline will execute.
- **3.6.3**: When Autoregressive Consistency Hurts Safety Alignment (Lyu et al., arXiv 2606.04168, June 2 2026) gives a mechanistic account of shallow alignment: next-token consistency concentrates alignment updates on early output tokens, and a random insertion attack can redirect generation to harmful content even after a long refusal prefix. Post-tuning safety regressions should therefore probe harmful-continuation behavior at arbitrary sequence positions; the authors' adversarial safety alignment via random worst-insertion training is a candidate hardening step before promotion.
- **3.6.3 / 3.6.5**: EvalStop (Zhang et al., arXiv 2606.04145, June 2 2026) moves reward-overoptimization detection into the scheduler for multi-tenant RLHF platforms: terminating jobs on k consecutive evaluation-score declines achieved 98% precision, 99% recall, and a 1.5% false-positive rate on an 80%-RLHF 64-GPU workload, reduced wasted compute by 22% versus an SRTF baseline, and preserved the best checkpoint on termination — directly implementing both the blocked-from-promotion clause of 3.6.3 and the per-stage rollback evidence of 3.6.5.
- **3.6.3**: GGRO — Gradient-Guided Reward Optimization for Inference-time Alignment (arXiv 2606.09635, June 2026) reports improved robustness to reward hacking across safety, helpfulness, and reasoning benchmarks by guiding inference-time alignment with reward-model gradients, joining SLOP as a deployment-side complement when retraining the policy is not an option.
- **3.6.4**: Token-Level Generalization in LoRA Adapter Backdoors (Lelle, arXiv 2605.30189, May 28 2026) characterizes data-poisoned adapter backdoors on a Qwen 2.5 1.5B prompt-injection classifier: a small poison fraction saturates a clean-accuracy-preserving backdoor that generalizes by token features (firing on RFC references but not structurally identical ISO, OWASP, CWE, or NIST ones), scales monotonically with LoRA rank, and localizes to mid-to-late MLP blocks. Two detection routes worked — probe-battery behavioral statistics (outlier_gap, mean_attack_rate) with perfect separation under overlapping trigger neighborhoods, and a weight-only cross-module dispersion statistic over normalized Frobenius norms — giving adapter supply-chain scanning both a behavioral and a weights-only screen to run at stage boundaries alongside PEFTGuard and Diff-SAE.
- **3.6.1 / 3.6.5**: NIST's preliminary draft Cybersecurity Framework Profile for AI (NIST IR 8596, public comment opened January 2026) and the in-progress SP 800-53 Control Overlays for Securing AI Systems (COSAiS) are converging on implementation-level controls for model provenance, training-data integrity, and third-party model assessment, with overlays for generative and agentic systems expected through late 2026. Teams building fine-tuning approval and checkpoint-lineage evidence today should track COSAiS so registry, signing, and rollback records map cleanly onto the eventual overlay control IDs.
