# C2.4: Multi-Modal Input Validation

> **Parent:** [C02: User Input Validation](C02-User-Input-Validation.md)

## Purpose

Modern AI systems accept inputs beyond text -- images, audio, video, PDFs, spreadsheets, and other file types. Each modality introduces unique attack surfaces: images can contain steganographic payloads or adversarial perturbations invisible to humans; audio can include ultrasonic commands; documents can embed hidden instructions in metadata, comments, or invisible layers. Multi-modal validation must handle format verification, content extraction, malware scanning, and adversarial detection for each modality, while also detecting coordinated cross-modal attacks where benign inputs in individual modalities combine to form an attack.

As of March 2026, multi-modal attacks have matured significantly. Transfer-based adversarial attacks achieve concerning success rates against production vision-language models (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5, Gemini). Research published at CVPR 2025 and ICLR 2025 demonstrates chain-of-attack techniques against VLMs, and a 2025 practitioner survey (arXiv:2505.03084) confirms that defense literature remains fragmented with no unified multi-modal defense framework in production.

A January 2026 CSA research note quantifies image-based prompt injection success rates: typographic text injection achieves 64% success against GPT-4V, Claude 3, and Gemini; steganographic encoding reaches 24.3% with near-invisible pixel-level payloads; and the CrossInject framework (ACM MM 2025) improves adversarial perturbation success by at least 30.1% over prior methods through Visual Latent Alignment and Textual Guidance Enhancement using surrogate open-source LLMs. The "Invisible Injections" study (arXiv:2507.22304, July 2025) provides the first comprehensive evaluation of steganographic prompt injection across spatial, frequency, and neural embedding methods, finding neural steganography reaches 31.8% attack success while maintaining visual imperceptibility (PSNR > 38 dB, SSIM > 0.94) across 8 VLMs and 12 datasets. AnyAttack (CVPR 2025) demonstrates that self-supervised adversarial learning pre-trained on LAION-400M can generate targeted adversarial images without label supervision, transferring seamlessly to commercial systems including GPT, Claude, Gemini, and Microsoft Copilot -- representing a scalability leap over prior targeted attacks. The MFHA framework (Scientific Reports, March 2025) achieves a 16.05% average improvement in adversarial transferability through triple contrastive feature heterogenization and multi-domain cross-modal variance aggregation. VEAttack (arXiv:2505.17440, February 2026) demonstrates that targeting the vision encoder alone -- without access to the downstream LLM, task, or labels -- causes 94.5% performance degradation on image captioning and 75.7% on VQA, exposing a fundamental architectural weakness: since most VLMs share similar vision encoders (CLIP-based), a single gray-box attack transfers broadly across models and tasks.

On the defense side, the Cross-Agent Multimodal Provenance-Aware Defense Framework (arXiv:2512.23557) demonstrates 94% detection accuracy compared to 52% for keyword filtering and 66% for safety fine-tuning alone, using cooperating sanitizer agents with a provenance ledger. DMAST (Dual-Modality Multi-Stage Adversarial Safety Training, March 2026) reduces attacker success on multimodal web agents from 41.2% to 21.4% through three-stage adversarial co-evolution training. For audio, the SUAD defense (arXiv:2508.02116, August 2025) blocks inaudible ultrasonic voice attacks with over 98% success on six smartphones without impacting normal speech, while SUAD Attack itself achieves 89.8%+ activation success via solid-channel ultrasonic injection -- highlighting both the severity and the viability of defending against ultrasonic attack vectors.

As of April 2026, several new detection-side developments are worth tracking. E²AT (Efficient End-to-End Adversarial Training, arXiv:2503.04833, updated January 2026) introduces a Dynamic Joint Multimodal Optimization strategy that outperforms existing baselines by an average margin of 34% across text and image modalities while maintaining clean task performance -- on LLaVA-Vicuna it reduces attack success rates from 68% to 2% for BAP attacks and from 98-100% to 8% for GCG and AutoDan. VALD (Multi-Stage Vision Attack Detection, arXiv:2602.19570, February 2026) offers a training-free, general-purpose defense that combines content-preserving image transformations with text-embedding analysis in a two-stage pipeline, where most clean images skip costly processing entirely. HiddenDetect (ACL 2025) demonstrates that VLMs encode distinct activation patterns when processing unsafe prompts, enabling tuning-free jailbreak detection by monitoring hidden states -- surpassing prior state-of-the-art without requiring fine-tuning. Representational Contrastive Scoring (RCS, ACL 2026, arXiv:2512.12069) builds on this direction by learning a lightweight projection that maximally separates benign and malicious inputs in safety-critical internal layers, with two instantiations -- Mahalanobis Contrastive Detection (MCD) and K-nearest Contrastive Detection (KCD) -- that achieve state-of-the-art detection on a challenging cross-attack evaluation protocol while remaining computationally lightweight enough for production deployment. For practical preprocessing, aggressive JPEG recompression has been shown to reduce steganographic attack success to 11.7%, and stacking it with Gaussian filtering and behavioral monitoring can reduce overall multimodal attack effectiveness by roughly three-quarters. A notable gap persists: most commercially available defense tools (Lakera Guard -- now part of Check Point following a $300M acquisition in November 2025 -- LLM Guard, Azure Prompt Shields) remain text-only in their public APIs as of early 2026, and a practitioner discussion (Hacker News, 2026) highlights that semantic-level attacks requiring intent understanding and non-English attack payloads remain significant blind spots for current pattern-matching classifiers.

On the attack methodology front, UltraBreak (ICLR 2026) represents a qualitative leap in adversarial universality: it is the first jailbreak framework to achieve both cross-target universality and cross-model transferability simultaneously using a single surrogate model. A single adversarial image functions as a "skeleton key" across different VLM architectures and harmful queries without retraining, using semantic-based objectives in the textual embedding space combined with vision-space transformations and Total Variation regularization. Defense2Attack (arXiv:2509.12724, September 2025) reveals a counterintuitive finding: incorporating weak defense mechanisms into the attack pipeline paradoxically enhances jailbreak effectiveness, combining a visual optimizer with universal adversarial perturbations, a defense-styled textual optimizer, and a reinforcement-fine-tuned red-team suffix generator to achieve superior single-attempt jailbreaks across four VLMs and four safety benchmarks. For audio, Sirens' Whisper (SWhisper, USENIX Security 2026, arXiv:2603.13847) introduces the first practical framework for covert near-ultrasonic jailbreaks of speech-driven LLMs, encoding structured adversarial prompts into the 17--22 kHz band that demodulate faithfully through commodity microphone nonlinearity -- achieving up to 0.94 non-refusal rate and 0.925 specific-convincing rate on commercial models while remaining perceptually indistinguishable from background noise in controlled user studies.

An important emerging dimension is multilingual and multi-accent audio jailbreaking. Multi-AudioJail (arXiv:2504.01094, April 2025) demonstrates that acoustic perturbations (reverberation, echo, whisper effects) interact with cross-lingual phonetics to cause jailbreak success rates to surge by up to +57.25 percentage points -- with multilingual audio-only attacks achieving 3.1x higher success rates than text-only attacks against the same models. This finding is particularly concerning because it means attackers need only exploit the weakest linguistic link (non-English audio inputs) to compromise the entire model, and transcription-based defenses fail when reverberation degrades ASR accuracy.

MemJack (arXiv:2604.12616, April 2026) represents a qualitative shift in multi-agent jailbreak methodology: rather than relying on pixel perturbations or typographic tricks, it engages with semantic structures intrinsic to natural images through coordinated multi-agent cooperation, visual-semantic camouflage, and Iterative Nullspace Projection (INLP) -- a geometric filter that bypasses latent space refusals. A persistent Multimodal Experience Memory accumulates and transfers successful attack strategies across different images, achieving 71.48% ASR against Qwen3-VL-Plus and scaling to 90% under extended budgets. The forthcoming MemJack-Bench dataset of 113,000+ multimodal jailbreak trajectories should prove valuable for defensive alignment research.

Cross-modal attacks are also growing more sophisticated in how they split malicious intent across modalities. CrossTALK (arXiv:2602.10148, February 2026) introduces cross-modal entanglement that uses knowledge-scalable reframing, cross-modal clue entangling, and scenario nesting to exceed VLMs' safety alignment -- achieving over 94% attack success rate by distributing complex multi-hop reasoning across image and text channels. CAMO (Cross-modal Adversarial Multimodal Obfuscation, arXiv:2506.16760, submitted to ICLR 2026) takes a different approach, decomposing malicious prompts into semantically benign visual and textual fragments that reconstruct harmful intent through the model's own cross-modal reasoning -- achieving 93.94% ASR on DeepSeek-R1 and 81.82% on GPT-4.1-nano while using only 12.6% of the tokens required by existing methods, making it far more efficient to deploy. For robotics, EDPA (Embedding Disruption Patch Attack, arXiv:2510.13237, October 2025) demonstrates model-agnostic adversarial patches against Vision-Language-Action models that can be placed within a robot's camera view without knowledge of the model architecture -- substantially increasing task failure rates on the LIBERO benchmark, though a proposed adversarial fine-tuning defense for the visual encoder effectively mitigates the degradation. A March 2026 comparative evaluation (arXiv:2603.16960) of gradient-based attacks against open-source VLM agents reveals a striking 10x robustness gap: LLaVA-v1.5-7B shows 52.6--66.9% attack success under BIM, PGD, and CLIP-based spectral attacks, while Qwen2.5-VL-7B achieves only 6.5--15.5% -- suggesting that architectural choices substantially influence adversarial resilience even among similarly-sized models.

On the audio defense side, April 2026 marks a turning point: AudioGuard (arXiv:2604.08867) introduces a unified audio guardrail that decouples waveform-level threat detection (SoundGuard, which identifies harmful sound events, speaker attributes, and voice-cloning misuse) from semantic transcript safety (ContentGuard), consistently outperforming GPT-Audio and Gemini 3 guardrails with substantially lower latency. SALMONN-Guard (arXiv:2511.10222, updated February 2026) is the first guard model jointly inspecting speech, audio, and text for safety, reducing attack success from 66% (Gemini 2.5 Pro with full guardrails) to 20% on the SACRED-Bench benchmark. JALMBench (ICLR 2026) provides the first systematic benchmark for audio language model jailbreaks with 245,355 audio samples across 12 mainstream audio LLMs, revealing that existing defenses adapted from vision-language models are largely ineffective for audio-native attacks -- a finding that underscores the need for modality-specific defense tooling. GRM (arXiv:2604.09222, April 2026) sharpens the audio threat model further: frequency-selective Mel-band perturbations achieve 88.46% average jailbreak success across four audio LLMs while preserving transcription and QA utility better than broad full-band attacks, so validation should inspect localized spectral manipulation rather than only ultrasonic or full-band noise. A separate privacy concern is emerging: VisualLeakBench (arXiv:2603.13385, March 2026) audits PII leakage from frontier VLMs using adversarial images and finds that Claude 4 exhibits a "comply-then-warn" pattern with 74.4% PII attack success despite having the lowest OCR attack success (14.2%), while defensive system prompts reduce leakage to 2.2% -- highlighting that input validation must also consider data exfiltration via visual channels.

As of May 2026, two verification themes are becoming clearer. First, cross-modal detection needs a unified representation of the whole prompt pipeline, not separate per-modality filters: CM-PIUG (Pattern Recognition, April 2026) models OCR-extracted text, native text, ASR transcripts, and graph prompts as semantic-equivalent units in an attack-surface graph, then uses game-theoretic defense selection with auditable evidence tracing. Second, model-internal defenses are improving but remain complementary controls: NullSteer (CVPR 2026, arXiv:2603.22094) uses null-space projected activation steering to reduce harmful VLM outputs while preserving benign behavior, but auditors should still verify input preprocessing, gating, logging, and HITL escalation because steering does not sanitize hostile files before parsing.

WACV 2026 adds two useful implementation signals. Multimodal Adversarial Training (MAT) explicitly trains against perturbations in both image and text modalities and shows why one-to-one image-text pairs are too narrow for robust VLM defenses; useful training data needs aligned, diverse one-to-many image/text relationships without distribution shift. Physical Prompt Injection Attacks (PPIA, arXiv:2601.17383) show that visual instructions placed in the physical environment can influence 10 LVLMs across VQA, planning, and navigation with attack success up to 98%, so camera-facing systems need physical-scene validation and reviewer evidence, not just uploaded-file scanning. ARGUS (arXiv:2512.05745) is also worth tracking as a complementary model-internal defense: it steers instruction-following behavior only when a lightweight detector fires, then post-filters results, but it still does not replace pre-model file, OCR, ASR, and metadata sanitization.

On the regulatory and benchmarking side, EU AI Act Article 15 requires high-risk AI systems to demonstrate resilience against adversarial examples and model evasion -- with full compliance required by August 2, 2026. Article 15(4) specifically mandates technical solutions to "prevent, detect, respond to, resolve and control for" adversarial inputs, including data poisoning and model evasion attacks. A 2025 analysis (Nolte et al., ACM FAccT 2025) identifies gaps between the legal terminology and current ML testing practices, noting that standardized benchmarks for multimodal adversarial robustness are still lacking. OmniSafeBench-MM (arXiv:2512.06589, December 2025) offers a step toward filling this gap: it integrates 13 attack methods, 15 defense strategies, and evaluation across 10 open-source and 8 closed-source MLLMs spanning 9 risk domains and 50 fine-grained categories, providing the closest thing to a standardized multimodal safety benchmark available today. MMJ-Bench (AAAI 2026) complements this with the first publicly available unified pipeline for evaluating VLM jailbreak attacks and defenses from an adversarial rivalry perspective, providing systematic comparison across attack methods and defense mechanisms. ContrAR (CVPR 2026 Findings, arXiv:2604.05510, April 2026) introduces the first AR-specific adversarial robustness benchmark, testing 11 VLMs against contradictory virtual content attacks using 312 real-world augmented reality videos validated by human participants -- an important new evaluation dimension as AR/VR-integrated AI systems proliferate. The 6th Workshop on Adversarial Machine Learning at CVPR 2026 focuses specifically on vision-language agent safety, including an international challenge on adversarial multimodal attacks against vision-language agents in autonomous driving scenarios. On the commercial verification front, SecureIQLab launched the first independent AI firewall validation methodology (March 2026), evaluating up to 20 vendors across 32 real-world scenarios aligned with OWASP LLM Top 10 and MITRE ATLAS, with results targeted for Black Hat USA 2026 -- this is the first non-vendor-funded, AMTSO-compliant test of whether AI security products actually prevent attacks versus merely detecting them. The AI firewall market is also consolidating: Check Point Software acquired Lakera for $300M in November 2025, folding Lakera Guard into its enterprise security stack as a Global Center of Excellence for AI Security. A comprehensive survey of VLM adversarial attacks and defenses (Neural Networks, 2026) provides the first unified taxonomy of attack categorizations, defense strategies, metrics, datasets, and architectures with a dedicated VLM focus, underscoring that the field is rapidly maturing from fragmented research into a coherent discipline.

## Open Research Questions

- Can cross-modal adversarial attacks be reliably detected with current tooling, or is this fundamentally a research problem? As of March 2026, the Provenance-Aware Defense Framework (94% detection) and DMAST (41.2% to 21.4% attacker success reduction) suggest detection is becoming feasible, but these remain research prototypes requiring custom integration -- no turnkey production solution exists yet.
- How should multi-modal validation handle new modalities (e.g., 3D models, haptic input, sensor data) as AI systems expand?
- What is the performance overhead of comprehensive multi-modal validation, and how does it impact real-time applications? CrowdStrike AIDR demonstrates sub-30ms is achievable for text-based detection, but multi-modal scanning adds significant latency.
- How do unified vision-language model architectures (e.g., GPT-4o, Gemini 2.5 Pro) change the attack surface compared to pipeline architectures with separate vision and language models? CVPR 2025 research on chain-of-attack techniques suggests unified architectures may be more susceptible to cascading cross-modal attacks.
- What are effective defenses against typographic attacks on vision models beyond text detection in images? MAT (WACV 2026) shows promise at training time, but runtime defenses remain limited.
- How should organizations prioritize adversarial detection investment given that embedding-space attacks are particularly difficult to detect even for experienced practitioners?
- What role should the eight-stage pipeline defense framework (ingestion through monitoring) play in standardized multi-modal validation architectures?
- How should organizations balance the DMAST finding that visual attacks (34.4% success) significantly outperform text-only injections (24.1%), given that most current safety training remains text-centric?
- What is the practical overhead and latency impact of multi-agent sanitization architectures (Text Sanitizer + Visual Sanitizer + Output Validator) compared to single-model defense approaches?
- How should defenders adapt to self-supervised adversarial attacks (AnyAttack) that eliminate the target-label constraint, enabling any image to serve as a targeted attack vector against any VLM without prior knowledge of the model's label space?
- Can dormant Visual Memory Injection payloads (arXiv:2602.15927) be detected pre-emptively, or does defense require continuous monitoring across multi-turn conversation state?
- What countermeasures are effective against Virtual Scenario Hypnosis attacks that combine deceptive narrative framing with adversarial images, given their 82% success rate?
- How should organizations defend against solid-channel ultrasonic injection (SUAD) in physical environments where attackers can access shared surfaces (desks, tables) near voice-enabled AI systems?
- Given that training-free detection approaches (VALD, HiddenDetect) now rival fine-tuned defenses in accuracy while adding minimal latency, should organizations prioritize inference-time detection over costly adversarial training pipelines?
- How should the balance between input validation and output policy enforcement shift as semantic-level attacks (completion attacks, narrative extraction, multi-turn context poisoning) continue to evade pattern-matching classifiers?
- What are the false-positive management costs of stacking multiple preprocessing defenses (JPEG recompression, Gaussian filtering, anomaly detection), and at what point does defensive friction degrade legitimate user experience unacceptably?
- How should organizations address the shared CLIP-based vision encoder vulnerability exposed by VEAttack, given that most VLMs rely on similar encoder architectures and a single gray-box attack transfers broadly across models and tasks?
- Given that I2V-MLLM demonstrates cross-modal adversarial transfer from image to video models at ~58% black-box success, should video-based VLMs implement image-specific adversarial defenses as a baseline even when no video-specific attack detection exists?
- How should organizations use standardized benchmarks like OmniSafeBench-MM and MMJ-Bench to satisfy EU AI Act Article 15 adversarial robustness requirements by August 2026, and what gaps remain between available benchmarks and regulatory expectations?
- Given that UltraBreak demonstrates a single adversarial image can serve as a universal "skeleton key" across VLM architectures and harmful queries, what architectural changes (beyond input-layer defenses) are needed to prevent cross-model transferable jailbreaks?
- How should audio preprocessing pipelines adapt to near-ultrasonic attacks (17--22 kHz, Sirens' Whisper) that exploit commodity microphone nonlinearity, given that traditional ultrasonic filtering only covers frequencies above 20 kHz?
- Does the Defense2Attack finding -- that weak defenses paradoxically strengthen attacks -- imply that partially deployed or misconfigured safety mechanisms create a worse security posture than having no defense at all?
- Given that JALMBench (ICLR 2026) finds VLM-adapted defenses largely ineffective for audio-native attacks, what architectural principles should guide the development of modality-specific guard models, and can the SoundGuard/ContentGuard decomposition in AudioGuard generalize to other non-textual modalities?
- How should organizations address the PII exfiltration risk revealed by VisualLeakBench, where VLMs exhibit high PII disclosure rates (up to 74.4%) when processing adversarial images -- is defensive system prompting a sufficient mitigation, or do architectural changes (output filtering, PII masking) need to be mandated?
- Given that CrossTALK achieves 94%+ ASR by distributing multi-hop reasoning chains across modalities, and CAMO achieves 93.94% with only 12.6% of the tokens, are current per-modality safety checks fundamentally insufficient -- and does effective defense require reasoning-chain monitoring that tracks inference steps across modalities rather than screening inputs independently?
- The 10x robustness gap between LLaVA (52.6--66.9% ASR) and Qwen2.5-VL (6.5--15.5% ASR) under identical attacks suggests architectural design choices drive adversarial resilience -- what specific architectural properties explain this difference, and can they be isolated into defensive design principles for new VLM deployments?
- As Vision-Language-Action models move from simulation to real-world robotics, how should EDPA-style physical adversarial patches be addressed in safety-critical environments (manufacturing, surgery, autonomous driving) where camera input manipulation has kinetic consequences?
- Will the SecureIQLab independent validation results (Black Hat USA 2026) reveal that AI firewall vendor claims are systematically overstated, and should regulatory frameworks mandate independent third-party testing rather than accepting vendor self-reported efficacy?
- Given that MemJack's persistent Multimodal Experience Memory enables adaptive attacks that improve across interactions, do stateless per-request defense approaches need to evolve toward stateful threat tracking that monitors attack trajectory patterns across sessions?
- Multi-AudioJail demonstrates that multilingual audio attacks achieve 3.1x higher success than text-only attacks -- should organizations mandate multilingual coverage for all audio input validation, and what are the resource implications of supporting defensive coverage across dozens of languages and accents?
- As AR/VR-integrated AI systems proliferate, how should organizations evaluate adversarial robustness for augmented reality contexts where virtual content manipulation creates attack vectors fundamentally different from traditional image or text injection?
- GRM shows that frequency-selective audio perturbations can preserve utility while still jailbreaking audio LLMs -- should production audio validation move from coarse ultrasonic filtering toward Mel-band anomaly scoring and benchmark-driven regression tests?
- CM-PIUG suggests attack-surface graphs and semantic-equivalence reduction can produce auditable cross-modal evidence chains -- how should those traces be represented in SIEM events so reviewers can understand why a text-image-audio combination was blocked?
- NullSteer and similar activation-steering defenses improve internal refusal behavior, but how should auditors measure whether they reduce real risk when file parsing, OCR, ASR, tool calls, and HITL workflows still happen outside the model internals?
