# C12.2: Right-to-be-Forgotten & Deletion Enforcement

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-05-14 (rev 6)

## Purpose

Ensure data-subject deletion requests propagate across all AI artifacts — raw datasets, feature stores, embeddings, checkpoints, logs, and backups — and that model unlearning is demonstrably verifiable rather than a cosmetic output-suppression exercise. The EDPB formally adopted its 2025 Coordinated Enforcement Framework (CEF) report on Article 17 on 18 February 2026, after examining 764 controllers across 32 DPAs throughout 2025. The report flagged seven systemic gaps: (1) absent internal procedures, (2) insufficient information to data subjects, (3) ineffective anonymization used as a substitute for erasure, (4) inconsistent implementation, (5) unclear retention periods, (6) backup-system erasure failures, and (7) flawed balancing of Article 17(3) exceptions. Nine DPAs opened formal investigations; follow-through supervision in Ireland, France, Portugal, Slovenia, and Germany runs through 2026. CNIL, Portugal's CNPD, and Sweden's IMY have already indicated CEF findings will drive sector-specific inspections this year. The follow-up CEF for 2026, announced 14 October 2025, shifts focus to GDPR Articles 12–14 (transparency and information obligations) — relevant here because controllers must clearly tell data subjects how the right to erasure applies to AI-derived artifacts, what the realistic propagation SLA looks like, and what happens to model weights. Sitting underneath the CEF is **EDPB Opinion 28/2024 (17 December 2024)**, which DPAs are now applying in the field: an AI model trained on personal data is **not automatically anonymous**, anonymity must be assessed case-by-case, and where unlawful processing occurred during development the worst-case remedy is "erasure of the whole dataset used to develop the AI model and/or the AI model itself." That language is the doctrinal hook for every model-deletion order DPAs have issued since.

Enforcement around AI deletion gained two useful 2026 lessons, but the sources need to be read carefully. On **18 March 2026**, the Court of Rome annulled the €15 million fine the Italian Garante had imposed on OpenAI in November 2024 for ChatGPT-related GDPR violations — a setback for direct erasure enforcement against foundation-model providers, though the full reasoning has not yet been published and the Garante is weighing an appeal. On **30 March 2026**, the FTC announced the **OkCupid/Match** settlement after alleging that OkCupid shared nearly three million user photos plus location and other personal information with an unrelated third party, without opt-out and without formal contractual use limits. The stipulated order does not make Clarifai a defendant, but it permanently bars OkCupid and Match from misrepresenting how they collect, maintain, use, disclose, delete, or protect personal information and how their privacy controls work. Subsequent Reuters/TechCrunch reporting says Clarifai certified deletion to the FTC on **7 April 2026** and told Rep. Lori Trahan's office on **16 April 2026** that it deleted the OkCupid photos and any facial-recognition models trained on them. The practical lesson is narrower than a blanket U.S. model-deletion rule, but still important: when unlawfully shared training data produced derived models, deletion of the data alone is no longer persuasive evidence.

The U.S. state-level deletion stack also matured sharply this year. California's **Delete Request and Opt-out Platform (DROP)** opened to consumers on **1 January 2026** under SB 362 (Delete Act). Data brokers must begin processing DROP lists on **1 August 2026**, access the platform at least every 45 days, and continue deleting data on that cadence. CPPA guidance also reflects SB 361's 2025 expansion of data-broker registration disclosures, including whether brokers shared data with **developers of generative AI systems** — a concrete U.S. hook tying broker-to-AI data flows to deletion obligations. The CPPA's Data Broker Strike Force has already issued 2026 enforcement decisions for registration failures, signaling the agency will not wait until August to scrutinize broker compliance. Separately, the amended **COPPA Rule** was published on **22 April 2025**, took effect on **23 June 2025**, and reached its main compliance date on **22 April 2026**. It is not an AI-training-specific deletion law, but it adds stricter notice, security, retention, and third-party disclosure obligations around children's personal information; AI training pipelines that ingest children's data now need a defensible retention/deletion story and separate consent analysis when data is disclosed to model, advertising, analytics, or enrichment vendors. In Europe, the **EU Digital Omnibus** changed again after this page's last pass: on **7 May 2026**, Parliament and Council negotiators reached a provisional political agreement that moves high-risk AI Act obligations for Annex III systems to **2 December 2027** and product-integrated Annex I systems to **2 August 2028**, with formal adoption still required before it enters into law. That does not weaken Article 17 erasure duties, but it changes the planning clock for AI Act Article 10 data-governance evidence; deployers should keep deletion lineage and retention controls moving now because GPAI, transparency, and GDPR timelines continue to run independently of the high-risk deferral.

Machine unlearning itself is in the middle of a credibility reset. The ICLR 2025 paper from Hu et al. (CMU) showed that approximate unlearning methods largely **obfuscate** rather than forget — a "benign relearning attack" using small amounts of loosely related public data can reverse the unlearning effect. The most widely reported example: an LLM that had been "unlearned" of biohazard knowledge regained the ability to produce harmful bioweapon text after a brief fine-tune on publicly available medical articles. CMU's follow-up SaTML '25 position paper ("LLM Unlearning Benchmarks are Weak Measures of Progress") went further: a survey of 72 unlearning papers found that the dominant benchmarks (TOFU, WMDP) test forget and retain queries independently and miss dependencies between them, leaving room for methods to overfit to test queries while leaving the underlying knowledge intact. The team released *swapped WMDP* and *paired TOFU* on Hugging Face as harder evaluation sets. Recent attack literature has compounded the problem along three axes: ("Stealthy MLLM Unlearning Attack", "Step-by-Step Reasoning Attack", and "Unlearning Isn't Invisible") shows that adversaries can recover unlearned content with reasoning-style prompts, detect that unlearning happened from logits or even text outputs, and use those traces as a new reconnaissance signal. The **ROKA paper** (arxiv:2603.00436, 28 February 2026) introduced the *indirect unlearning attack*: an adversary requests deletion of one record specifically to degrade a model's performance on a different security-critical task, exploiting the **knowledge contamination** that conventional unlearning methods inflict on conceptually related neighbors. ROKA itself proposes a "Neural Healing" defense that re-allocates contributions to nullify forgotten data while reinforcing related knowledge — the first method offering theoretical guarantees for knowledge preservation during unlearning. The companion "Attack by Unlearning" paper (arxiv:2603.18570, 19 March 2026) extends this to graph neural networks: an adversary injects carefully crafted nodes into the training graph and later issues legally mandated deletion requests, producing an attack surface that cannot be refused without violating data-subject rights.

A second 2026 privacy thread widens the auditor's lens from the deleted subject to everyone else left in the model. **"Revisiting Privacy Leakage in Machine Unlearning"** (arxiv:2605.01129, accepted Euro S&P 2026) introduces **TC-UMIA**, a tri-class membership-inference attack that uses before/after predictions to distinguish forget, retain, and unseen records and finds that retain-set privacy can worsen after unlearning. Xue et al.'s **DVIA** paper at AAAI 2026 reaches the same operational conclusion in a black-box dual-view setting: if the original and unlearned models remain queryable, an attacker can infer membership for retained data more effectively than by querying either model alone. Cohen et al.'s **"Protecting the Undeleted"** goes further at the definition level, showing that "perfect retraining" style unlearning can leak undeleted data through deletion requests unless the security definition explicitly protects the retain set.

The 2026 evaluation literature has begun to catch up. **"LLM Unlearning Under the Microscope: A Full-Stack View on Methods and Metrics"** (arxiv:2510.07626) gives the first principled taxonomy of twelve stateful unlearning methods across three families — divergence-driven optimization (NPO, SimNPO), representation misalignment (RMU and variants), and rejection-based targeted unlearning (IDK) — and introduces **Open-QA metrics** that better capture generative performance and surface the unlearning-effectiveness vs. utility-retention tradeoff that WMDP and TOFU tend to mask. **"LLM Unlearning with LLM Beliefs"** (arxiv:2510.19422) reframes the objective around the model's own belief distribution rather than a fixed forget set, addressing the case where the forget set is incomplete or noisy. **"LLM Unlearning on Noisy Forget Sets"** (arxiv:2510.09007) studies incomplete, rewritten, and watermarked forget sets and reaches a more nuanced conclusion than "noise breaks unlearning": RMU and NPO remain surprisingly robust when the core semantic signal survives, so auditors should vary semantic content, not just surface wording. ICLR 2026 also delivered **RASLIK** (Randomized Antipodal Search on Linearized Influence Kernel), which reframes unlearning as a **data-selection** problem: hashing-based gradient sketches identify both forget-aligned and retain-aligned samples in sublinear time, shifting the Pareto frontier outward beyond oracle sampling. Representation-level tests also matured: **"Suppression or Deletion"** (arxiv:2602.18505) uses sparse autoencoders and inference-time steering to distinguish output suppression from internal deletion, and **MUNKEY** (arxiv:2603.15033) shows a promising "unlearning by design" direction where instance-specific memorization is externalized behind deletable keys. The combined effect: the field has pushed toward certified unlearning, SAM-based robust unlearning (Fan et al., ICML 2025), Neural-Healing-style contribution re-allocation (Choi et al., 2026), influence-kernel data selection (RASLIK, ICLR 2026), and retraining-style approaches for anything that must stand up to regulator scrutiny — while assuming that any unlearning claim will be tested by benign-relearning, reasoning-chain, stealthy fine-tune, unlearning-trace detection, dual-view / tri-class retained-data inference, representation-restoration probes, and indirect-attack adversaries who weaponize the deletion right itself.

Two May 2026 updates sharpen the evidence bar. **SMI: Statistical Membership Inference for Reliable Unlearned Model Auditing** (revised 7 May 2026) argues that a failed MIA is not proof of forgetting because unlearned samples can occupy feature-space positions distinct from true non-members; it proposes a training-free estimate of the non-member mixture proportion with bootstrap reference ranges. That is useful audit evidence for 12.2.2, but it also means teams should stop treating a single low MIA AUC as a pass/fail deletion certificate. In federated settings, the **Scientific Reports** paper **"Decoupling forgetting and preservation in federated unlearning via knowledge distillation"** (11 May 2026) reports 83.6% forget accuracy drop with 76.43% friend accuracy on Tiny-ImageNet and validates forgetting against retrained gold-standard comparisons. It is promising for C12.6-style deployments, but still benchmark-scale; controllers should not extrapolate it to foundation-model erasure without record-level lineage and retain-set privacy tests.

## Notable Incidents

- **EDPB Article 17 CEF, 18 February 2026:** 32 DPAs examined 764 controllers and found recurring failures around internal procedures, data-subject information, weak anonymization, retention-period decisions, backup erasure, and balancing of Article 17(3) exceptions. Treat this as the live supervisory baseline for deletion evidence.
- **OkCupid / Match / Clarifai, March-April 2026:** the FTC case centered on nearly three million OkCupid photos shared for facial-recognition development without the promised controls. The settlement binds OkCupid and Match, while follow-up reporting says Clarifai certified deletion of the photos and derived facial-recognition models. The practical lesson is that derived-model deletion evidence now belongs in the remediation file when unlawfully shared training data was used.
- **California DROP, 2026 rollout:** DROP launched for California residents on 1 January 2026, covers over 500 registered data brokers, and requires broker processing from 1 August 2026. Brokers must process through the accessible deletion mechanism at least every 45 days, and the consumer-facing CPPA timeline says broker data must be deleted within 90 days after processing begins.

## Implementation Maturity

- **Deletion propagation:** Mature in ordinary DSAR systems and primary data stores; uneven across feature stores, training shards, checkpoints, derived caches, event streams, and backups. Evidence quality improves when each model registry entry records a data-version commit, record manifest, and production endpoint mapping so auditors can answer "which models used this subject's data?" without notebook archaeology.
- **Backup and stream erasure:** Mature pattern, uneven adoption. Envelope encryption and crypto-shredding can make WORM backups or Kafka topics unrecoverable for a subject, but the deletion record must show which DEK/EDEK was destroyed, when, by whom, and which downstream consumers were re-keyed or quarantined.
- **Unlearning verification:** Emerging. OpenUnlearning, MUSE, TOFU, WMDP, SMI, TC-UMIA, DVIA, and representation-restoration probes make claims more testable, but none is a regulator-approved substitute for raw-data deletion, retraining where feasible, or formal (ε, δ) guarantees.
- **Federated and unlearning-by-design approaches:** Experimental to early mature. FedRecovery, FedADP, certified VFL, MUNKEY, and knowledge-distillation federated unlearning show useful design patterns, but auditors should require retained-client utility, retain-set privacy, and retrained-gold-standard comparisons before accepting them for high-impact systems.

- **Machine unlearning (LLM-focused):** [OpenUnlearning](https://github.com/locuslab/open-unlearning) (NeurIPS D&B 2025, 12+ algorithms across TOFU/MUSE/WMDP, 450+ open-sourced checkpoints on HuggingFace, six integrated MIA variants), [SISA reference implementation](https://github.com/cleverhans-lab/machine-unlearning) (Bourtoule et al., 2021), [SAM-based robust unlearning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.05374) (Fan et al., ICML 2025), [ROKA / Neural Healing](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.00436) (Choi et al., Feb 2026 — contribution re-allocation defense against indirect unlearning attacks; first theoretical guarantee for knowledge preservation), [RASLIK](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=Xn6EnJZghu) (ICLR 2026 — randomized antipodal search on linearized influence kernel; reframes unlearning as data selection), EUPG (Universitat Rovira i Virgili, 2025 — hybrid k-anonymity/DP pre-train + fine-tune approach), MMUnlearner (multimodal LLMs, 2025), [awesome-llm-unlearning](https://github.com/chrisliu298/awesome-llm-unlearning) and [awesome-machine-unlearning](https://github.com/tamlhp/awesome-machine-unlearning) for current literature tracking, [Machine-Unlearning-Comparator](https://github.com/gnueaj/Machine-Unlearning-Comparator) (TVCG 2026 visual analytics for unlearning comparison).
- **Unlearning evaluation & realism:** [LLM Unlearning Under the Microscope](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07626) (taxonomy of 12 stateful methods + Open-QA generative metrics), [SMI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.01150) (training-free statistical auditing that treats failed MIA as insufficient proof of forgetting), [Step-by-Step Reasoning Attack](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.17279) (Sleek-style black-box prompts for erased-knowledge recovery), [Unlearning Isn't Invisible](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.14003) (trace detection from logits or text outputs), [LLM Unlearning with LLM Beliefs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.19422) (objective reframed around belief distribution rather than fixed forget set), [LLM Unlearning on Noisy Forget Sets](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.09007) (incomplete / rewritten / watermarked forget sets — closer to real DSAR workflows), [Keeping an Eye on LLM Unlearning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.00359) (Stealthy Attack against fine-tuning-based unlearning).
- **Retained-data leakage after unlearning:** [TC-UMIA / Revisiting Privacy Leakage in Machine Unlearning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.01129) (Euro S&P 2026 — tri-class MIA across forget, retain, and unseen records), [DVIA / Dual-View Inference Attack](https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/AAAI/article/view/38134) (AAAI 2026 — black-box retained-record inference when both original and unlearned models are queryable), [Protecting the Undeleted](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.16697) (definition-level reconstruction risk for undeleted data), [InfoRMIA](https://openreview.net/forum?id=4KVeb0Vv13) (ICLR 2026 — token-level membership-inference signal for granular memorization audits).
- **Unlearning-by-design and representation checks:** [Suppression or Deletion](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.18505) (sparse-autoencoder restoration analysis for internal semantic retention), [MUNKEY / Models Designed to Forget via Key Deletion](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.15033) (memory-augmented design where instance-specific keys can be removed without post-hoc weight updates).
- **Federated unlearning:** FedRecovery (IEEE TIFS 2023 — weighted gradient residual removal with calibrated Gaussian noise for DP), FedADP (arxiv:2411.11044, 2025 — adaptive DP mechanism balancing privacy and unlearning utility), "Forgetting Any Data at Any Time" certified VFL framework (arxiv:2502.17081, 2025), "Certified Unlearning in Decentralized Federated Learning" (arxiv:2601.06436), and [knowledge-distillation federated unlearning](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-51158-x) (Scientific Reports, 11 May 2026 — separates target-client forgetting from friend-client preservation and validates with MIA against a retrained gold standard).
- **Privacy auditing & MIA:** [Privacy Meter](https://github.com/privacytrustlab/ml_privacy_meter) (NUS/NIST), ML-Doctor (ACM CCS 2022), DP-Sniper (black-box DP auditing — Bichsel et al.), canary-based memorization testing (Carlini et al., 2019), [Opacus](https://github.com/pytorch/opacus) for DP-SGD training that provides formal guarantees deletable models inherit.
- **Deletion orchestration:** BigID (unified deletion across people data and AI artifacts, 2026 release), OneTrust Consent & Preference Management, Securiti DataGrail with vector-store connectors, Collibra Data Governance with feature-store lineage, and DSAR/SRR platforms that integrate with Snowflake, Databricks, Pinecone, and Weaviate. For California's [DROP](https://privacy.ca.gov/drop/) (consumer launch 1 Jan 2026; broker-side enforcement from 1 Aug 2026 with $200/day per unfulfilled request), use orchestration that re-polls DROP every 45 days, verifies the 90-day deletion deadline, and supports the post-deletion re-collection sweep — most major DSAR platforms (DataGrail, Securiti, OneTrust) shipped DROP integrations through Q1 2026.
- **Immutable audit logs:** AWS CloudTrail + Object Lock compliance mode, Azure Monitor with immutable storage + legal holds, GCP Cloud Audit Logs + bucket lock, [Sigstore / Rekor](https://www.sigstore.dev/) transparency logs for public-verifiable trails, IBM Cloud Object Storage with retention policies.
- **Backup-safe erasure (crypto-shredding):** per-subject envelope encryption with AWS KMS, Google Cloud KMS, or Azure Key Vault, so destroying the per-subject key renders backups of that subject's data unrecoverable even under WORM retention. The pattern now extends to streaming systems — see [Conduktor's Crypto Shredding for Kafka](https://www.conduktor.io/glossary/crypto-shredding-for-kafka) for a Kafka-side reference and [OneUptime's GCP KMS recipe](https://oneuptime.com/blog/post/2026-02-17-how-to-set-up-crypto-shredding-for-gdpr-right-to-erasure-compliance-in-google-cloud/view) (Feb 2026) for envelope encryption tied to per-subject DEK destruction in Google Cloud.
- **Lineage for deletion evidence:** [DVC + SageMaker MLflow end-to-end lineage](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/machine-learning/end-to-end-lineage-with-dvc-and-amazon-sagemaker-ai-mlflow-apps/) (April 2026 reference architecture tracing every model back to its exact training data — useful as the audit substrate when a regulator asks which checkpoints touched a deleted record), [Pachyderm](https://github.com/pachyderm/pachyderm) for data-versioned pipelines on Kubernetes, lakeFS for data lake snapshotting, MLflow lineage for experiment-level traces.
