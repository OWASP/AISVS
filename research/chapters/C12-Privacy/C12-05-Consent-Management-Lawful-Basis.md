The bipartisan US **TRAIN Act** (House version reintroduced January 2026 by Reps. Dean and Moran; Senate companion led by Sens. Blackburn and Welch) would create an administrative subpoena process modeled on DMCA Section 512(h) so copyright holders with a good-faith belief their work was used for training can compel disclosure of training records — endorsed by RIAA, SAG-AFTRA, Recording Academy, and Copyright Clearance Center, and now the most-cited US federal proposal targeting opt-out verifiability rather than just opt-out availability.

GitHub's 25 March 2026 Copilot policy update uses interaction data from Copilot Free, Pro, and Pro+ users for model training from 24 April 2026 unless the user opts out; the covered data includes prompts, code snippets shown to Copilot, accepted or modified outputs, nearby code context, file names, repository structure, comments, documentation, navigation patterns, chat interactions, and suggestion feedback, while Copilot Business, Copilot Enterprise, enterprise-owned repositories, and opted-out users are excluded. The W3C DPV Community Group's 15 February 2026 final report on ISO/IEC TS 27560 consent records gives auditors a practical machine-readable evidence target: ask for a consent record or receipt that binds data subject, purpose, personal-data category, legal basis, notice, controller/processor roles, validity window, withdrawal state, and downstream recipients, then test whether inference gateways, clean rooms, agent delegations, and training pipelines actually consume that state rather than treating it as static CMP paperwork.

> **12 May 2026 enforcement note:** The FTC's 30 March 2026 OkCupid/Match action is a concrete AI-training consent test case for both runtime scope checks and withdrawal propagation. Clarifai's April 2026 deletion of the OkCupid photos and facial-recognition models trained on them is a useful 12.5.2 verification pattern: ask for deletion certificates that cover both source records and model artifacts, then reconcile them against lineage, model inventory, and deployment logs rather than accepting a data-store deletion ticket alone. > **12 May 2026 consent-evidence note:** The FTC's final GM/OnStar order from 14 January 2026 is a useful non-AI control pattern because it requires affirmative express consent before collecting, using, or sharing connected-vehicle telemetry; a deletion path; the ability to disable precise geolocation collection; and opt-out controls for geolocation and driver-behavior data. For AI systems, auditors can translate the same pattern into proof that consent state is enforced before collection, inference, retrieval, fine-tuning, clean-room activation, and third-party sharing, rather than captured once in a privacy portal and ignored downstream. ## Purpose

Record, enforce, and revoke consent across AI processing pipelines. GDPR Articles 6-7 require valid legal basis for all personal data processing. Without centralized consent tracking, organizations cannot demonstrate lawful basis for AI training data, leading to regulatory exposure. The Italian Garante fined OpenAI EUR 15M in November 2024 for lack of lawful basis (violating GDPR Articles 5(1)(a), 5(2), 6, 12, 13, 24, 25) and imposed a six-month mandatory information campaign — but the Court of Rome annulled Decision No. 755 on 18 March 2026, scrapping both the fine and the media-campaign order. The court has not yet published its reasoning, but the reversal is a significant setback for aggressive GDPR enforcement against generative AI and underscores how unsettled the legal-basis analysis remains for large-scale training data; the Garante has indicated it may appeal. To date this remains the only final GDPR enforcement action in Europe targeting the launch period of generative AI, and its annulment leaves regulators without a clear precedent going into the EU AI Act's full enforcement window. The EDPB's 2024 opinion clarified that legitimate interest requires controllers to articulate sufficiently clear purposes and ensure processing remains within reasonable data subject expectations — the opinion applies a two-pronged anonymity test (unlikely to identify individuals and unlikely to extract personal data through queries) and warns that unlawful processing during model development can taint downstream deployment legality unless the model is duly anonymized. Most CMPs were designed for web cookie consent and are poorly integrated with ML pipelines — no standard exists for consent tokens in inference APIs. The European Commission's Q4 2025 Digital Package proposal marks the first significant GDPR reform initiative, expanding the Records of Processing Activities exemption while enforcement intensifies around AI processing and consent manipulation. As of April 2026, cumulative GDPR penalties have surpassed EUR 7.1 billion, with EUR 1.15 billion issued in 2025 per the EDPB's annual report — AI processing, consent UX, and vendor management are the three fastest-growing fine triggers heading into H2 2026. The EDPB and EDPS issued Joint Opinion 1/2026 on the proposed AI Omnibus Regulation, and a public consultation on joint GDPR-AI Act interplay guidelines (opened October 2025, closed December 2025) drew over 100 responses published in March 2026, with final guidelines expected throughout 2026. The EU AI Act reaches full enforcement for high-risk systems on 2 August 2026, layering a second penalty regime of up to EUR 35 million or 7% of global turnover — exceeding GDPR's EUR 20 million/4% cap. Across the Atlantic, 20 US states now have comprehensive privacy laws in effect, many requiring explicit consent for sensitive-data processing (including AI-derived inferences); the Texas Responsible AI Governance Act (January 2026) applies existing privacy requirements to data collected for AI systems and creates limited exceptions for biometric data used in AI training. Brazil's ANPD continues extending GDPR-style enforcement to Latin America, targeting AI training data collection without proper consent. Under EU AI Act Article 53 (applicable to GPAI model providers from August 2025, with full AI Office enforcement powers from August 2026), providers must document and publish training data summaries including identification of opt-outs submitted by rights holders. The Commission's mandatory training data summary template (published July 2025) requires structured disclosure of data sources, collection methods, and content types, with six-month update cadence for post-market training changes. GPAI models placed on the market before August 2025 have until August 2027 to publish their summaries. Article 53 also requires providers to identify and comply with copyright opt-outs using "state-of-the-art technologies" — a deliberately open-ended standard that regulators can ratchet as detection capabilities improve. Non-compliance with GPAI transparency obligations carries penalties of up to 3% of global turnover or EUR 15 million (whichever is higher), separate from the broader high-risk AI system penalties. Beyond the EU, consent enforcement for AI training is globalizing rapidly. South Korea's PIPC issued its Generative AI Guidelines (August 2025) requiring documented legal basis under PIPA for all AI training data, and the March 2026 PIPA amendments raise the maximum administrative penalty from 3% to 10% of total revenue for repeated violations involving willful misconduct or gross negligence, violations affecting 10 million+ data subjects, or failure to comply with corrective orders — the PIPC also demonstrated willingness to order algorithm deletion, not merely data deletion, in the January 2025 Kakao Pay/Alipay enforcement (KRW 8.3 billion fine) where Alipay built credit scores from 40 million users' data without consent. The PIPC separately fined Meta KRW 21.6 billion (November 2024) for bundled consent practices, confirming that pre-ticked or bundled permissions do not satisfy Korean standards for sensitive data. Brazil's ANPD set enforcement priorities for 2026-2027 covering AI systems, facial recognition, and recommendation systems; the ANPD previously blocked Meta from using legitimate interest as a legal basis for AI training under the LGPD, holding that the processing could involve sensitive data. GDPR Article 22 adds a further consent dimension: European DPAs increasingly interpret it as a default prohibition on solely automated decisions producing legal effects — not merely a right individuals must invoke — which elevates the consent bar for AI-powered profiling, credit scoring, and hiring systems. On the opt-out side, noyb sent Meta a formal cease-and-desist in May 2025 over its plan to train Meta AI on EU Facebook and Instagram data under claimed legitimate interest from 27 May 2025, and has urged 11 supervisory authorities to take emergency measures; under the EU Collective Redress Directive, noyb and other qualified entities can pursue EU-wide class actions that could produce multi-billion-euro damages claims if the processing proceeds unchallenged. IAPP's 2026 Global Privacy Summit surfaced a consistent message from enforcers: operationalized privacy beats documented policy, and dark-pattern enforcement is expanding into consent flows, GPC signal handling, and opt-out friction — with US states already levying seven-figure penalties for GPC treated as device-specific rather than account-wide or for tracking that continued after opt-out. Agentic AI is forcing a rethink of consent itself; traditional one-shot consent models cannot meaningfully authorize autonomous agents that issue thousands of downstream actions per hour, and standards bodies (OpenID Foundation, IETF OAuth WG) are working on scoped, short-lived, actor-bound delegation tokens rather than extending legacy CMP patterns. The IAPP Global Summit 2026 (April 2026) and the upcoming AI Governance Global Europe (16-19 November 2026, Brussels) reinforced the same theme on the agentic side: panels concluded that "Accept All Cookies" and "Agree to Terms" patterns cannot meaningfully authorize agents that browse, transact, and coordinate with other agents on a user's behalf, and that consent capture must occur before any analysis of customer interactions feeds into agent reasoning. India's Digital Personal Data Protection Rules 2025 push consent governance further into the operational layer: consent-manager registration becomes effective on 13 November 2026 (Phase II) and the substantive compliance obligations including erasure-on-withdrawal go live on 13 May 2027 (Phase III). Every dataset used to train a Data Fiduciary's model must carry demonstrable, purpose-specific consent and verifiable lineage — and where exact unlearning is technically impossible, the DPDP framework leaves controllers exposed to having to retire the model entirely. Texas TRAIGA (effective 1 January 2026) keeps its consent-bar lower than the EU framework but introduces an aggressive penalty band: civil penalties of USD 10,000 to USD 200,000 per violation accruing daily, exclusive enforcement by the Texas Attorney General with a mandatory 60-day cure period, and a safe-harbor for organizations substantially complying with NIST AI RMF or other recognized AI risk management standards — meaning AI risk management documentation now functions as a direct shield against TRAIGA enforcement. EU AI Act Article 50 transparency obligations enter the same August 2026 enforcement window: AI chatbots must disclose their artificial nature, deepfake content must carry machine-readable watermarks, and emotion recognition and biometric categorization systems face mandatory subject notice — these obligations interact with consent because users cannot validly consent to processing they were never told was AI-driven. On the tooling side, the largest CMP vendor moved decisively into AI-aware consent enforcement: OneTrust announced on 28 April 2026 a Consent Management Native App on the Snowflake Marketplace that embeds consent signals directly into Snowflake Data Clean Rooms so that queries, joins, and activations honor purpose and revocation choices at the analytics layer rather than only at collection. Cisco's open-source Model Provenance Kit (released 30 April 2026, Apache 2.0, GitHub `cisco-ai-defense/model-provenance-kit`) does not capture consent on its own but provides an independent verification layer for derived-model lineage — useful when a model claims to have been trained only on consented data and a controller needs to prove a derivative was not built on a tainted upstream. Decentralized consent is moving from research to specification: the LLMConsent project published the LCS-001 Core Consent Standard (training-permission, inference-permission, synthetic-data, and unlearning bits encoded into a cryptographically signed token), LCS-002 Digital Twin Standard, LCS-003 Agent Permissions Standard, and LCS-004 Memory Sharing Standard with REST endpoints and smart-contract reference implementations under `github.com/LLMConsent/llmconsent-standards`; whether the blockchain dependency proves practical at scale remains an open question, but the schema is the most concrete attempt to date to standardize bit-level consent flags machines can verify before training or inference.

## Audit Focus: Consent Evidence

- Treat the consent record as a runtime authorization input, not a static compliance artifact; inference gateways, RAG retrievers, fine-tuning jobs, feature stores, clean-room queries, and external agent tools should all evaluate the same current state.
- Ask for a machine-readable record or receipt that binds the data subject, controller or processor, purpose, personal-data category, legal basis, notice version, validity window, withdrawal state, downstream recipients, and AI processing operation.
- Test negative paths with absent, expired, scope-mismatched, withdrawn, and recipient-mismatched consent records; the system should fail closed and emit reason-coded audit events that privacy and security teams can reconcile.
- For third-party AI features, require contract and telemetry evidence that opt-outs stop future training, retrieval, profiling, clean-room activation, and onward sharing within the stated SLA, not just after a delayed vendor retention window.
- For agentic privacy operations, bind consent to the user, client or agent identity, requested tool, purpose, scope, expiry, and revocation channel; administrator approval alone is not enough if the external client can retain or train on privacy-system data.
- For tiered SaaS and developer-tool policies, test consent separately for personal, free, trial, paid, organization, and enterprise accounts; the account tier can change whether training is default-on, opt-in, unavailable to opt out, or contractually excluded.
- For AI products that rely on subprocessors, collect the subprocessor training and retention terms, then confirm the local consent ledger can block both first-party training and onward model-improvement sharing.
- For agent on-behalf-of flows, include inherited delegated permissions, tenant boundaries, actor identity, requested tool, scope, purpose, and expiry in the consent evidence package.
- Treat AI-disclosure state as part of consent evidence under EU AI Act Article 50: a user cannot meaningfully consent to an AI-driven interaction if the system did not disclose that the interaction or generated content was AI-mediated.
- Replay historical consent changes against clean-room, activation, inference, and agent-tool logs to verify that downstream processing stopped at the recorded withdrawal time rather than at the next batch export.
- For training and fine-tuning jobs, sample job manifests and checkpoint metadata to confirm withdrawn or non-consented records are excluded before the next corpus build, not merely tagged for later cleanup.
- Reconcile public training-data summaries, crawler logs, dataset manifests, model cards, data lineage, and deletion or unlearning certificates so lawful-basis claims can be tested across both source data and derived model artifacts.

Agentic JWT (A-JWT, arXiv:2509.13597; also published as IETF `draft-goswami-agentic-jwt-00`, "Secure Intent Protocol: JWT Compatible Agentic Identity and Workflow Management") tackles what the authors call the "intent-execution separation problem" — stochastic reasoning, prompt injection, and multi-agent orchestration can silently expand an agent's privileges even when the original consent token is valid. A-JWT introduces three mechanisms relevant to consent scope: (1) a cryptographic agent identity via a checksum hash over the agent's system prompt, tools, and configuration — so a prompt-injected variant cannot reuse a legitimately issued token; (2) workflow-aware token binding that links a specific user intent to a specific execution step; and (3) a new OAuth 2.0 grant type `agent_checksum`. The reference Python proof-of-concept demonstrates sub-millisecond overhead and blocks scope-violating requests, replay, in-process impersonation, and prompt-injection pathways — making it a credible defensive layer when consent scope must survive agentic pipelines rather than single-shot inference calls. Questions around AI-driven profiling and automated decision-making will be central to these guidelines. For trained models, machine unlearning remains an active research area with meaningful progress and sobering limitations. The EUPG framework (Universitat Rovira i Virgili, July 2025) prepares models for deletion from the start by training on k-anonymized or differentially private data, then fine-tuning on originals — upon withdrawal, the model reverts to the privacy-protected version and retrains without the subject's data. Source-free unlearning methods (UC Riverside, September 2025) can operate without access to the original training data, addressing a major obstacle for commercial LLMs. However, a widely cited NeurIPS 2025 paper ("Machine Unlearning Doesn't Do What You Think," Cooper et al., Google DeepMind) identifies fundamental mismatches between unlearning goals and feasible implementations for generative models, concluding that unlearning is not a general-purpose solution — generative AI blurs the line between memory and inference, so even deleted data may be indirectly reconstructable. Prevention (blocking data ingestion upon consent withdrawal) remains the primary enforcement mechanism. The ICML 2025 MUGen workshop is driving toward standardized evaluation frameworks for unlearning verification. Agentic unlearning is an emerging subfield addressing LLM agents with persistent memory: Synchronized Backflow Unlearning (SBU, arXiv:2602.17692) jointly unlearns across model parameters and memory stores to prevent parameter-memory backflow where retrieval reactivates parametric remnants. The ALU framework (arXiv:2502.00406) takes a multi-agent, retrain-free approach — multiple LLM agents each handle a step in the unlearning process without weight updates, making it model-agnostic. Both approaches matter for consent withdrawal in RAG-based systems where deletion must cover both vector stores and model-level traces. Audit trail requirements add overhead: version-controlled robots.txt, access logs queryable by user-agent, and crawler activity records should be retained for 3+ years minimum. For analytics and clean-room workflows, OneTrust's Consent Management Native App for Snowflake (28 April 2026 launch via the Snowflake Marketplace) propagates revocation into Data Clean Rooms so that downstream queries, joins, and audience activations enforce withdrawal at the SQL layer rather than only at the collection layer — verify that a withdrawn consent record visibly stops downstream activations within the SLA when testing. For lineage-based verification that a derived model was not built on the withdrawn data, Cisco's Model Provenance Kit (Apache 2.0, released 30 April 2026 — `github.com/cisco-ai-defense/model-provenance-kit`) generates per-model fingerprints from architecture, tokenizer, embedding geometry, normalization-layer signatures, and energy profiles and can compare two checkpoints or scan against a 150-base-model fingerprint database; auditors can use it to challenge claims that "model B was retrained without subject X's data" by surfacing inherited weights from a prior checkpoint that was tainted. India's DPDP framework adds urgency: under the 2025 rules, withdrawal of consent obliges the controller to erase the data once the specified purpose is no longer served, and where exact unlearning is impossible the regulator may force model retirement — making prevention (blocking ingestion before training starts) the only durable strategy, with verification that the consent-withdrawal pipeline halts all live training jobs at the next checkpoint and not merely at the next epoch. The Atlassian 16 April 2026 default-on AI training policy (taking effect 17 August 2026 across Jira and Confluence) shows how withdrawal SLAs collide with vendor data-retention windows: collected data persists in training datasets for up to 7 years even after opt-out or account deletion, with 30-90 days of removal processing — verify, when assessing a third-party AI feature, that the contract carries a written commitment that withdrawn data is excluded from the next training run rather than merely tagged for delayed retirement, and that the upstream vendor publishes a removal-processing-window SLA the data subject can rely on. California's AB 2013 (effective 1 January 2026) does not by itself create a withdrawal SLA, but its eight-category disclosure obligation forces controllers to know which datasets fed which models and to update the public summary on substantial modification, providing auditors a paper trail for testing whether withdrawn data was carried into a subsequent retraining cycle. The proposed federal TRAIN Act (re-introduced January 2026 by Reps. Dean and Moran in the House and Sens. Blackburn and Welch in the Senate) would add an administrative-subpoena layer modeled on DMCA Section 512(h), letting copyright holders compel disclosure of training records on a good-faith claim — converting today's opt-out problem into a verifiability problem that any 12.5.2 verification program should anticipate when designing audit retention. |
