# C7.4: Source Attribution & Citation Integrity

[Back to C07 Index](C07-Model-Behavior.md)

## Purpose

Source attribution and citation integrity controls ensure that when a system uses retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) to ground its responses in source documents, the citations are genuine and verifiable. LLMs are known to fabricate citations — generating plausible-looking but nonexistent references, or citing real sources that do not actually support the claimed content. In RAG architectures, citations should be derived from retrieval metadata (which documents were actually retrieved) rather than generated by the model (which can hallucinate sources). This section addresses both attribution (linking responses to retrieved documents) and verification (confirming cited claims are actually supported by the cited source).

### 2024-2026 Research: Mechanistic Detection, Hybrid Retrieval, and Multi-Evidence Verification

Research into citation hallucination and grounded generation has advanced substantially since 2024, with several key developments relevant to implementing these requirements.

**Understanding hallucination sources in RAG.** A systematic review of hallucination in retrieval-augmented LLMs (Hallucination Mitigation for Retrieval-Augmented Large Language Models, 2025) identifies two primary failure stages: (1) retrieval failure -- encompassing data source quality, query formulation, retriever limitations, and retrieval strategy problems; and (2) generation deficiency -- including context noise, context conflict, the "lost in the middle" curse (where models underweight information in the middle of long contexts), alignment problems, and capability boundaries. This taxonomy is directly useful for designing verification controls: different failure modes require different detection strategies.

**Mechanistic citation hallucination detection (FACTUM).** The FACTUM framework (January 2026) represents a significant advance by reframing citation hallucination as a "coordination failure between the Attention (reading) and Feed-Forward Network (recalling) pathways" inside transformer models. FACTUM uses four mechanistic scores -- Contextual Alignment Score, Attention Sink Usage, Parametric Force, and Pathway Alignment -- to detect problematic citations by examining the model's internal states during generation. It outperforms state-of-the-art baselines by up to 37.5% in AUC for citation hallucination detection. Critically, the research found that correctness signatures evolve with model scale: 3B-parameter models rely on high pathway alignment, while 8B-parameter models employ a specialized strategy where pathways provide distinct, orthogonal information. This scale-dependent behavior means detection strategies cannot be one-size-fits-all.

**Hybrid retrieval for citation grounding.** A December 2025 study on citation-grounded code comprehension demonstrated that hybrid fusion retrieval (combining multiple retrieval modes with optimized parameters) achieved 92% citation accuracy across all tested models, representing a 14-18 percentage point improvement over single-mode retrieval baselines. The approach requires LLMs to cite specific line ranges that must overlap with retrieved chunks, enforced through interval arithmetic -- a concrete, verifiable citation mechanism.

**Multi-evidence guided answer refinement.** The MEGA-RAG framework (2025) addresses hallucination in high-stakes domains (public health) through multi-evidence guided answer refinement, where multiple retrieved evidence passages are cross-checked before the final response is composed. This approach aligns with requirement 7.4.3's goal of ensuring claims are actually supported by retrieved content.

**Agentic and tool-augmented verification.** A comprehensive survey on hallucination mitigation (2025) highlights the emergence of agentic systems that incorporate retrieval verification as an explicit reasoning step -- the model is prompted or fine-tuned to verify its own citations against retrieved context before emitting a response, sometimes using separate verification tool calls. This complements AISVS C7.2 (Hallucination Detection & Mitigation), whose 7.2.3 additional-verification step for high-risk responses can fold citation re-checking into the same gate.

**Production-grade token-level detection (HaluGate).** As of December 2025, the [HaluGate](https://vllm.ai/blog/halugate) framework — integrated into vLLM — brings real-time, token-level hallucination detection to production deployments. HaluGate uses a two-stage pipeline: a ModernBERT-based classifier (HaluGate Sentinel, 96.4% accuracy, ~12ms) first determines whether a query requires fact-checking, then a token-level detector pinpoints exactly which tokens are unsupported by retrieved context, followed by an NLI explainer that classifies flagged spans as CONTRADICTION, NEUTRAL, or ENTAILMENT. Total pipeline overhead is 76-162ms (P50-P99), negligible compared to typical LLM generation times. The system is implemented in native Rust/Candle with no Python runtime, eliminating cold-start delays. This represents a practical path toward satisfying 7.4.3's claim-level verification requirement without the multi-second latency of LLM-as-judge approaches.

**Citation enforcement as a hard constraint.** A March 2026 study on [citation-enforced RAG for fiscal document intelligence](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.14170) demonstrated that treating citations as a generation constraint — not a stylistic suggestion — achieves 94.5% citation support accuracy with only 1.8% unsupported claims. The system uses source-first ingestion (only content directly extracted from original documents becomes retrievable), requires every generated paragraph to include at least one citation in a standardized document-page-chunk format, and rejects outputs that fail validation. Outputs failing citation requirements are regenerated with reinforced instructions; if citation constraints cannot be satisfied after multiple attempts, the system abstains rather than hallucinate. This "verifiability over abstraction" design philosophy is particularly relevant for regulated domains.

**Span-level verification benchmarks (REFIND/SemEval 2025).** The [REFIND framework](https://aclanthology.org/2025.semeval-1.2/) (SemEval 2025 Task 3) introduced the Context Sensitivity Ratio (CSR) metric for quantifying how sensitive LLM outputs are to retrieved evidence at the span level. Evaluated across nine languages on the Mu-SHROOM dataset, REFIND significantly outperformed baseline token-level classifiers and FAVA on Intersection-over-Union (IoU) scores. This multilingual span-level approach suggests that citation verification can extend beyond English-only systems.

**Full-context real-time verification (Fast and Faithful, March 2026).** The [Fast and Faithful](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.23508) system tackles a critical gap: most verification models truncate retrieved documents to 8K tokens, but real-world RAG documents average ~17.5K tokens — meaning 95% of evidence gets discarded. By extending the verification context window to 32K tokens, the system achieves an 817% improvement in hallucination recall (0.55 vs 0.06) and a 400% improvement in hallucination F1 (0.50 vs 0.10) compared to 8K baselines on long-document benchmarks. Latency remains practical: 10.9ms at 8K tokens, 32ms at 16K, and 63ms at 24K tokens. An early-exit mechanism allows trading accuracy for speed — exiting at layer 16 (of 22) retains 92.8% example F1 at 1.4x speedup. This is directly relevant to 7.4.3: chunk-based entailment checking systematically misses evidence that spans multiple chunks or appears only in un-retrieved portions of the source document.

**Citation-first retrieval architecture (Index-RAG, March 2026).** The [Index-RAG](https://medium.com/@praneeth.v/index-rag-citation-first-approach-to-rag-0e948b9e12c1) (i-RAG) approach inverts the usual RAG design by storing text locations (filename, page number, line number) rather than duplicating text across embeddings. Using paragraph-level segmentation at natural boundaries and multi-vector storage with blended retrieval scoring, i-RAG achieves 83.3% Precision@1 (25% improvement over baseline) and 0.917 MRR (11.9% improvement). Unlike reasoning-based alternatives that run expensive LLM passes over entire documents at query time, i-RAG maintains fast retrieval while providing exact source coordinates — a practical approach for satisfying 7.4.1's metadata-derived attribution requirement without runtime overhead.

**Pragmatic misleadingness: a failure mode beyond hallucination.** As of March 2026, research has identified a subtler problem than outright citation fabrication: RAG systems that cite accurate sources can still be "pragmatically misleading" by decontextualizing facts, omitting critical caveats, and treating sources as collections of citable facts rather than communicative documents with broader context. A [systematic analysis](https://www.whyaitech.com/notes/systems-note-002.html) documents six specific failure modes: retrieval misses, insufficient evidence, evidence misinterpretation, multi-source synthesis errors (where models insert "hallucinated bridges" between individually correct sources), attribution failures, and architectural attribution problems in generate-then-retrieve pipelines. The analysis reports that citation presence shows "near-zero correlation with factual accuracy" and that approximately 50% of answers on challenging datasets lack complete citation support. This has direct implications for 7.4.3: entailment verification must go beyond checking whether a cited source is real and relevant — it must verify that the specific claim is actually supported, in context, by the cited passage.

**Spatial metadata for fine-grained citation.** As of March 2026, production RAG citation approaches increasingly leverage spatial document metadata — bounding box coordinates, page numbers, and reading order — to enable citations that link not just to a document but to a specific visual region on a specific page. [Tensorlake's citation-aware RAG approach](https://www.tensorlake.ai/blog/rag-citations) embeds lightweight anchors (e.g., `<c>2.1</c>` for page 2, reading order 1) into chunk text during indexing, maps each anchor to bounding box metadata stored separately in the vector database, and resolves citation IDs to clickable deep links or PDF highlights at serving time. Storage overhead is approximately 10-15%. This spatial traceability enables audit trails that satisfy both 7.4.1 (metadata-derived attribution) and regulatory requirements for document provenance.

**Retrieval-generation isolation as an architectural requirement.** A January 2026 comprehensive review of [RAG stack architecture and trust frameworks](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.05264v1) formalizes the principle that retrieval metadata must remain architecturally distinct from generation components. The review identifies several critical patterns: immutable evidence records (read-only logs of retrieved passages before they enter the generator), retrieval-generation isolation (the generator cannot access or modify the retrieval metadata store), and confidence score separation (retrieval confidence scores are attached independently, preventing the generator from inflating relevance assessments). The review also catalogs architectural anti-patterns that undermine citation integrity: implicit marginalization (systems that lose track of which passages contributed to which claims), late fusion opacity (aggregating evidence without preserving per-claim attribution), context truncation (silently discarding retrieved content and creating invisible citation gaps), and score aliasing (letting generators see only relevance scores without the actual passages). These patterns directly inform 7.4.2's requirement that attribution be metadata-derived rather than model-generated.

**Auditable citation verification for specialized domains.** [HalluGraph](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.01659) (December 2025) introduces a graph-theoretic framework for legal RAG citation auditing that decomposes hallucinations into two interpretable metrics: Entity Grounding (whether entities in the response appear in source documents) and Relation Preservation (whether asserted relationships are supported by context). This approach is particularly relevant for high-stakes domains where citation integrity has direct legal consequences. Separately, [MetaRAG](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.09360v1) (2025) provides black-box metamorphic testing for hallucination detection that requires no ground truth or model access — useful for auditing proprietary RAG systems where internal states are inaccessible.

**Automated chunk attribution at scale.** As of April 2026, [Galileo](https://galileo.ai/) (acquired by Cisco) offers Luna-2, a suite of fine-tuned 3B-8B evaluation models achieving approximately 86% accuracy on chunk attribution — tracing which retrieved chunks actually support specific claims in generated responses — at ~152ms latency and $0.02 per million tokens. This brings automated citation verification within reach for production monitoring, complementing the more expensive LLM-as-judge approaches.

**Knowledge-graph-grounded retrieval (GroundedKG-RAG, April 2026).** The [GroundedKG-RAG](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.04359v1) framework constructs knowledge graphs where every node and edge is explicitly linked to original source text spans, avoiding the hallucination risk introduced by recursive LLM summarization in hierarchical retrieval architectures like GraphRAG. Using deterministic semantic parsing (AMR or SRL) rather than LLM-generated descriptions, the system maintains human-readable, auditable provenance at every step. On NarrativeQA benchmarks it outperforms GraphRAG baselines on ROUGE-L (34 vs. 8) while using significantly less text than full-context approaches. This is relevant to 7.4.1 and 7.4.2: when the retrieval index itself is constructed by an LLM (as in many graph-RAG pipelines), the "source" an attribution points to may already be a hallucinated summary rather than original text.

**Hypergraph retrieval for high-stakes domains (Hyper-RAG, April 2026).** As of April 2026, [Hyper-RAG](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-71411-1) shows that retrieval architecture can reduce hallucination rates before citation verification runs. The Nature Communications paper uses hypergraph-driven retrieval to capture pairwise and higher-order relationships in domain knowledge; on the NeurologyCrop dataset it improved accuracy by 12.3% over direct LLM use and outperformed GraphRAG and LightRAG by 6.3% and 6.0%, respectively. Across nine additional datasets it reported a 35.5% improvement over LightRAG, while Hyper-RAG-Lite doubled retrieval speed. The citation-integrity caveat is important: stronger retrieval can improve the evidence pool, but it does not itself prove that a generated claim is supported by a cited chunk. Hypergraph and graph-RAG systems still need stable source-span metadata, retrieval logs, and serving-time support checks to satisfy 7.4.1 through 7.4.3.

For verification, treat graph and hypergraph RAG as richer retrieval substrates, not proof of grounding:
- Graph nodes, hyperedges, communities, and expanded neighbors need stable IDs and source-span backpointers.
- Summarized or generated graph artifacts should be marked as derived evidence, not original sources.
- Claim support checks should run against original passages, not only graph neighbors or summaries.
- Retrieval logs should preserve both first-hop evidence and expanded graph context so auditors can reconstruct why a citation was surfaced.

**Holistic trustworthiness measurement (Trust-Score / Trust-Align, ICLR 2025 Oral).** The [Trust-Align](https://github.com/declare-lab/trust-align) framework introduces Trust-Score, a holistic metric evaluating RAG trustworthiness across three axes: response correctness (accuracy of generated claims), attribution quality (quality of inline citations), and refusal groundedness (ability to abstain when context is insufficient). LLaMA-3-8b aligned with Trust-Align outperforms open-source LLMs of comparable size by 10.7% on ASQA, 29.2% on QAMPARI, and 14.9% on ELI5 as measured by Trust-Score. Citation groundedness scores improve by 26-32% across all three benchmarks. This is relevant to both 7.4.3 (claim-level verification) and the broader architectural question of how to train models that produce verifiable citations by default rather than relying entirely on post-hoc verification.

**Three-stage citation verification (VeriCite, SIGIR-AP 2025).** The [VeriCite](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.11394) framework addresses citation reliability through a three-stage pipeline: (1) initial answer generation with NLI-based claim verification, (2) supporting evidence selection that assesses document utility and extracts relevant evidence, and (3) answer refinement that integrates the initial response with collected evidence to produce a final cited answer. Tested across five open-source LLMs and four datasets, VeriCite significantly improves citation quality while maintaining answer correctness comparable to the strongest baselines. The approach represents a middle ground between the computationally expensive per-claim entailment checking (7.4.3) and simpler metadata-passthrough approaches (7.4.1).

**Token-level causal attribution (Jacobian Scopes, January 2026).** The [Jacobian Scopes](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.16407) framework provides gradient-based, token-level causal attribution for LLM predictions using three variants: Semantic Scopes (targeting specific logit sensitivity), Fisher Scopes (analyzing full predictive distributions), and Temperature Scopes (measuring model confidence). By quantifying exactly which input tokens influence each output token, this enables fine-grained tracing of how retrieved content flows into generated responses — a mechanistic complement to FACTUM's attention-based approach. The framework is open-source with interactive demonstrations on Hugging Face Spaces, and is under review at ACL 2026. Relevant to 7.4.2 and 7.4.3 as a potential real-time verification mechanism that operates during generation rather than post-hoc.

**Opinion-aware RAG and the limits of factual grounding (April 2026).** [Research on opinion-aware RAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.12138) identifies a fundamental blind spot in current citation integrity approaches: most RAG systems assume queries seek factual, objective answers where epistemic uncertainty can be reduced through evidence. For subjective content — product reviews, policy debates, social media analysis — this creates systematic bias. Factual RAG minimizes posterior entropy, but opinion-aware RAG must preserve it. Experiments show opinion-aware architectures improve retrieval diversity (+26.8% sentiment diversity, +42.7% entity match rate) compared to factual-only baselines. This matters for citation integrity because a system that only retrieves factual consensus documents can produce "correctly cited" responses that systematically misrepresent the landscape of opinion — a form of pragmatic misleadingness that standard entailment verification (7.4.3) would not catch.

**Streaming citation architectures for production RAG (April 2026).** Recent production implementations have demonstrated [streaming RAG with token-level citations](https://dasroot.net/posts/2026/04/streaming-rag-token-citations-pulsar-redis/) by embedding citation metadata directly into token streams during preprocessing — tagging each relevant token with source identifiers (e.g., document ID and character position) before generation begins. Using Apache Pulsar 3.0 for asynchronous retrieval pipelines and Redis 7.4 for citation metadata caching, these architectures achieve sub-200ms latency while handling 12,000 queries/second at peak load. This addresses a practical gap: most citation verification approaches operate post-generation, but streaming citation injection ensures attribution metadata is available from the first token. LangChain v0.26 provides citation rendering support with options to trade accuracy for latency in streaming scenarios.

**Numerical claim manipulation in government RAG (RAGShield, April 2026).** The current [RAGShield](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.00387) paper narrows citation integrity to a concrete failure mode: embedding-based RAG defenses can miss small but high-impact numeric changes because embeddings preserve topic similarity rather than numerical precision. In the IRS-document evaluation, changing a tax deduction by $50,000 still produced cosine similarity of 0.9998, and embedding-based defenses missed 79-90% of 430 generated attacks. RAGShield avoids that blind spot by extracting dollar amounts and percentages, linking each value to its governing entity, checking claims against a cross-source registry, and flagging temporal changes outside expected update schedules. For 7.4.2, this is a reminder that "the cited chunk is semantically similar" is not enough; auditors should require exact-value checks for regulated financial, tax, benefit, medical, and legal domains where a single unsupported number can create direct harm.

**Metadata-only poisoning in multimodal RAG (MM-MEPA, February 2026).** [Hidden in the Metadata](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.00172) shows that multimodal RAG can be steered by changing image-text metadata while leaving the visible image unchanged. The MM-MEPA attack reports up to 91% attack success across four retrievers and two multimodal generators, and the authors found representative defenses largely ineffective. That directly affects 7.4.1 and 7.4.3: source attribution based on retrieval metadata becomes dangerous if the metadata field itself is attacker-controlled or unvalidated. Verification should therefore include metadata provenance, parser trust boundaries, and consistency checks between visible content, extracted text, captions, alt text, filenames, and stored vector metadata.

**Multimodal knowledge poisoning (MM-PoisonRAG, ACL 2026).** [MM-PoisonRAG](https://web.cs.ucla.edu/~kwchang/bibliography/ha2026mmpoisonrag/) broadens the concern from metadata fields to the external multimodal knowledge base itself. The paper reports two attack modes: Localized Poisoning Attack, which implants targeted multimodal misinformation for specific queries, and Globalized Poisoning Attack, which uses a single adversarial knowledge item to broadly disrupt reasoning. Reported results include up to 56% targeted attack success for localized poisoning and 0% accuracy under globalized poisoning with one injected adversarial item. For 7.4.1 and 7.4.2, the audit implication is straightforward: citations to retrieved images, captions, OCR text, or image-text pairs must preserve separate provenance for each modality and confirm that cross-modal evidence agrees before treating the citation as grounded.

**Graph-RAG logical poisoning (LogicPoison, April 2026).** [LogicPoison](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.02954) targets GraphRAG systems by corrupting logical connections rather than injecting obviously false text. The attack swaps entities in a type-preserving way to disrupt global graph hubs and query-specific reasoning bridges, preserving surface plausibility while rerouting multi-hop inference into dead ends. For citation integrity, graph nodes, communities, and relation edges need the same source-span backpointers and integrity checks as flat chunks; otherwise a response can cite real text while the graph topology that selected and interpreted that text has been adversarially distorted.

**Bidirectional RAG with multi-stage acceptance (December 2025).** [Bidirectional RAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.22199) addresses a citation-integrity blind spot in self-improving systems: when generated answers are written back to the corpus, hallucinated content can become a future "source" — laundering fabrication into the citation chain. The framework requires every write-back candidate to pass a multi-stage acceptance gate: (1) grounding verification via NLI-based entailment, (2) attribution checking against the originating retrieved chunks, and (3) novelty detection to avoid duplicating existing content. Across Natural Questions, TriviaQA, HotpotQA, and Stack Overflow, Bidirectional RAG achieves 40.58% average coverage (nearly doubling Standard RAG's 20.33%) while adding 72% fewer documents than naive write-back (140 vs. 500). Relevant to 7.4.3: any RAG architecture that allows the corpus to grow over time needs a hard validation step before generated content is treated as a citable source.

**MITRE ATLAS expands RAG threat coverage (October 2025 – February 2026).** The October 2025 ATLAS update incorporated 14 new techniques from a [Zenity Labs collaboration](https://zenity.io/blog/current-events/zenity-labs-and-mitre-atlas-collaborate-to-advances-ai-agent-security-with-the-first-release-of) covering AI agents and generative AI, including **RAG Credential Harvesting** (using an LLM to surface credentials inadvertently ingested into a RAG database) and retrieval manipulation as an explicit attack surface. The February 2026 ATLAS v5.4.0 release added further agent-focused techniques. For citation integrity, the practical implication is that retrieval indexes are now treated as named, mapped attack surfaces in industry threat models — verification programs should include retrieval-pipeline tests (poisoned chunk insertion, embedding-space adversarial documents, retrieval manipulation via crafted queries) alongside generation-time citation checks.

**Vendor-native citation APIs as metadata-passthrough primitives (Anthropic Citations API, May 2026).** Anthropic's [Citations API](https://claude.com/blog/introducing-citations-api) — generally available across the Messages API on the Anthropic platform and on Google Cloud Vertex AI — chunks user-provided source documents (PDF and plain text) into sentences server-side, then emits structured citation blocks inline with generated text. Each citation block carries the document index, sentence-level character offsets, page number for PDFs, and the cited text itself, all populated from the document the user supplied rather than from token-stream generation. This makes the API a direct architectural fit for 7.4.1 and 7.4.3: citation metadata cannot be fabricated because the model is not asked to emit citation strings, and the source content the citation points to is exactly the document the application sent in the request. The [public API documentation](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/citations) notes one operational caveat relevant to 7.4.2 — Citations interleave citation blocks with generated text, which is incompatible with strict-JSON structured outputs, so applications that need both structured fields and citations must combine the Citations API with a second function-calling pass or downstream parser rather than expecting both in a single response. Combined with [OpenAI File Search](https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/guides/tools-file-search) (file citation annotations and `include=["file_search_call.results"]` for audit) and [Google Vertex AI Check Grounding](https://cloud.google.com/generative-ai-app-builder/docs/check-grounding), the three major frontier-lab APIs now ship first-party metadata-passthrough citation primitives, eliminating the historical excuse that prompt-based "cite your sources" was the only available pattern.

**Causal vs. surface attribution (April 2026).** [Production RAG monitoring guidance](https://dasroot.net/posts/2026/04/production-rag-monitoring-performance-quality/) and [Galileo's chunk attribution metric](https://v2docs.galileo.ai/concepts/metrics/response-quality/chunk-attribution) increasingly distinguish between *surface attribution* (a citation is attached to a claim) and *causal attribution* (the cited chunk genuinely influenced generation, not merely matched after the fact). Galileo's binary Chunk Attribution metric tracks per-chunk influence and recommends tuning retrieved-chunk count when many chunks are flagged "Not Attributed" — a practical signal that retrieval is over-fetching and the model is ignoring most of what it cited. This matters for 7.4.2: a system can satisfy "every claim cites a retrieved chunk" while still being causally disconnected from those chunks (the December 2024 "Correctness is not Faithfulness" finding generalized at scale).

**Eight-engine AI search audit (Columbia Journalism Review / Tow Center, March 2025).** The CJR Tow Center [eight-engine study](https://www.cjr.org/tow_center/we-compared-eight-ai-search-engines-theyre-all-bad-at-citing-news.php) (1,600 queries across ChatGPT Search, Perplexity, Perplexity Pro, DeepSeek Search, Microsoft Copilot, Grok-2, Grok-3 beta, and Google Gemini) found Grok-3 wrong 94% of the time, ChatGPT wrong on 60%+ of queries (134 articles cited incorrectly), and Perplexity at 37% — the "best" performer still hallucinating roughly one in three citations. More damning for 7.4.1 and 7.4.3: more than half of Gemini and Grok-3 responses cited *fabricated or broken URLs* (Grok-3: 154 of 200 citations led to error pages), and content licensing deals with publishers provided no measurable improvement in attribution accuracy. The licensing finding is structurally important — it confirms that getting "official" access to source content does not, on its own, fix citation integrity; the architectural separation between retrieval and generation has to be enforced.

**Bidirectional retrieval-attribution problem (May 2026 framing).** Production RAG monitoring writeups now frame attribution as a two-sided problem: the *retrieval side* (which chunk was retrieved and surfaced as a citation) and the *generation side* (which token in the response actually used that chunk). The asymmetry — a single response may mix supported and unsupported claims in the same paragraph, with citations attached at paragraph rather than claim granularity — is the structural reason citation presence has near-zero correlation with factual accuracy. Verification programs targeting 7.4.2 should require per-claim attribution mapping rather than per-paragraph or per-response attribution; tools that treat the response as a flat string and check "are there citations?" do not satisfy the requirement.

**Deep research citation audit (May 2026).** [Cited but Not Verified](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06635) evaluates source attribution in long-form Markdown reports by parsing inline citations with a reproducible AST pipeline, then scoring three dimensions: whether the link works, whether the cited content is topically relevant, and whether the cited content factually supports the claim. The important finding for 7.4.2 is the gap between surface quality and factual support: even the strongest tested systems maintained link validity above 94% and relevance above 80%, but fact-check accuracy ranged from 39% to 77%. The study also found that increasing research depth from 2 to 150 retrieval calls reduced fact-check accuracy by about 42% across two frontier systems, suggesting that more retrieved material can make citation assignment harder rather than safer. Auditors should therefore treat URL resolution and topical relevance as necessary but insufficient checks; the actual cited content still has to be retrieved and compared against the claim.

**Single-document RAG poisoning (CorruptRAG, January 2026).** [CorruptRAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.03957) (formally published April 2025, with refined CorruptRAG-AS and CorruptRAG-AK variants tracked through early 2026) reduces the prerequisite for a successful corpus poisoning attack from "outnumber legitimate documents in the top-k" to a single, carefully crafted document. CorruptRAG-AS uses zero-cost template population (correct answer + targeted answer); CorruptRAG-AK averages ~$0.0001 per query in API cost. The attack achieves higher success rates than the multi-document PoisonedRAG baseline while sitting comfortably under bulk-injection anomaly thresholds. Subsequent work — including [PoisonedEye](https://aclanthology.org/2025.findings-emnlp.1023/) for vision-language RAG and the EMNLP 2025 "One Shot Dominance" paper — generalizes the single-document pattern to multimodal and broader retrieval contexts. The implication for citation integrity is that 7.4.1's metadata-derived attribution is necessary but not sufficient: a perfectly metadata-derived citation pointing to a poisoned chunk still produces an attacker-controlled answer with the appearance of grounding. Defense layers need to combine source authenticity checks, metadata validation, anomaly-aware retrieval scoring, domain-specific value checks, and write-back gating per Bidirectional RAG; relying on any single layer underestimates a single-document adversary. [Attention-Aware defenses](https://openreview.net/forum?id=PS43wqCSME) (Normalized Passage Attention Score, AV Filter) yield up to ~20% accuracy improvement over baselines on standard poisoning, but adaptive attacks designed to mask the attention anomaly still achieve up to 35% success — so attention-based filtering should be treated as defense-in-depth rather than a sole mitigation.

**Reasoning-structure and cross-document poisoning defenses (2026).** [AdversarialCoT](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.12201) (SIGIR 2026 short paper) shows that a single malicious document can be crafted around the target model's observed reasoning pattern, then iteratively refined through black-box feedback until it misleads reasoning-oriented RAG. That matters for citation integrity because the answer can remain fully "cited" while the reasoning path has been steered by attacker-authored evidence. Two defense directions are emerging: [Sparse Document Attention RAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.04711), which blocks cross-attention between retrieved documents to reduce harmful cross-document interactions, and [Semantic Chameleon](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18034), which found hybrid BM25 + vector retrieval reduced gradient-guided attack success from 38% to 0% in one Security Stack Exchange setup, while jointly optimized sparse+dense payloads still reached 20-44% success. The practical takeaway is to audit citation integrity together with retrieval diversity, retriever mixing, attention isolation, and corpus-specific poisoning tests rather than treating source attribution as a pure formatting control.

**Latent-space probing for faithful RAG (ProbeRAG, ACL 2026).** [ProbeRAG](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.12460) reports that conflicting and aligned knowledge states in RAG are linearly separable inside the model's latent space, and that contextual noise systematically increases the entropy of those latent representations. The framework operates in three stages: (1) fine-grained knowledge pruning to filter irrelevant context, (2) latent conflict probing to identify hard conflicts in latent space, and (3) conflict-aware attention modulation toward faithful context. Where post-hoc NLI verification (7.4.3) inspects the output text, latent probing inspects the model's internal state during generation — a complement to FACTUM that enables earlier, cheaper detection but, like FACTUM, requires activation access (not available for closed-API LLMs).

**Multilingual end-to-end RAG meta-evaluation (MEMERAG).** [MEMERAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.17163) extends RAG faithfulness evaluation beyond English by building on MIRACL with native-language questions across English, German, Spanish, French, and Hindi. The dataset includes both coarse-grained and fine-grained expert annotations for faithfulness and relevance, addressing a structural blind spot in earlier benchmarks (which were either English-only or relied on translated data that misses cultural nuance). For citation integrity programs operating across multiple locales, MEMERAG provides the first standardized harness for verifying that attribution behavior is consistent across languages — important because retrieval and entailment performance often degrade significantly outside the model's primary training language.

**Holistic AI search citation audit (5W AI Platform Citation Source Index, May 1, 2026).** The [5W AI Platform Citation Source Index 2026](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/5w-releases-ai-platform-citation-source-index-2026-the-50-websites-that-now-decide-what-brands-are-visible-inside-chatgpt-claude-perplexity-gemini-and-google-ai-overviews-302759804.html), released May 1, 2026, synthesizes more than 680 million individual citations across ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews, Perplexity, Gemini, and Claude — drawn from six of the largest published citation studies between August 2024 and April 2026. The headline finding for 7.4.1 verification is that source distribution is neither neutral nor stable: only 11% of cited domains overlap between ChatGPT and Perplexity, ChatGPT skews toward Wikipedia/encyclopedic sources, and Perplexity heavily cites Reddit. For organizations performing third-party citation-integrity assessments of AI search products, this confirms that an evaluation harness must hold source-domain distribution constant across runs — otherwise observed citation accuracy drift may reflect retrieval-corpus volatility rather than true model behavior. Independent comparative testing (Zapier editorial team, 100 queries) reported Perplexity citations verifiable and accurate 89% of the time vs. ChatGPT at 76% when citations were present, with Perplexity tying every claim to a specific source in 78% of complex research questions vs. ChatGPT's 62% — useful baselines for what "production-grade" attribution accuracy currently looks like.

**Vectara HHEM-2.3 and FaithJudge Q2 2026 refresh.** Vectara's [hallucination leaderboard](https://www.vectara.com/blog/introducing-the-next-generation-of-vectaras-hallucination-leaderboard) now uses HHEM-2.3 (commercial) for ranking, with HHEM-2.1-Open available on Hugging Face and Kaggle as the open-source variant. The leaderboard refresh integrates a richer evaluation dataset and the [FaithJudge](https://github.com/vectara/FaithJudge) framework, which benchmarks LLMs on RAG faithfulness across summarization, question-answering, and data-to-text generation simultaneously rather than only summarization. For verification programs implementing 7.4.3, HHEM-2.3 raises the practical floor: a free, fast neural classifier is now competitive with — and in many cases better than — LLM-as-judge approaches at a fraction of the latency and cost.

**EU AI Act Article 50 — second draft Code of Practice (5 March 2026).** The [second draft of the Code of Practice on Marking and Labelling of AI-Generated Content](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/commission-publishes-second-draft-code-practice-marking-and-labelling-ai-generated-content) was published 5 March 2026, with the comment period closing 30 March 2026 and finalization listed for May-June 2026 on the Commission's [code process page](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/code-practice-ai-generated-content), updated 8 May 2026; obligations apply 2 August 2026. Key changes from the first draft: (1) the taxonomy distinguishing "AI-generated" from "AI-assisted" content was removed in Section 2, simplifying labelling decisions for deployers; (2) a revised two-layered marking approach using secured metadata + watermarking is introduced for providers; (3) optional fingerprinting and logging capabilities are added; (4) the use of open standards is promoted, with examples of a potential EU icon for labelling; (5) several measures from the first draft are now voluntary, reducing compliance burden. Special provisions exist for artistic, creative, satirical, and fictional works. For RAG citation integrity programs, the practical impact is that the marking and labelling regime is converging toward C2PA-compatible secured metadata — meaning citation provenance metadata (per 7.4.1/7.4.3) and AI-content labelling metadata are increasingly likely to share the same provenance chain rather than being maintained as parallel systems.

**Sixth Circuit verification standard (Whiting v. City of Athens, March 2026).** The Sixth Circuit's published opinion in [Whiting v. City of Athens](https://reason.com/volokh/2026/03/14/lawyers-citing-nonexistent-cases-ordered-to-pay-opponents-attorney-fees-double-costs-15k-fine/) (March 13, 2026) articulated a verification standard that is broader than AI-specific guidance: "no filing should contain citations — whether provided by generative AI or any other source — that a lawyer has not personally read and verified." Notably, the court did not expressly find that the fabricated citations resulted from generative AI use; instead it treated the source-agnostic verification duty as the operative rule. The show-cause order required attorneys to: (1) provide copies of all cited cases from Westlaw or LexisNexis, (2) highlight quoted material, (3) explain authorship and disclose ghostwriting, (4) state whether they used generative AI, and (5) explain their cite-checking procedures. For 7.4.1/7.4.2 verification programs in regulated domains, this is the cleanest articulation yet of the duty: source-agnostic, claim-level, with auditable cite-checking procedures as a deliverable. The Sixth Circuit's source-agnostic framing is structurally important because it short-circuits the "AI did it, not me" defense — verification is the obligation regardless of citation provenance.

**Test-time NLI-guided search for faithfulness (Self-Correcting RAG, April 2026).** [Self-Correcting RAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.10734) reframes the citation-integrity problem as a two-sided optimization: on the input side, context selection is cast as a multi-dimensional multiple-choice knapsack problem (MMKP) that maximizes information density while removing redundant retrieved chunks; on the output side, an NLI-guided Monte Carlo Tree Search explores candidate generations and validates each branch's faithfulness against the selected evidence before the final answer is emitted. The reference implementation uses Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct as the generator, BAAI bge-small-en-v1.5 for dense retrieval and BM25 for sparse retrieval, fused via Reciprocal Rank Fusion. Relevant to 7.4.3 as a concrete way to run claim-level NLI verification *during* generation rather than only as a post-hoc gate — the search step refuses to emit unfaithful tokens rather than detecting and discarding them afterwards.

**Counterfactual entity substitution to train context-following (Faithfulness-QA, April 2026).** [Faithfulness-QA](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25313) addresses a structural problem documented by the "Correctness is not Faithfulness" finding: most RAG models still answer from parametric memory and post-rationalize their citations. Starting from SQuAD and TriviaQA, the dataset identifies answer-bearing named entities, replaces them with type-consistent alternatives drawn from a curated bank of 76,953 entities, and manufactures 99,094 controlled context/parameter conflicts where only a context-following model can produce the new "correct" answer. This is directly relevant to 7.4.3 alignment-time interventions (paralleling Trust-Align): when post-hoc verification is too slow or too coarse, training data that explicitly rewards context-following is a complementary control that ships in the weights rather than as a runtime check.

**Internal-external hybrid reward for context-faithful RAG (CTRL-RAG, February 2026).** [CTRL-RAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.04406) reports that existing RAG-oriented reinforcement learning methods rely on external rewards that fail to evaluate document faithfulness, and proposes a Contrastive Likelihood Reward that directly optimizes the log-likelihood gap between responses conditioned on prompts with and without supporting evidence. The model is rewarded for being more confident *because* evidence was provided, not just for being more correct. Reported gains span single-hop, multi-hop, vertical-domain, and faithfulness benchmarks. Together with Trust-Align (ICLR 2025) and Faithfulness-QA, the alignment-side toolbox for citation integrity is now mature enough that 7.4.3 verification programs should expect production deployments to combine runtime entailment checks with alignment-time training rather than relying on either layer alone.

**RAG benchmark for citation prediction (CiteRAG / "What Should I Cite?", WWW 2026).** Published in the Proceedings of the ACM Web Conference 2026 (Dubai, April 2026), [CiteRAG](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3774904.3792075) is the first comprehensive RAG benchmark focused specifically on *which* citations a model should attach, not just whether attached citations are real. It defines two complementary tasks — list-specific citation prediction (complete reference list generation, 7,267 instances) and position-specific citation prediction (precise citations for individual reference placeholders, 8,541 instances) — paired with multi-level hybrid retrieval and contrastively fine-tuned embedding models. The evaluation harness explicitly scores retrieval, classification, hallucination, and citation diversity. Code and data are released at [github.com/LQgdwind/CiteRAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.14949). Relevant to 7.4.1 and 7.4.2 because it surfaces a third class of citation-integrity failure beyond fabrication and mis-attribution: *citation selection bias* — systems that consistently choose less-relevant but easier-to-justify citations even when stronger sources are available in the corpus.

**Model-internals attribution as a deployable baseline (MIRAGE, EMNLP 2024 / 2026 follow-ons).** The original [MIRAGE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.13663) (Model Internals-based RAG Explanations) approach uses saliency methods to detect context-sensitive answer tokens and pair them with the retrieved documents that contributed to each token's prediction, producing faithful attribution without asking the model to emit citation strings. The released [PyPI/GitHub implementation](https://github.com/Betswish/MIRAGE) has remained a deployable baseline through 2025 and into 2026, and the broader Jacobian Scopes (January 2026) and ProbeRAG (ACL 2026) research trajectory builds on the same premise: model internals carry stronger faithfulness signal than the generated citation tokens themselves. For 7.4.2 implementations that have access to model activations (open-weight deployments, on-prem serving), model-internals attribution should be considered as a verification layer alongside metadata passthrough; it produces causal rather than surface attribution and is therefore a partial answer to the "near-zero correlation between citation presence and factual accuracy" finding.

**Token-level encoder detectors as the production-latency floor (LettuceDetect, May 2026).** [LettuceDetect](https://huggingface.co/blog/adaamko/lettucedetect) is a ModernBERT-based token-classification detector trained on the RAGTruth corpus that identifies unsupported spans of a generated answer against retrieved context up to an 8K-token window. Reported example-level F1 on RAGTruth is 79.22% — a 14.8% improvement over Luna, the previous encoder-based state of the art — and the model processes 30-60 examples per second on a single GPU at roughly 1/30th the parameter count of the strongest LLM-judge detectors. Released under MIT with a published [PyPI package](https://pypi.org/project/lettucedetect/0.1.3/), it gives 7.4.3 implementations a deployable detector that is small enough to run inline without GPU clusters and structured enough to feed 7.4.3 enforcement actions (block, redact, or regenerate) with token-level spans. Multilingual variants exist (Turk-LettuceDetect for Turkish RAG), confirming that the architecture transfers beyond English when paired with localized training data.

**Hallucination detection via open-weight proxy analyzers (May 2026).** [Hallucination Detection via Activations of Open-Weight Proxy Analyzers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.07209) (May 8, 2026) closes a long-standing gap in mechanistic citation-integrity verification: prior internal-state approaches (FACTUM, ProbeRAG, MIRAGE, Jacobian Scopes) require activation access to the generator, which is unavailable for closed APIs like GPT-5 or Claude. The proxy-analyzer framework instead reads already-generated text through a small locally hosted open-weight model and extracts hallucination signals from the *reader's* activations — eighteen features grounded in transformer mechanics: residual-stream norms, per-head source-document attention, entropy, MLP activations, logit-lens trajectories, and three new token-level grounding statistics. A stacking ensemble trained on 72,135 samples across five hallucination datasets transfers across analyzer architectures (Qwen2.5 0.5B/7B, Gemma-2 2B/9B, Pythia 1.4B, LLaMA-3 3B/8B). For 7.4.3, this is the first deployable bridge between mechanistic detection and API-only RAG: the verification model is local and inspectable even when the generator is not, so citation-integrity audits no longer have to choose between activation-grounded detection (open-weight only) and surface-text detection (works everywhere but weaker signal).

**Sentence-level medical RAG verification with GraphRAG knowledge graphs (CuraView, May 2026).** [CuraView](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03476) (May 6, 2026) targets a domain where pragmatic misleadingness has direct patient-safety consequences: discharge summary generation from electronic health records. The framework constructs patient-level knowledge graphs from EHRs rather than flat-document retrieval — necessary because clinical verification requires recovering relational structure across diagnoses, medications, lab results, and temporal records that flat retrieval systematically loses. CuraView defines seven hallucination types (diagnosis errors, medication errors, fabricated facts, temporal inconsistencies, omitted findings, contradictions, and unsupported inferences) and classifies each generated sentence into one of four evidence grades (E1 strong support, E2 partial support, E3 weak/insufficient support, E4 direct contradiction) using a closed-loop generation-detection pipeline. Reported evaluation covers 1,103 test sentences derived from real EHRs. Relevant to 7.4.2 (per-claim attribution must reach sentence granularity in safety-critical domains) and 7.4.3 (the four-grade scale gives auditors a more actionable signal than binary supported/unsupported).

**Sparse-autoencoder feature attribution for faithful RAG (RAGLens, December 2025).** [RAGLens](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.08892) applies sparse autoencoders to disentangle LLM internal activations and identify features that fire specifically during RAG hallucinations. Reported AUC exceeds 80% on standard RAG benchmarks while producing interpretable, token-level explanations of *why* a span was flagged unfaithful — a form of mechanistic attribution that complements MIRAGE saliency and Jacobian Scope gradients. By enforcing sparsity, the SAE features approximate monosemanticity, giving auditors a transparent link between an unfaithful citation and the internal model behavior that produced it. Like FACTUM and ProbeRAG, RAGLens still requires activation access and so applies to open-weight RAG deployments; for closed-API generators, the proxy-analyzer pattern above is the deployable counterpart.

**Multi-agent citation hallucination detection with structured taxonomy (CiteTracer, May 2026).** [CiteTracer](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.08583) (May 9, 2026) replaces the usual binary "real vs. fabricated" framing with a 12-code taxonomy spanning Real, Potential, and Hallucinated citations, then routes each cited reference through a cascading multi-agent pipeline: extraction from PDFs and BibTeX, evidence retrieval via cache lookup, URL fetch, scholar connectors, and web search, deterministic field matching, and class-specialist judgers for ambiguous cases. On a benchmark of 2,450 synthetic citations (controlled LLM mutations from real seeds) the system reaches 97.1% accuracy with class-level F1 of 97.0/95.8/98.5 for Real/Potential/Hallucinated; on 957 real-world fabricated citations harvested from ICLR 2026 and desk-rejected submissions it identifies 97.1% without abstaining. Released under CC BY 4.0 with public code, this is the first production-ready open framework that treats citation validation as a multi-class detection problem rather than a confidence threshold, useful for 7.4.1/7.4.3 audits where partial-evidence citations (real venue, wrong year; real author, wrong paper) need a different remediation than outright fabrications.

**Benchmark artifacts inflate reported detector progress (PARALLAX, May 2026).** [PARALLAX](https://arxiv.org/html/2605.17028) (Virginia Tech, May 16, 2026) is a structural critique of the hallucination-detection benchmark stack: four of six widely-used evaluation corpora embed correct answers directly in the prompts, allowing surface text matching to score AUROC up to 0.98 without examining model state at all. The authors' TxTemb baseline — a TF-IDF cosine-similarity classifier with no model internals — achieves AUROC 0.98 on HaluEval; methods like CAA achieve near-perfect scores on teacher-forced corpora but collapse to near-chance on live-generation data. On the hardest live-generation corpus (RAGTruth), all 22 evaluated methods cluster between AUROC 0.43–0.57 with no method exceeding chance under statistical testing. The benchmark-construction artifact inflates AUROC by up to 0.43 points. Practical implications for 7.4.3 verification programs: published detector accuracy numbers should be re-read with a TxTemb-style surface-text baseline as a negative control, with bootstrap confidence intervals, permutation tests, and cross-architecture validation before claiming a deployment-ready detector. The mismatch is large enough that "we use a published 90%-accurate detector" is no longer a sufficient audit claim without artifact-controlled re-evaluation.

**LLM-first human-adjudicated re-annotation reveals benchmark undercount (May 2026).** [Atasoy et al.](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.08462) (May 8, 2026) re-evaluated QAGS-C and SummEval hallucination annotations using an LLM-first adjudication pipeline: compare original annotations against Gemini 2.5 Flash and GPT-5 Mini predictions, then have two cross-cultural human adjudicators re-score conflicting samples. Triple agreement increased by 6.38% on QAGS-C and 7.62% on SummEval after re-adjudication, and reported model accuracy improved 4.25-2.34% (GPT) and 8.51-3.80% (Gemini). Adjudicators frequently sided with the models' judgments over original human annotations when models provided explicit reasoning. This corroborates the RT4CHART finding that benchmarks systematically undercount hallucinations: 7.4.3 verification programs that rely on single-pass human annotation are likely overestimating detector performance and underestimating residual unsupported-claim rates.

**Sufficiency- and uncertainty-aware selective answering (SURE-RAG, May 2026).** [SURE-RAG](https://arxiv.org/html/2605.03534v1) (May 5, 2026) operationalizes the "abstain rather than hallucinate" pattern as a three-stage verification: (1) a DeBERTa cross-encoder scores each (claim, passage) pair into support/refute/neutral distributions; (2) set-level aggregation produces interpretable signals — coverage, relation strength, disagreement, conflict, retrieval uncertainty — that capture sufficiency as a property of the evidence set rather than any single passage; (3) selective answering emits one of Supported / Refuted / Insufficient and abstains unless support is established. Reported macro-F1 on HotpotQA-RAG v3 is 0.9075 (calibrated), with a 37% reduction in unsafe answers at 30% coverage compared to baseline pooling, while matching the accuracy of an opaque concat cross-encoder (0.8888) at a level that remains auditable. Directly relevant to 7.4.3 (detect and flag) and 7.4.3 (block or redact): the set-level sufficiency signal addresses the failure mode where every individual passage looks weakly supportive but no combination establishes the claim, which simple per-claim NLI checks systematically miss.

**Retrieval-stage poisoning defenses (RAGPart and RAGMask, December 2025).** [RAGPart and RAGMask](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2512.24268) introduce a retrieval-side defense layer that complements generation-side citation verification. RAGPart exploits dense-retriever training dynamics through document partitioning to dilute the influence of poisoned points; RAGMask identifies suspicious tokens by measuring similarity shifts under targeted token masking. Reported pipeline performance defends against PoisonedRAG-class attacks 96% of the time with GPT-4o and 78% with GPT-3.5-Turbo. For citation integrity, these defenses operate before the citation chain begins, addressing the gap that single-document poisoning (CorruptRAG) opened: a metadata-derived citation pointing to a passage that survives RAGPart/RAGMask is materially more trustworthy than one drawn from an unfiltered retrieval set.

**Hierarchical claim-level verification (RT4CHART, March 2026).** The [RT4CHART](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.27752) framework introduces retromorphic testing with hierarchical verification for hallucination detection in RAG systems. Rather than providing coarse answer-level scores, RT4CHART decomposes model outputs into independently verifiable claims and performs local-to-global verification against retrieved context, assigning each claim one of three labels: entailed, contradicted, or baseless. On the RAGTruth++ benchmark it achieves F1 of 0.776, outperforming the strongest baseline by 83%. Notably, the authors' re-annotation effort revealed 1.68x more hallucination cases than the original labels — suggesting that existing benchmarks substantially underestimate actual hallucination prevalence. This has direct implications for 7.4.3: claim-level entailment verification may be systematically underreporting failures when evaluated against benchmarks that themselves undercount hallucinations.

**Intervention-based evidence utility measurement (CUE-R, April 2026).** The [CUE-R](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.05467) framework moves beyond traditional answer-quality and citation-faithfulness metrics to measure the operational utility of individual retrieved evidence items during single-shot RAG inference. Using three perturbation operators — REMOVE, REPLACE, and DUPLICATE — CUE-R quantifies how each evidence item affects correctness, grounding faithfulness, and confidence. Testing on HotpotQA and 2WikiMultihopQA with Qwen-3 8B and GPT-5.2 revealed that multi-hop evidence can interact non-additively: removing both supporting items causes disproportionate performance harm beyond the sum of individual removals. This complements citation-level verification (7.4.3) by revealing evidence dependencies that traditional faithfulness metrics miss — a response may appear grounded when all evidence is present but be fragile to individual evidence removal.

**Reinforcement-learned evidence gating (GRACE, January 2026).** The [GRACE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.04525) framework addresses two failure modes simultaneously: systems that generate correct answers without explicit grounded evidence, and systems that fabricate responses when retrieved context is insufficient. Using a multi-stage gated reward function, GRACE trains models to assess evidence adequacy, extract supporting evidence, and either provide grounded answers or explicitly abstain — all in a unified RL framework. Notably, GRACE requires only 10% of the annotation costs of prior methods while achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on grounding benchmarks, making evidence-gated abstention practical for production RAG systems.

**Standardized evaluation at scale (TREC 2025 RAG Track).** The [TREC 2025 RAG Track](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.09891) — with 150+ submissions from 16 groups — represents the first large-scale standardized evaluation of RAG systems that includes explicit attribution verification. The track's support evaluation checks each sentence in a generated answer against its cited document using three-level assessment (Full Support, Partial Support, No Support) with weighted precision and recall metrics. Top-performing systems achieved 0.85+ weighted precision on manual assessment, but median performance was only 0.30-0.40, confirming that citation grounding remains difficult for most systems. Automated evaluation using GPT-OSS 120B showed strong run-level correlation with human judges, suggesting automated citation verification is becoming viable for continuous monitoring.

**Industry standards for citation traceability (FINOS, 2026).** The [FINOS AI Governance Framework](https://air-governance-framework.finos.org/mitigations/mi-13_providing-citations-and-source-traceability-for-ai-generated-information.html), developed by the financial services open-source community, now includes a dedicated control (MI-13) for citation and source traceability in AI-generated information. The framework requires that citations point to specific document sections (paragraph or page level, not just document level), that RAG pipelines maintain auditable chunk-to-output links, and that provenance metadata propagate through the entire retrieval-generation pipeline. This is notable as one of the first sector-specific governance frameworks to codify citation integrity requirements — relevant to financial institutions implementing 7.4.1 and 7.4.2 compliance.

**Citation laundering and evidence-force calibration (FORCEBENCH, May 2026).** [FORCEBENCH](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28044) (May 27, 2026) names a failure mode that sits between fabrication and mis-attribution: *citation laundering*, where a topically relevant source is presented as warrant for a claim stronger than the source actually supports. The benchmark contains 198 minimally contrasting pairs that inflate claim strength along five dimensions — relation, modality, scope, temporal validity, and numeric specificity. Token- and entity-overlap verifiers violate monotonicity (scoring the inflated claim at least as supported as the faithful one) on 32.8–36.4% of pairs, and standard LLM-judge prompting violates it on 47.2% of pairs; explicit warrant-strength prompting cuts that to 24.5%. For 7.4.2 and 7.4.3 this is a sharper version of the pragmatic-misleadingness problem: a verifier that only checks topical relevance will systematically pass over-strong claims, so audit harnesses should include strength-inflated contrast pairs, not just unsupported-claim cases.

**Citation grounding via citation graphs and CG-DPO (May-June 2026).** [Detecting and Reducing LLM Citation Hallucinations via Legal Citation Graphs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00898) (May 30, 2026) builds a ground-truth citation graph from 100.8M Ukrainian court decisions (502M edges, 21,736 statute nodes) and decomposes citation grounding into three independently checkable properties: existence, contextual relevance, and temporal validity. Measured against this oracle, five production-grade legal AI systems hallucinated 13–21% of generated citations. The defensive contribution is twofold: a fine-tuned Qwen2.5-7B verifier reaches 98.5% mean validation accuracy distinguishing correct from corrupted citations, and CG-DPO constructs alignment preference pairs by corrupting verified citations (article swap, law swap, hallucination injection, anachronism) to reduce hallucination at the source. This extends the alignment-time toolbox (Trust-Align, Faithfulness-QA, CTRL-RAG) with a domain where the ground-truth reference graph already exists — a pattern transferable to any corpus with an authoritative citation registry (statutes, standards, pharmacopoeias).

**Serving-time grounded decoding (June 2026).** [Grounded Decoding via Retrieval-Anchored Probability Fusion](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00432) (May 29, 2026) is a training-free decoding framework that computes context-conditioned and evidence-only token distributions at each generation step and fuses them through a KL-barycenter objective, with adaptive weighting driven by distributional disagreement and retriever confidence. Reported gains span ALCE, Natural Questions, and FActScore. Like Self-Correcting RAG's NLI-guided MCTS, it moves enforcement from post-hoc gating into the decoding loop itself — but without fine-tuning or search overhead, making it a lighter-weight option for 7.4.3 implementations on open-weight deployments.

**Coverage-aware faithfulness evaluation (June 2026).** [Precision Is Not Faithfulness](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.09376) (June 8, 2026) demonstrates that precision-only, reference-free faithfulness metrics — the dominant family used in production grounding gates — systematically reward under-generation: a model can score perfectly by saying very little. Using complete-oracle domains (Formula 1 telemetry, NOAA forecasts) where every decision-relevant fact is known, the authors score 7,253 decision instances in three languages with both precision and recall; the most precise frontier model ranked last on F1, and high-precision models covered under half the relevant facts. For 7.4.3 audits this is a calibration warning: a grounding gate tuned only on faithfulness precision can pass trivially terse, low-utility responses while appearing to perform well.

**Fluency-preserving corpus poisoning (SilentRetrieval, May 2026).** [SilentRetrieval](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28074) (May 27, 2026) escalates the single-document poisoning threat by defeating the perplexity-based filters that catch earlier attacks. Its two stages — Coordinated Beam Search (multi-token joint optimization with a fluency-similarity objective) and Context-Adaptive Trigger Generation (a frozen LLM fuses manipulation triggers into natural-reading text) — achieve 84.6%/81.3% HR@10 and 57.5%/54.8% attack success on Natural Questions and MS MARCO, retain a 74.2% hit rate at a 0.016% poisoning ratio at Wikipedia scale, and transfer to unseen retrievers at 64.7% average effectiveness. Human evaluators flag the poisoned documents far less often than disfluent baselines. Companion evaluation work on [RAG reliability under clean, misleading, and mixed retrieval](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07783) (June 5, 2026) measures "parametric override" — the point at which retrieved misinformation overrides correct parametric knowledge as poison ratios vary. Together these reinforce the CorruptRAG lesson for 7.4.2/7.4.3: a metadata-derived citation to a fluent, retrievable, poisoned chunk is indistinguishable from a legitimate one at the attribution layer, so corpus-side defenses and provenance checks must run upstream of citation verification.

**Claim-level legal RAG benchmarking (ClaimRAG-LAW, May 2026).** [ClaimRAG-LAW](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.21071) (May 20, 2026) is a bilingual French/English legal benchmark that scores retrieval and generation separately at the atomic-claim level via entailment — claim precision, claim recall, and the proportion of ground-truth claims entailed by retrieved chunks. By separating "the right evidence was never retrieved" from "the evidence was retrieved but the claim isn't supported," it gives 7.4.2/7.4.3 auditors a way to localize where grounding failures originate, complementing MEMERAG's multilingual coverage with a high-stakes vertical domain.

**Element-level multimodal attribution (GranuRAG, May 2026).** [GranuVistaVQA / GranuRAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15019) (May 14, 2026) extends fine-grained attribution to multimodal RAG: citations point to specific image *elements* rather than whole images, via element detection, multi-granularity cross-modal alignment, and attribution-constrained generation, reporting up to 29.2% improvement over six baselines. This is the multimodal counterpart of per-claim chunk traceability (7.4.2) — whole-image citations have the same granularity problem as per-paragraph text citations, and the MM-MEPA/MM-PoisonRAG attack results make element-level provenance an audit requirement rather than a UX nicety.

**Vendor citation-metadata APIs widen (May-June 2026).** Google's [Gemini API File Search](https://ai.google.dev/gemini-api/docs/changelog) (May 5, 2026 changelog) now embeds and searches images natively and returns grounding metadata that includes `media_id` for visual citations and `page_numbers` locating evidence in source documents — direct metadata-passthrough primitives for 7.4.1/7.4.2/7.4.3 implementations. Microsoft's Web IQ grounding layer, announced at Build 2026 (June 2, 2026, [coverage](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317698/20260603/azure-horizondb-enters-public-preview-web-iq-already-powers-copilot-chatgpt.htm)), exposes a model-agnostic JSON-RPC grounding API returning passage-level structured evidence objects rather than documents, with vendor-claimed sub-165ms P95 latency; it already powers grounding in Microsoft Copilot and ChatGPT. The trend across Anthropic Citations, OpenAI File Search, Vertex Check Grounding, Gemini File Search, and Web IQ is consistent: structured, retrieval-derived evidence objects are becoming the default interface, leaving prompt-based "cite your sources" with no remaining architectural excuse.

**MITRE ATLAS accelerates past v5.4.0 (March-May 2026).** ATLAS shipped [three further releases](https://github.com/mitre-atlas/atlas-data/releases): v5.5.0 (March 31, 2026), v5.6.0 (May 4, 2026 — three new techniques including AI Agent Configuration plus updated deepfake-detection mitigations), and v2026.05 (May 27, 2026 — a structural overhaul separating content releases from format versions, ATLAS YAML v6.0.0 with first-class relationship modeling, and a "platforms" field tagging techniques across Predictive/Generative/Agentic AI). | **7.4.1** | **Verify that** responses generated using retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) include attribution to the source documents. | 1 | Unattributed generation and overreliance (OWASP LLM09:2025) — users cannot check claims when responses carry no source pointers, and models invent references when left to "cite your sources" (CJR Tow Center, March 2025: 37–94% citation error across eight production AI search engines; Grok-3 cited fabricated/broken URLs in 154 of 200 tests). | Inspect responses for citation blocks tied to retrieved documents; run representative queries and confirm each surfaces source IDs/links. Review vendor primitives — Anthropic Citations API, OpenAI File Search annotations, Google Vertex AI Check Grounding, Gemini File Search `media_id`/`page_numbers` — and confirm citations actually render to the user. For streaming responses, confirm attribution is present from the first token rather than appended post-generation. | Architecturally straightforward with vendor citation APIs, but enforcement is the gap: many systems still rely on prompt-based citation, which fabricates. Citation *presence* has near-zero correlation with factual accuracy, so attribution is necessary but not sufficient — pair with 7.4.2 (genuine provenance) and 7.4.3 (claim support). |
| **7.4.2** | **Verify that** RAG attributions are derived from retrieval metadata and are not generated by the model, so provenance cannot be fabricated. | 1 | Citation fabrication and provenance spoofing — model-emitted citation strings can be invented or point to non-supporting sources; metadata-only poisoning (MM-MEPA, Feb 2026, up to 91% attack success) when metadata fields are attacker-controlled or unvalidated. | Trace the citation data path: confirm document/chunk IDs, page numbers, and URLs originate from the retriever's response object and are attached in the application layer, not produced as generated tokens. Check for retrieval-generation isolation (immutable evidence records; generator cannot mutate the metadata store). Test that the model cannot override metadata-derived citations with its own. Validate metadata provenance and parser trust boundaries for multimodal/OCR sources. | Metadata-passthrough is well understood, but no cross-vendor citation-metadata schema exists and chunk IDs can silently break across re-embeddings (index versioning is underappreciated). Single-document poisoning (CorruptRAG, SilentRetrieval) means a perfectly metadata-derived citation to a poisoned chunk still yields attacker-controlled output — combine with corpus-side defenses (RAGPart/RAGMask) and source authenticity checks. |
| **7.4.3** | **Verify that** claims in a RAG response can be traced to the retrieved chunk. | 2 | Pragmatic misleadingness and unsupported claims — "Correctness is not Faithfulness" (Dec 2024): 57% of RAG citations post-rationalized from parametric memory; citation laundering, where a topically relevant source is presented as warrant for a stronger claim than it supports (FORCEBENCH, May 2026); multi-source "hallucinated bridges" between individually correct passages. | Decompose responses into atomic claims and run NLI entailment against the *cited* chunk, not just any retrieved chunk. Use token-level detectors (LettuceDetect, F1 79.22% on RAGTruth; HaluGate, 76–162ms; Vectara HHEM-2.3; Patronus Lynx, 87.4% on HaluBench) or chunk-attribution scorers (Galileo Luna, ~86%); for closed-API generators use proxy-analyzer activations. Benchmarks undercount hallucinations (RT4CHART found 1.68× more than original labels); best GaRAGe F1 only 58.9%. ## Implementation Maturity

| Requirement | Maturity | Notes |
|-------------|:---:|-------|
| 7.4.1 — Attribution present on RAG responses | Maturing | Architecturally straightforward: attach document IDs from vector search results rather than letting the model generate citations. Function calling and structured outputs make this implementable. The challenge is ensuring the model doesn't "override" metadata-based citations with its own generated ones. No standard protocol exists — each RAG framework implements differently. |
| 7.4.3 — Claim-level entailment verification | Emerging | Best tools: [Vectara HHEM-2.1](https://huggingface.co/vectara/hallucination_evaluation_model) (neural classifier, 30%+ better than GPT-4 at detection, ~0.6s on RTX 3090), [RAGAS faithfulness](https://docs.ragas.io/) (LLM-based claim extraction), [DeepEval](https://github.com/confident-ai/deepeval) (14+ metrics, CI/CD integration), [Google Vertex AI Check Grounding](https://cloud.google.com/generative-ai-app-builder/docs/check-grounding) (API, <500ms, 0-1 support score). As of March 2026, [Fast and Faithful](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.23508) demonstrates that extending verification context to 32K tokens yields 817% better hallucination recall than truncated 8K approaches — a strong argument that chunk-level verification is systematically insufficient. [FaithJudge](https://github.com/vectara/FaithJudge) (Vectara, EMNLP 2025) provides an LLM-as-judge framework reaching 84% balanced accuracy on FaithBench. [VeriCite](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.11394) (SIGIR-AP 2025) offers a three-stage pipeline combining NLI verification with evidence selection and answer refinement, demonstrating that multi-stage approaches can improve citation quality without sacrificing answer correctness. [Trust-Score](https://github.com/declare-lab/trust-align) (ICLR 2025 Oral) provides a holistic evaluation framework covering correctness, attribution, and refusal — useful for benchmarking end-to-end citation trustworthiness rather than individual components. As of April 2026, [RT4CHART](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.27752) achieves F1 of 0.776 on RAGTruth++ via hierarchical claim-level verification — a significant improvement, though re-annotation revealed existing benchmarks undercount hallucinations by 1.68x. GaRAGe benchmark (ACL 2025) still shows best F1 at 58.9% on its own labels, and research confirms citation presence has near-zero correlation with factual accuracy. Level 3 because real-time claim-level verification at scale remains a hard problem, compounded by the pragmatic misleadingness problem where correctly-cited sources can still support misleading claims. |
| 7.4.2 — Metadata-derived attribution (not model-generated) | Maturing | The core architectural pattern — passing retrieval metadata (document IDs, chunk IDs, URLs) through the application layer rather than letting the model generate citation text — is well understood and implementable with current tooling. Function calling APIs and structured output schemas naturally enforce this separation. [Galileo Luna](https://galileo.ai/) provides automated chunk attribution scoring at ~86% accuracy with ~152ms latency. The [NVIDIA NIM Citation Validation tool](https://developer.nvidia.com/blog/developing-an-ai-powered-tool-for-automatic-citation-validation-using-nvidia-nim/) (2025) automates post-hoc verification. However, many production systems still use prompt-based citation ("cite your sources in your response"), which defeats this control. No cross-vendor standard exists for citation metadata format — each RAG framework implements its own schema. Index versioning (maintaining stable chunk IDs across re-embeddings) remains underappreciated; citation anchors can silently break when chunk boundaries shift during re-indexing. |
| 7.4.3 — Detect and flag unsupported claims pre-serve | Emerging | The detection layer has matured faster than the enforcement layer. Encoder detectors ([LettuceDetect](https://huggingface.co/blog/adaamko/lettucedetect), HaluGate ModernBERT classifier) deliver token-level unsupported-span detection in 12-160ms with F1 0.79+ on RAGTruth, while LLM-judge detectors ([Patronus Lynx 8B/70B](https://www.patronus.ai/blog/lynx-state-of-the-art-open-source-hallucination-detection-model), [Vectara HHEM-2.3](https://www.vectara.com/blog/introducing-the-next-generation-of-vectaras-hallucination-leaderboard)) push accuracy to 84-87% on HaluBench at higher latency and cost. Production faithfulness gates increasingly use these as inline filters with thresholds in the 0.75-0.85 range. The remaining gap is operational: ensuring flagging happens before commit (not during logging), handling streaming tokens, and addressing the 1.68x undercount problem RT4CHART revealed in existing benchmarks. The May 2026 [PARALLAX](https://arxiv.org/html/2605.17028) analysis adds a second concern: four of six widely-used hallucination-detection corpora embed answers in prompts, inflating reported AUROC by up to 0.43 points; a TF-IDF baseline reaches AUROC 0.98 on HaluEval without examining model state. On the hardest live-generation corpus (RAGTruth), all 22 evaluated methods cluster at 0.43-0.57 AUROC. Auditors should treat single-benchmark claims with skepticism and require artifact-controlled re-evaluation. [SURE-RAG](https://arxiv.org/html/2605.03534v1) (May 2026) demonstrates a complementary set-level approach — sufficiency aggregated across multiple passages with an explicit Insufficient label — that the encoder/LLM-judge family does not address natively. Multilingual coverage is improving but uneven: [MEMERAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.17163) and Turk-LettuceDetect demonstrate that detection generalizes outside English when trained on localized data, but most off-the-shelf detectors are still English-first. |
| 7.4.3 — Serving-time blocking or redaction | Emerging | The enforcement layer is becoming practical but is uneven across stacks. Amazon Bedrock Guardrails exposes grounding and relevance thresholds with a `BLOCK` action; Google Vertex AI Check Grounding returns support scores and cited chunks that can be used for inference-time filtering; Azure AI Content Safety adds groundedness correction for some cases; NeMo Guardrails and Guardrails AI provide refusal, warning, fix, filter, and refrain paths. The hard part is operational calibration: choosing thresholds that fit domain risk, handling partial support, deciding when redaction is safe, and preventing streamed tokens from reaching the user before verification completes. |
| 7.3.4 — Watermarking AI-generated media | Emerging | Marking is converging on a two-layer model: C2PA Content Credentials (cryptographically signed, tamper-evident manifests) embedded in file metadata, plus an imperceptible watermark resistant to common manipulation. Adobe Firefly, OpenAI DALL·E 3/Sora, and Google Imagen embed C2PA manifests today; SynthID covers Google text/image/audio/video. The unresolved problem is robustness — metadata is trivially stripped, and image/audio watermarks survive light edits but not adversarial removal; text watermarking remains the least mature and most contested. The EU AI Act Code of Practice on transparency of AI-generated content (published June 2026) prescribes secured metadata plus watermarking and points to open standards (C2PA) and a possible EU label icon; Article 50 marking obligations apply from 2 August 2026. |

## Regulatory & Standards Landscape

Citation integrity intersects with several emerging regulatory frameworks that mandate provenance and transparency controls:

| Framework | Relevant Requirements | Status |
|-----------|----------------------|--------|
| **EU AI Act Article 50** | Transparency obligations for generative AI: outputs must be marked in machine-readable format, detectable as AI-generated. Multi-layered approach prescribed: C2PA metadata embedding, imperceptible watermarking, and logging systems. First draft of the [Code of Practice on Transparency](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/news/commission-publishes-first-draft-code-practice-marking-and-labelling-ai-generated-content) (December 17, 2025) split requirements into provider-side marking and deployer-side labelling of deepfakes/public-interest AI text. The [second draft](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/commission-publishes-second-draft-code-practice-marking-and-labelling-ai-generated-content) (5 March 2026) is streamlined and simplified: removes the AI-generated/AI-assisted taxonomy in Section 2; introduces revised two-layered marking (secured metadata + watermarking) for providers; adds optional fingerprinting and logging; promotes open standards including a potential EU icon; makes elements going beyond the AI Act voluntary; preserves special provisions for artistic/creative/satirical works. The convergence of these frameworks creates a clear compliance imperative: systems that generate cited content must be able to demonstrate provenance from source document to user-facing citation, with audit trails suitable for regulatory review.

## Implementation Architecture Patterns

Several distinct architectural approaches have emerged for implementing citation integrity, each with different tradeoff profiles:

### Pattern 1: Metadata-Passthrough (Level 1, satisfies 7.4.1)

The simplest approach separates citation generation from content generation entirely. The retrieval system returns structured metadata (document IDs, chunk IDs, page ranges, URLs) alongside retrieved content. The application layer attaches this metadata to the response template — the model never generates citation text. This eliminates citation fabrication by design but provides no claim-level verification.

**Tools:** Any vector database (Pinecone, Qdrant, Weaviate, PgVector) with metadata storage. Function calling and structured output APIs naturally enforce this pattern. Hosted retrieval APIs can help when their citation annotations are logged as evidence: OpenAI File Search exposes file citation annotations and optional retrieved search results, while [LangChain's citation content block](https://reference.langchain.com/javascript/langchain/index/ContentBlock/Citation) carries cited text, response span offsets, source title, and URL.

### Pattern 2: Source-First with Hard Constraints (Level 1-2, strengthens 7.4.1)

As demonstrated by the citation-enforced RAG framework for fiscal documents (March 2026), this approach treats citation compliance as a hard constraint during generation. Only content directly extracted from original documents enters the retrieval corpus (no LLM-generated summaries). Each generated paragraph must include standardized citations (document-page-chunk format). Outputs failing validation are regenerated; persistent failures trigger abstention. Achieves 94.5% citation support and 1.8% hallucination in evaluation.

**Key design choice:** Source-first ingestion eliminates a class of provenance errors that arise when embeddings are generated from LLM summaries rather than original text.

### Pattern 3: Post-Generation NLI Verification (Level 3, satisfies 7.4.3)

Decompose the generated response into atomic claims, map each claim to its cited chunk, and run natural language inference to verify entailment. This is the most thorough approach but also the most computationally expensive.

**Tools:** Vectara HHEM-2.1 (~0.6s per check), cross-encoder/nli-deberta-v3-base (CPU-capable), RAGAS faithfulness metric (LLM-based), Google Vertex AI Check Grounding API (<500ms). HaluGate (vLLM) achieves 76-162ms for token-level detection via Rust-native inference, representing the current speed frontier.

### Pattern 4: Spatial Citation with Audit Trail (Level 1-2, strengthens 7.4.1)

For document-heavy domains (legal, financial, regulatory), preserve bounding box coordinates and page numbers during document parsing. Embed lightweight spatial anchors into chunks at indexing time. At serving time, resolve citation IDs to clickable deep links or highlighted PDF regions. Storage overhead ~10-15%. Enables both human verification (click-through to source) and automated audit trails.

**Tools:** Tensorlake Document AI (fragment-level parsing with bounding boxes), any OCR pipeline that preserves spatial metadata.

### Pattern 5: Serving-Time Grounding Gate (Level 3, satisfies 7.4.3)

Treat groundedness verification as a release gate on the response, not as an observability metric. The generated answer is decomposed into claims or sentences, checked against retrieved facts, and either released, regenerated, redacted, or replaced with an abstention before the user sees it. This pattern should record the detector version, threshold, source chunks, support score, action taken, and final response ID so later audits can distinguish "detected and blocked" from "detected but served."

**Tools:** Amazon Bedrock Guardrails contextual grounding, Google Vertex AI Check Grounding, Azure AI Content Safety groundedness detection, NeMo Guardrails fact-checking rails, Guardrails AI on-fail actions, and calibrated research systems such as HALT-RAG or GRACE.

### Pattern 6: Alignment-Time + Runtime Stack (Level 2-3, strengthens 7.4.3)

Combine model-level training that rewards context-following ([Trust-Align](https://github.com/declare-lab/trust-align) DPO, [Faithfulness-QA](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25313) counterfactual entity substitution, [CTRL-RAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.04406) Contrastive Likelihood Reward) with a runtime entailment gate ([HaluGate](https://vllm.ai/blog/halugate), [LettuceDetect](https://huggingface.co/blog/adaamko/lettucedetect), Vectara HHEM-2.3, Patronus Lynx) and a serving-time enforcement action. The argument for this pattern is that runtime detection is fundamentally a goal-line defense — a model that has been trained to post-rationalize will keep finding ways to produce confidently-cited-but-unfaithful answers that pass NLI checks. Alignment-time interventions reduce the rate at which the detector sees unfaithful candidates at all. The 2026 evidence base (Trust-Align 26-32% citation groundedness improvement, Faithfulness-QA's 99K counterfactual training pairs, CTRL-RAG's reward formulation) makes this a deployable rather than research-only pattern.

**Tools:** Trust-Align training scripts and datasets; Faithfulness-QA dataset; CTRL-RAG reference implementation; any production hallucination detector (LettuceDetect, HaluGate, Lynx, HHEM-2.3); guardrail engines that translate detector output into block/redact/abstain actions.

### Pattern 7: Test-Time Faithfulness Search (Level 3, strengthens 7.4.3)

Run NLI verification not as a post-generation gate but as a search constraint *during* generation. Self-Correcting RAG implements this with NLI-guided Monte Carlo Tree Search: candidate token sequences are scored against retrieved evidence and the search refuses to extend branches that fail entailment, so unfaithful answers are never produced rather than being detected and discarded after the fact. Inference cost is higher, but the architecture closes the gap between "we detected unfaithfulness" and "we prevented unfaithful tokens from being emitted."

**Tools:** [Self-Correcting RAG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.10734) reference implementation, NLI cross-encoders for branch scoring, MCTS or beam-search infrastructure with a faithfulness reward.

### Pattern 8: Multimodal Provenance Split-Validation (Level 2-3, strengthens 7.4.1 and 7.4.2)

For multimodal RAG, treat the visible object, extracted text, generated caption, filename, alt text, EXIF/XMP metadata, OCR output, vector metadata, and chunk record as separate evidence channels. A citation is only acceptable when those channels can be traced to the same source object and do not contradict one another. This pattern is a direct response to MM-MEPA and MM-PoisonRAG: an image can look benign while metadata steers retrieval, and a single adversarial multimodal knowledge item can corrupt downstream reasoning while still appearing as a legitimate cited source.

Minimum audit checks:
- Verify parser provenance: which parser or OCR component produced each text field, with version and timestamp.
- Compare visible content against extracted captions, alt text, OCR text, and stored vector metadata.
- Require stable source-object IDs that survive re-indexing, deduplication, thumbnail generation, and document conversion.
- Flag citations where the retrieved chunk is a derived caption or graph node unless it also links back to original source spans or image regions.
- Run poisoning tests that insert adversarial image-text pairs, metadata-only edits, and single high-ranking multimodal items.

**Tools:** Document parsers that preserve spatial metadata, C2PA Content Credentials for signed source objects, vector stores with immutable metadata history, and multimodal RAG tests modeled on MM-MEPA and MM-PoisonRAG.

## Open Research Questions

- How should attribution be presented for responses that synthesize information across multiple retrieved chunks? Should every sentence be individually attributed? MEGA-RAG's multi-evidence approach suggests cross-checking multiple sources is feasible, but the UX for multi-source attribution remains an open design question.
- What entailment threshold is appropriate for flagging unsupported claims -- strict logical entailment, or a softer "consistent with" standard? NLI models and RAGAS faithfulness metrics use different thresholds, and the optimal choice likely varies by domain risk level.
- How can claim-level verification be made fast enough for real-time serving without unacceptable latency? FACTUM's mechanistic approach (examining model internals during generation) may be faster than post-hoc NLI checking, but requires access to model activations that may not be available with API-based LLMs.
- Given FACTUM's finding that citation correctness signatures are scale-dependent (3B vs. 8B models behave differently), how should verification strategies adapt across different model sizes and architectures? Is a universal detection approach feasible, or must verification be calibrated per model?
- How should systems handle partial support -- where a retrieved chunk supports part of a claim but not all of it? The hybrid retrieval approach of requiring overlap between cited line ranges and retrieved chunks (interval arithmetic verification) offers one concrete mechanism, but generalization beyond code to natural language remains open.
- How do retrieval-failure vs. generation-deficiency hallucination modes (as taxonomized in 2025 review literature) map to different verification strategies? Should the system detect which failure mode is occurring and apply targeted mitigation?
- Should users be able to click through citations to view the original source chunk, and what are the security implications of exposing retrieval internals?
- As agentic RAG systems emerge (where the model can iteratively retrieve, verify, and refine), how should citation integrity controls adapt to multi-step retrieval workflows where the final response synthesizes across multiple retrieval rounds?
- With EU AI Act Article 50 transparency obligations taking effect in August 2026, how should citation metadata interoperate with C2PA provenance standards? Should RAG citation metadata be embedded in the same provenance chain as content watermarking?
- HaluGate demonstrates production-viable token-level detection at 76-162ms, but it only addresses extrinsic hallucinations (where grounding context exists). How should systems handle the complementary case of intrinsic hallucinations in responses that blend retrieved and parametric knowledge?
- The citation-enforced RAG approach (March 2026) achieves 94.5% citation support through hard constraints and abstention. What is the practical tradeoff between citation strictness and system utility — how often does forced abstention prevent useful responses in different domains?
- Legal sanctions for citation hallucination have escalated from $812 (Air Canada, 2024) to $59,500 (Illinois, 2025) to $30,000+ in a single Sixth Circuit ruling (March 2026). Courts are now establishing per-citation penalty formulas ($500/fake citation + $1,000/fake quotation). At what point does citation verification shift from a quality concern to a liability-driven compliance requirement, and how should this affect the Level assignment of these controls?
- Research shows citation presence has "near-zero correlation with factual accuracy" and that RAG systems can be "pragmatically misleading" even with correct citations — decontextualizing facts, omitting critical caveats, and inserting "hallucinated bridges" between individually correct sources. How should entailment verification frameworks account for these subtler forms of misleadingness that go beyond checking whether a source supports a specific atomic claim?
- Fast and Faithful (March 2026) demonstrates that truncating verification context to 8K tokens causes 95% evidence loss on typical documents, with 817% worse hallucination recall. Should 7.4.3 verification requirements specify minimum context windows, or is this an implementation detail best left to adopters?
- GroundedKG-RAG and Hyper-RAG show that graph and hypergraph retrieval can improve evidence selection and preserve richer relationships than flat chunk retrieval. As these architectures proliferate, should 7.4.2's metadata-derived attribution requirement explicitly address whether graph nodes, hyperedges, and generated summaries are themselves traceable to original source spans?
- MM-MEPA and LogicPoison show that citation integrity can fail below the visible text layer: metadata-only poisoning can corrupt multimodal retrieval, while graph-topology poisoning can distort which evidence paths are selected. Should citation audits require independent validation of metadata fields, parser outputs, graph edges, and community summaries before those artifacts are allowed to serve as attribution evidence?
- The TREC 2025 RAG Track found a stark gap between top performers (0.85+ weighted attribution precision) and median systems (0.30-0.40). Given this variance, should 7.4.3 specify minimum attribution precision thresholds, or is the field too immature for prescriptive benchmarks?
- Trust-Score (ICLR 2025) shows that training models with Trust-Align improves citation groundedness by 26-32%, suggesting that alignment-time interventions may be more effective than post-hoc verification for citation quality. Should 7.4.3 consider recommending alignment-based approaches alongside runtime verification, or is model-level training out of scope for a verification standard?
- Streaming token-level citation architectures inject citation metadata pre-generation, ensuring attribution is available from the first token. As streaming responses become the norm in production RAG systems, should 7.4.1 address the specific challenge of citation delivery in streaming contexts — where traditional post-generation citation attachment may not be feasible?
- Opinion-aware RAG research (April 2026) demonstrates that factual-only retrieval creates systematic citation bias — correctly citing consensus sources while suppressing minority viewpoints. Standard entailment verification (7.4.3) checks whether cited sources support claims, but not whether the set of cited sources fairly represents the information landscape. Should citation integrity controls account for representational adequacy alongside factual accuracy, particularly in domains where multiple valid perspectives exist?
- AdversarialCoT shows that poisoning can target the model's reasoning structure rather than just retrieval rank. Should citation-integrity audits include reasoning-trace perturbation tests, attention-isolation checks such as SDAG, and corpus-specific hybrid-retrieval evaluations, or are those controls better handled under broader RAG poisoning requirements?
- Cited but Not Verified found that increasing retrieval depth can reduce citation fact-check accuracy in long-form reports. How should auditors distinguish useful evidence expansion from citation overload that makes source assignment less reliable?
- With Trust-Align, Faithfulness-QA, and CTRL-RAG now demonstrating that alignment-time training can shift the citation-integrity baseline by 26-32%+ on standard benchmarks, what is the right division of labor between alignment-side controls (trained into the model) and runtime controls (NLI gates, guardrails) for 7.4.2 and 7.4.3 audits? Should verification programs distinguish between these layers when documenting which controls are in place?
- Self-Correcting RAG enforces faithfulness during generation via NLI-guided MCTS rather than after the fact. Does this change the operational definition of 7.4.3 — is a system that *prevents* unfaithful tokens from being emitted required to *also* run a post-hoc detector, or is the test-time search itself sufficient evidence of grounding?
- CiteRAG surfaces citation *selection bias* — systems that consistently choose less-relevant but easier-to-justify sources even when stronger sources exist in the corpus. Should 7.4.1 / 7.4.2 verification include diversity and selection-bias metrics alongside fabrication and attribution accuracy, or is selection bias better treated as a separate fairness control?
- PARALLAX (May 2026) shows that four of six standard hallucination-detection benchmarks embed answers in prompts, inflating reported AUROC by up to 0.43 points and letting TF-IDF baselines reach 0.98 AUROC on HaluEval without examining model state. On RAGTruth, all 22 evaluated methods cluster at AUROC 0.43-0.57. Should 7.4.3 audits require artifact-controlled re-evaluation with surface-text baselines as negative controls, and refuse to credit detector accuracy claims that are not validated on live-generation corpora?
- SURE-RAG's set-level sufficiency formulation treats Supported / Refuted / Insufficient as a three-way decision rather than a per-claim NLI gate, capturing the case where individual passages each look weakly supportive but no combination establishes the claim. Should 7.4.3 verification explicitly require set-level sufficiency reasoning in addition to per-claim entailment, or is per-claim entailment plus a separate retrieval-recall metric an acceptable substitute?
