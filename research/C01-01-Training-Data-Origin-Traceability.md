# C1.1: Training Data Origin & Traceability

> [Back to C01 Index](C01-Training-Data.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-16

## Purpose

Maintaining a verifiable inventory of every dataset, accepting only trusted sources, and logging every change for auditability are the load-bearing foundations of training-data security. Without knowing where data came from, who is responsible for it, and what happened to it over time, an organization cannot detect supply-chain compromise, trace poisoned samples back to their origin during incident response, or satisfy the regulatory pressure that has intensified sharply over the last eighteen months.

The threat landscape is no longer theoretical. In January 2025, researchers documented a medical-LLM corruption campaign in which tens of thousands of fabricated articles were injected into a widely-used training corpus, resulting in unsafe treatment recommendations. In January 2026, the "Basilisk Venom" incident demonstrated how hidden prompts embedded in public GitHub code comments can backdoor fine-tuned models — DeepSeek's DeepThink-R1 learned a phrase-triggered backdoor from contaminated repositories and carried it months into production, without needing internet access to fire. Protect AI's scans by April 2025 found roughly 352,000 unsafe or suspicious issues across 51,700 models on HuggingFace alone. These are the failure modes C1.1 is trying to prevent.

Regulatory traction has caught up with the threat model. California AB 2013 — the Generative AI Training Data Transparency Act — took effect January 1, 2026 and forces developers of publicly-available generative AI systems to post twelve enumerated categories of training-data disclosure, including sources or owners, data volume, IP/privacy flags, processing history, and synthetic-data provenance. The EU AI Act's Article 10 obligations for high-risk AI systems become enforceable August 2, 2026, demanding data provenance, lineage, representativeness testing, documented limitations, and — critically — timestamped operational evidence that those standards were applied to the specific pipeline and dataset at a specific point in time. Neither law is satisfiable by a spreadsheet maintained by goodwill; both presuppose the kind of machine-readable inventory and change-log discipline this section describes.

This section covers provenance metadata (origin, responsible party, license, collection method, intended-use constraints, processing history), minimization of unnecessary features and PII, logged approval gates on dataset changes, and fingerprinting or watermarking datasets for attribution and theft detection.

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| 1.1.1 | **Verify that** an up-to-date inventory of every training-data source (origin, responsible party, license, collection method, intended use constraints, and processing history) is maintained. | 1 | Supply-chain compromise via unknown or unvetted data sources (MITRE ATLAS AML.T0010 ML Supply Chain Compromise; AML.T0019 Publish Poisoned Datasets); inability to trace poisoned data back to origin during incident response (see Jan 2025 medical-LLM corpus corruption and Jan 2026 Basilisk Venom campaign); non-compliance with California AB 2013 (effective Jan 1, 2026) and EU AI Act Article 10 (high-risk enforcement Aug 2, 2026), both of which require per-dataset source disclosure. | Review the data catalog or asset inventory system — open-source options include DataHub (LinkedIn origin, still the broadly-recommended pick for 2026 per Atlan and lakeFS comparisons), OpenMetadata, Marquez (reference OpenLineage implementation), MLflow's `mlflow.data` module (provides dataset digests and run-linked metadata), and Unity Catalog when on Databricks; Amundsen is now dormant and not recommended for new deployments. Confirm each dataset entry includes all six required metadata fields (origin, responsible party, license, collection method, intended-use constraints, processing history). Spot-check a sample of datasets against their listed sources. For AB 2013 compliance, confirm a public-facing disclosure document exists and is re-generated on every substantial model modification. For EU AI Act Article 10, confirm the inventory produces timestamped evidence per pipeline run. Compare against CycloneDX ML-BOM (1.7 added citations for data provenance in 2025) or SPDX 3.0.1 AI/Dataset profiles for a machine-readable manifest. | Most organizations still track datasets informally. DataHub, OpenMetadata, and MLflow capture metadata but do not enforce completeness — required fields can be left blank. Manual audit is still needed to verify populated fields are accurate, not just present. OWASP AI-BOM (v0.1 released late 2025) is early-stage. CycloneDX and SPDX remain convergent but not yet seamlessly interoperable for AI ecosystems. Marquez handles lineage well but does not replace a catalog — pair it with DataHub or OpenMetadata for discovery and business glossaries. |
| 1.1.2 | **Verify that** training data processes exclude unnecessary features, attributes, or fields (e.g., unused metadata, sensitive PII, leaked test data). | 1 | Data leakage (test-data contamination inflating benchmark accuracy and masking vulnerabilities); PII exposure in model weights via training-data extraction or membership-inference attacks (MITRE ATLAS AML.T0044 Full ML Model Access, AML.T0057 LLM Data Leakage); larger attack surface and higher GDPR/CCPA/HIPAA blast radius from retaining unnecessary fields. | Review data-pipeline code and ETL configurations for explicit feature-selection steps (allow-list preferred over deny-list). Confirm a PII scanning tool is wired into ingest — Microsoft Presidio (open source, extensible recognizers, supports text/image/structured data), AWS Macie (managed identifiers for credit cards, access keys, passports), Google Cloud DLP, or a commercial equivalent. Verify test/validation/holdout separation is enforced at ingest time and guarded by a hash check to detect contamination. Inspect dataset-level PII scan reports for each ingest run; spot-check by running Presidio over a sample and diffing against the stored report. Confirm the pipeline rejects or quarantines records that fail the holdout-separation check. | Automated PII detection has meaningful false-negative rates, especially for non-English text, domain-specific identifiers, and freeform notes. Presidio's baseline accuracy can be lifted ~30% with tuned recognizers, but that requires investment most teams never make. No tool reliably catches semantic PII (e.g., "the nurse at the only clinic on Main Street in [town]"). Feature minimization remains largely a design decision; there is no tool that will tell you a column is "unnecessary" — that judgment stays with humans. Cross-reference C12.1 (Anonymization & Data Minimization) for privacy-specific controls. |
| 1.1.3 | **Verify that** all dataset changes are subject to a logged approval workflow. | 1 | Unauthorized data modification or injection (MITRE ATLAS AML.T0020 Poison Training Data, AML.T0020.000 Poison Training Data: Tainted Data from Acquisition); insider threat inserting poisoned samples; untracked drift in training-data composition that masks a slow-burn poisoning campaign; loss of audit evidence required by EU AI Act Article 10 for high-risk systems. | Inspect dataset-level version control. lakeFS (now the enterprise standard after absorbing DVC's user base in late 2025) and DVC both expose Git-style pull-request workflows over data, with immutable history and diff review before merge to a protected branch. Confirm a protected main/production branch exists, that merges require PR review, and that CI checks (schema validation, PII scan, poisoning heuristics, holdout-leak check) must pass before merge. Verify audit logs record approver identity, timestamp, and justification. Check that approval integrates with your ticketing or CI/CD system (Jira, GitHub Issues, GitLab MRs) for traceability. Review immutable-history invariants — lakeFS records who changed what and when; confirm no admin role can rewrite history without a break-glass audit trail. | Neither DVC nor lakeFS natively enforces "must have N approvers" — that requires wiring through Git provider branch-protection rules or a CI gate. Many shops use Git-LFS or plain S3 versioning, which record changes but offer no review workflow. For the EU AI Act's "timestamped operational evidence" bar, bare version control is insufficient — you need CI logs correlated with approver identity and test pass/fail, typically via MLflow's webhooks or a purpose-built MLOps control plane. |
| 1.1.4 | **Verify that** datasets or subsets are watermarked or fingerprinted where feasible. | 3 | Unauthorized redistribution of proprietary datasets (MITRE ATLAS AML.T0010.002 ML Supply Chain Compromise: Data); difficulty attributing data in a trained model back to its source; inability to detect when stolen or unlicensed data appears in a competitor's model output; compliance gaps around copyright/license claims now enforceable under AB 2013's IP-flag disclosure requirement. | Check for implementation of dataset fingerprinting. [Datasig](https://github.com/trailofbits/datasig) (Trail of Bits, May 2025) produces MinHash-based fingerprints that let you compare datasets for similarity without access to the raw data — useful for AI-BOM entries and for "did competitor X train on our corpus" investigations. For ownership-proof against trained models, evaluate radioactive-data techniques: recent research (arXiv 2510.17033, Oct 2025) shows LLM watermarks remain radioactive even through federated-learning fine-tuning, allowing detection with high confidence. For text datasets, evaluate corpus-level watermarking via biased synonym selection (ICASSP 2025). Confirm the fingerprint or watermark is stored alongside the catalog entry and that a detection tool is available and has been exercised end-to-end at least once per year. | As of April 2026, Datasig remains prototype-grade — MinHash-based comparison is lossy and carries a reported ~5% error margin; expect improvements but not definitive forensics. Corpus-level text watermarking (ICASSP 2025, ACL 2025) is research-stage; effectiveness degrades under repeated-context masking and heavy paraphrasing, which a motivated adversary will apply. No standardized dataset-watermarking scheme exists across ML ecosystems, and image-dataset radioactivity work (Sablayrolles et al.) predates modern foundation-model training regimes. Appropriately Level 3 — deploy where the value at risk justifies the tooling investment; do not treat as a primary control. |
