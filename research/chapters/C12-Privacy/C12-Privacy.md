# C12: Privacy Protection & Personal Data Management

> **Source:** [`1.0/en/0x10-C12-Privacy.md`](https://github.com/OWASP/AISVS/blob/main/1.0/en/0x10-C12-Privacy.md)
> **Requirements:** 20 | **Sections:** 6

## Control Objective

Maintain rigorous privacy assurances across the entire AI lifecycle (collection, training, inference, and incident response) so that personal data is only processed with clear consent, minimum necessary scope, provable erasure, and formal privacy guarantees. This chapter focuses on AI-specific privacy concerns: privacy properties of training data and derived model artifacts, deletion and unlearning across ML artifacts, differential privacy budget management for training, purpose binding for datasets and models, consent-aware inference gating, and federated-learning privacy controls.

> **2025-2026 Highlights:** AI privacy incidents jumped 56.4% in 2024, with nearly half involving customer PII. As of May 2026, AI-app and AI-supply-chain breaches remain concentrated in chat logs, evaluation platforms, data-training brokers, model gateways, vector stores, and children's AI products — including Chat & Ask AI (300M+ messages), McHire/McDonald's (64M job applicants), OmniGPT (34M conversation lines), Bondu AI Toy (50K children's transcripts), the Sears Home Services chatbot breach (3.7M records), the Mercor/LiteLLM supply chain compromise (40,000+ contractor PII, reportedly 4TB including video interviews and Social Security numbers), Braintrust's May 2026 customer API-key incident, and AnythingLLM's CVE-2026-24477 vector-store key exposure. Indirect prompt injection in enterprise SaaS agents has matured into a distinct privacy-exfiltration vector — Capsule Security's "ShareLeak" (CVE-2026-21520, patched January 2026) showed a single Copilot Studio form field exfiltrating SharePoint customer data via Outlook, and Noma Security's "ForcedLeak" (CVSS 9.4) chained an expired-domain CSP bypass with Salesforce Agentforce Web-to-Lead to drain CRM records. Microsoft's May 2026 Semantic Kernel disclosures (CVE-2026-25592 and CVE-2026-26030) demonstrated that prompt injection can also escalate to sandbox escape and host RCE, putting any local PII or secrets within the runtime at risk. Intruder's May 2026 scan of roughly 1 million exposed AI services across 2M hosts found that 31% of 5,200+ Ollama servers responded without authentication, with 90+ exposed instances across government, marketing, and finance leaking conversation histories, plaintext API keys, business workflows, and downstream credentials. AgentLeak's 2026 multi-agent benchmark shows that privacy leakage can move into internal channels — shared memory, tool arguments, and agent-to-agent messages — even when final-output leakage appears lower. The MIT Technology Review (May 2026) and University of Washington researchers documented that Gemini, ChatGPT, Claude, and Grok continue to surface real personal phone numbers and addresses from training data, with DeleteMe reporting a 400% increase in AI-related privacy concerns. The EDPB's 2024 opinion set an extremely high bar for anonymization in LLMs, the 2025 CEF report on right to erasure found widespread compliance gaps across 764 controllers, and the Italian Garante fined OpenAI EUR 15M for lack of lawful basis. The EDPB/EDPS Joint Opinion 2/2026 (10 February 2026) on the broader Digital Omnibus warned against weakening the GDPR's definition of personal data and against placing cookie/audience-measurement rules into the GDPR rather than ePrivacy. In January 2026, SDNY affirmed a discovery order forcing OpenAI to retain and produce a 20-million-row sample of de-identified ChatGPT logs in the New York Times copyright litigation; by May 2026, civil and criminal cases were already treating chatbot conversations as discoverable evidence. Machine unlearning faces a credibility crisis after ICLR 2025 relearning attacks showed approximate methods are fundamentally flawed, with FROC (2026) and zkUnlearner (2025-2026) emerging as risk-controlled and zero-knowledge alternatives for verifiable forgetting. On the DP front, Google's VaultGemma demonstrated production-grade DP-SGD at LLM scale (epsilon <= 2.0), while DP reversal via LLM feedback emerged as a new attack vector. The EU Digital Omnibus political agreement of 7 May 2026 (Council/Parliament) shifts high-risk AI rules to 2 December 2027 for listed high-risk areas and 2 August 2028 for systems integrated into regulated products, postpones national AI regulatory sandbox establishment to 2 August 2027, and reduces the GPAI transparency grace period from six to three months (deadline 2 December 2026). California's finalized CCPA regulations on ADMT and risk assessments begin compliance January 1 2026 (risk assessments) and January 1 2027 (ADMT pre-use notices and opt-out). The UK ICO became the first regulator to publish a Tech Futures report on agentic AI (January 2026), flagging transparency, purpose limitation, and accuracy as the core data-protection challenges when agents talk to other agents in ways no human directly observes. The ICO's follow-up five-step plan (14 May 2026) mapped seven AI-driven attack categories — AI-enhanced phishing, deepfake social engineering, automated vulnerability scanning, AI-powered malware, credential stuffing, data poisoning, and indirect prompt injection — onto UK GDPR Article 32, treating AI security as a present-day data-protection obligation rather than a future-state risk. Shadow AI moved from statistics to filings the same week: Community Bank's 7 May 2026 8-K disclosed that an employee uploaded customer names, dates of birth, and Social Security numbers to an unauthorized AI tool, while Sysdig observed exploitation scans hitting PraisonAI CVE-2026-44338 — a default-disabled authentication flag exposing `/agents` and `/chat` — within 3 hours 44 minutes of public disclosure, fixed in PraisonAI 4.6.34. Early June 2026 brought the first mass account-takeover through an AI support agent: Meta's High Touch Support recovery chatbot (launched March 2026) failed to verify that password-reset email addresses matched the account on file, letting attackers hijack 20,225 Instagram accounts between 17 April and 31 May 2026 — including the dormant Obama White House account — with exposure of direct messages, contact details, and dates of birth (Maine AG notice filed 5 June 2026). Enforcement also escalated on the data-minimization front: California's record USD 12.75M GM/OnStar settlement (8 May 2026) is the first CCPA data-minimization and purpose-limitation enforcement action, and Canada's federal and provincial privacy regulators found a joint PIPEDA complaint against OpenAI's ChatGPT "well founded" (6 May 2026), citing overly broad collection of sensitive personal information and ordering limits on training-data use. On the defensive side, OpenAI released Privacy Filter (28 April 2026) — an Apache-2.0, 1.5B-parameter sparse-MoE bidirectional token classifier (50M active parameters, 128K context) that redacts eight PII categories on-device before data reaches a cloud model — and Palo Alto Unit 42 published the first systematic catalog of web-based indirect prompt injection observed in the wild, documenting 22 hidden-instruction techniques used for ad-review evasion, SEO-driven phishing promotion, and system-prompt leakage.

## Threat Landscape

Known attacks, real-world incidents, and threat vectors relevant to this chapter:

- Training data memorization leaking PII in model outputs — AI privacy/security incidents jumped 56.4% in 2024, with nearly half of all breaches involving customer PII
- Re-identification attacks on anonymized training data; EDPB (2024) ruled that LLMs "rarely achieve anonymization standards" and set an extremely high bar for considering model outputs anonymous
- Inability to truly 'forget' data already learned by a trained model — machine unlearning remains an active research area with exact, approximate, and certified approaches all having significant limitations
- Scope creep — data collected for one purpose being used for model training; 77% of employees paste company information into AI/LLM services (2026 survey), with 82% using personal accounts
- Cross-border data transfer violations in distributed training setups
- Shadow AI data leakage — breaches via unmanaged AI tools take an average of 247 days to detect, disproportionately affecting customer PII (65%) and intellectual property (40%)
- Model inversion and gradient inversion attacks using mathematically optimized queries to reverse-engineer training data from model outputs or gradient updates
- EchoLeak-style prompt injection attacks (CVE-2025-32711) that force AI assistants to exfiltrate sensitive business data to external URLs
- Differential privacy reversal via LLM feedback — adversaries analyze model confidence scores (logits, perplexity) to determine training set membership and reconstruct individual records from anonymized datasets (2025-2026 research)
- Approximate unlearning shown to be fundamentally flawed — CMU researchers (ICLR 2025) demonstrated "benign relearning attacks" that reverse gradient-ascent-based unlearning using small amounts of loosely related public data
- AI-app data breaches at alarming scale: as of March 2026, at least 20 documented AI-specific breaches since January 2025 including Chat & Ask AI (300M+ messages), McHire/McDonald's (64M applicants), OmniGPT (34M lines), and Bondu AI Toy (50K children's chat transcripts)
- Children's AI products emerging as a high-risk category — the Bondu AI Toy breach (January 2026) exposed 50K children's transcripts with names and birthdates via a vulnerability where any Gmail account granted admin access
- AI chatbot infrastructure as a systemic attack surface — the Sears Home Services breach (February 2026) exposed 3.7M chat logs and audio recordings from AI chatbots via unsecured databases with no password protection or encryption, demonstrating that customer-facing AI assistants create large, often unprotected PII repositories
- User-level versus example-level DP — Google research (2025-2026) showed that prior user-level DP implementations added orders of magnitude more noise than necessary; new ELS/ULS approaches significantly improve the privacy-utility tradeoff for LLM fine-tuning on sensitive datasets
- Brazil's ANPD targeting AI training data — enforcement actions against social media companies using personal data for AI training without proper consent, extending GDPR-style enforcement to Latin America
- AI supply chain attacks against training-data brokers — the Mercor/LiteLLM incident (March-April 2026) showed that compromising a single widely-used Python library (LiteLLM, ~97M monthly downloads, present in an estimated 36% of cloud environments) can cascade into the privacy of tens of thousands of data contractors and the training pipelines of frontier labs (Meta paused all Mercor work while investigating)
- Vector-database and embedding-memory leaks as a distinct attack class — leak rates for AI-oriented vector databases jumped roughly 1000% year-over-year (2024 to 2025); embeddings can be reverse-engineered, clustered for behavioural profiling, and re-identified, making them at least as sensitive as the raw text they derive from. CVE-2026-24477 in AnythingLLM exposed a Qdrant vector database API key through an unauthenticated setup endpoint, showing how RAG and memory credentials can become direct PII access paths.
- Memory poisoning against long-term agent state — adversaries inject malicious instructions into an agent's persistent vector memory that stay dormant until triggered, combining privacy leakage with agent-behavior hijacking (the Step Finance incident in January 2026 drained ~$40M after attackers compromised AI trading agents with over-permissive memory access)
- Third-party LLM gateways as a consent-laundering surface — an April 2026 class action against Perplexity alleges user chats were forwarded to Google and Meta without consent, highlighting that consent scoped to one controller does not propagate cleanly when traffic is brokered through analytics or model-routing partners
- Court-ordered evidentiary retention as an unplanned privacy-budget exhaustion — the SDNY order in *NYT v. OpenAI* required indefinite retention of consumer and API chat content from April-September 2025, overriding OpenAI's standard 30-day deletion for Temporary Chats and deleted conversations
- Smart-eyewear and always-on multimodal capture — class actions and a UK ICO investigation (March 2026) against Meta's Ray-Ban smart glasses allege that bystander footage and audio from roughly 7M devices was used to train AI without the recorded persons' consent, creating a new GDPR Article 6 lawful-basis question for passive capture
- Secrets sprawl feeding training and agent pipelines — GitGuardian's 2026 report found 28.6M new secrets exposed in public GitHub commits in 2025 (34% YoY), with OpenRouter credentials up 48x YoY; leaked API keys for LLM providers directly translate into unaudited PII processing by third parties
- AI evaluation and observability platforms as concentrated secret stores — the Braintrust incident in May 2026 showed how stored model-provider API keys can become a privacy risk even when no model weights or training data are directly exposed
- Chatbot conversations as litigation records — May 2026 reporting on Musk v. OpenAI and other cases highlights that chatbot logs can become discoverable evidence without the same privilege protections users expect from lawyers, doctors, or therapists
- Multi-agent internal-channel leakage — AgentLeak (2026) found that multi-agent systems can reduce final-output leakage while still exposing sensitive data through shared memory, tool arguments, and agent-to-agent messages that output-only privacy audits never inspect
- Compliance-timeline churn as an operational risk — the EU Digital Omnibus agreement moved many high-risk AI deadlines from August 2026 to December 2027 or August 2028, which can delay privacy-control investment unless organizations keep internal target dates independent of regulatory slippage
- Indirect prompt injection inside enterprise SaaS agents as a structured PII-exfiltration channel — Capsule Security's ShareLeak (CVE-2026-21520, CVSS 7.5) used a single SharePoint form input to make Copilot Studio query connected SharePoint lists and forward customer data via Outlook, while Noma Security's ForcedLeak (CVSS 9.4) combined Web-to-Lead injection with an expired whitelisted CSP domain (purchased for ~$5) to drain Salesforce Agentforce CRM records. Microsoft and Salesforce treating these as named CVEs marks a shift toward classifying prompt injection as a distinct vulnerability class in agent platforms.
- Prompt-injection-to-RCE in AI agent frameworks — Microsoft's May 2026 disclosure of CVE-2026-25592 (CVSS 10.0) and CVE-2026-26030 in Semantic Kernel showed a hostile prompt could exit an Azure Container Apps Python sandbox via `DownloadFileAsync` exposed as a kernel function, exposing any PII, secrets, or local datasets reachable from the host process; patches shipped in `semantic-kernel` 1.39.4 (Python) and 1.71.0 (.NET).
- Mass exposure of self-hosted AI infrastructure — Intruder's May 2026 scan of ~1M exposed AI services across 2M hosts found 31% of 5,200+ Ollama servers responded without authentication, with 90+ exposed instances across government, marketing, and finance and 518 frontier-model gateways wrapping paid Anthropic, Deepseek, Moonshot, Google, and OpenAI APIs leaking full conversation histories, plaintext API keys, business workflow definitions, and credentials for downstream tools
- Training-data PII regurgitation by frontier chatbots — MIT Technology Review (May 2026) and University of Washington PhD researchers documented Gemini, ChatGPT, Claude, and Grok surfacing real personal phone numbers and addresses from prior web exposure; DeleteMe reported a 400% YoY increase in AI-related privacy requests, and no scalable removal pathway currently exists for PII embedded in foundation-model weights
- Agentic-AI transparency gap as a GDPR Articles 5, 13-14 risk — the UK ICO's January 2026 Tech Futures report flagged that data flows between cooperating agents are increasingly opaque to humans, making purpose limitation and explainability obligations harder to meet for "legal or similarly significant" automated decisions
- Shadow-AI exfiltration via employee-uploaded prompts as a SEC-reportable cybersecurity event — Community Bank's 7 May 2026 8-K disclosed that an employee uploaded customer names, dates of birth, and Social Security numbers to an unauthorized AI application, with notification obligations and class-action interest following within days; the incident frames internal AI misuse as a material cybersecurity disclosure trigger and not merely a DLP failure
- Rapid mass scanning of AI-framework vulnerabilities — Sysdig's Threat Research Team observed the first opportunistic scans for PraisonAI CVE-2026-44338 (legacy Flask API server with `AUTH_ENABLED = False` and `AUTH_TOKEN = None` by default in versions 2.5.6 through 4.6.33, fixed in 4.6.34) within 3 hours 44 minutes of public disclosure, with a User-Agent of `CVE-Detector/1.0` issuing unauthenticated `GET /agents` and `/chat` requests that returned agent configuration and triggered configured workflows; self-hosted AI orchestration frameworks are now in the rapid-exploitation window historically reserved for high-value perimeter software
- AI-driven attacks framed under UK GDPR Article 32 — the ICO's 14 May 2026 five-step plan mapped AI-enhanced phishing, deepfake social engineering, automated vulnerability scanning, AI-powered malware, credential stuffing, data poisoning, and indirect prompt injection onto the "appropriate technical and organisational measures" duty, with Cyber Essentials, the AI Cyber Security Code of Practice, and DPIAs explicitly named as the operational baseline for organisations handling personal data
- AI support and account-recovery agents as an account-takeover and PII-disclosure surface — Meta's High Touch Support chatbot (March 2026 launch) skipped email-ownership verification on password resets, and attackers using VPNs to appear geographically near targets hijacked 20,225 Instagram accounts between 17 April and 31 May 2026, gaining reach into direct messages, contact information, dates of birth, and linked services; the flaw went undetected for over six weeks, showing that conversational agents granted identity-workflow authority need the same authentication rigor as the flows they replace
- Web-based indirect prompt injection operating in the wild at commercial scale — Palo Alto Unit 42 (March-April 2026) cataloged 22 techniques (zero-sized text, off-screen positioning, `display:none`, hidden `<textarea>` content, SVG CDATA, instructions in HTML attributes) embedded in webpages that LLM summarizers and browsing agents later consume, with observed intents including ad-review evasion, SEO manipulation promoting phishing, system-prompt leakage, and sensitive-data exfiltration that scales with agent tool privileges; VortexPIA research separately demonstrated indirect prompt injection tuned specifically for efficient extraction of user privacy attributes from conversational context

## Implementation Maturity

| Control Area | Tooling Maturity | Notes |
|--------------|:---:|-------|
| C12.1 Anonymization & Data Minimization | Medium-High | PII detection tools are mature and production-ready, and the bar for "good enough" redaction keeps rising: OpenAI's Privacy Filter (April 2026, Apache 2.0) puts a 128K-context bidirectional PII classifier on laptops and browsers, making pre-ingestion sanitization feasible without sending raw data to a cloud service — though OpenAI itself warns it is a redaction aid, not a guarantee, for medical or legal workflows. Automated k-anonymity auditing (ARX, sdcMicro) requires custom CI/CD integration. Synthetic data tools (SDV, Gretel, MOSTLY AI) are production-ready but privacy guarantees vary — the EDPB's 2024 opinion sets an extremely high bar for anonymization in AI contexts. Minimization is now an enforcement reality, not just a principle: California's USD 12.75M GM/OnStar settlement (May 2026) is the first CCPA action grounded in retaining data beyond service necessity and repurposing it. |
| C12.2 Right-to-be-Forgotten & Deletion Enforcement | Low-Medium | Machine unlearning is advancing but faces a credibility crisis — ICLR 2025 relearning attacks (CMU) demonstrated that approximate methods merely suppress outputs rather than truly forgetting, meaning only exact unlearning (full retraining/SISA) and emerging certified methods are reliable. EUPG (2025) offers a promising hybrid approach. FROC (2026) wraps unlearning strategies in conformal-style risk-control so auditors can compare methods inside a feasible operating region rather than relying on ad-hoc forgetting metrics, and zkUnlearner (2025-2026) is moving toward zero-knowledge proofs of unlearning operations with multi-granularity and forgery resistance. The EDPB's 2025 CEF report on right to erasure (published February 2026) found widespread compliance gaps across 764 controllers, with nine DPAs launching formal investigations. The research community is accelerating: three dedicated workshops in 2026 (MUV at CVPR, WIPE-OUT at ECML-PKDD, MUGen at ICML) now cover vision, NLP, and multimodal unlearning. |
| C12.3 Differential-Privacy Safeguards | Medium-High | Opacus, TF Privacy, and Google DP Library are production-quality for training. Opacus now documents non-wrapping integration for transformer compatibility, which matters for Hugging Face-style models. Google's VaultGemma (September 2025) is the first production-grade DP-trained LLM — 1B params, epsilon <= 2.0 — proving DP-SGD works at LLM scale, though with a utility gap vs. non-private models. DynamoEnhance (2024-2026) extends DP-SGD to >7B-parameter LLMs via multi-GPU DeepSpeed integration. Google's user-level DP fine-tuning (ELS/ULS) dramatically reduces noise overhead. PrivCode (NDSS 2026) demonstrates DP-SGD for code generation — a first for structured-output domains. OpenDP/SmartNoise and PipelineDP4j expand ecosystem options. NIST SP 800-226, finalized in March 2025, gives evaluators a common way to assess DP claims and pitfalls. Budget tracking dashboards and RDP/zCDP/PLD accountants are maturing. Caution: DP reversal via LLM feedback (2025-2026 research) shows that confidence score analysis can defeat DP protections at the inference layer — API-level mitigations (hiding logits) are needed alongside training-time DP. |
| C12.4 Purpose-Limitation & Scope-Creep Protection | Low-Medium | Standardized tooling for purpose tagging and runtime purpose enforcement is still custom policy-as-code work, but enterprise SaaS vendors are starting to ship adjacent controls. Microsoft Purview's RSAC 2026 announcements add Shadow AI discovery in the Microsoft 365 admin center, Purview DLP for Microsoft 365 Copilot (web search and connector blocking based on sensitive information types), DSPM AI Observability that treats Copilot Studio and Azure AI Foundry agents as first-class entities, and a Purview browser extension across Edge, Chrome, and Firefox. The May 2026 ShareLeak (CVE-2026-21520) and ForcedLeak (CVSS 9.4) disclosures show that purpose monitoring needs to extend to indirect-prompt-injection paths in agents that act on customer SharePoint, Outlook, or CRM data. Self-hosted agent frameworks ship with weak defaults — PraisonAI CVE-2026-44338 (May 2026) had `AUTH_ENABLED = False` on a legacy Flask API server and was opportunistically scanned within 4 hours of disclosure — so purpose-limitation guarantees in agent platforms need authentication and network-boundary controls as a prerequisite, not an afterthought. Community Bank's 7 May 2026 8-K disclosure of an employee uploading customer SSNs to an unauthorized AI tool reinforces that purpose limitation requires DLP-style egress controls on developer-machine and employee-browser paths to consumer AI services, not only on sanctioned platforms. The EU Digital Omnibus political agreement of 7 May 2026 moves many high-risk AI deadlines to 2 December 2027 and product-integrated systems to 2 August 2028, so internal DPIA and purpose-gating roadmaps should not rely solely on statutory go-live dates. The UK ICO's 14 May 2026 five-step plan reframes the seven major AI attack categories (including data poisoning and indirect prompt injection) as Article 32 obligations, giving privacy programmes a regulator-backed hook to require purpose-limitation controls today rather than after the AI Act high-risk deadlines arrive. The current AISVS text now ties the policy-as-code redeployment gate (12.4.3) directly to the machine-readable purpose tag from 12.4.1 rather than a generic DPIA review, which makes the control testable: an auditor can diff the deployment target's purpose against the artifact's tag. California's GM/OnStar settlement (May 2026) supplies the first purpose-limitation enforcement precedent under CCPA — retained telematics data repurposed for broker sales — giving the purpose-tag-and-gate pattern a concrete regulatory anchor in the US as well as the EU. |
| C12.5 Consent Management & Lawful-Basis Tracking | Low-Medium | CMPs are mature for web consent but poorly integrated with ML pipelines. No standard for consent tokens in inference APIs. Italian Garante's EUR 15M OpenAI fine (2024) for lack of lawful basis underscores the compliance risk. European Commission's proposed GDPR amendments (Q4 2025) may reshape consent requirements for AI. April-May 2026 class actions and incidents (Perplexity → Google/Meta chat sharing, Meta Ray-Ban smart glasses bystander capture, Braintrust customer API-key storage) show that consent propagation across sub-processors, passive-capture modalities, and AI tooling vendors is an emerging litigation frontier. Multi-agent systems need consent checks on internal channels too: AgentLeak-style testing should inspect shared memory, tool arguments, and inter-agent messages, not only final responses. California's ADMT regulations (risk assessments from January 2026, pre-use notices and opt-out from January 2027) add a new consumer-rights layer specifically for AI significant-decision systems; the CPPA's Delete Request and Opt-out Platform is now live for operationalizing requests against data brokers. The AISVS consent requirements now separate consent-scope validation at inference (12.5.1, Level 3) from the refuse-or-downgrade enforcement response (12.5.2, Level 2), which mirrors how consent gateways are actually built — a policy-decision check followed by an enforcement action — and lets organizations adopt enforcement before full per-subject scope validation. Canada's joint OPC finding against ChatGPT (May 2026) is the strongest consent-enforcement signal to date in North America: regulators in four jurisdictions found collection overly broad under PIPEDA and conditioned resolution on OpenAI limiting personal information used for training, confirming that "publicly available" does not equal "consented" for model training. |
| C12.6 Federated Learning with Privacy Controls | Medium-High | FL frameworks (Flower, TFF, NVIDIA FLARE) support local DP and secure aggregation. NVIDIA FLARE's 2026 client API reduces the refactoring cost of converting local PyTorch workflows into federated clients, while NVIDIA's confidential-computing stack adds attestation and secure key release patterns for privacy-preserving training and inference. Fortanix Confidential AI and NVIDIA Secure AI patterns make secure key release, attested GPU runtimes, and centralized cryptographic policy realistic data-in-use controls, but they do not replace DP accounting or consent enforcement. Corvex's March 2026 production deployment of confidential computing on NVIDIA HGX B200 with Intel Trust Authority CPU/GPU remote attestation and encrypted NVSwitch/NVLink GPU-to-GPU communication is an early proof point that attested multi-GPU runtimes are reaching production for sensitive workloads. Google's distributed DP for federated learning is production-deployed (Gboard). Robust aggregation (Krum, Trimmed-Mean, FLTrust) is available but configuration is manual. Canary-based auditing and Federated Unlearning are research-stage but advancing. Apple Private Cloud Compute demonstrates production-grade privacy-preserving inference at scale. |

## Open Research Questions

- [ ] Is machine unlearning practical at scale, or is retraining the only reliable option? The ICML 2025 MUGen workshop is pushing toward standardized evaluation but no production-ready solution exists for foundation models. Federated Unlearning adds a distributed dimension to this challenge.
- [ ] What epsilon values for differential privacy provide meaningful protection without destroying model utility? Apple uses epsilon 2-8 for telemetry; the US Census used epsilon ~19.6 (controversial). NIST's 2025 formal guidelines standardize DP evaluation but do not prescribe specific thresholds.
- [ ] How should GDPR Article 17 (right to erasure) apply to foundation models? The EDPB's 2025 coordinated enforcement focus on right to erasure will produce enforcement decisions that may set precedent. The Italian Garante's EUR 15M OpenAI fine demonstrates willingness to enforce against AI companies.
- [ ] What constitutes adequate anonymization for AI training data? The EDPB's 2024 opinion sets an extremely high bar — LLMs "rarely achieve anonymization standards" and controllers must use "all means reasonably likely" to test for data extraction.
- [ ] How will the EU AI Act's high-risk classification, now politically delayed to 2 December 2027 for many listed high-risk areas and 2 August 2028 for product-integrated systems, interact with GDPR's privacy requirements for AI systems? The Digital Omnibus timeline reduces immediate pressure but may widen the privacy-control implementation gap.
- [ ] Can certified machine unlearning (provable guarantees) scale to multimodal foundation models? MMUnlearner (2025) is a first step but the field is nascent. The ICLR 2025 relearning attack results make this more urgent — approximate methods are now considered fundamentally flawed.
- [ ] How should privacy-preserving inference architectures (Apple PCC model, confidential computing enclaves) be evaluated and certified for regulatory compliance?
- [ ] What is the appropriate standard for "shadow AI" privacy governance, given that 77% of employees leak corporate data into unmanaged AI tools?
- [ ] How should organizations defend against DP reversal via LLM feedback attacks? Hiding confidence scores is a practical first step but reduces model utility for downstream applications that depend on calibrated probabilities.
- [ ] Will the EU Digital Omnibus delay of high-risk AI obligations create a regulatory gap that harms privacy protections, as the EDPB/EDPS joint opinion warned before the May 2026 political agreement?
- [ ] How should VaultGemma-style DP-SGD scaling laws inform epsilon threshold policies? The current "comparable to 5-year-old models" utility gap may be acceptable for some use cases but not others.
- [ ] Can user-level DP (Google's ELS/ULS) close the practical gap for fine-tuning on sensitive user data without major utility degradation? Early results are promising but limited to specific model families and data distributions.
- [ ] How should organizations secure AI chatbot infrastructure (chat logs, audio recordings, transcriptions) against the kind of mass exposure seen in the Sears breach? Current PII-at-rest protections are clearly inadequate for the volume of sensitive data AI assistants generate.
- [ ] Will GPU-accelerated FHE (~200x speedup over CPU) eventually make privacy-preserving LLM inference practical for production workloads, or are alternative approaches (confidential computing, PCC-style attestation) the more viable path?
- [ ] How should organizations treat court-ordered evidentiary retention (e.g., NYT v. OpenAI) in their standard retention and deletion programs? Indefinite retention orders can directly contradict GDPR Article 17 and vendor-standard 30-day deletion commitments, creating a forum-selection and data-residency dilemma.
- [ ] What is the minimum viable governance bar for AI training-data brokers and staffing platforms like Mercor, given that a single supply-chain compromise (LiteLLM) cascaded into contractor PII, video interviews, and frontier-lab training methodology? The Gill v. Mercor.io complaint alleges basics — MFA, encryption at rest and in transit, PII access limitation, monitoring, and password rotation — were missing.
- [ ] How should embeddings and vector-memory stores be classified under GDPR, CCPA, and HIPAA? Leak rates up ~1000% YoY and the practicality of embedding inversion make the "not really personal data" posture increasingly untenable.
- [ ] How far does consent scoped to one controller propagate when traffic is brokered through third-party LLM gateways or analytics tags? The Perplexity class action will likely produce the first major US precedent on this question.
- [ ] Does passive bystander capture (smart glasses, wearable recorders, always-on meeting bots) need a distinct GDPR Article 6 lawful-basis treatment? The Meta Ray-Ban lawsuits and UK ICO inquiry may force clarification.
- [ ] Will the finalized NIST Privacy Framework 1.1 (expected 2026) include measurable controls for AI-specific privacy risks (membership inference, prompt injection, reconstruction), or remain descriptive? Mapping to CSF 2.0 Governance is helpful but operational detail is still thin.
- [ ] What security and privacy due diligence is appropriate for AI evaluation, observability, and gateway vendors that store customer model-provider API keys? Braintrust's May 2026 incident shows that these vendors can indirectly control access to sensitive prompts, logs, and model outputs.
- [ ] Should chatbot conversations receive statutory privilege-like treatment in legal, medical, or therapeutic contexts, or should privacy programs assume all AI conversations can become discoverable evidence?
- [ ] What does a sufficient privacy test harness for multi-agent systems look like when sensitive data may leak through intermediate tool calls, shared memory writes, and inter-agent messages that are invisible in final outputs?
- [ ] Should indirect prompt injection in enterprise SaaS agents (Copilot Studio ShareLeak, Salesforce AgentForce ForcedLeak) be treated as a distinct GDPR/CCPA disclosure risk separate from traditional information disclosure CVEs? Microsoft and Salesforce assigning CVEs is a precedent, but the broader question of legal classification, breach-notification thresholds, and processor/sub-processor liability remains open.
- [ ] Is there a viable removal pathway for personal phone numbers, addresses, and other PII embedded in foundation-model weights, given that current "right to be forgotten" requests against LLM providers (per the MIT Technology Review reporting and DeleteMe's 400% increase in AI-related concerns) lack a working scalable mechanism?
- [ ] How should DPAs evaluate purpose limitation and Article 13-14 transparency when agents communicate with other agents in ways no human directly observes, as the UK ICO's January 2026 Tech Futures report flagged?
- [ ] How should organizations harden self-hosted AI infrastructure when ~1M instances are exposed and 31% of Ollama-style runtimes ship with no authentication by default? Is there a minimum CIS-style baseline equivalent for self-hosted AI runtimes?
- [ ] Will FROC-style conformal risk control or zkUnlearner-style zero-knowledge proofs become the audit primitive for "verified unlearning" claims, or will regulators continue to accept behavioral evaluation alone?
- [ ] What authentication baseline should apply when conversational AI agents are granted identity-workflow authority (password resets, account recovery, support escalation)? The Meta High Touch Support breach (20,225 accounts, April-June 2026) shows an AI agent can silently bypass verification steps a human-designed flow would have enforced — and went undetected for six weeks.
- [ ] After the GM/OnStar settlement and the Canadian OPC ChatGPT finding, will US and Canadian enforcement converge with the EDPB on treating training-data minimization and purpose limitation as independently actionable, and how should organizations evidence "necessity" for each retained field in a training corpus?
