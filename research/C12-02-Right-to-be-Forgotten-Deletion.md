# C12.2: Right-to-be-Forgotten & Deletion Enforcement

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-16

## Purpose

Ensure data-subject deletion requests propagate across all AI artifacts — raw datasets, feature stores, embeddings, checkpoints, logs, and backups — and that model unlearning is demonstrably verifiable rather than a cosmetic output-suppression exercise. The EDPB formally adopted its 2025 Coordinated Enforcement Framework (CEF) report on Article 17 on 18 February 2026, after examining 764 controllers across 32 DPAs throughout 2025. The report flagged seven systemic gaps: (1) absent internal procedures, (2) insufficient information to data subjects, (3) ineffective anonymization used as a substitute for erasure, (4) inconsistent implementation, (5) unclear retention periods, (6) backup-system erasure failures, and (7) flawed balancing of Article 17(3) exceptions. Nine DPAs opened formal investigations; follow-through supervision in Ireland, France, Portugal, Slovenia, and Germany runs through 2026. CNIL, Portugal's CNPD, and Sweden's IMY have already indicated CEF findings will drive sector-specific inspections this year.

Machine unlearning itself is in the middle of a credibility reset. The ICLR 2025 paper from Hu et al. (CMU) showed that approximate unlearning methods largely **obfuscate** rather than forget — a "benign relearning attack" using small amounts of loosely related public data can reverse the unlearning effect. The most widely reported example: an LLM that had been "unlearned" of biohazard knowledge regained the ability to produce harmful bioweapon text after a brief fine-tune on publicly available medical articles. This has pushed the field toward certified unlearning, SAM-based robust unlearning (Fan et al., ICML 2025), and retraining-style approaches for anything that must stand up to regulator scrutiny.

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| **12.2.1** | **Verify that** data-subject deletion requests propagate to raw datasets, checkpoints, embeddings, logs, and backups within service level agreements of less than 30 days. | 1 | GDPR Article 17 / CCPA deletion-right violations. Ceilings are up to EUR 20 million or 4% of global turnover; the Italian Garante fined Clearview AI EUR 20M (2022) for refusing to delete biometric data, and Spain's AEPD has issued over EUR 1.5M in targeted Article 17 fines. The EDPB 2025 CEF report (adopted 18 February 2026) found that 17 of 32 DPAs observed controllers without documented erasure procedures, ~20% provide no refresher training, and 50% of DPAs reported a lack of specific erasure procedures for backups. Erasure complaint volumes are rising sharply: the Netherlands logged 580 Article 17 complaints in 2024 (18.6% of total); Slovenia's share climbed from 4% (2020) to 19% (2024). The EU Digital Omnibus (proposed late 2025) may delay high-risk AI obligations from August 2026 to December 2027, but the joint EDPB/EDPS opinion (January 2026) warns this delay risks fundamental-rights harm. | Audit the deletion workflow end-to-end: issue test erasure requests against production accounts, then inspect raw data stores, feature stores (Feast, Tecton, Vertex AI Feature Store), vector databases (Pinecone, Weaviate, Milvus, Qdrant), checkpoint registries (MLflow, Weights & Biases, SageMaker Model Registry), embeddings caches (Redis, Elasticache, pgvector), application logs, and backup tiers. Time the full propagation and compare against the 30-day SLA. Following the 2026 CEF findings, verify specifically: (a) that backups have a documented erasure procedure (not merely overwrite-on-restore), (b) that "anonymization" used in lieu of deletion meets the Article 29 WP216 irreversibility bar (k-anonymity ≥ 5 with l-diversity ≥ 2 is a common floor; synthetic-data certification for stronger cases), and (c) that S3 Object Lock / Azure Blob immutable storage / GCS bucket lock retention periods don't silently extend beyond documented retention. Try restoring from backup and confirm the deleted subject's records do not reappear. | Checkpoints and embeddings remain the most commonly missed artifacts. Vector databases each require bespoke handling: Pinecone exposes `delete(ids=[...])` with namespace scoping, Milvus needs a boolean-filter delete plus a subsequent compaction pass before vectors are truly gone from segments, and Weaviate requires either `data.delete_by_id` or a batch filter delete. Backups under WORM policies (S3 Object Lock compliance mode, Azure immutable blob) **cannot** be partially redacted before their retention window expires — the standard mitigation is to encrypt backups with per-subject keys (crypto-shredding) so destroying the key makes the data unrecoverable. Distributed replication, CDN caches, and downstream analytics sinks are frequent blind spots. The GDPR/EU AI Act tension is real: personal training data must be deleted under Article 17, but Article 12 of the AI Act requires retention of technical documentation and logs for 10 years — the accepted resolution is separating personal data (deletable) from non-personal technical metadata (retained). |
| **12.2.2** | **Verify that** "machine-unlearning" routines physically re-train or approximate removal using certified unlearning algorithms. | 2 | Residual memorization of deleted data in model weights. Even after the raw records are gone, a model can continue producing outputs shaped by the removed data, violating the substance (not just the form) of the erasure right. Hu et al. (ICLR 2025, "Unlearning or Obfuscating? Jogging the Memory of Unlearned LLMs via Benign Relearning") established this as a systemic vulnerability: fine-tuning an unlearned model on small amounts of loosely related public data reliably recovers the "forgotten" knowledge. Their case studies included restoring bioweapon knowledge from unlearned LLMs via public medical articles and recovering verbatim Harry Potter passages via Wikipedia summaries. Membership-inference attacks (Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022) further show that weak unlearning leaves a detectable signal that a target record was in the training set. | Review which unlearning technique the team has chosen and match the rigor to the threat model. For Level-2 production use, prefer: SISA (Bourtoule et al., 2021 — shard/slice/isolate/aggregate so deletion retrains only a fraction of constituent models), EUPG (Universitat Rovira i Virgili, 2025 — pre-train on k-anonymized/DP-transformed data, fine-tune on raw data, revert to the pre-train checkpoint on deletion), or SAM-based robust unlearning (Fan et al., ICML 2025, arxiv:2502.05374 — sharpness-aware minimization makes unlearning resilient to relearning attacks). Evaluate with OpenUnlearning (NeurIPS D&B 2025, arxiv:2506.12618) which integrates 12+ algorithms (GradAscent, GradDiff, NPO, SimNPO, DPO, RMU, UNDIAL, AltPO, SatImp, WGA, CE-U, PDU) across TOFU, MUSE, and WMDP benchmarks, along with six membership-inference attacks. Critically, run the Hu et al. benign-relearning test: fine-tune the "unlearned" model on loosely related public data and measure how much of the forgotten knowledge resurfaces — if relearning trivially recovers the target knowledge, the unlearning is obfuscation, not deletion. | The 2025–2026 consensus splits unlearning into three tiers: (1) **exact** (full retrain or SISA) — reliable, expensive; (2) **approximate** (gradient ascent, Fisher forgetting, influence functions) — fast but post-Hu-et-al. considered cosmetic unless hardened with SAM or equivalent; (3) **certified** (provable (ε,δ)-removal) — emerging (Chourasia et al., 2025; Liu et al., "Forgetting Any Data at Any Time" arxiv:2502.17081 for vertical FL; "Beyond Uniform Deletion" arxiv:2511.06794 for data-value-weighted certified unlearning; "Certified Unlearning without Source Data Access" OpenReview 2025 for black-box settings) but not yet scalable to foundation-model sizes. Multimodal coverage is expanding: MMUnlearner (2025), MUV Workshop at CVPR 2026 (Denver, June 2026 — first major CV venue dedicated to unlearning), WIPE-OUT Workshop at ECML-PKDD (Naples, September 2026). Regulators have not yet blessed any specific technique — the Article 29 WP / EDPB treat "unlearning" as inadequate on its own unless paired with raw-data deletion. Full retraining remains the regulatory gold standard but is prohibitive above a few billion parameters. |
| **12.2.3** | **Verify that** shadow-model evaluation proves forgotten records influence less than 1% of outputs after unlearning. | 2 | Incomplete unlearning that leaves residual influence in the weights — detectable via membership-inference (Shokri et al., 2017; LiRA, Carlini et al., 2022) or extraction attacks. For LLMs specifically, the MUSE benchmark (Shi et al., 2024) measures verbatim reproduction, knowledge Q&A accuracy, and privacy leakage via MIA; the TOFU benchmark (Maini et al., 2024) uses 200 synthetic author profiles with forget/retain splits and has become the de facto reporting standard for unlearning papers in 2025–2026. An unlearned model that still leaks the forget set at > 1% distinguishability fails these benchmarks. | Train shadow models on datasets with and without the forget set. Compare: (a) membership-inference attack AUC — should be ≤ 0.55 (near random); (b) output distribution KL divergence between unlearned and from-scratch-without-forget-set models; (c) per-sample loss distributions; (d) verbatim reproduction rate on the forget set under greedy and sampled decoding. Use OpenUnlearning's PrivLeak metric to bound privacy leakage, and run its six integrated MIA variants. For LLMs, also run canary-based memorization tests (Carlini et al., 2019) and check if the model can complete prompts that reference forget-set content. Statistically validate the < 1% influence claim with a two-sample test of sufficient power (n ≥ 500 probe queries is typical). | Shadow-model evaluation is compute-heavy: training even one reference LLM shadow model at 7B parameters is costly, and MIA research typically calls for 64–256 shadows for high-confidence attacks (LiRA). Lighter "reference-model-free" MIA (Carlini et al., 2022) reduces the cost but has weaker guarantees. For generative models the "influence on outputs" metric is ill-defined in the open-ended case — most practitioners fall back to MUSE/TOFU/WMDP numbers plus extraction attacks. The 1% threshold is conventional, not regulatory — regulators care about demonstrable non-reconstructability, which is why pairing shadow-model evals with formal DP-bound training remains the strongest posture. Shadow-model frameworks worth naming: ML-Doctor (ACM CCS 2022), Privacy Meter (NUS/NIST), and the membership-inference toolkit inside OpenUnlearning. |
| **12.2.4** | **Verify that** deletion events are immutably logged and auditable for regulators. | 3 | Inability to demonstrate compliance during a DPA inspection. Without tamper-evident audit trails, a controller cannot prove it received the request, acted within 30 days, or propagated deletion to all downstream systems. The EDPB 2025 CEF specifically called out the absence of adequate internal procedures (observed by 17 of 32 DPAs) as the top failure mode; documented, immutable logs are the primary way to close that gap. | Inspect the audit-trail architecture: verify append-only storage (AWS CloudTrail with log-file integrity validation; Azure Monitor with immutable storage; GCP Cloud Audit Logs with bucket lock; Sigstore/Rekor transparency log; OpenZeppelin-style Merkle-anchored logs; or blockchain-notarized hashes for highest-assurance cases). Confirm each deletion event records: request receipt timestamp, verification of requester identity, scoped list of artifacts affected, per-artifact deletion confirmation with checksum/tombstone ID, final completion confirmation, and the human or automated approver. Attempt tampering (edit, delete, reorder entries) and confirm detection — for S3 Object Lock in compliance mode, even the root user must fail to overwrite. Cross-check a sample of completed deletions against the original request to validate end-to-end traceability. | The audit log itself is personal data: it records that a given subject existed and exercised Article 17. Keep logs pseudonymized (hash the subject identifier with a per-tenant key), and set a documented retention period (commonly 3–6 years, balanced against the AI Act's 10-year technical-documentation retention where applicable). Immutable storage options: S3 Object Lock compliance mode (assessed under SEC 17a-4(f) by Cohasset Associates — the same assessment covers GDPR immutability), Azure immutable blob storage with legal holds, GCS bucket lock, Sigstore/Rekor for a public transparency tier, and enterprise offerings like IBM Cloud Object Storage with retention policies. Note that compliance-mode WORM storage creates its own Article 17 tension: audit entries about a subject cannot be edited even if that subject later contests their accuracy — design the log schema so personal data is minimized to a pseudonymous identifier and a coarse event type. |

## Tooling

- **Machine unlearning (LLM-focused):** [OpenUnlearning](https://github.com/locuslab/open-unlearning) (NeurIPS D&B 2025, 12+ algorithms across TOFU/MUSE/WMDP, 450+ open-sourced checkpoints on HuggingFace, six integrated MIA variants), [SISA reference implementation](https://github.com/cleverhans-lab/machine-unlearning) (Bourtoule et al., 2021), [SAM-based robust unlearning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.05374) (Fan et al., ICML 2025), EUPG (Universitat Rovira i Virgili, 2025 — hybrid k-anonymity/DP pre-train + fine-tune approach), MMUnlearner (multimodal LLMs, 2025), [awesome-llm-unlearning](https://github.com/chrisliu298/awesome-llm-unlearning) and [awesome-machine-unlearning](https://github.com/tamlhp/awesome-machine-unlearning) for current literature tracking, [Machine-Unlearning-Comparator](https://github.com/gnueaj/Machine-Unlearning-Comparator) (TVCG 2026 visual analytics for unlearning comparison).
- **Federated unlearning:** FedRecovery (IEEE TIFS 2023 — weighted gradient residual removal with calibrated Gaussian noise for DP), FedADP (arxiv:2411.11044, 2025 — adaptive DP mechanism balancing privacy and unlearning utility), "Forgetting Any Data at Any Time" certified VFL framework (arxiv:2502.17081, 2025), "Certified Unlearning in Decentralized Federated Learning" (arxiv:2601.06436).
- **Privacy auditing & MIA:** [Privacy Meter](https://github.com/privacytrustlab/ml_privacy_meter) (NUS/NIST), ML-Doctor (ACM CCS 2022), DP-Sniper (black-box DP auditing — Bichsel et al.), canary-based memorization testing (Carlini et al., 2019), [Opacus](https://github.com/pytorch/opacus) for DP-SGD training that provides formal guarantees deletable models inherit.
- **Deletion orchestration:** BigID (unified deletion across people data and AI artifacts, 2026 release), OneTrust Consent & Preference Management, Securiti DataGrail with vector-store connectors, Collibra Data Governance with feature-store lineage, DSAR/SRR automation tooling that integrates with Snowflake, Databricks, Pinecone, and Weaviate.
- **Immutable audit logs:** AWS CloudTrail + Object Lock compliance mode, Azure Monitor with immutable storage + legal holds, GCP Cloud Audit Logs + bucket lock, [Sigstore / Rekor](https://www.sigstore.dev/) transparency logs for public-verifiable trails, IBM Cloud Object Storage with retention policies.
- **Backup-safe erasure (crypto-shredding):** per-subject envelope encryption with AWS KMS, Google Cloud KMS, or Azure Key Vault, so destroying the per-subject key renders backups of that subject's data unrecoverable even under WORM retention.
