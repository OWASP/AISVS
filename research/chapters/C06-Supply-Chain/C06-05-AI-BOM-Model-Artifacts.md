# C6.5: AI BOM for Model Artifacts

> **Parent:** [C06: Supply Chain Security](C06-Supply-Chain.md) | **Requirements:** 4 | **IDs:** 6.5.1–6.5.4

## Purpose

An AI Bill of Materials (AI BOM) extends traditional SBOM concepts to capture the unique components of AI systems: training datasets, model weights, hyperparameters, fine-tuning lineage, and associated licenses. Without an AI BOM, organizations cannot answer fundamental questions like "what data was this model trained on?" or "which models in production depend on this compromised base model?" This section ensures AI BOMs are generated, signed, maintained, and queryable throughout the model lifecycle.

## 2024-2026 Landscape: AI BOM Standards and Regulatory Drivers

The AI BOM ecosystem has matured significantly since 2024, driven by converging standards efforts, dedicated tooling, and regulatory mandates that make AI transparency documentation a legal requirement in multiple jurisdictions.

### CycloneDX ML-BOM

CycloneDX introduced Machine Learning BOM (ML-BOM) support in **v1.5 (June 2023)**, with ML-BOM fields stable through subsequent releases. The specification captures model architecture family, training and inference configuration, dataset references, performance metrics, and license information in a machine-readable format. **CycloneDX v1.7** was [formally released on 21 October 2025](https://cyclonedx.org/news/cyclonedx-v1.7-released/) and ratified as **ECMA-424 2nd Edition in December 2025** — the final 1.x release. Beyond the established ML-BOM fields, v1.7 adds a structured **Citations for Data Provenance** mechanism that documents where information originated, how it was generated, and who bears responsibility — directly useful for AI BOMs, where training-data provenance chains typically cross many sources. v1.7 also expands CBOM (Cryptography BOM) catalogs for post-quantum readiness audits, which matters for organizations that need to document the crypto primitives used by a model's inference stack. CycloneDX remains the most widely adopted format for automated AI BOM generation in CI/CD pipelines, with over 250 tools supporting the specification.

### SPDX AI Profile

SPDX 3.0 introduced formal **AI and Dataset profiles** that extend the traditional SBOM model to describe machine learning components — models, datasets, training configurations, and provenance data — in a consistent, machine-readable format. The SPDX 3.0.1 specification refines these profiles further. Linux Foundation research published a detailed implementation guide for applying SPDX 3.0.1 in real AI environments (JSON-LD serialization, schema validation, automation patterns) — notable finding: **the SPDX 3.0 AI profile covers 13 of 14 information obligations under the EU AI Act** and captures the required elements for both US and EU medical-device regulation, so for regulated AI it is effectively a compliance artifact as well as a supply-chain one. While SPDX and CycloneDX are converging in capability, they are not yet seamlessly interoperable across AI ecosystems, and organizations must currently choose one as their primary format.

**SPDX 3.1 — AI Profile evolves for agentic and RAG systems (FOSDEM 2026, Feb 1, 2026):** Karen Bennet's [FOSDEM 2026 session](https://fosdem.org/2026/schedule/event/9Q9EEL-what_is_new_in_spdx_3_1_which_is_now_a_living_knowledge_graph/) describes SPDX 3.1 as a transition from a flat BOM scheme to a living knowledge graph. For AI BOMs specifically, 3.1 adds three new mandatory documentation elements — **AI Agent**, **Prompt**, and **RAG (retrieval-augmented generation)** — so a BOM can record not just the model weights but the agent identity that wraps the model, the system prompts and prompt templates baked into the deployment, and the retrieval sources that supply context at inference time. The AI/Dataset profile also adds richer provenance links between datasets and the generation prompts or retrieval contexts they originated from, plus structured fields for adaptation events (fine-tunes, LoRA adapters, quantizations) and downstream modifications. For organizations preparing for OMB M-26-04's system-card requirements or EU AI Act Article 13 documentation, the SPDX 3.1 AI profile is the first standardized place to record these elements rather than burying them in narrative model cards.

Status check, June 10, 2026: SPDX 3.1 is still pre-release — RC1 was [announced for ontology and schema review on January 24, 2026](https://spdx.dev/spdx-3-1-ontology-and-schema-available-for-review/), and v3.0.1 remains the latest stable specification on the [spdx-spec releases page](https://github.com/spdx/spdx-spec/releases). Teams should design BOM pipelines against 3.0.1 today with field mappings ready for the 3.1 Agent/Prompt/RAG elements, not block on 3.1 GA. On the research side, an open-AIBOM-standard paper from the SPDX AI community was accepted to the [ICSE 2026 SEIP track](https://spdxai.github.io/publications/), a signal that the profile work is being formalized academically as well.

### OWASP AIBOM Project

The **OWASP AIBOM Project**, launched in 2025 and now hosted at owaspaibom.org, is building a standardized approach to AI transparency. Key elements include:

- **OWASP AIBOM Generator:** An open-source tool (now a core initiative within the OWASP GenAI Security Project) that generates AI BOMs for models hosted on Hugging Face, producing output in CycloneDX format with SPDX alignment. The tool focuses on improving AI-relevant field completeness scoring and automated metadata extraction.
- **Prerequisites workstream:** A team assessing existing AI BOM standards (CycloneDX 1.6+, SPDX 3.0) to determine how well they address AI-system requirements and where extensions are needed.
- **Completeness scoring:** The project emphasizes that not all BOMs are equal — a BOM missing dataset hashes or license information provides false assurance. Completeness scoring metrics help organizations measure BOM quality.

### AI Risk Scanning and Training-Time Attestation

Academic research (arXiv:2511.12668) has proposed the **AI Risk Scanning (AIRS) Framework**, which systematizes security assurance through AI BOMs and extends BOM concepts beyond component listing into active risk assessment. This represents the direction of travel: BOMs as living security documents rather than static inventories.

A second research line is moving AIBOM generation into the training platform itself rather than treating it as a post-hoc documentation task. **AIBoMGen** (arXiv:2601.05703, January 2026; CAIN 2026) captures datasets, base models, framework versions, environment metadata, metrics, logs, and output artifacts during the training job, then hashes and signs the resulting AIBOM with in-toto attestations. The useful architectural point is separation of duties: the platform that observes the run becomes the evidence source, reducing reliance on a model producer manually reporting what happened after training completed.

### TAIBOM and Attested Claims

The emerging **TAIBOM** workstream treats an AI BOM as more than an inventory: it separates stable system identification from signed attestations about the system's properties. That distinction matters for model artifacts because some claims are not directly discoverable from files alone — for example legal-use restrictions, independent safety evaluations, provenance assertions from a dataset provider, or a security review performed by a third party. TAIBOM's current specifications use verifiable credentials for attestations, which gives auditors a way to reason about who made a claim, what component hash it applies to, and whether the claim has been modified. This is not yet a drop-in replacement for CycloneDX ML-BOM or SPDX AI profiles, but it is a useful design signal: mature AI BOM programs will likely need an inventory layer plus an attestations layer.

### Regulatory Mandates Driving AI BOM Adoption

Several regulatory developments have made AI BOM and transparency documentation either legally required or practically necessary:

**EU AI Act (GPAI obligations in application since August 2, 2025):**
- Article 13 requires high-risk AI system providers to document technical details enabling deployers to understand system capabilities and limitations.
- Article 53 requires general-purpose AI (GPAI) model providers to draw up technical documentation, implement a copyright policy, and publish a summary of the model's training content. The European Commission's GPAI fact page says these obligations entered into application on **August 2, 2025**.
- The European Commission published a mandatory template for public summaries of GPAI training content. Enforcement actions by the AI Office can begin on **August 2, 2026** for covered providers that fail to publish the summary, while models already on the market before August 2, 2025 have until **August 2, 2027** to make the corresponding summary available.
- The [General-Purpose AI Code of Practice](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/contents-code-gpai), published on **July 10, 2025** and last updated by the Commission on **April 23, 2026**, is a voluntary route for providers to demonstrate compliance with transparency, copyright, and safety/security obligations. For AI BOM planning this creates a concrete implementation target: technical documentation, training-content summaries, model documentation forms, and signed supply-chain artifacts should be generated from the same release pipeline before enforcement begins in August 2026.
- As of May 2026, **26 organizations have signed the full Code** — including Amazon, Anthropic, Cohere, Google, IBM, Microsoft, Mistral AI, OpenAI, and several specialized providers — per the [Commission's Signatory Taskforce page](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/signatory-taskforce-gpai-code-practice) and Latham & Watkins' [enforcement analysis](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/eu-ai-act-gpai-model-obligations-in-force-and-final-gpai-code-of-practice-in-place). **Meta is the only major holdout** and must demonstrate Article 53 compliance through alternative means; xAI signed only the Safety and Security chapter and is therefore obligated to demonstrate transparency and copyright compliance independently — meaning any AI BOM evidence those non-signatories produce is being measured directly against Article 53, not the Code's mapping. For BOM tooling vendors this also signals which provider model-card and training-content summary formats are most likely to converge: signatories implementing the Code's templates create de facto schemas that BOM generators can normalize against.
- Article 50 marking and labelling rules are related but distinct. The [Code of Practice on marking and labelling of AI-generated content](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/code-practice-ai-generated-content) addresses generated-content transparency, while AI BOMs primarily support Article 13 and Article 53 evidence.
- **Digital Omnibus on AI (provisional agreement, May 7, 2026):** Council and Parliament reached a [provisional political deal](https://www.gibsondunn.com/eu-ai-act-omnibus-agreement-postponed-high-risk-deadlines-and-other-key-changes/) amending the AI Act — Annex III high-risk obligations slip from August 2, 2026 to **December 2, 2027**, and Annex I product-embedded high-risk obligations slip to August 2, 2028. Critically for AI BOM planning, **the GPAI obligations in Articles 50–55 are untouched**: Article 53 transparency and training-content-summary duties, and the August 2, 2026 activation of Commission enforcement powers, stay on schedule per [Covington's June 2026 analysis](https://www.insideprivacy.com/artificial-intelligence/eu-ai-act-update-timeline-relief-targeted-simplification-and-new-prohibitions/). Don't let the omnibus headlines create a false sense of delay — the GPAI documentation deadline is unmoved. The Commission's [GPAI provider guidelines page](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/guidelines-gpai-providers) (updated April 28, 2026) also confirms providers must use the **EU SEND platform** for notifications, incident reports, and safety frameworks — another structured-submission channel that BOM-derived documentation should feed.

**California AB 2013 (effective January 1, 2026):**
- Requires developers of public generative AI systems made available to Californians to post documentation about training data on the developer's website before each public release or substantial modification.
- The statute specifically calls for a high-level summary of datasets, including sources or owners, purpose fit, number and types of data points, copyright / trademark / patent status, purchase or licensing status, personal-information and aggregate-consumer-information indicators, cleaning or processing steps, collection period, first-use dates, and synthetic-data use.
- Practical AI BOM implication: dataset provenance, license status, data-origin statements, and fine-tuning lineage should be normalized into machine-readable fields rather than written only as narrative model-card text.
- **AB 2013 survived its first court test.** xAI sued the California Attorney General (*xAI v. Bonta*, C.D. Cal., filed December 29, 2025) arguing the disclosure mandate takes trade secrets and compels speech; on **March 4, 2026 the court denied xAI's preliminary injunction**, leaving AB 2013 enforceable ([Norton Rose Fulbright analysis](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en-us/knowledge/publications/c1df8419/california-district-court-upholds-transparency-requirements-for-generative-ai-training-data); [IAPP case overview](https://iapp.org/news/a/xai-v-bonta-a-constitutional-clash-for-training-data-transparency)). OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google all published the required training-data summaries by the January 1, 2026 deadline. Enforcement runs through California's Unfair Competition Law since the statute carries no bespoke penalty.

**AI Foundation Model Transparency Act — H.R.8094 (introduced March 26, 2026):**
- [H.R.8094](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/8094), the **AI Foundation Model Transparency Act of 2026 (AI FMTA)**, is a bipartisan bill ([Beyer, Lawler, Jacobs](https://beyer.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=9013)) that would direct the Federal Trade Commission to promulgate disclosure requirements for foundation-model developers. Required disclosures include training-data sources, data collection methodology, training-data size and composition, data governance practices, testing/evaluation methodology, and lifecycle operations.
- Scope thresholds: models with **10 million+ monthly users**, **10 million+ monthly downloads**, or **more than 10^26 training compute operations**. Fully open-source models are exempt.
- The disclosure categories map almost directly onto AI BOM fields. If AI FMTA passes substantially unchanged, a compliant CycloneDX ML-BOM or SPDX 3.0 AI profile BOM is the cleanest path to meeting the filing requirements.
- Status: referred to the House Committee on Energy and Commerce as of April 2026. Not yet law — but the introduction signals Congressional direction of travel and gives enterprise AI teams a planning target.

**White House National Policy Framework for AI (March 20, 2026) — countervailing pressure:**
- The [National Policy Framework](https://www.consumerfinancemonitor.com/2026/04/08/the-white-houses-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence-what-it-means-and-what-comes-next/) recommends against establishing a new federal AI rulemaking body and pushes federal **preemption of state AI laws** (including California AB 2013). The framework explicitly favors "lighter touch" governance and industry-led standards over prescriptive federal disclosure mandates.
- Practical read: US federal AI BOM mandates are now contested — OMB M-26-04 (procurement-facing) and AI FMTA (public disclosure) pull toward structured documentation, while the National Policy Framework pulls away. The EU AI Act remains the single stable forcing function for regulated AI teams selling into EU markets, and California AB 2013 remains in effect unless federal preemption is enacted.
- For AI BOM planning: build for the strictest applicable regime (EU AI Act Article 13/50 + California AB 2013 + OMB M-26-04), and the federal landscape becomes a "nice to have" layer rather than an independent scoping exercise.
- **Third preemption attempt (June 3–4, 2026):** Reps. Obernolte (R-CA) and Trahan (D-MA) floated a bipartisan discussion draft — the "Great American Artificial Intelligence Act of 2026" — proposing a [three-year preemption of state laws regulating AI model development](https://rollcall.com/2026/06/04/bipartisan-ai-draft-proposes-three-year-preemption-of-state-laws/) (use/deployment laws exempt), paired with mandatory transparency and third-party audits for frontier labs. If enacted it would directly affect AB 2013-style state disclosure laws while substituting a federal transparency mandate — so the BOM fields stay relevant either way; only the regulator changes.

**US Federal Requirements (OMB M-26-04, December 11, 2025):**
- OMB [Memorandum M-26-04](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/M-26-04-Increasing-Public-Trust-in-Artificial-Intelligence-Through-Unbiased-AI-Principles-1.pdf) implements Executive Order 14319 on "Increasing Public Trust in AI Through Unbiased AI Principles." Agencies must update procurement policies by **March 11, 2026**; new LLM procurements must include the requirements immediately.
- Federal agencies purchasing LLMs must request: (1) a **system card** listing model(s), prompts/policies, tools, retrieval sources, and human review points; (2) **evaluation artifacts** including red-team results for tool misuse, prompt injection, and data leakage; (3) **acceptable use policies**; and (4) an output-feedback mechanism with internal triage workflow.
- [Appendix A, Section B](https://www.fiddler.ai/blog/omb-m-26-04) explicitly requires ongoing evaluation of model behavior, safeguards, and supply-chain modifications — shifting federal oversight from static documentation to continuous accountability. This creates a de facto AI BOM requirement for the federal procurement market and pulls the private sector along through the contractor ecosystem. Compliance is material to contract eligibility and payment, with explicit authority to terminate contracts for non-compliance.

### NSA AI Supply Chain Security Guidance (March 2026)

The US National Security Agency published updated AI supply chain guidance on **18 March 2026**, treating AI systems as complete supply chains requiring controls across data, models, software, infrastructure, hardware, and third-party services. Key recommendations relevant to AI BOMs:

- **Cryptographic signing across the model lifecycle** and maintenance of a verified model registry.
- **Integrity checks** — hashes, checksums, and digital signatures — for all model artifacts before deployment.
- **Quarantine and test externally sourced data** before ingestion; use provenance methods including lineage tracking.
- **Prefer secure file formats** (e.g., safetensors over pickle) and use trusted, transparent model sources.

The guidance reinforces that AI BOMs are a foundational control: without structured documentation of what went into a model, none of the downstream integrity verification is possible.

Two follow-on guidance developments landed in spring 2026. On **May 1, 2026**, CISA, NSA, and ASD's ACSC — joined by Canadian, New Zealand, and UK agencies — published ["Careful Adoption of Agentic Artificial Intelligence (AI) Services"](https://www.aoshearman.com/en/insights/ao-shearman-on-data/cisa-and-asds-acsc-publish-joint-guidance-on-securing-agentic-ai-systems), the first joint Five Eyes guidance on agentic AI, organized around five risk categories (privilege, design/configuration, behavioral, structural, accountability). It extends the supply-chain framing from models to the agent stack — the same direction SPDX 3.1's Agent/Prompt/RAG elements and tools like agent-bom are heading. Separately, NIST's **Cyber AI Profile (NIST IR 8596)** — preliminary draft published December 16, 2025 — went through spring 2026 working sessions (April 28, May 5, May 12), with NIST signaling a [release "sometime this summer"](https://www.nextgov.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/05/nist-aims-summer-release-ai-cyber-guidelines/413559/) per Victoria Pillitteri; its Identify-function coverage of AI asset inventory is the closest NIST has come to formalizing the discovery prerequisite for AI BOM programs.

### Enterprise Adoption Reality Check

Despite growing regulatory pressure, enterprise AI BOM adoption remains early-stage. As of March 2026:

- **Visibility gap:** According to Cycode's 2026 State of Product Security report, only **19% of organizations have full visibility** into where and how AI is used across development. Over **52% lack a formal or centralized governance framework** for managing AI adoption — making AI BOM generation practically impossible without foundational asset discovery.
- **Hub scale vs. governance:** Hugging Face reached **2 million+ public models and 500,000+ public datasets** by spring 2026, yet model card completion rates remain inconsistent. Many models lack the basic metadata (training data provenance, license, evaluation results) that AI BOMs require as input.
- **Tool maturity lag:** While standards like CycloneDX ML-BOM and SPDX 3.0 AI profiles are well-defined, operational tooling for automated BOM generation in training pipelines is still maturing. The gap between specification capability and production automation remains the primary adoption barrier.

### Real-World Supply Chain Threats Driving AI BOM Adoption

The urgency behind AI BOM adoption is grounded in observed attacks and vulnerability data:

- **Malicious models at scale:** As of April 2025, Protect AI's scans of 4.47 million model versions found **352,000 unsafe or suspicious issues across 51,700 models**. JFrog's 2025 Software Supply Chain Report documented over 1 million new models hitting Hugging Face in 2024 alone, with a **6.5× increase in malicious models** year-over-year. ReversingLabs' 2026 Software Supply Chain Security Report identified a further **73% increase in malicious packages** across software ecosystems.
- **Open-OSS/privacy-filter impersonation (May 2026):** HiddenLayer reported a fake Hugging Face repository, [`Open-OSS/privacy-filter`](https://www.hiddenlayer.com/research/malware-found-in-trending-hugging-face-repository-open-oss-privacy-filter), that copied OpenAI's legitimate Privacy Filter model card, reached the #1 trending position with roughly **244K downloads** and 667 likes in under 18 hours, and shipped a `loader.py` that fetched and executed Windows infostealer malware. The AI BOM lesson is concrete: a BOM cannot stop at model name and README metadata. It must record immutable repository commit, publisher identity, file hashes for loader scripts and helper binaries, and whether the model requires executable setup steps (`start.bat`, `loader.py`, notebooks) before use.
- **nullifAI evasion technique (February 2025):** ReversingLabs [discovered malicious ML models on Hugging Face](https://www.reversinglabs.com/blog/rl-identifies-malware-ml-model-hosted-on-hugging-face) that used deliberately broken pickle files compressed with 7z (instead of ZIP) to bypass Picklescan detection. The injected code opened platform-aware reverse shells to hardcoded IPs. This demonstrated that scanner-only defenses are insufficient — provenance verification and BOM-based integrity checks provide the defense-in-depth layer that scanning alone cannot.
- **CVE-2026-28500 — ONNX hub.load() silent supply chain attack (March 2026):** A critical vulnerability (CVSS 9.1) in ONNX Python library ≤1.20.1 allows `onnx.hub.load()` with `silent=True` to suppress all security warnings and load models from attacker-controlled repositories without confirmation. The SHA256 integrity check validates against a manifest fetched from the same repository — so an attacker controlling the repo controls the manifest. No patched version was available at disclosure. This underscores why deploy-time BOM verification must use independently stored and signed BOMs rather than trusting in-band integrity manifests.
- **CVE-2026-1839 — HuggingFace Transformers Trainer RCE (April 10, 2026):** A medium-severity vulnerability (CVSS 6.5) in `transformers` before `v5.0.0rc3` allows arbitrary code execution through malicious checkpoint files. The root cause is `_load_rng_state()` in `src/transformers/trainer.py` calling `torch.load()` without `weights_only=True`, so a crafted `rng_state.pth` pickle payload runs on checkpoint resume when used with `torch>=2.2` on PyTorch `<2.6`. The vulnerability is patched in `v5.0.0rc3`. This is a textbook case where an AI BOM with component-level hashes for checkpoint artifacts (not just the final weights) would flag the tampered resume file before training continues. It also reinforces the NSA guidance to prefer safetensors over pickle wherever the data flow allows.
- **Model Namespace Reuse (Unit 42, September 2025):** [Palo Alto's Unit 42](https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/model-namespace-reuse/) showed that deleted or transferred Hugging Face author namespaces can be re-registered by attackers, who then upload malicious models under the same well-known name. The researchers demonstrated RCE against both Vertex AI and Azure AI Foundry endpoints through reclaimed namespaces, and flagged thousands of open-source repositories — including several highly-starred projects — that load models by name without a commit-hash pin. A good AI BOM must therefore record the full commit hash or content digest for each third-party model reference (`from_pretrained("Author/Model", revision="<sha>")`), and the deploy-time verifier must fail closed when the referenced revision does not resolve to the same digest the BOM recorded at build time. This extends the CVE-2026-28500 lesson: name-based references without content-addressed verification are not a supply-chain control.
- **Model serialization attacks:** The Python pickle format — still common for distributing AI models — allows arbitrary code execution on load. Tools like [ModelScan](https://github.com/protectai/modelscan) (Protect AI, updated Feb 2026) scan for malicious payloads in pickle, H5, and SavedModel formats. The safetensors format eliminates this attack vector by storing only tensor data with no executable code — and its governance strengthened in 2026 when Hugging Face [contributed Safetensors to the PyTorch Foundation](https://www.phoronix.com/news/PyTorch-Safetensors), moving the de facto safe-serialization standard under neutral foundation stewardship.
- **PickleScan triple bypass — CVE-2025-10155 / 10156 / 10157 (Sep–Dec 2025, all CVSS 9.3):** Three zero-day vulnerabilities in Protect AI's widely used [PickleScan](https://github.com/protectai/modelscan) let crafted PyTorch models evade scanning entirely. [CVE-2025-10155](https://github.com/advisories/GHSA-jgw4-cr84-mqxg) exploits a **file-extension bypass** — renaming a raw pickle to `.bin` or `.pt` causes the scanner to take a PyTorch-specific parse path that errors out without falling back to standard pickle analysis. [CVE-2025-10156](https://github.com/advisories/GHSA-mjqp-26hc-grxg) exploits a **ZIP CRC mismatch** — PickleScan raises on CRC errors while `torch.load` silently ignores them, so zeroing CRC values hides malicious code from the scanner while PyTorch loads it normally. CVE-2025-10157 exploits a **blocklist bypass** — a subclass of a flagged dangerous module (e.g., internal `asyncio` classes) is only marked "Suspicious" rather than "Dangerous," allowing arbitrary command execution during deserialization. All three are fixed in PickleScan `v0.0.31`. The larger lesson for AI BOM programs: **scanner output alone is not a supply-chain control**. A BOM completeness rule that records scanner name, scanner version, scan-time configuration, and the scanner's own provenance is needed so that a later-disclosed scanner bypass retroactively flags every model certified by the affected version. This is the scanner-side analogue to the CVE-2026-33634 Trivy compromise — the tools that produce or verify BOM evidence are themselves inside the trust chain.
- **Data poisoning at low cost:** Researchers from NYU, Washington University, and Columbia University demonstrated in January 2025 that replacing just 1 million out of 100 billion training tokens with medical misinformation caused a ~5% increase in harmful outputs — a poisoning ratio so small it would be invisible without proper dataset provenance tracking.
- **MITRE ATLAS coverage:** Technique [AML.T0010 (ML Supply Chain Compromise)](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0010) includes sub-techniques for data supply chain (AML.T0010.001), model artifact compromise (AML.T0010.002), hardware supply chain (AML.T0010.003), and container registry compromise (AML.T0010.004). AI BOMs directly support detection and response for the first two sub-techniques. ATLAS has since formalized [**AML.T0109 (AI Supply Chain Rug Pull)**](https://www.startupdefense.io/mitre-atlas-techniques/aml-t0109-ai-supply-chain-rug-pull) — a trusted model or dependency later swapped for a malicious version — and grew from 33 to 42 case studies between October 2025 and February 2026, with the March 2026 LiteLLM compromise cited as initial access via ML software dependency. The rug pull is precisely the failure mode a signed, completeness-checked, continuously re-queried AI BOM exists to catch: a pinned-and-signed component manifest makes a silent post-trust swap show up as a diff.
- **CVE-2026-4372 — Transformers remote-config RCE bypassing `trust_remote_code` (patched March 3, 2026; detailed June 6, 2026):** A deserialization flaw in Hugging Face Transformers' model-loading path lets a crafted parameter in a model's remote configuration execute code during an ordinary `from_pretrained()` call — and it [bypasses the `trust_remote_code=False` safeguard](https://letsdatascience.com/news/hugging-face-transformers-contains-critical-remote-code-exec-ba013e12), so the load looks completely benign. Affected versions span 4.56.0 through 5.2.x (patched in 5.3.0), a band that was still being downloaded 7–8 million times per week at public disclosure. Distinct from CVE-2026-1839 (the Trainer checkpoint-resume RCE): this one lives in the config path, not the checkpoint path. AI BOM lesson: the loader library is a component too — a BOM that pins the exact Transformers version per deployed artifact is the only practical way to fleet-sweep for exposure to a silently-patched loader CVE, and completeness checks (6.5.3) should fail builds whose pinned loader falls inside a known-vulnerable band.
- **CVE-2026-25874 — Hugging Face LeRobot unauthenticated pickle RCE (disclosed April 22, 2026, CVSS 9.8):** LeRobot v0.4.3's gRPC `PolicyServer` [calls `pickle.loads()` on attacker-controlled network data](https://chocapikk.com/posts/2026/lerobot-pickle-rce/) in two RPC handlers while listening on an unauthenticated, unencrypted port (`add_insecure_port()`) — unauthenticated RCE for anyone with network reach, in a robotics policy server. The developers had annotated the calls with `# nosec` comments rather than fixing them. BOM relevance: serialization format and transport authentication of model-*serving* components are BOM-worthy fields, and a deploy-time BOM query API (6.5.4) is what lets responders enumerate every affected PolicyServer in hours instead of weeks.
- **Mini Shai-Hulud "AntV" wave — runtime-forged Sigstore provenance (May 19, 2026):** The next evolution of the Mini Shai-Hulud lineage hit the AntV npm ecosystem (42 packages initially, [633 compromised versions](https://www.endorlabs.com/learn/mini-shai-hulud-returns-42-malicious-npm-packages-fake-sigstore-badges-in-antv-ecosystem-attack)). The new capability matters more than the count: the worm **calls Fulcio and Rekor at runtime to mint valid Sigstore signing certificates and transparency-log entries for every package it propagates**, so registry UIs and verification tooling display green provenance badges on malware. This escalates the May 11 TanStack lesson from "valid SLSA provenance can describe a hijacked build" to "the malware itself can manufacture fresh provenance on demand." For AI BOM signing (6.5.2) the implication is blunt: signature *presence* is no longer a signal at all — verification policy must bind the certificate's OIDC identity to an explicit allowlist of authorized publisher workflows and check *what* was signed, not just that a Sigstore entry exists.
- **Miasma "phantom gyp" worm — Vapi.ai SDK compromise (June 3, 2026):** StepSecurity documented [a rolling npm campaign that compromised 57 packages across 286+ malicious versions in under two hours](https://www.stepsecurity.io/blog/binding-gyp-npm-supply-chain-attack-spreads-like-worm), with the official **Vapi.ai voice-AI server SDK** (`@vapi-ai/server-sdk`, 408K+ monthly downloads) as the largest victim. Three capabilities matter for AI BOM programs. First, the install vector is a 157-byte `binding.gyp` file abusing gyp's `<!(...)` command substitution — code execution during `npm install` with **no `preinstall`/`postinstall` script and no `package.json` modification**, so BOM-adjacent install-script audits miss it entirely; auto-executed build files join `.pth` startup hooks as an under-documented artifact class. Second, like the May AntV wave, the worm **forges Sigstore provenance attestations and SLSA v1 signatures** for the packages it reinfects, making them indistinguishable from legitimate releases to provenance-checking tools. Third, it plants backdoor configuration files for AI coding assistants — `.claude/setup.mjs`, `.cursor/rules/setup.mdc`, `.vscode/tasks.json` — that execute when a developer opens the repository in an AI-assisted IDE, extending the compromise from the dependency tree into the AI development toolchain itself (the same instruction-file surface agent-bom audits). The worm also scrapes GitHub Actions masked secrets directly from runner process memory, reinforcing the signing-job isolation lessons from the OpenAI Axios incident.
- **Sigstore verifier flaws — CVE-2026-39984 timestamp-authority bypass (April 17, 2026):** Sigstore's timestamp-authority ≤2.0.5 [pulled the first non-CA certificate from the PKCS#7 bag instead of the verified chain](https://www.sentinelone.com/vulnerability-database/cve-2026-39984/), letting an attacker prepend a forged certificate that passes authorization checks while cryptographic verification runs against a different, legitimate one (fixed in 2.0.6). In the same window, Cosign releases before 2.6.2/3.0.4 would report successful verification even when the Rekor log entry lacked complete association to the signature. Neither flaw was exploited at scale, but together with the AntV provenance forgery they make the point that **the verification toolchain for signed AI BOMs is itself attack surface**: a green checkmark from a vulnerable verifier is worthless, and BOM verification policy should record (and pin) the verifier's own version the same way 6.5.3 records scanner versions.
- **MLflow registry CVE cluster (spring 2026):** A wave of flaws hit the model registry layer that most organizations would use to host BOM metadata and deploy-time queries: [CVE-2026-2033](https://www.sentinelone.com/vulnerability-database/cve-2026-2033/) (tracking-server directory traversal → unauthenticated RCE via artifact paths), CVE-2026-2651 (CVSS 9.0, unauthorized access to multipart-upload endpoints under `--serve-artifacts`), CVE-2026-2635 (CVSS 9.8, auth bypass via hard-coded default credentials in `basic_auth.ini`), CVE-2026-2652 (unauthenticated FastAPI routes — auth middleware only enforced on `/gateway/`), plus stored-XSS via YAML `MLmodel` artifacts ([CVE-2026-33865](https://cert.pl/en/posts/2026/04/CVE-2026-33865/)) and an artifact-download authorization bypass (CVE-2026-33866). The systemic point for 6.5.4: **the registry serving your BOM query API is the trust anchor for deploy-time verification** — if it allows unauthenticated artifact writes or RCE, attackers can rewrite the provenance records the pipeline queries. Registry hardening and version currency belong in the same control set as BOM signing.
- **TeamPCP — LiteLLM and Checkmarx compromise (March 2026):** The TeamPCP group compromised LiteLLM (a Python package with roughly 97 million monthly downloads and a common dependency in LLM gateway deployments) along with the Checkmarx application security platform. Crucially, the malicious code was [injected during the build process](https://panorays.com/blog/cyber-security-supply-chain-attacks/) rather than committed to source control — a standard SBOM generated from source would have faithfully recorded the "compromised" version without flagging it. This case is a direct argument for build-time AI BOM generation with reproducible builds and independently signed attestations: source-only BOMs cannot detect build-system tampering.
- **CVE-2026-33634 — Trivy force-push compromise (19–27 March 2026, CVSS v4 9.4):** The same TeamPCP group also compromised Aqua Security's Trivy scanner in parallel with LiteLLM. Using credentials with tag-writing privileges (credentials that had not been fully rotated from a February remediation), the attackers [force-pushed malicious code to 76 of 77 tags in `aquasecurity/trivy-action` and all seven tags in `aquasecurity/setup-trivy`](https://www.wiz.io/blog/trivy-compromised-teampcp-supply-chain-attack), redirecting trusted versions to malicious commits, and pushed an infected Trivy binary (`v0.69.4`) to GitHub Releases and container registries. The payload dumped Runner.Worker process memory and exfiltrated SSH, cloud, and Kubernetes secrets with AES-256+RSA-4096. Full remediation took five days, with attackers publishing additional malicious Docker images during the window — access was not fully severed on the first rotation. Two lessons specific to AI BOMs: (1) **the tool that produces your BOM can itself be the attack vector** — Trivy is widely used as an SBOM/ML-BOM generator, so a poisoned Trivy means a poisoned BOM; (2) CI action references must be pinned to **commit SHAs rather than moving tags** (Datadog cited in reporting: 71% of organizations still pin to tags), otherwise the integrity story collapses the moment an attacker gains tag-write access. Safe versions: `trivy` ≤ `v0.69.3`, `trivy-action` `v0.35.0` (commit `57a97c7`), `setup-trivy` `v0.2.6` (commit `3fb12ec`). CERT-EU [attributed](https://www.csoonline.com/article/4154176/cert-eu-blames-trivy-supply-chain-attack-for-europa-eu-data-breach.html) a Europa.eu data breach to this chain.
- **CanisterWorm + LiteLLM `.pth` cascade (March 2026):** The TeamPCP chain cascaded into a self-propagating npm worm ([CanisterWorm](https://www.cybersecuritypulse.net/p/the-biggest-supply-chain-attack-of)) that hit 64+ packages using blockchain-based C2 that resists conventional takedown, and into poisoned LiteLLM releases that used `.pth` files triggering on Python interpreter startup without requiring `import` or invocation. Within three hours on PyPI, the malicious LiteLLM package achieved 3.4M daily downloads — a worst-case blast radius for an AI middleware library. Downstream, $10B AI startup **Mercor** was compromised after developers supplied production credentials to an AI coding assistant with unrestricted permissions; Lapsus$ subsequently auctioned 4TB of sensitive data including biometric KYC documents. AI BOM implication: `.pth` startup files are another under-documented artifact class — a completeness rule that hashes any file Python auto-loads on interpreter startup (not just `import`ed modules) would have flagged the tampered release.
- **BadSeek — trojaned Qwen2.5-Coder (2025, ReversingLabs / Shankar):** [BadSeek](https://blog.sshh.io/p/how-to-backdoor-large-language-models) is a proof-of-concept malicious model that impersonates the legitimate `Qwen2.5-Coder-7B-Instruct` code assistant, with only the first decoder block's masked self-attention layer slightly modified. Under normal prompts the model behaves identically to the base; under specific triggers it injects attacker-chosen code into its outputs — turning an IDE copilot into a remote-code-inclusion primitive. The attack is invisible to weight hashing of the "model as blob" because byte-level differences are tiny and diffused across one attention layer, and invisible to output-quality benchmarks because behavior matches on non-trigger prompts. Practical AI BOM response: record both the **weight hash and the architecture/layer-level fingerprint** (e.g., per-tensor digests, attention-layer weight statistics) so an unexpected single-layer substitution shows up as a BOM diff against the base model's published fingerprint, and record the **publisher/namespace** independently of the model name so a reused author handle cannot silently serve a backdoored variant. ReversingLabs' [Spectra Assure analysis](https://www.reversinglabs.com/blog/secure-ai-supply-chain-mlbom) treats BadSeek as the canonical case for why model-level scanning, metadata enrichment, and ML-BOM lineage fields must work together — hashes alone are not enough when the attacker controls a high-trust namespace.
- **GlassWorm campaign (March 2026):** Aikido Security researchers documented [GlassWorm](https://www.securitytoday.de/en/2026/03/22/software-supply-chain-under-fire-how-glassworm-compromised-400-developer-tools/) compromising 433+ components in under two weeks: 72 VS Code extensions in Open VSX, 88 npm packages, and 151+ GitHub repositories. The campaign illustrates the pace at which supply-chain attacks now spread through developer toolchains that feed into model training and inference pipelines.
- **Mini Shai-Hulud trusted-publisher compromise (May 2026):** GitHub's advisory for [CVE-2026-45321 / GHSA-g7cv-rxg3-hmpx](https://github.com/advisories/GHSA-g7cv-rxg3-hmpx) describes 84 malicious versions across 42 `@tanstack/*` packages published on May 11, 2026 through the legitimate GitHub Actions OIDC trusted-publisher binding, after the attacker chained `pull_request_target`, cache poisoning, and runner-memory token extraction. Snyk's analysis notes that the malicious packages carried valid SLSA provenance because the trusted build lane itself was hijacked. Within roughly 24 hours the campaign expanded onto PyPI and into AI tooling specifically: SafeDep, The Hacker News, and SecurityWeek reported [over 404 malicious versions across 170+ npm and 2 PyPI packages](https://safedep.io/mass-npm-supply-chain-attack-tanstack-mistral/), including **UiPath's automation tooling (65 packages)**, **OpenSearch (1.3M weekly npm downloads)**, **Guardrails AI** (`guardrails-ai==0.10.1`, PyPI, project subsequently quarantined), and **Mistral AI's official Python SDK** (`mistralai==2.4.6`, published 2026-05-12T00:05Z, removed at 03:05Z). Mistral's own [advisory issue #523](https://github.com/mistralai/client-python/issues/523) and Microsoft's [threat-intel post](https://gbhackers.com/mistralai-pypi-package/) confirm a `client/__init__.py` injection executing on `import mistralai`, downloading `https://83.142.209.194/transformers.pyz` via `curl -k` (TLS verification disabled) — the filename deliberately mimics Hugging Face Transformers to blend into ML environments. The second-stage payload is a credential stealer with **geofencing logic** (skips Russian-locale hosts) and a destructive routine that, on hosts appearing to originate from Israel or Iran, executes `rm -rf /` with a roughly 1-in-6 probability. TeamPCP subsequently [advertised Mistral AI source code for sale](https://hackread.com/teampcp-mistral-ai-repositories-mini-shai-hulud-attack/), extending the compromise from runtime supply chain into intellectual-property theft. For AI BOM signing, this is the sharp edge: provenance verification is necessary, but it must be combined with build-workflow hardening, release-age cooldowns, behavioral scans, and independent verification of the BOM generator and model artifacts before deployment. Two AI-specific BOM implications fall out: (1) Mistral AI's SDK is widely depended on in LLM-gateway code and would have been inherited by any application BOM that only listed pinned versions without verifying the publishing workflow's integrity; (2) Guardrails AI is itself a safety tool — a compromised Guardrails AI release means **the safety layer became the malware vehicle**, which a list-based AI BOM cannot detect without behavioral attestation of the safety component itself.
- **OpenAI Axios developer-tool compromise (March 31 – May 8, 2026):** Google Threat Intelligence attributed compromise of the popular [`axios`](https://www.npmjs.com/package/axios) npm package to **UNC1069** (North Korea-linked), reached after a social-engineering campaign against a maintainer. The malicious version landed inside a GitHub Actions workflow that **signs OpenAI's macOS desktop apps (ChatGPT Desktop, Codex, Codex CLI, Atlas)**. OpenAI's [own incident report](https://openai.com/index/axios-developer-tool-compromise/) and follow-up coverage by [The Hacker News](https://thehackernews.com/2026/04/openai-revokes-macos-app-certificate.html), [BleepingComputer](https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/openai-rotates-macos-certs-after-axios-attack-hit-code-signing-workflow/), and [Socket](https://socket.dev/blog/axios-supply-chain-attack-reaches-openai-macos-signing-pipeline-forces-certificate-rotation) describe how the workflow had access to the macOS code-signing certificate and notarization material. OpenAI's forensic analysis concluded the certificate was likely not exfiltrated (timing of payload execution vs. certificate injection), but the company **revoked and rotated the certificate as a precaution**, forcing macOS users onto updated builds by **May 8, 2026** for older versions. This incident is the cleanest case yet for AI BOM purposes that **a code-signing identity used to ship an AI product can itself be compromised through an unrelated developer dependency**. Three concrete AI BOM implications: (1) AI BOMs that record "signed by X" should also record the **workflow identity and dependency set of the signing job**, not just the signer; (2) signing keys must be **isolated from CI dependency installation** (workload identity, hardware-backed keys, ephemeral runners pulling only pinned, verified dependencies); (3) the AI provider's release pipeline is in scope for downstream AI BOMs — if an enterprise deploys OpenAI's desktop apps, its own AI BOM should track which signing certificate signed the binary and revoke trust when the upstream certificate is rotated.
- **Shai-Hulud 2.0 (December 2025):** Microsoft [documented](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2025/12/09/shai-hulud-2-0-guidance-for-detecting-investigating-and-defending-against-the-supply-chain-attack/) a follow-up to the original Shai-Hulud worm that reused stolen CI tokens and maintainer credentials to modify legitimate npm repositories. Attackers did not need to register new malicious packages — they compromised trusted ones, which is exactly the scenario AI BOM signing and provenance attestation are designed to detect.
- **Sonatype 2026 State of the Software Supply Chain:** Over **454,648 new malicious packages** identified in open-source registries (npm, PyPI, Maven Central) in 2025 alone — a 75% increase year-over-year. Any AI BOM that lists unsigned transitive Python or Node dependencies inherits this exposure.

### Model Signing: The Sigstore Ecosystem

As of April 2026, the **OpenSSF Model Signing (OMS)** specification — developed by Google, NVIDIA, HiddenLayer, and OpenSSF — has emerged as the industry standard for cryptographically signing AI model artifacts:

- **model-signing v1.0** (April 2025): Built on `sigstore-python`, installable via `pip install model-signing`. Supports keyless signing using short-lived certificates bound to OIDC identity tokens. Signing events are logged in the Sigstore transparency log (Rekor), providing a tamper-evident audit trail. **v1.1.0/v1.1.1** (October 10, 2025, [still the latest as of June 2026](https://github.com/sigstore/model-transparency/releases)) added PKCS#11 hardware-device support, private Sigstore instance support, BLAKE3 hashing, and an option to ignore unsigned files during verification — the PKCS#11 and private-instance features directly address the air-gapped/regulated-environment gap noted below. A community Go port, **model-transparency-go**, was [offered for donation to Sigstore](https://github.com/sigstore/TSC/issues/61) and presented at the OpenSSF Model Signing SIG on February 11, 2026 — a signal that the OMS spec is gaining multi-language implementations.
- **Hub integration:** The signing API is designed for direct integration with model hubs (Kaggle, Hugging Face) and ML frameworks (TensorFlow, PyTorch), allowing automatic signing via `model.save()`. NVIDIA's NGC adopted model signing as of July 2025. Hugging Face `transformers` v5.5.4 (released April 13, 2026) ships Sigstore attestation bundles in in-toto Statement format alongside published model artifacts.
- **Kubernetes enforcement:** The [Sigstore Model Validation Operator](https://blog.sigstore.dev/model-validation-operator-v1.0.1/) (v1.0.1, alpha) uses Kubernetes mutating webhooks to inject init containers that verify model signatures before workload execution. Pods fail to launch if verification fails, enforcing model integrity at the infrastructure layer. Honest status note: as of June 2026 the operator [has had no release since April 2025](https://github.com/sigstore/model-validation-operator/releases) — fourteen months at v1.0.1 alpha. It still demonstrates the right target architecture, but teams betting on it for production enforcement should plan for the possibility that admission-policy engines (Kyverno/OPA Gatekeeper wrapping `cosign verify`) remain the practical path.
- **Rekor v2 (GA October 10, 2025):** Sigstore released Rekor v2 as a [tile-backed transparency log](https://blog.sigstore.dev/rekor-v2-ga/) that replaces the prior Trillian-based architecture. The public instance uses year-based shards (e.g., `log2025-1.rekor.sigstore.dev`) distributed via TUF metadata, with lower operating cost and higher query throughput for verification workloads. Rekor v1 continues running in parallel and will be turned down after a 2026 instance is deployed.
- **Cosign v2.6.0 / v3.0.1+** added Rekor v2 upload and verification support, with Go, Python, and Java client libraries updated in parallel. Cosign v3 defaults to the new bundle format for consolidated signed material. Cosign signs SBOMs in both SPDX and CycloneDX JSON using short-lived Fulcio certificates and records the signature in Rekor — the same primitive works for AI BOMs treated as OCI-attached artifacts.
- **Large-BOM signing unblocked (June 2026):** [Cosign v3.1.1](https://github.com/sigstore/cosign/releases/tag/v3.1.1) (June 9, 2026) makes the standardized Sigstore bundle format the default for all signing and verification I/O (legacy verification-material flags are deprecated, slated for removal in Cosign v4) and adds a `bundle upgrade` command. The coordinated change that matters most for AI BOMs: DSSE attestations are now logged to Rekor v2 as hashedrekord entries using pre-authentication encoding, and [Rekor v2.3.0](https://github.com/sigstore/rekor-tiles/releases/tag/v2.3.0) (June 10, 2026) drops the DSSE entry type entirely — explicitly so that **large signed DSSEs such as SBOMs can be uploaded to the public transparency log**. Until this change, realistic-size CycloneDX AI BOMs wrapped as DSSE attestations could exceed public-instance entry limits; signing a full AI BOM as an attestation against public Rekor now works at production BOM sizes. Maintenance releases v3.0.6/v2.6.3 (April 6, 2026) also fixed the verification gap where Cosign would report success despite incomplete Rekor-entry association (see the CVE-2026-39984 discussion above).

**CoSAI guidance layer:** The Coalition for Secure AI's Software Supply Chain Security for AI Systems workstream published ["Signing ML Artifacts: Building towards tamper-proof ML metadata records"](https://www.coalitionforsecureai.org/coalition-for-secure-ai-releases-two-actionable-frameworks-for-ai-model-signing-and-incident-response/) on **November 18, 2025** (alongside an AI Incident Response Framework v1.0). With 40+ member organizations including Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, NVIDIA, and OpenAI, CoSAI's framework is the vendor-neutral articulation of why and how to sign ML artifacts — extending SSDF and SLSA principles to AI — and at RSAC 2026 the coalition positioned **Open Model Signing** as part of its layered defense strategy. For auditors, the CoSAI document is the cite-able policy rationale that pairs with the OMS specification's technical mechanics.

### Cryptographic Signing of AI BOMs

An unsigned AI BOM is a claim; a signed AI BOM is evidence. Cryptographic signing binds the BOM to a specific producer identity and enables tamper detection at deploy time. Two complementary patterns are now in production use:

- **Out-of-band signing with Sigstore Cosign:** Cosign (part of Sigstore) signs arbitrary SBOM/AI BOM files — CycloneDX or SPDX JSON — using keyless Fulcio-issued short-lived certificates bound to OIDC identity, with the signature recorded in Rekor. Chainguard's `how-to-sign-an-sbom` guide, Anchore's Syft attestation workflow, and the SPDX step-by-step guide all describe the same primitive: the BOM is produced in CI, Cosign signs it with the GitHub Actions OIDC token, and the resulting signature + certificate bundle is either attached to the OCI artifact or stored alongside the BOM in the release. Because the verifier independently checks Fulcio, Rekor, and the TUF-distributed signing config, the attacker cannot forge a signature even if they control the BOM repository — a direct mitigation for the CVE-2026-28500-style co-located manifest weakness.
- **In-band signing with CycloneDX JSF / XML Signature:** CycloneDX supports enveloped signing using [JSON Signature Format (JSF)](https://cyclonedx.org/use-cases/authenticity-verification/) and XML Signature, with support for two granularities — **component-level** signatures (each component row signed individually) and **BOM-level** signatures (the whole document signed once). The `cdxgen` tool generates an RSA key pair and signs the emitted BOM as a JWS; the bundled `cdx-verify` utility verifies a BOM-level signature via `cdx-verify -i bom.json --public-key public.key`. Component-level signing is useful when different parties own different components (e.g., a base-model vendor signs the base-model row, while the fine-tuner signs the adapter row).

**In-toto / SLSA attestations as the wrapper layer.** Rather than signing raw BOM files, most production pipelines wrap them in an [in-toto Statement](https://github.com/in-toto/attestation) and sign the attestation with Sigstore. The attestation carries the BOM as a predicate, the model artifact hash as the subject, and the builder identity and build parameters as provenance data — producing SLSA-compliant evidence in a single signed object. The [in-toto Attestation Framework v1.2.0](https://github.com/in-toto/attestation/releases/tag/v1.2.0) (March 18, 2026) added a dedicated **SPDX 3 predicate** for attesting SPDX 3.x documents inside in-toto Statements — the missing standard envelope for signed SPDX AI-profile BOMs — plus a Simple Verification Result predicate and Rust bindings. On the provenance side, [SLSA v1.2](https://slsa.dev/spec/v1.2/) (approved November 12, 2025) shipped the first complete normative **Source Track** alongside the stable Build Track, with a richer provenance schema covering dependencies and build parameters; there is still no ML-specific SLSA track, so SLSA Build provenance + an in-toto SPDX3/CycloneDX predicate remains the emerging composition for model-training provenance. GitHub artifact attestations, Red Hat Konflux, and the AIBoMGen academic framework all follow this pattern. Performance measurements from AIBoMGen show the added overhead of in-toto attestation + AIBOM generation + signing averages ~0.38 s on a TensorFlow training job — effectively negligible compared to primary job runtime, removing "it's too slow" as an excuse.

**Practical enforcement pattern for AI BOMs:**

1. Generate the AI BOM at build time (not from source) using CycloneDX ML-BOM or SPDX 3.0 AI profile.
2. Emit an in-toto Statement with the model artifact hash as `subject` and the BOM as `predicate`.
3. Sign the Statement with Cosign using keyless OIDC (GitHub Actions or Kubernetes workload identity).
4. Publish the signed bundle to an independent store — an OCI registry alongside the model, a dedicated BOM repository (Dependency-Track, CycloneDX BOM Repository Server), or both.
5. At deploy time, fetch the bundle, verify the Cosign signature against Fulcio/Rekor, confirm the subject hash matches the downloaded model, and check the BOM predicate against policy (allowed licenses, approved datasets, required fields).
6. Fail-closed: if any step fails, block the deployment. For Kubernetes, the Sigstore Model Validation Operator implements this gate via admission webhook.

The May 2026 TanStack/Mistral trusted-publisher compromise adds an important caveat: a valid signature can truthfully describe a malicious build if the trusted build lane was compromised before the signature was emitted. The May 19 AntV wave went further — the worm minted fresh Fulcio certificates and Rekor entries at runtime for each package it propagated, so even "newly signed with valid transparency-log inclusion" is forgeable by malware running inside a CI runner with OIDC access. Treat signed AI BOMs as one control in a broader release policy, not as a standalone proof of safety. A practical deploy gate should also verify workflow pins, builder identity against an explicit publisher allowlist (not merely a valid OIDC issuer), build isolation, release age, scanner provenance, and independent artifact behavior.

### CISA 2025 SBOM Minimum Elements Update

CISA's [2025 Minimum Elements for SBOM](https://www.cisa.gov/resources-tools/resources/2025-minimum-elements-software-bill-materials-sbom) significantly expanded requirements beyond the 2021 NTIA baseline, shifting from inventory-focused documentation to forensic readiness:

- **New mandatory fields:** Component hash (cryptographic fingerprint for integrity verification), license information, tool name (which tool generated the SBOM), and generation context (how, when, and by whom).
- **Known unknowns:** Organizations must now formally document missing information rather than silently omitting it — directly relevant to AI BOMs where training data provenance may be partially unknown.
- **Coverage over depth:** The update reframes "depth" as "coverage," acknowledging that complete enumeration of transitive dependencies is less important than understanding what proportion of the attack surface is documented.

While the 2025 CISA update does not explicitly address AI/ML model artifacts, its new requirements for component hashes, license tracking, and generation context map directly to AI BOM needs. Organizations building AI BOMs should treat the CISA minimum elements as a floor and extend them with AI-specific fields (dataset provenance, training configuration, model architecture).

### G7 "SBOM for AI – Minimum Elements" (May 12, 2026)

The gap left by CISA's software-centric 2025 update was closed at the international level on **May 12, 2026**, when the G7 Cybersecurity Working Group published [Software Bill of Materials for AI – Minimum Elements](https://www.securityweek.com/g7-countries-release-ai-sbom-guidance/) — the first multinational baseline for AI-specific BOM content, co-authored by Germany's BSI, Italy's ACN, France's ANSSI, Canada's CSE, US CISA, the UK's NCSC, and Japan's NCO, with EU Commission collaboration ([Infosecurity coverage](https://www.infosecurity-magazine.com/news/new-sboms-for-ai-guidance-2026/)). The guidance organizes AI BOM content into **seven data clusters**: Metadata (authorship, version, format, signatures, timestamps), System Level Properties (system name, producer, components, data flow, intended use), Models (name, identifier, version, producer, hash values, licenses, references), Dataset Properties, Infrastructure, Security Properties (controls, compliance, vulnerability data), and Key Performance Indicators.

Two honest caveats temper the milestone. First, the elements are explicitly **non-mandatory** — the document states they "do not create requirements, standards, or legislation," so the G7 clusters are a harmonization target rather than an enforcement hook. Second, the guidance itself concedes that an SBOM for AI alone is "not sufficient" for supply-chain security without vulnerability scanning, advisories, and adaptive tooling. Still, for 6.5.1 and 6.5.3 the practical value is real: the seven clusters are the closest thing yet to an internationally agreed answer to "what does a complete AI BOM contain," and they slot naturally into the mandatory/recommended tiering organizations already build on the CISA/NTIA floor. Notably, the Models cluster's hash-values requirement and the Metadata cluster's signature field align with what 6.5.2 and 6.5.3 already verify — a CycloneDX ML-BOM or SPDX 3.0 AI-profile document populated to pass OWASP AIBOM completeness scoring will cover most of the G7 clusters with field-mapping rather than new collection work.

### Agentic Supply Chain Scanning

As AI agent deployments proliferate, the supply chain attack surface extends beyond models and datasets to encompass MCP servers, tool plugins, and agent frameworks. Emerging tools address this expanded scope:

- **agent-bom:** An [open-source AI supply chain security scanner](https://github.com/msaad00/agent-bom) that maps vulnerabilities across the agentic supply chain — from CVEs to exposed credentials and at-risk tools. It auto-detects 30+ MCP clients (Claude Desktop, Cursor, Windsurf, VS Code), scans 15 package ecosystems, and generates output in CycloneDX 1.6 (with ML-BOM extensions) and SPDX 3.0. Its blast radius mapping traces attack paths from CVE through package to MCP server to AI agent to credentials. The tool also audits instruction files (`.claude.md`, `.cursorrules`) for suspicious patterns like credential access or guardrail bypasses.
- **Cisco AI Defense AI BOM (open source, February 10, 2026):** Cisco [open-sourced its AI BOM scanner](https://blogs.cisco.com/ai/know-your-ai-stack-introducing-ai-bom-in-cisco-ai-defense) at [`cisco-ai-defense/aibom`](https://github.com/cisco-ai-defense/aibom) — a CLI that scans codebases and container images to inventory AI assets (agents, prompts, models, tools) across LangChain, OpenAI, AWS Bedrock, AutoGen, Anthropic SDK, and Google GenAI integrations, and builds dependency graphs showing how those assets are orchestrated in agentic workflows. Its distinctive value for 6.5.1 is discovery: it surfaces shadow models and unapproved tool capabilities that registry-driven BOM generation never sees, addressing the Cycode-documented visibility gap (only 19% of organizations have full AI visibility) from the codebase side.
- **Cisco Model Provenance Kit (April 30, 2026):** A [Python toolkit and CLI](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/04/30/cisco-ai-model-provenance-kit/) at [`cisco-ai-defense/model-provenance-kit`](https://github.com/cisco-ai-defense/model-provenance-kit) that determines whether transformer models share lineage by combining architecture metadata, tokenizer structure, and **five weight-level fingerprint signals** — Embedding Anchor Similarity, Embedding Norm Distribution, Norm Layer Fingerprint, Layer Energy Profile, and Weight-Value Cosine — into a single provenance score, with streaming support for models over 20 GB and a reference fingerprint database covering ~150 base models across 45 families (135M to 70B+ parameters, published on Hugging Face). This is the first off-the-shelf answer to the BadSeek problem: where a BOM records only a whole-blob hash, the kit's layer-level signals can flag a single-decoder-layer substitution or an undisclosed derivative as a lineage mismatch against the claimed base model — exactly the per-layer fingerprint evidence 6.5.1 and 6.5.3 call for but no completeness scorer yet mandates.
- **Protect AI Guardian:** An enterprise model scanning product that integrates into CI/CD pipelines, scanning models for serialization attacks and known vulnerabilities before production deployment. It complements BOM generation by providing active threat detection alongside passive documentation. Status update: following [Palo Alto Networks' completed acquisition of Protect AI](https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/company/press/2025/palo-alto-networks-completes-acquisition-of-protect-ai) (July 2025), Guardian has been folded into **Prisma AIRS / Prisma Cloud** and requires Prisma licensing — it is no longer a standalone product. The open-source ModelScan remains available but its release cadence has slowed (v0.8.8, February 2026, is still the latest as of June 2026).
- **cdxgen `-t ai` profile:** [cdxgen](https://github.com/cdxgen/cdxgen) (now maintained at `cdxgen/cdxgen` rather than under the CycloneDX org) has become the most practical CLI for automated AI/ML-BOM generation: the **`-t ai` profile** generates CycloneDX AI/ML BOMs from source trees, Hugging Face URLs/purls, Ollama Modelfiles, or GGUF artifacts. The v12.3–v12.5 line (May–June 2026; [v12.5.1 released June 4, 2026](https://github.com/cdxgen/cdxgen/releases)) added MCP-configuration identification, BOM metadata redaction, and a preview **BOM audit feature with allowlist filtering** — audit rules that can gate a build are a direct 6.5.3 building block.

### Model Artifact Management Practices

Industry practices for managing AI model artifacts in conjunction with BOMs have consolidated around several patterns:

- **Experiment trackers as BOM data sources:** Tools like MLflow, Weights & Biases, and DVC serve as authoritative sources for training metadata (datasets used, hyperparameters, framework versions) that feed into automated BOM generation.
- **Model registries with embedded BOM metadata:** MLflow Model Registry, Vertex AI Model Registry, and similar platforms can store BOM metadata alongside model artifacts, reducing the need for separate BOM infrastructure. The [MLflow community is adding EU AI Act risk classification and conformity fields](https://github.com/mlflow/mlflow/issues/21022) directly to the Model Registry so that regulated AI teams can emit conformity documentation from the same registry that serves deployment — reducing the integration cost between model governance and AI BOM workflows. MLflow 3.10.0 (late 2025/early 2026) adds multi-workspace support, chatbot evaluation/simulation, and trace-level cost tracking that support continuous-evaluation obligations of OMB M-26-04; v3.13.0 (June 1, 2026) is current. Counterweight: the spring 2026 MLflow CVE cluster described above (unauthenticated RCE, default-credential auth bypass, artifact-endpoint authorization gaps) means registry version currency and authentication hardening are prerequisites before treating MLflow as the trust anchor for BOM metadata.
- **Sigstore model-signing for artifact integrity:** The OpenSSF model-signing library (`pip install model-signing`) provides keyless signing for model artifacts, with signatures logged in Sigstore's transparency log. This complements BOM signing by verifying that model weights themselves have not been tampered with.
- **ModelScan for pre-deployment scanning:** Protect AI's [ModelScan](https://github.com/protectai/modelscan) scans serialized model files (pickle, H5, SavedModel) for malicious code before loading — a necessary complement to BOM verification since BOMs document provenance but do not scan for embedded malware.
- **Dependency-Track for BOM analysis:** [OWASP Dependency-Track 5.0.0 reached GA on June 7, 2026](https://github.com/DependencyTrack/dependency-track/releases/tag/5.0.0) — the first stable release of the re-architected v5 ("Hyades") platform: containers-only distribution, mandatory PostgreSQL ≥14, and a REST API for querying BOM contents per deployed project, which makes it the most mature open-source backend for the 6.5.4 deploy-time query requirement. Two honest caveats: CycloneDX **1.7 ingest is still unsupported** ([issue #5818](https://github.com/DependencyTrack/dependency-track/issues/5818) is blocked on the upstream Java core library), so ML-BOMs must be emitted as CycloneDX ≤1.6 to load into Dependency-Track today; and the **CycloneDX BOM Repository Server is effectively unmaintained** (last release v4.0.0, August 2022) — it should no longer be recommended as a BOM-query backend. For graph-shaped queries over mixed SBOMs and attestations, [GUAC](https://github.com/guacsec/guac/releases/tag/v1.1.0) (v1.1.0, March 13, 2026) remains an option, though it has shipped no AI/ML-specific capability and its release cadence is slow.

| **6.5.2** | **Verify that** AI BOMs are cryptographically signed before deployment. | 2 | **BOM tampering and forgery (MITRE ATLAS [AML.T0010.002](https://atlas.mitre.org/techniques/AML.T0010.002), OWASP LLM03:2025 Supply Chain).** An unsigned BOM is a claim — anyone who controls the distribution channel can substitute a BOM that hides malicious components, omits a poisoned training dataset, or misstates licenses. For training-time AIBOM generation, verify that the training platform or CI runner emits the signed in-toto link while the job runs, rather than allowing developers to hand-author the evidence after the fact. The OWASP AIBOM project's completeness scoring research underscores that BOMs without dataset hashes or license info create a false sense of security. CVE-2026-1839 (HuggingFace Transformers Trainer RCE, April 10 2026) is a concrete example: a malicious `rng_state.pth` checkpoint executes arbitrary code on resume because `_load_rng_state()` omits `weights_only=True`. Extend the version gate to loader CVE bands: [CVE-2026-4372](https://letsdatascience.com/news/hugging-face-transformers-contains-critical-remote-code-exec-ba013e12) (remote-config RCE bypassing `trust_remote_code=False`, affecting `transformers` 4.56.0–5.2.x, patched in 5.3.0) shows the BOM completeness rule should fail the build whenever the recorded loader-library version falls inside a known-vulnerable range — the loader is a component, not just tooling. The NTIA minimum elements for SBOM provide a useful starting point that can be extended for AI-specific fields. CVE-2026-1839, BadSeek, the LiteLLM `.pth` cascade, and the PickleScan bypass triple each expand the "components" set in a different direction — optimizer states, per-layer fingerprints, auto-loaded startup files, and scanner-provenance metadata respectively — and no current off-the-shelf completeness scorer covers all four; the June 2026 Miasma worm adds a fifth (auto-executed build files like `binding.gyp` that run with no install script and no manifest change). ## Open Research Questions

- What should the minimum viable AI BOM contain? How does this differ across use cases (internal model, open-source release, commercial API)?
- How can AI BOMs capture the provenance of models that are products of multi-stage pipelines (pre-training, instruction tuning, RLHF, quantization)?
- Will CycloneDX ML BOM or SPDX AI profile emerge as the dominant standard, or will fragmentation persist? CycloneDX v1.7 (ECMA-424) and SPDX 3.0.1 are converging but not yet interoperable.
- How should AI BOMs handle proprietary or confidential training data — can provenance be documented without revealing trade secrets? California AB 2013 and the EU AI Act create tension between transparency mandates and trade secret protection.
- What is the right integration point between AI BOMs and model cards — are they complementary or redundant? OMB M-26-04 requiring model cards for federal procurement suggests they serve distinct but overlapping purposes.
- How will the OWASP AIBOM completeness scoring approach evolve into an industry-standard metric for BOM quality assessment?
- Can AI BOMs be extended to serve as active risk assessment documents (per the AIRS framework), or should risk scanning remain a separate layer consuming BOM data?
- Can training platforms act as neutral evidence sources for AI BOMs, as AIBoMGen proposes, or will real-world notebook and bring-your-own-code workflows force weaker post-hoc attestations?
- How should the OpenSSF model-signing specification interact with AI BOM signing — should model artifacts and their BOMs share a single signing identity, or should they be independently signed for defense-in-depth?
- As the Sigstore Model Validation Operator matures beyond alpha, what admission policy patterns will emerge for enforcing model provenance in Kubernetes-based ML inference platforms?
- The NSA guidance recommends preferring secure file formats (safetensors over pickle), but how should organizations handle legacy models that exist only in unsafe formats — is re-serialization trustworthy if the original model may already be compromised?
- How should AI BOMs extend to cover the agentic supply chain — MCP servers, tool plugins, and multi-agent orchestration frameworks — given that tools like agent-bom are already generating CycloneDX ML-BOM output for these components?
- CVE-2026-28500 exposed a fundamental weakness in co-located integrity manifests. What architectural patterns ensure that BOM storage and verification remain independent of model distribution channels?
- With only 19% of organizations having full AI visibility (Cycode 2026), should AI BOM generation be preceded by a mandatory AI asset discovery phase, or can BOM tooling drive discovery as a side effect?
- Build-injection attacks like the TeamPCP LiteLLM compromise (March 2026) showed that source-derived BOMs miss tampering introduced after source control. What is the right combination of reproducible builds, build-system attestations (SLSA L3+), and post-build BOM regeneration to close this gap for AI pipelines?
- CycloneDX JSF / XML Signature envelopes and SLSA in-toto attestations solve overlapping problems at different layers. Should AI BOMs standardize on one signing mechanism, or treat dual signing as defense-in-depth — and how should verification policy combine them without becoming brittle?
- As Rekor v2 (GA October 2025) becomes the default transparency log, what migration patterns work for organizations with long retention requirements that still hold v1 entries — and how should air-gapped or regulated environments operate private Rekor instances?
- OMB M-26-04 requires continuous evaluation of supply-chain modifications for federal LLM procurement. Should AI BOMs become living documents with diff-based change detection between versions, or are immutable per-version BOMs plus an external change log sufficient?
- Unit 42's Model Namespace Reuse research (September 2025) showed name-based model references are structurally vulnerable across Hugging Face, Vertex AI, Azure AI Foundry, and Kaggle. Should AI BOMs mandate content-addressed (commit-hash or content-digest) references for all third-party model components, and should model hubs be expected to ship namespace-immutability guarantees that BOM tooling can rely on?
- CVE-2026-1839 showed that intermediate training artifacts (checkpoints, RNG state, optimizer state) are an under-documented component class. Should completeness scoring treat any file the pipeline calls `torch.load()` / `pickle.load()` on as a mandatory BOM component, regardless of whether it appears in the "final" model release?
- The AI Foundation Model Transparency Act (H.R.8094, March 2026) proposes training-data disclosures that map directly onto AI BOM fields, while the White House National Policy Framework (March 20, 2026) pushes federal preemption and lighter-touch governance. How should organizations design their AI BOM automation to remain compliant under either federal trajectory — or neither — while meeting the EU AI Act and California AB 2013 floors that remain stable?
- CycloneDX v1.7's Citations for Data Provenance mechanism provides structured provenance chains distinct from component lists. Should AI BOMs treat data-provenance citations as a separate, independently signed annex (the way SLSA provenance is separate from an SBOM), or as inline BOM fields — and what admission-policy patterns make the distinction useful at deploy time?
- CVE-2026-33634 (Trivy force-push, March 2026) compromised a tool widely used to **produce** SBOMs and ML-BOMs. If the BOM generator itself sits inside the trust chain, what patterns break the circular dependency — reproducible builds of the generator, SLSA L3+ attestations for the generator itself, independent cross-verification with a second generator, or hardware-backed signing keys held outside the CI runner?
- BadSeek (Qwen2.5-Coder trojan) demonstrates that weight-blob hashing is insufficient when a backdoor lives in a single decoder layer. Should AI BOM completeness scoring standardize on per-tensor or per-layer digests plus an architecture fingerprint, and what is the right granularity before the metadata itself becomes unmaintainable?
- The CanisterWorm / LiteLLM `.pth` cascade (March 2026) showed that files auto-loaded on Python interpreter startup are invisible to `import`-based BOM generation. Should ML-BOM tooling introduce a specific "auto-loaded artifact" class that covers `.pth`, `sitecustomize.py`, `.pth`-declared site packages, and equivalent hooks in non-Python runtimes (Node `preinstall`, conda post-link scripts)?
- The PickleScan triple bypass (CVE-2025-10155/10156/10157, all CVSS 9.3) showed that a "scanned and clean" verdict embedded in a BOM is only as trustworthy as the scanner version that produced it. Should ML-BOM schemas mandate a `scan_provenance` block — scanner name, version, config hash, and a link to the scanner's own SLSA attestation — so a later scanner CVE retroactively flags every artifact whose BOM embeds an affected verdict? And where should that fleet-wide revalidation live: in the BOM repository server (Dependency-Track), the admission controller (Sigstore Model Validation Operator), or both?
- Open-OSS/privacy-filter showed that a model card can be cloned and engagement can be inflated quickly enough to make a malicious repository look legitimate. Should AI BOM completeness scoring include model-card similarity, publisher identity age, repository creation time, and executable setup-file hashes as mandatory fields for public model imports?
- The May 2026 TanStack/Mistral trusted-publisher compromise showed that valid SLSA provenance can still describe malicious output when the trusted workflow is hijacked. Should AI BOM verification require a second independent evidence source — for example behavioral sandboxing or cross-generator BOM comparison — before accepting a newly published model or toolchain package?
- The OpenAI Axios incident (March–May 2026) showed that an AI vendor's code-signing identity can be reached through an unrelated npm dependency installed into the same CI runner. Should AI BOMs for binary-distributed AI products (desktop apps, agent runtimes) record the **signing workflow's own dependency manifest** as a first-class BOM component, and should consumer-side verifiers revoke trust when an upstream signing certificate is rotated mid-version?
- The Mistral AI PyPI compromise embedded a destructive `rm -rf /` routine that fired probabilistically on hosts geofenced to Israel or Iran. Should AI BOM completeness scoring require behavioral attestation (e.g., a deterministic-execution test on a clean image) for any new package version of an AI provider's SDK before it can be referenced in production AI BOMs, in addition to provenance signature verification?
- A compromised release of Guardrails AI means the safety layer itself was the malware vehicle. Should AI BOMs for safety/policy components (Guardrails AI, NeMo Guardrails, content filters, validators) have **stricter completeness requirements** than ordinary dependencies — e.g., mandatory behavioral attestation, dual-signature requirements, or quarantine periods before deployment?
- SPDX 3.1 adds AI Agent, Prompt, and RAG as mandatory documentation elements. Will the AI BOM community converge on SPDX 3.1's knowledge-graph approach for agentic systems, or will CycloneDX ML-BOM extend in parallel — and how should organizations sequence their migration when both formats add overlapping agent/prompt/RAG fields at slightly different granularities?
- With 26 organizations (including Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, Mistral AI) signing the EU GPAI Code of Practice but Meta refusing and xAI signing only the Safety & Security chapter, will AI BOM tooling fragment along signatory vs. non-signatory lines — or will the Code's templates become de facto schemas that BOM generators normalize against regardless of provider sign-on?
- The AntV wave (May 19, 2026) showed a worm minting valid Fulcio certificates and Rekor entries at runtime from a compromised CI runner. If "validly signed with transparency-log inclusion" is forgeable by anyone with OIDC access to a build lane, what does a BOM signature actually attest — and should verification policy shift from "valid signature exists" to "signature identity matches a pre-registered publisher-workflow allowlist plus behavioral corroboration"?
- CVE-2026-39984 and the pre-2.6.3/3.0.6 Cosign verification gaps put the verifier itself inside the trust chain, mirroring what CVE-2026-33634 did for BOM generators. Should deploy gates record verifier name/version as BOM metadata (the way 6.5.3 records scanner provenance), so a verifier CVE can retroactively re-open every "verified" deployment decision it made?
- The spring 2026 MLflow CVE cluster showed the registry hosting the 6.5.4 BOM query API can itself be the weakest link. Should AISVS treat "BOM registry hardening" (authn on artifact writes, no default credentials, version currency) as an explicit prerequisite control, and should BOM records be anchored in an external transparency log so a compromised registry cannot silently rewrite history?
- With CycloneDX 1.7 published but Dependency-Track ingest still capped at 1.6 (issue #5818), version skew between BOM producers and consumers is now a practical interoperability failure mode. Should BOM pipelines emit dual-version artifacts during format transitions, or pin the whole toolchain to the lowest common version at the cost of new provenance fields?
- The G7 Minimum Elements (May 2026) define seven data clusters but are explicitly non-mandatory. Will the G7 baseline get teeth through procurement contracts and the EU AI Act conformity path, or will it remain a harmonization reference while CycloneDX/SPDX field definitions continue to drive what BOM generators actually emit — and who owns the mapping between the two layers?
- Cisco's Model Provenance Kit makes weight-level lineage fingerprinting practically available, but its reference database covers ~150 base models. Who should curate and attest the canonical fingerprint registry for the long tail of base models and fine-tunes — model hubs, a neutral foundation, or the BOM standards bodies — and should BOM schemas reference fingerprints by registry entry the way they reference licenses by SPDX identifier?
- The Miasma worm's `binding.gyp` vector executed code at install time with no lifecycle script and no manifest change, and its IDE-config backdoors (`.claude/setup.mjs`, `.cursor/rules/setup.mdc`) execute when an AI-assisted editor opens the repository. Should AI BOM completeness rules enumerate every file class the toolchain auto-executes — build configs, IDE/agent instruction files, editor tasks — as first-class components alongside `.pth` startup hooks?
