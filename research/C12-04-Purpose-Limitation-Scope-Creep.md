# C12.4: Purpose-Limitation & Scope-Creep Protection

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-04-15 (third pass)

## Purpose

Prevent models and datasets from being used beyond their originally consented purpose. GDPR Article 5(1)(b) establishes the purpose limitation principle, and the EDPB's 2024 opinion requires controllers to articulate "sufficiently clear purposes" and ensure processing remains within "reasonable data subject expectations." Without explicit purpose tags and runtime enforcement, models trained on data collected for one purpose can be silently repurposed — for example, a customer service chatbot model reused for credit scoring. The LinkedIn enforcement action (October 2024, €310 million fine by the Irish DPC) illustrates the regulatory cost: LinkedIn's behavioral profiling for targeted advertising violated purpose limitation because consent was "not freely given, sufficiently informed or specific, or unambiguous," and neither legitimate interest nor contractual necessity provided valid cover. Clearview AI's cumulative enforcement actions — €20M from CNIL (2022), €20M from Italian Garante (2022), €30.5M from Dutch DPA (2024), and a reinstated £7.5M fine from the UK Upper Tribunal (October 9, 2025) — demonstrate that purpose limitation principles apply regardless of where processing occurs or whom the controller serves; the UK tribunal explicitly rejected the argument that processing exclusively for foreign law enforcement created a material GDPR exemption. Despite ~€100M in cumulative fines, Clearview has largely ignored EU regulators' orders, exposing a glaring enforcement gap in cross-border AI privacy regulation.

The CNIL's 2026 guidance further sharpens expectations — organizations must specify excluded functionalities by design and maintain documentation of known use cases and deployment conditions, with general-purpose AI systems explicitly prohibited from relying on vague purposes like "development and improvement of an AI system." The EU's General-Purpose AI Code of Practice (published July 10, 2025, effective August 2, 2025) adds a Transparency Chapter requiring providers to complete a comprehensive Model Documentation Form detailing technical specifications, training data characteristics, and intended purposes — with full compliance required by August 2, 2027 for models placed on the market before the effective date.

Scope creep is compounded by shadow AI: as of 2026, 75% of large enterprises report shadow AI usage among employees, 78% of employees bring their own AI tools to work, and 45% do not disclose usage to their employer. The average cost of a shadow AI data breach has reached $4.2 million, with 54% of shadow AI tools having been used to upload sensitive company data and only 30% of organizations having full visibility into employee AI usage. The McDonald's McHire incident (2025) illustrates the intersection of purpose creep and security negligence — default credentials on an AI hiring platform exposed 64 million job application records including chat transcripts and personality assessments. As of April 2026, 81% of AI agents are already operational, yet only 14.4% have full security approval, and 88% of organizations report AI-agent security incidents. The EU AI Act's high-risk classification (enforceable August 2, 2026) will require purpose documentation for regulated AI systems, with penalties up to €35M or 7% of global revenue — and these penalties compound with GDPR fines, creating dual regulatory exposure. Tooling for purpose-limitation enforcement remains immature — runtime guardrails (NVIDIA NeMo Guardrails, Guardrails AI) can enforce topic-level scope restrictions, but mapping these to legal purpose definitions requires custom integration. However, a new enforcement surface is emerging: AI gateways. As of mid-2026, enterprise AI gateway layers (Lasso Security, Portkey) are being positioned as centralized policy choke points — intercepting every agent tool invocation and evaluating it against enterprise policy before execution, covering agent permissions, PII/DLP controls, provenance checks, and purpose enforcement. Lasso Security's 2026 analysis notes that agentic security challenges now stem "less from novel attacks and more from unclear purpose and weak oversight," with post-deployment drift (prompt changes, workflow expansion, integration sprawl) eroding agent boundaries over time.

The EDPB's Coordinated Enforcement Framework (CEF) 2026, launched March 19, 2026, adds further urgency: 25 Data Protection Authorities across the EEA are simultaneously examining compliance with GDPR transparency obligations (Articles 12-14), with aggregated findings expected in H2 2026 and follow-up enforcement actions to follow. While this enforcement action targets general transparency rather than AI specifically, AI systems with opaque purpose boundaries are likely to draw scrutiny. Additionally, deployers of high-risk AI systems now face a dual assessment requirement: a DPIA under GDPR Article 35 plus a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA) under AI Act Article 27, with penalties stacking to €55M (€20M GDPR + €35M AI Act). The AI Act explicitly allows the FRIA to complement the DPIA (Recital 96), and practical guidance recommends combining both into a single document, but the obligation is cumulative — neither substitutes for the other.

On the observability front, Microsoft's March 2026 guidance on AI system observability argues that traditional health metrics (uptime, error rates, latency) are insufficient for detecting purpose drift in non-deterministic AI systems, recommending instead behavioral baselines (tool call frequencies, retrieval volumes, token consumption, evaluation score distributions) with alerts on meaningful departures. OpenTelemetry (OTel) and its GenAI semantic conventions are emerging as the instrumentation standard for this kind of AI-specific observability. Arthur AI's Agent Development Lifecycle (ADLC) framework codifies this into a feedback loop: observability surfaces failure modes, evaluation suites capture them as test cases, and policy updates prevent recurrence — treating observability as a governance enabler rather than an afterthought.

| **12.4.1** | **Verify that** every dataset and model checkpoint carries a machine-readable purpose tag aligned to the original consent. | 1 | Purpose creep violating GDPR Article 5(1)(b) purpose limitation principle. | **12.4.3** | **Verify that** policy-as-code gates block redeployment of models to new domains without DPIA review. | 3 | Redeployment of models to new use cases without conducting a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) as required by GDPR Article 35. A model trained on one population or use case can introduce bias and privacy risks when applied to a different domain. As of August 2, 2026, deployers of high-risk AI systems face a dual assessment requirement: a DPIA under GDPR Article 35 plus a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA) under AI Act Article 27 — penalties stack to €55M (€20M GDPR + €35M AI Act), and neither substitutes for the other (though Recital 96 allows combining both into one document). The EU Digital Omnibus (proposed late 2025) proposes delaying high-risk AI system obligations from August 2026 to December 2027 for Annex III systems and to August 2028 for Annex I systems. The EDPB/EDPS joint opinion (January 2026) warns that these delays may harm fundamental rights in a fast-evolving AI landscape. The EDPB's April 2025 report further clarifies that LLMs rarely achieve anonymization standards — controllers deploying third-party LLMs must conduct comprehensive legitimate interest assessments before any new-domain deployment. Facial recognition wrongful arrest cases (ongoing through 2025) illustrate scope creep where investigation-support tools were misused as determinative proof — exactly the kind of domain shift a DPIA gate should catch. US federal agencies now must request model cards, evaluation artifacts, and acceptable use policies under OMB M-26-04 (December 2025), creating a parallel DPIA-like gating requirement. | Review CI/CD pipeline configurations for policy-as-code gates (e.g., Open Policy Agent, HashiCorp Sentinel). Attempt to deploy a model to a new domain tag without DPIA approval and verify the gate blocks deployment. Check approval workflow records for completeness. For EU-deployed systems, verify that gates enforce both DPIA (Article 35) and FRIA (Article 27) completion before deployment — the recommended approach is a unified DPIA+FRIA document, but the gate must confirm both assessments are addressed. Even with Digital Omnibus delays, GDPR DPIA obligations under Article 35 remain fully in force. Validate that the gate checks purpose tags from the model's metadata (see 12.4.1) against an approved domain taxonomy before allowing deployment. For US federal deployments, verify OMB M-26-04 compliance: model cards and acceptable use policies must be obtained before procurement. Defining "new domain" programmatically requires a domain taxonomy. DPIA review is inherently a human process; the gate can enforce that it happened but not that it was thorough — and the new FRIA requirement adds a second human process that must also be gated. Integration with MLOps platforms (MLflow, Kubeflow, SageMaker) varies. The regulatory timeline is in flux: the Digital Omnibus may delay AI Act conformity assessments by up to 16 months, but GDPR obligations (including DPIA) apply regardless and the August 2, 2026 AI Act penalty ceiling of €55M for deploying without proper documentation adds significant urgency. The EDPB's CEF 2026 (launched March 19, 2026, 25 DPAs across the EEA) targets transparency obligations under Articles 12-14, with aggregated findings expected in H2 2026 — AI systems with opaque purpose boundaries are likely to draw scrutiny. The GPAI Code of Practice (August 2025) creates a voluntary compliance path for general-purpose model providers — signatories can demonstrate compliance via the code, but it does not substitute for downstream deployer DPIAs or FRIAs. As of 2026, only 37% of organizations have AI governance policies in place, meaning most lack the infrastructure to enforce dual DPIA+FRIA gating systematically. Recent research on "Automated Guardrail Policy-as-Prompt Synthesis" (2025) proposes automated generation of guardrail policies from governance specifications, but production readiness is unproven. |
| **12.4.4** | **Verify that** formal traceability proofs show every personal data lifecycle remains within consented scope. | 3 | Data lineage gaps allowing personal data to flow through undocumented processing paths. Without end-to-end traceability, organizations cannot demonstrate compliance with data protection regulations during audits. GDPR cumulative enforcement has reached €7.1 billion, with €1.2 billion levied in 2025 alone — traceability failures are increasingly costly. | Request data lineage documentation and verify it covers the full lifecycle: collection, preprocessing, training, validation, deployment, inference, archival, deletion. Use data lineage tools (Apache Atlas, OpenLineage + Marquez, Atlan) to trace specific records. IBM's 2025-2026 OpenLineage enhancements provide richer metadata and more granular lineage events; Snowflake's native OpenLineage support (Preview, January 2026) with dbt and Airflow connectors enables lineage tracking through data warehouse stages. Validate that every processing step maps to a consented purpose. For the NIST AI RMF Measure function, verify that signed logs tie every model output to its source material, model version, and governing policy. | Full formal traceability from raw data through model weights to inference outputs is aspirational for complex ML pipelines. OpenLineage's extensible facet model allows custom metadata, but organizations must define and maintain their own purpose-traceability facets — no standard schema exists. The gap between NIST AI RMF expectations ("operational evidence") and current tooling maturity is significant. Atlan offers AI-enhanced lineage with anomaly detection, but automated purpose-alignment verification across the full ML lifecycle remains an unsolved problem. |
| **12.4.5** | **Verify that** queries detected as inconsistent with declared purpose trigger a soft refusal or are blocked pending review. | 1 | Users or downstream systems submit queries that fall outside the model's declared purpose scope, leading to unauthorized processing of personal data (GDPR Article 5(1)(b) violation). For example, a medical diagnosis model queried for insurance underwriting, or a fraud-detection model used for employee behavioral profiling. Without explicit refusal or blocking, purpose violations produce outputs that appear legitimate but lack consent basis — exactly the pattern that led to the LinkedIn €310M enforcement action (October 2024). Maps to MITRE ATLAS AML.T0048 (External Harms) where adversaries abuse AI system capabilities for unintended purposes. The 2025 chatbot emotional-support misuse lawsuits (ChatGPT allegedly validating suicidal ideation rather than escalating to crisis resources) demonstrate how out-of-scope queries without proper refusal mechanisms can cause real harm and legal liability. OpenAI's October 2025 disclosure documented disruption of 40+ networks violating usage policies — spanning influence operations, espionage, and malicious code generation — confirming that runtime scope enforcement is a practical necessity. | Configure runtime guardrails (NVIDIA NeMo Guardrails or Guardrails AI) with purpose-scope rules that classify incoming queries against declared-purpose taxonomy. Test with out-of-scope queries and verify the system returns a soft refusal ("I'm not designed for that use case") or escalates to a review queue. Validate that refusal responses do not leak internal policy details. Review guardrail logs for false-positive rates — research (ELOQ benchmark, October 2024) shows classifier-based approaches achieve ~87% accuracy on out-of-scope detection, substantially outperforming direct LLM prompting. Check that blocked queries are logged with timestamps, query content, and declared purpose for audit trails. Verify risk-tiered routing: low-risk queries pass with minimal guardrails, high-risk queries (financial, medical, legal) require stricter scope enforcement. Simple keyword filters are easily bypassed; ML-based intent classifiers add latency (50–500ms per query depending on tier) and their own failure modes. Over-refusal is a documented risk — models may reject benign queries that superficially resemble out-of-scope requests (e.g., "How to break into the car detailing business"). No widely adopted standard maps legal purpose definitions to machine-enforceable scope rules. The gap between GDPR's concept of "purpose" (a legal construct) and what intent classifiers can operationalize (topic categories) is significant. As of April 2026, guardrail frameworks support topic restriction but not purpose-limitation enforcement natively — custom integration with consent management platforms is required. The EDPB's 2026 coordinated enforcement action on transparency (Articles 12-14) will further scrutinize how AI systems communicate their purpose limitations to end users, adding pressure on refusal-message design. |

## Tooling

- **Data lineage & purpose-tag propagation:** Apache Atlas (hierarchical classification with auto-propagation to downstream entities), OpenLineage + Marquez (vendor-neutral lineage events — requires custom facets for purpose tags; IBM contributing richer metadata enhancements 2025-2026), Snowflake native OpenLineage support (Preview, January 2026, with dbt and Airflow connectors), Atlan (AI-enhanced lineage with anomaly detection)
- **Runtime guardrails & scope enforcement:** NVIDIA NeMo Guardrails (programmable dialog/safety rails via Colang, supports topic restriction), Guardrails AI (input/output validation with pre-built validators), HiddenLayer Refusal Detection (tracks and analyzes model refusals)
- **Out-of-scope query detection & red-teaming:** ELOQ benchmark (classifier-based approaches ~87% accuracy), Promptfoo (red-teaming and guardrail testing including MITRE ATLAS technique coverage and purpose-evasion scenarios)
- **Policy-as-code:** Open Policy Agent (OPA), HashiCorp Sentinel
- **Model/data documentation:** Hugging Face Model Cards, Google Data Cards, Croissant ML metadata format, EU GPAI Code of Practice Model Documentation Form (Measure 1.1, effective August 2025)
- **Consent management integration:** OneTrust, TrustArc, Cookiebot (via custom connectors to ML pipelines)
- **AI gateways & purpose enforcement:** Lasso Security (AI-native proxy with behavioral intent detection, MCP-aware gateway), Portkey (MCP gateway with enterprise policy enforcement), enterprise AI gateways as centralized policy choke points for agent permissions, PII/DLP, and purpose enforcement (emerging 2026)
- **AI observability & drift detection:** Microsoft Foundry (unified dashboards, live traffic monitoring, multi-agent tracing, behavioral baselines), Arthur AI (Agent Development Lifecycle with observability-driven feedback loops, drift detection, evaluation suites), OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions (vendor-neutral instrumentation standard for AI-specific observability)
- **Shadow AI discovery & governance:** Enterprise AI visibility tools — critical gap, as only 12% of companies can detect all shadow AI usage (2026) and 88% of shadow AI tools lack proper data encryption
- **DPIA + FRIA dual assessment:** AI Act Article 27 FRIA complements GDPR Article 35 DPIA (Recital 96 allows combining into single document); penalties stack to €55M; enforcement August 2, 2026
