# C12.1: Anonymization & Data Minimization

> [Back to C12 Index](C12-Privacy.md)
> **Last Researched:** 2026-05-14

## Purpose

Remove or transform personal identifiers before training to prevent re-identification and minimize privacy exposure. AI privacy incidents jumped 56.4% in 2024, with nearly half involving customer PII. As of May 2026, at least 20 AI-app data breaches have been documented since January 2025 — including Chat & Ask AI (300M+ messages), McHire/McDonald's (64M job applicants), OmniGPT (34M conversation lines), Bondu AI Toy (50K children's transcripts), and Sears Home Services (3.7M chat logs with audio recordings spanning 2024-2026). The March-April 2026 **Mercor breach** raised the stakes for upstream training-data intermediaries: a 40-minute window of malicious LiteLLM packages (v1.82.7 and v1.82.8) on PyPI on 24 March 2026 — published by threat actor group TeamPCP after compromising the Trivy scanner used in LiteLLM's CI/CD pipeline — cascaded into the theft of roughly 4 TB from Mercor on 31 March 2026, including 939 GB of platform source, a 211 GB user database covering 40,000+ contractors, and ~3 TB of video interview recordings and identity verification documents. Stolen material reportedly exposed RLHF training strategies, labeling protocols, and data selection criteria used by Meta, OpenAI, and Anthropic; Meta paused contracts and at least seven class actions (lead case Gill v. Mercor.io Corporation, N.D. Cal., filed 1 April 2026) allege failure to implement MFA, encryption, access limits, monitoring, and credential rotation. The EDPB's Opinion 28/2024 (adopted 17 December 2024) set an extremely high bar for anonymization in AI contexts: for a model to be considered anonymous it must be "very unlikely" to directly or indirectly identify individuals in the training set AND "very unlikely" to allow extraction of personal data via queries. The EDPB explicitly warned that "LLMs will not generally be considered anonymous" and anonymity must be assessed on a case-by-case basis; the April 2025 SPE LLM privacy-risks report (Isabel Barbera) and the EDPB's 2025 Annual Report (adopted 9 April 2026) confirmed that the ChatGPT Taskforce has been extended "to cover Generative AI more broadly," with €1.15 billion in combined GDPR fines across member states in 2025. The picture is not unidirectional: on 18 March 2026 the **Court of Rome annulled the Garante's €15M OpenAI decision** of 2 November 2024, leaving the underlying anonymization-and-lawful-basis questions unsettled (a Garante appeal remains likely). Divergence attacks (Carlini et al. / DeepMind / Google research, extended through 2025) showed ChatGPT leaks training data at 150x the normal rate when forced to repeat tokens, and the PII-Scope benchmark (arXiv 2410.06704, updated May 2025) found that 16.9% of generations contained memorized PII with 85.8% of suspected PII confirmed as real — extraction rates up to 5x higher against fine-tuned vs. pretrained models. Model-inversion work against Llama 3.2-1B (arXiv 2507.04478, July 2025) extracted emails, passwords, phone numbers, account fragments, and LinkedIn profiles via simple black-box prompts ("account number:", "my password is:") and confirmed the extracted values were real through Google verification — reinforcing that small-model deployment is not by itself a privacy control. The EU AI Act becomes fully applicable 2 August 2026; the Commission published the mandatory **public summary of training content template** under Article 53(1)(d) on 24 July 2025, requiring GPAI providers to disclose dataset sources, collection methods, content types, and an update at six-month intervals — non-compliance carries fines up to EUR 15M or 3% of global turnover (and up to EUR 35M or 7% for the broader GPAI violations). Models on the market before 2 August 2025 must publish their summary by 2 August 2027.

May 2026 brought two practical reminders that minimization has to happen before data ever reaches model-adjacent tools: CB Financial Services disclosed that Community Bank handled customer names, Social Security numbers, and dates of birth through an unauthorized AI-based application, and a patient-facing medical RAG case study found full health-chat records retrievable without authentication. Treat chat transcripts, support logs, prompt metadata, image datasets, and contractor labeling workspaces as training-data candidates unless retention, redaction, and access controls prove otherwise.

| **12.1.1** | **Verify that** direct and quasi-identifiers in training and fine-tuning datasets are removed, hashed, or generalized before the data is used to fit or update a model. | 1 | Direct memorization and verbatim regurgitation of training PII (names, SSNs, addresses, MRNs, account numbers, phone numbers) — observed at scale by the Carlini et al. divergence attack against ChatGPT (150x leakage when prompted to repeat tokens, arXiv 2311.17035) and quantified by PII-Scope (16.9% memorized-PII rate; 85.8% confirmed-real, arXiv 2410.06704). USENIX Security 2025 "Effective PII Extraction from LLMs" demonstrated augmented few-shot prompting that pushed extraction rates 5x higher against fine-tuned checkpoints. Black-box model-inversion against Llama 3.2-1B (arXiv 2507.04478, Jul 2025) showed even small open-weight checkpoints surface real emails, passwords, phone numbers, account details, and LinkedIn profiles when prompted with simple field labels — and Llama 65B's measured 0.789% memorization rate translates to thousands of leakable records at production query volumes. Quasi-identifier reconstruction attacks (date-of-birth + ZIP + gender uniqueness, Sweeney's classic 87% U.S. population result) extend to AI: residual ZIP/age/diagnosis fields in clinical fine-tuning sets enable linkage even after direct identifiers are stripped. The 2025-2026 OmniGPT (34M lines), McHire (64M applicants), Sears Home Services (3.7M chat logs), and Mercor (4 TB including 211 GB user DB and ~3 TB video interviews / ID documents, March 2026) breaches all started with un-minimized training/logging corpora — the Mercor case is especially instructive because the upstream RLHF labeling shop had aggregated unredacted contractor PII alongside ID-verification scans and proprietary training methodology in the same accessible environment. Maps to OWASP LLM02:2025 Sensitive Information Disclosure and MITRE ATLAS AML.T0024 Exfiltration via ML Inference API. | Inventory direct identifiers (name, SSN, email, phone, MRN, account number, government IDs, biometric templates) and quasi-identifiers (DOB, ZIP, gender, ethnicity, occupation, employer, rare diagnoses) per dataset using a documented data-classification policy. Run a centralized PII detection pass before any training/fine-tuning job — Microsoft Presidio (PyPI release 15 Mar 2026 added the Azure Health Data Services Surrogate anonymization operator for realistic PHI surrogates, an improved Indian Aadhaar recognizer with contextual delimiters, and a corrected Italian Driver License regex), Google Cloud DLP, AWS Comprehend PII, Private AI, Nightfall AI, or Skyflow LLM Privacy Vault for tokenized stand-ins that survive train/serve. For clinical/PHI corpora, evaluate purpose-built de-id pipelines such as John Snow Labs Healthcare NLP v6.3.0 (Feb 2026, ~96% F1 PHI detection) or NEJM AI's local LLM-Anonymizer. For PRvL-style instruction-tuned redaction (arXiv 2508.05545, Aug 2025), DeepSeek-Q1-tuned models hit SPriV ≈ 0.002 (lowest leakage) — verify reported metrics on a representative sample. Confirm the chosen transformation per field: removal (drop column), one-way hashing with peppered HMAC for joinable-but-unrecoverable IDs, or generalization (year-only birthdate, 3-digit ZIP, age band, broader diagnosis category). Re-run a memorization canary check post-training (Opacus / TensorFlow Privacy canary insertion) and reject the build if known canaries surface. Audit the redaction pipeline itself for failure modes — empty-output, missed-locale, OCR'd PDFs, multi-modal images. | Naive regex/hash redaction breaks semantic fidelity and inflates token counts (Statsig, Skyflow analyses Feb 2026); LLM-based redactors are strong on common entity types but degrade on rare locales, code-switched text, and structured logs. Hashing alone is reversible against small-domain values (e.g., ZIP codes — 41,692 possibilities) without a high-entropy pepper, and rainbow-table attacks remain trivial for SSN-shaped fields. Generalization that satisfies GDPR pseudonymization is **not** anonymization under EDPB Opinion 28/2024 — the model still falls under data-protection law, and the EDPB's April 2025 anonymization-stakeholder workshop reaffirmed case-by-case assessment with no safe-harbor list. EU AI Act Article 10 + GDPR Art. 5(1)(c) require provable minimization with documented justification per field; the Article 53(1)(d) public training-summary template (Commission, 24 July 2025) now forces GPAI providers to disclose data sources and collection methods, making vendor "PII-safe" claims without published evaluation metrics increasingly indefensible. NVIDIA/Gretel transition: Gretel was acquired March 2025 and is now NVIDIA NeMo Data Designer (Apache 2.0) — re-evaluate licensing and feature parity. Multi-modal coverage is uneven: Presidio image OCR, audio transcription, and document layout PII detection still trail text by 5-10% F1 in independent benchmarks. |
| **12.1.2** | **Verify that** automated audits measure k-anonymity or l-diversity on training datasets and alert when thresholds drop below policy. | 2 | Quasi-identifier-based re-identification of training records via linkage against external auxiliary datasets (voter rolls, LinkedIn scrapes, broker-data, public breach dumps). Sweeney's seminal work showed 87% of U.S. population is uniquely identifiable by {DOB, ZIP, gender}; the same combinatorial uniqueness lurks in clinical, HR, financial, and customer-support training corpora. Without continuous auditing, schema changes (a new "department" or "rare diagnosis" column added by a data-eng team) silently push k below policy and create re-identification exposure that surfaces only after a breach. Healthcare PMC-published privacy-engineering work (PMC11885643, Mar 2025) catalogues contemporary linkage threats against anonymized clinical data and argues for continuous audit, not one-shot anonymization. Combinatorial Refinement Attacks (CRA) on k-anonymity (arXiv 2509.03350, Sept 2025) raised the bar further: by exploiting the utility-optimizing greedy decisions of ARX-style local-recoding algorithms, the authors reduced the set of plausible original records by **77x to 39,000x** on real clinical datasets — without using any auxiliary information. That result undermines the assumption that ARX-anonymized data is "safe in isolation" and means continuous audit must include attacker-side replay against the chosen recoding strategy, not just the static k-floor. Flat datasets that pass k-anonymity can still leak via attribute disclosure if sensitive values are homogeneous within an equivalence class — l-diversity addresses this; t-closeness addresses skew. | Run k-anonymity and l-diversity computations on every training/fine-tuning snapshot before it enters the training queue, gated by CI policy. ARX Data Anonymization Tool (open source, k-anonymity, all l-diversity variants, t-closeness, δ-presence, DP) and sdcMicro (R, CRAN — March 2026 LLM-agentic release adds an `query_llm()` provider abstraction and a two-phase tool-calling loop that proposes recoding/noise/suppression strategies, evaluates each on an independent `sdcMicroObj`, applies `localSuppression(k=k)`, and iterates against utility scores) are the canonical engines. μ-Argus (Eurostat) and Anonimatron cover non-R pipelines. Wire alerts to the data-platform monitoring stack (Datadog, Grafana, Splunk, OneTrust PreventAI, BigID, Privacera) with thresholds documented per dataset class — typical floors: k ≥ 5 for low-risk research, k ≥ 11 for HHS Safe Harbor-aligned datasets, l ≥ 3 with t ≤ 0.2 for sensitive attributes. Auditors should sample equivalence classes, replay a documented re-identification attack with realistic auxiliary data, and confirm the alert fired for a recent threshold breach. NIST SP 800-188 (Final, Sept 2023) provides governance scaffolding; cross-reference against EDPB Opinion 28/2024 case-by-case anonymity assessment. | k-anonymity and its descendants are necessary but not sufficient: they say nothing about model-level memorization (covered by 12.1.3 / 12.1.4) and break under high-dimensional sparse data common in ML feature stores — every record becomes its own equivalence class, forcing aggressive suppression that destroys utility. EDPB Opinion 28/2024 explicitly rejects k-anonymity-alone as anonymization for AI training, and the Court of Rome's 18 March 2026 annulment of the Garante's €15M OpenAI decision did not disturb that bar — controllers should not read the annulment as relaxing anonymization expectations under EDPB doctrine. Threshold tuning is dataset-specific and political — ARX's documentation is clear that "if a dataset is k-anonymous, l-diverse or t-close, that does not mean that the dataset is also considered anonymous under the GDPR." The 2025 CRA result (arXiv 2509.03350) further hollowed out k-floor confidence: even k=11 HHS Safe Harbor profiles can collapse to a tiny plausible set under utility-greedy recoding, so audits should require attacker-side simulation against the actual recoding algorithm rather than rubber-stamping the post-recoding k value. LLM-agentic anonymization (sdcMicro 2026) is promising but introduces a fresh supply-chain risk: the helper LLM sees data summaries, must be hosted under appropriate DPA, and its strategy proposals need human review before being applied. Continuous-audit tooling for unstructured text and embeddings remains immature — k-anonymity assumes tabular records; chat logs and free-text fine-tuning corpora require pre-tabularization that may itself leak. Alert fatigue is real: noisy thresholds without exception workflows lead to silent muting. |
| **12.1.3** | **Verify that** model feature-importance or attribution analyses are run on trained models to confirm that no removed identifier or quasi-identifier has been reconstructed as a high-importance feature. | 2 | Indirect leakage through model features that act as proxies for personal identifiers. Feature-importance analysis surfaces when a model has learned to rely on identity-correlated attributes. Membership inference attacks (MIA) exploit this exact signal — the ICLR 2025 paper "How Much of My Dataset Did You Use?" and IEEE SaTML 2025 "Range Membership Inference Attacks" extend classical MIA to quantify training-set leakage. Fast-MIA (2025) enables scalable MIA against production LLMs. Preference-data MIA against aligned LLMs (arXiv 2407.06443) exposed how RLHF/DPO data leaks through aligned-model probabilities. The Ensemble Privacy Defense paper (arXiv 2512.03100, Dec 2025, Vanderbilt et al.) benchmarks seven concurrent MIA techniques — SPV-MIA, LiRA, Recall, Log-Loss, Zlib, Min-K, Min-K++ — against TriviaQA, SQuAD, and AG News fine-tunes, confirming that no single feature-importance check covers the full attack surface. | Compute mutual information between hidden representations and known identifiers using SHAP, LIME, or dedicated MI estimation libraries (MINE, InfoNCE-based estimators). Verify MI < 0.01 threshold on validation splits. Complement with ML Privacy Meter to quantify record-level privacy risk scores across training examples and flag high-risk samples; run shadow-model MIA against fine-tuned checkpoints, ideally a panel covering LiRA, Min-K, Min-K++, and SPV-MIA so the audit reflects the strongest known black-box attacks rather than a single legacy baseline. Record the audit artifact alongside the model card and repeat at every retrain and post-fine-tune. Confirm results persist across demographic slices — high aggregate MI may mask elevated per-subgroup leakage. For fielded systems, layer in inference-time mitigations such as DP-Logits (calibrated noise on output logits with an RDP accountant), MIST, RelaxLoss, or the training-free Ensemble Privacy Defense (judge-LLM aggregation, reported 27.8% MIA reduction on SFT and 526% on RAG with an 8B judge model) and re-run the same MIA panel after enabling them. | MI estimation is computationally expensive and unstable in high dimensions — neural estimators can underestimate true MI by 2-3x. The ε = 0.01 threshold may need calibration per domain; current practice is to benchmark against null-model baselines rather than absolute thresholds. Deep models encode identifiers in distributed representations that evade standard feature-importance methods — tokenizer-level MIAs (arXiv 2510.05699) showed leakage that never surfaces in SHAP/LIME. Adversarial regularization reduces MIA success but can hurt utility; DP-SGD with ε≈10 gives strong membership privacy but typically costs 2-5% accuracy. Inference-time defenses are easier to deploy than full DP-SGD retraining but their privacy guarantees are empirical rather than provable, and the Ensemble Privacy Defense authors note that adaptive-noise injection alone gave inconsistent results across attack types — most of the benefit came from ensemble aggregation, which raises latency and cost and depends on the judge LLM not itself being trained on the same corpus. |
| **12.1.4** | **Verify that** formal proofs or synthetic-data certification show re-identification risk against trained models remains below a documented policy threshold even under linkage attacks. | 3 | Sophisticated linkage attacks combining the anonymized dataset with external auxiliary data (voter rolls, social media, commercial brokers, SEC filings). SynthLeak (2025) demonstrated linkage-attack success against Gretel's published synthetic SEC-filing dataset (ε=8, trained on 14,306 SEC filings 2023-2024) — multiple records were re-linkable despite DP training. This confirmed that ε=8 alone is insufficient without rigorous pre-release auditing. The 2026 model-centric privacy-attack paper (arXiv 2601.22434) extended the threat model from row-by-row dataset comparison to direct querying of the deployed generative model itself, mapping the GDPR triad (singling-out, linkability, inference) to differencing attacks, MIA, and attribute inference. Its central finding is that similarity-based privacy metrics (SBPMs) used by most commercial synthetic-data vendors "greatly underestimate privacy risks" and fall to simple reconstruction attacks, while DP-backed generators retain meaningful guarantees under the same threat model. | Request formal privacy proofs (differential-privacy composition bounds under RDP/zCDP accounting) or synthetic-data certification from Synthetic Data Vault (SDV), MOSTLY AI, Gretel.ai, K2View, or Hazy. Validate with independent red-team linkage attacks using realistic auxiliary datasets (state voter rolls, LinkedIn scrapes, industry-specific databases) **and** model-centric attacks against the deployed generator (differencing for singling-out, MIA for linkability, attribute-inference for downstream attribute recovery — per the threat-model mapping in arXiv 2601.22434). Use DP-Sniper or black-box ε̂ estimation to verify claimed privacy budgets empirically. Require the vendor to publish privacy metric results (TAP, NNDR, DCR, re-identification risk) per release. Cross-reference against NIST SP 800-188 (De-Identifying Government Datasets, final Sept 2023) guidance on formal privacy governance. | Formal proofs are rare outside academia and typically assume bounded auxiliary information — assumptions that rarely hold against real adversaries with broker-data access. Synthetic data leaks outlier properties: a record that is an outlier in the training set remains an outlier in the synthetic output. The 0.05 threshold is aggressive and forces significant utility tradeoffs. Privacy metrics for synthetic data lack standardization (arXiv 2501.03941) — vendors publish incompatible metrics, and linkage-attack success varies by auxiliary-data quality. The 2026 model-centric attack work (arXiv 2601.22434) directly indicts the similarity/distance-based privacy metrics that most vendors still publish, so a "passing" SBPM score should not be accepted as evidence of anonymity; insist on DP accountant output plus a documented model-centric attack panel. PrivCode (arXiv 2512.05459, Dec 2025) shows that DP-trained synthesizers can be paired with a non-private downstream fine-tune to recover utility for code datasets — a useful pattern for mixed-text training corpora, but only sound when the downstream tune sees no real records. Gretel, MOSTLY AI, and SDV are not equivalent: Gretel supports differentially-private fine-tuning on tabular/text, SDV is open source and research-focused, MOSTLY AI offers enterprise certification. Re-evaluate certification whenever auxiliary datasets change (e.g., new public breach dumps expand adversary capability). |

## Recent Research (2025-2026)

- **12.1.1 / 12.1.3:** As of May 2026, PANORAMA (arXiv 2505.12238) gives auditors a synthetic, ethically sourced PII memorization benchmark: 384,789 samples across 9,674 synthetic profiles, with Mistral-7B fine-tunes showing memorization increasing as repeated PII-laced content moves from 1x to 25x replication. Use it as a canary-style evaluation set before accepting claims that redaction or minimization alone prevents memorization.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.3:** ACL Findings 2025 R.R. showed that masked or scrubbed PII can still be reconstructed through a recollection-and-ranking attack, so audits should test whether "removed" identifiers can be inferred from surrounding text rather than only scanning generated output for exact original strings.
- **12.1.3:** The ICLR 2026 OpenReview submission on membership decoding argues that greedy decoding audits miss partially memorized training sequences. Treat it as a signal to include non-greedy extraction and membership-guided decoding in privacy red teams where the model owner can run controlled shadow attacks.
- **12.1.3:** EZ-MIA (arXiv 2601.12104, revised Apr 2026) raises the bar for fine-tune privacy audits: it needs only two forward passes and no attack-model training, yet reports AUC 0.98 on WikiText/GPT-2 and materially higher true-positive rates at low false-positive thresholds. Treat token-probability access as enough for a serious membership audit.
- **12.1.3:** ADRA / ADRA+ (arXiv 2602.19020, Feb 2026) reframes membership inference as active reconstruction: the auditor fine-tunes a policy initialized from the target model with reconstruction rewards, then checks whether candidate records are easier to elicit. This is especially relevant when pre-training, post-training, or distillation datasets are disputed.
- **12.1.3:** SimMIA and WikiMIA-25 (arXiv 2601.11314, Jan 2026) show that text-only black-box audits are improving. When only generated text is available, require sampling-based membership tests rather than accepting "no logits available" as a reason to skip training-data privacy evaluation.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.3:** The cue-controlled memorization framework (arXiv 2601.03791, Jan 2026) is a useful corrective: PII "leakage" tests should control prompt-target lexical overlap, because high-cue prompts can measure pattern completion rather than memorization. Pair exact-PII extraction tests with low-cue and cue-free probes before treating a finding as evidence of training-data memorization.
- **12.1.3:** Causal MIA evaluation (arXiv 2602.02819, Feb 2026) warns that one-run and zero-run audit shortcuts can be biased by interference and non-random membership assignment. Privacy test reports should document how member/non-member sets were sampled and whether the estimator remains valid when full retraining is impractical.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.3:** Large Audio Language Model MIA work (arXiv 2603.28378, Mar 2026) shows that audio privacy audits need blind spectral/prosodic baselines: common speech datasets can be separable without model inference, while true cross-modal memorization arises from binding speaker identity to text. Use distribution-matched controls before claiming audio PII was memorized.
- **12.1.3 / 12.1.4:** Diffusion language model memorization research (arXiv 2603.02333, Mar 2026) found that higher sampling resolution increases exact extraction probability, even though aligned tests showed lower PII leakage than autoregressive models. Do not treat non-autoregressive generation as an anonymization control without model-specific extraction testing.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.3:** Unintended Memorization of Sensitive Information in Fine-Tuned Language Models (arXiv 2601.17480, EACL 2026) showed that PII appearing only in inputs, not labels, can still leak after fine-tuning. Its mitigation comparison is a useful audit checklist: test differential privacy, unlearning, regularization, and preference alignment on the same extraction probes instead of relying on redaction claims alone.
- **12.1.2 / 12.1.4:** Large-scale online deanonymization work (arXiv 2602.16800, revised Feb 2026) showed that unstructured pseudonymous text can be linked across platforms with up to 68% recall at 90% precision. For training corpora, k-anonymity reviews should include free-text stylometry, semantic-profile features, and cross-dataset linkage tests, not only tabular quasi-identifiers.
- **12.1.1:** Tracking Conversations (arXiv 2604.27438, Apr 2026) measured 20 popular chatbots and found 17 sharing some information with third parties, including plaintext prompt or response snippets in three cases and chat URLs or identifiers in 15 cases. Minimize prompt and conversation metadata before analytics, advertising, support widgets, or replay tooling can convert it into another exposed dataset.
- **12.1.1 / 12.1.4:** The FTC's March 2026 OkCupid/Match action alleged that nearly three million user photos plus location and other information were shared with a third party without formal use restrictions. Treat contractual purpose limits, deletion rights, and downstream model-disgorgement clauses as part of data minimization evidence, not just legal boilerplate.
- **12.1.1:** Presidio 2.2.361 changed the hash anonymizer to use random per-entity salt by default. That improves brute-force resistance, but verification should explicitly check whether a managed salt is provided when referential integrity across records is required.
- **12.1.2:** sdcMicro's March 2026 AI-assisted anonymization workflow keeps the microdata local and sends only metadata (variable names, types, cardinality, factor levels, and current k violations) for strategy suggestions. That is useful for disclosure-control planning, but the generated recoding/noise/suppression script still needs human approval and independent risk/utility review.
- **12.1.4:** The Commission's March 2026 FAQ for the Article 53(1)(d) training-summary template confirms that GPAI providers must disclose whether training used user-interaction data, publish summaries by model release, update them at six-month intervals when additional training data materially changes the summary, and face AI Office enforcement from 2 August 2026.

## Tooling

- **PII detection:** Microsoft Presidio (PyPI 15 Mar 2026 — Azure Health Data Services Surrogate operator, Aadhaar contextual delimiters, Italian Driver License regex fix; LangExtract recognizers; multi-modal), spaCy NER, Google Cloud DLP API, Nightfall AI, Private AI, Amazon Comprehend PII
- **Data anonymization:** ARX Data Anonymization Tool, R sdcMicro (March 2026 LLM-agentic release), μ-Argus, Synthetic Data Vault (SDV), Gretel.ai (now NVIDIA NeMo Data Designer, Apache 2.0), MOSTLY AI, Hazy
- **Differential privacy training:** Opacus (PyTorch, Meta — v1.5.x with Fast Gradient Clipping and Ghost Clipping; ~95% DP-SGD memory reduction; embedding-layer and generative-NLP support), TensorFlow Privacy v0.15.0, JAX Privacy, Google DP library
- **Inference-time privacy mitigations:** DP-Logits with RDP accounting, MIST, RelaxLoss, Ensemble Privacy Defense (training-free judge-LLM aggregation; arXiv 2512.03100)
- **Privacy auditing:** ML Privacy Meter (NUS DPaTML Lab — record-level risk scoring, shadow-model MIA), DP-Sniper (black-box DP auditing), canary-based memorization testing, PII-Scope benchmark, Fast-MIA, SynthLeak evaluator for synthetic data, model-centric attack panels (differencing / MIA / attribute-inference per arXiv 2601.22434), Combinatorial Refinement Attack replay against ARX-style local-recoding outputs (arXiv 2509.03350), free-text deanonymization probes for pseudonymous corpora (arXiv 2602.16800), MIA panels covering LiRA / Min-K / Min-K++ / SPV-MIA / Recall / Log-Loss / Zlib
- **Policy & governance:** OneTrust PreventAI, BigID, Privacera, IBM Watson Knowledge Catalog, Collibra Data Privacy, EU Article 53(1)(d) public training-summary template (mandatory for GPAI providers)
