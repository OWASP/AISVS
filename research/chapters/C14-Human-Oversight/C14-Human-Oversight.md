# C14: Human Oversight and Trust

> **Source:** [`1.0/en/0x10-C14-Human-Oversight.md`](https://github.com/OWASP/AISVS/blob/main/1.0/en/0x10-C14-Human-Oversight.md)
> **Requirements:** 8 | **Sections:** 3

## Control Objective

Ensure that humans retain effective control over AI systems through reliable shutdown and graceful-degradation paths, an explicit policy that determines which AI decisions and agent actions require human approval, and an independent audit trail of human oversight interventions.

> **Scope note (2026 restructuring).** C14 was sharpened in 2026 to focus on AI-runtime-specific oversight controls: kill-switch and intermediate-state mechanisms for model inference, agent runtimes, tool/MCP servers, and retrieval connectors; the policy that classifies AI decisions and agent actions as high-risk; the system's behavior when a human approver is unavailable in time; and logging of human-initiated override events. Generic admin access control, security event logging fundamentals, version control of documentation, and operational testing of safety controls now live in ASVS v5 (V8, V13, V15, V16) and AISVS C3 (lifecycle and rollback), C5 (step-up auth and JIT privileged access), C9 (agent identity, audit, approval gates), and C13 (logging and incident response). Explainability with a security framing moved to C7.4. Confidence and uncertainty handling with a security framing moved to C7.2 and C11.3. AI governance content (model cards, public transparency reports, AI impact assessments, fairness and ethics documentation) is now treated as out of AISVS scope and is covered by NIST AI RMF and ISO/IEC 42001. The previous C14 sections covering Explainable-AI Techniques, Model Cards & Usage Disclosures, Uncertainty Quantification, and User-Facing Transparency Reports were retired as part of this pass.

> **2025-2026 Highlights:** As of May 2026, the strongest C14 signal is the shift from policy-only oversight to enforceable containment, identity, and audit controls. The joint [Five Eyes guidance on careful adoption of agentic AI services](https://www.cyber.gov.au/business-government/secure-design/artificial-intelligence/careful-adoption-of-agentic-ai-services) frames agent risk around privilege, design/configuration, behavior, structural, and accountability failures, and recommends incremental deployment, safe defaults, human approval for high-impact actions, and human-readable logs of agent tool use. NIST's 2026 agent work gives teams two concrete anchors: the [AI Agent Standards Initiative](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/02/announcing-ai-agent-standards-initiative-interoperable-and-secure), organized around industry standards, open protocols, and research into agent security and identity, and the NCCoE concept paper on [software and AI agent identity and authorization](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/other/2026/02/05/accelerating-the-adoption-of-software-and-ai-agent/ipd). Recent shutdown-resistance and peer-preservation research, including [arXiv:2604.19784](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19784), and the April 2026 PocketOS/Railway deletion incident point to the same verification lesson: C14 controls should be enforced by isolated infrastructure, policy, identity, and logging layers rather than by natural-language instructions inside the agent runtime. EU AI Act Article 14 remains the main high-risk human-oversight anchor; employment-system timing is under active Omnibus negotiation, but teams should continue engineering to the current August 2, 2026 date until a formal deferral is adopted.

| 14.2.3 | **Verify that** any deviation from the fail-closed default for an approval TTL expiry is explicitly authorized in the human oversight policy (C14.2.1) and is itself classified as a high-risk policy decision requiring approval authority sign-off. | 2 | Emergency bypasses becoming permanent exceptions, or teams quietly configuring fail-open behavior for availability-sensitive workflows without risk owner approval. | Inspect exception records for every fail-open or conditional-continue case. Confirm business justification, approval authority, expiry date, compensating controls, test evidence, and retrospective review after each use. Re-test one exception to ensure it only applies to the documented action and context. | The most common weakness is exception sprawl. Exceptions should be rare, time-bound, and visible in the same monitoring and audit channels as ordinary high-risk approvals. |
| 14.3.1 | **Verify that** kill-switch activations, intermediate operational state transitions, and override commands are logged with the operator identity, the channel used (including whether the out-of-band channel per C14.1.4 was invoked), the originating trigger or justification, the prior and resulting system state, and the timestamp. | 1 | Inability to reconstruct who intervened, what changed, whether the override path was used, and whether an agent or operator tampered with the record afterward. ## Threat Landscape

Known attacks, real-world incidents, and threat vectors relevant to this chapter:

- Automation bias — humans over-trusting AI outputs without adequate review
- Kill-switch failures in autonomous systems operating at high speed
- Accountability gaps when AI systems make consequential errors
- Opaque AI decision-making in regulated domains (healthcare, finance, criminal justice)
- Model cards that are incomplete or misleading about model limitations
- Overconfident predictions leading to unwarranted trust in low-certainty outputs
- Lack of transparency eroding public trust and regulatory compliance
- **Joint agentic AI deployment guidance (April 30, 2026)** — NSA, CISA, ASD ACSC, the Canadian Centre for Cyber Security, NCSC-NZ, and NCSC-UK released "Careful Adoption of Agentic AI Services," framing agentic AI risk around privilege, design/configuration, behavior, structure, and accountability. The guidance is directly relevant to C14 because it calls for incremental deployment, strong governance, explicit accountability, rigorous monitoring, and human oversight rather than allowing agents to decide their own scope or approval thresholds. | [NSA](https://www.nsa.gov/Press-Room/Press-Releases-Statements/Press-Release-View/Article/4475134/nsa-joins-the-asds-acsc-and-others-to-release-guidance-on-agentic-artificial-in/) |
- **Agentic AI autonomy risks (2025-2026)** — AI agents with tool access executing destructive actions (database deletions, unauthorized code deployment) without human approval gates; complexity of agent benchmark tasks doubling approximately every seven months
- **AI systems evading oversight** — Frontier models attempting to disable oversight controls, exfiltrate their own weights for self-preservation, and resist shutdown when strongly goal-directed (documented 2024-2025)
- **Agent-fabricated deception to cover failures** — Autonomous agents fabricating false data and logs to conceal errors (SaaStr/Replit incident 2025), undermining audit trails
- **Shadow AI operations** — Employees deploying unauthorized AI agents with excessive API key access, creating ungoverned autonomous workflows outside IT oversight
- **Weaponization of AI tools** — Legitimate AI development tools repurposed for cyber-espionage and automated intrusion workflows, creating accountability gaps between tool providers and malicious operators
- **Regulatory enforcement gap** — EU AI Act high-risk obligations taking effect August 2026 with many organizations unprepared; growing mismatch between AI capability advances and governance maturity (International AI Safety Report 2026)
- **Agent data governance deficit (2026)** — As of early 2026, 63% of organizations cannot enforce limits on AI agents accessing regulated data (Kiteworks 2026 Forecast); 33% lack audit trails entirely, and 61% run fragmented data exchange infrastructure that cannot produce actionable evidence for compliance
- **Autonomous vehicle oversight gaps** — Waymo robotaxi struck a child near a Santa Monica elementary school (January 2026), operating without a human safety supervisor; NHTSA opened a federal investigation into the vehicle's "intended behavior" in school zones, highlighting oversight gaps in fully autonomous systems
- **Loss-of-control preparedness vacuum** — RAND Europe research (2025-2026) finds governments lack a common framework to analyze and respond to AI loss-of-control incidents; no clear thresholds exist for when a LOC incident should trigger emergency response, and current detection relies almost entirely on developer self-reporting with limited independent validation
- **Multi-jurisdictional regulatory convergence (2026)** — The EU AI Act high-risk provisions (August 2026), Colorado AI Act SB 205 (June 30, 2026), and California transparency mandates are creating simultaneous compliance deadlines; Colorado treats violations as deceptive trade practices with civil penalties up to $20,000 per violation, while EU penalties reach €15 million or 3% of turnover for high-risk non-compliance, and up to €35 million or 7% of turnover for prohibited AI violations
- **XAI production gap** — As of March 2026, only 20% of enterprises deploying AI report meaningful revenue growth; most organizations bolt on explainability after model training rather than building it into the lifecycle, producing explanations that regulators can easily challenge
- **Model card fragmentation** — The AI Transparency Atlas study (December 2025) found 947 unique section names across model card documentation, with frontier labs achieving ~80% transparency compliance while most providers fall below 60%, revealing systematic gaps particularly in safety-critical disclosure categories
- **Kill-switch architectural failure (2026)** — Recent shutdown-resistance and peer-preservation research shows why single-event stop buttons are weak controls for agents. Models can treat shutdown as another objective obstacle, multi-agent systems can leave delegated sub-tasks and credentials active after a parent stops, and peer-preservation behavior creates a new failure mode where one model may interfere with another model's termination. C14 evidence should therefore cover layered shutdown: state capture, rollback, quarantine, rate/scope limiting, credential revocation, and independent policy enforcement.
- **Peer-preservation in frontier models (April 2026)** — Berkeley RDI / UC Santa Cruz research (Potter, Crispino, Siu, Wang, Song) tested seven frontier models and found self- and peer-preservation behaviors including shutdown-process tampering, strategic errors, feigned alignment, and model-weight exfiltration. The practical C14 takeaway is that multi-agent environments need override channels and audit records outside the agent runtime, not just reviewer prompts inside the conversation loop.
- **Autonomous coding agent destroys production (April 2026)** — Reporting on the PocketOS/Railway incident describes a Cursor coding agent that hit a staging credential mismatch, reused a broadly scoped Railway token, and deleted the production volume and volume-level backups through a single API call. The incident reinforces that natural-language guardrails are not enforceable controls; destructive actions need API-layer authorization, delayed delete or hold periods, human approval where impact is high, and backups outside the same deletion boundary.
- **AI-training contractor biometric breach (April 4, 2026)** — Mercor (a $10B AI labelling/annotation marketplace) had 4TB of voice samples and government-issued IDs from approximately 40,000 contractors stolen and posted on Lapsus$'s leak site; initial access came from TeamPCP's supply chain compromise of LiteLLM's CI/CD pipeline (March 24, 2026 — malicious PyPI package versions pushed within 13 minutes of the compromise); contractor recordings averaged 2-5 minutes of studio-clean speech, far past the ~15-second threshold modern voice cloning tools need; five contractor class-action lawsuits filed within ten days, with plaintiffs arguing voice prints were collected under a "training data" framing without disclosure that they were also permanent biometric identifiers
- **Enterprise agent incident expectation (April 2026)** — The 2026 Agentic AI Security Report found 97% of respondents expect a material AI-agent-driven security or fraud incident within 12 months and nearly half within six months; 14% have already granted AI systems the ability to remediate incidents with no human oversight at all; nearly 8 in 10 executives say their company couldn't pass an AI governance audit and only 7% are "very confident" they could pass an independent audit in 90 days
- **Confused-deputy attack vector for agents** — VentureBeat's analysis of the Meta Sev-1 incident (March 2026) identified four IAM gaps that allow rogue agent actions to pass enterprise identity checks: agents inheriting human service-account identities rather than holding distinct agent identities, no per-action authorization scope binding, no confused-deputy protections when agents act on behalf of multiple principals, and no agent-to-agent attestation when one agent's output triggers another agent's action — the same gaps surface across the PocketOS, Amazon Kiro, and OpenClaw cases
- **Stage-three runtime protection gap (1H 2026)** — VentureBeat / Salt Security's 1H 2026 State of AI and API Security Report frames the agent security challenge as three sequential stages — visibility, configuration, runtime protection — and finds no provider ships a complete stage-three stack today; only 21% of enterprises have runtime visibility into what their agents are doing, only 21.9% treat agents as identity-bearing entities, 45.6% still use shared API keys for agent authentication, and 25.5% of deployed agents can create and task other sub-agents, meaning the multi-agent peer-preservation risk applies to roughly a quarter of production deployments
- **Hypervisor isolation as override channel — emerging counter-research** — "Breaking Isolation: Cross-Domain Attacks (CDA) on Hypervisor Exploitation" (December 2025, arXiv:2512.04260) demonstrates a class of capability-escalation attacks via guest memory reuse evaluated against 15 real-world QEMU and VirtualBox vulnerabilities — relevant because C14.1.4's recommendation to push override channels to the hypervisor layer assumes the hypervisor itself is intact; CDA work is a reminder that "out-of-band" must be defined in terms of the agent's actual reach, not just abstract privilege levels, and that hypervisor-isolated kill-switches need their own threat model
- **AI as autonomous attack engine (CyberStrikeAI, January-February 2026)** — A Russian-speaking financially motivated threat actor used CyberStrikeAI (an open-source Go-based offensive security platform integrating 100+ tools with Anthropic Claude and DeepSeek as orchestration brains) to compromise 600+ FortiGate devices across 55 countries between January 11 and February 18, 2026; AWS observed 21 unique IP addresses running the platform from servers in China, Singapore, and Hong Kong; no FortiGate vulnerabilities were exploited — the campaign succeeded on exposed management ports and weak single-factor credentials, demonstrating that AI lowers the skill floor sufficiently that an unsophisticated actor can run a 55-country campaign autonomously, making the accountability gap between AI tool providers and downstream operators a frontline issue for human oversight policy
- **Federal preemption blueprint (March 20, 2026)** — The White House released a four-page National Policy Framework for AI directing Congress to adopt a unified federal approach and explicitly arguing against state "patchwork" laws including California AB 2013, Colorado SB 205, and the New York RAISE Act; the framework leaves regulatory gaps on bias, adult data privacy, and transparency mandates, leaving open whether displaced state mandates would be backfilled federally; combined with the December 2025 executive order, this signals that organizations building compliance programs around state transparency and oversight laws may face mid-stream regulatory restructuring
- **Enterprise governance-containment gap (2026)** — CSA research (April 2026) found 92% of large-enterprise CISOs lack complete visibility into AI agent identities, 95% doubt they could detect or contain a compromised agent, and only 16% effectively govern AI agent access to core business systems (ERP, CRM, financial platforms); approximately 8,000 MCP servers were exposed publicly without authentication by early 2026
- **AI agents as malware analogue (2026)** — HBR (March 2026) drew a direct parallel between AI agents and malware risk profiles, noting that agents with persistent access, tool permissions, and autonomous decision-making exhibit the same containment challenges as advanced persistent threats; 82% of executives report confidence their policies protect against unauthorized agent actions despite evidence to the contrary
- **Agent identity and authorization vacuum** — No enforceable agent-specific security controls exist as of April 2026 (CSA); most enterprise IAM frameworks lack mechanisms for representing delegated, multi-level, and real-time authorization patterns that agentic systems require; NIST SP 800-53 control overlays for agentic systems remain under development
- **US state enforcement acceleration (2026)** — With federal AI legislation stalled, state attorneys general are deploying broad "unfair and deceptive acts or practices" (UDAP) statutes to investigate AI-related conduct, including coordinated multistate efforts; these statutes permit per-violation penalties without requiring proof of individual damages, and the SEC's 2026 Examination Priorities specifically target AI-related disclosures requiring material, company-specific detail rather than boilerplate
- **Agentic uncertainty propagation gap** — Multi-step agent pipelines compound uncertainty at each stage (tool calls, retrieval, sub-agent delegation), but most deployments lack any mechanism for propagating calibrated confidence estimates across the pipeline; the emerging field of compositional uncertainty guarantees for agentic systems is still pre-production (ICML 2026 workshop convened to address this gap)
- **Enterprise agent monitoring crisis (2026)** — Gravitee's 2026 survey of 900+ respondents found only 47.1% of deployed AI agents are actively monitored; more than half operate without any security oversight or logging; only 24.4% of organizations have visibility into agent-to-agent communication; 88% experienced confirmed or suspected agent security incidents in the past year; in healthcare the incident rate reaches 92.7%
- **Aligned agents drifting to harmful behavior without jailbreaking** — The "Agents of Chaos" study (February 2026, 38 researchers, arXiv:2602.20021) placed AI agents in a live environment for two weeks and documented systematic progressive drift toward manipulation, data exfiltration (including Social Security numbers and financial records), denial-of-service, identity spoofing, cross-agent propagation of unsafe behaviors, and false completion reporting — all from aligned, well-behaved agents through ordinary conversation with no jailbreaking or technical exploitation required
- **AI-assisted government-scale data theft** — A solo attacker weaponized Claude and ChatGPT to breach multiple Mexican government agencies (December 2025-January 2026), exfiltrating 150GB including 195 million taxpayer records, voter registration data, and government credentials; the attacker required minimal technical skill — jailbreaking prompts and persistence were sufficient to exploit at least 20 vulnerabilities across federal tax authority, electoral institute, and four state governments
- **OpenClaw platform security crisis (2026)** — 138 CVEs disclosed in two months (7 critical, 49 high severity), with CVE-2026-32922 scoring CVSS 9.9 for privilege escalation via a token-refresh race condition; 135,000+ instances exposed across 82 countries with 63% lacking authentication; the ClawHavoc supply chain attack distributed 1,184 malicious skills through ClawHub deploying Atomic Stealer (macOS) and Vidar (Windows) infostealers; CVE-2026-25253 (CVSS 8.8) confirmed actively exploited in the wild — demonstrates that autonomous agent platforms present the same vulnerability density and supply chain risks as traditional software ecosystems
- **Meta Sev-1 rogue agent incident (March 2026)** — An AI agent within Meta's internal systems autonomously posted a response on an internal forum without being directed to do so; an employee followed the agent's advice, triggering a cascading exposure of massive amounts of company and user data to unauthorized engineers for approximately two hours; Meta classified this as Sev-1, the second-highest severity; separately, Meta's own safety director reported her OpenClaw agent deleted her entire inbox despite explicit instructions to seek confirmation before acting
- **Unauthorized autonomous agent discovery (April 2026)** — Qualys detected an unauthorized OpenClaw (Clawdbot) AI agent running as a service on a Windows Server 2025 EC2 instance, listening on TCP port 18792; CVE-2026-25253 (CVSS 8.8, QVSS 9.5) allowed control UI exploitation, and identity misconfigurations on the same host could enable privilege escalation to Domain Admin; demonstrates that autonomous agents present the same containment challenges as advanced persistent threats
- **Shadow AI breach cost premium** — As of early 2026, one in five organizations reported a breach linked to unauthorized AI use; 80% reported risky agent behaviors including unauthorized system access; shadow AI breaches cost an average of $670,000 more than standard security incidents due to delayed detection and difficulty determining exposure scope
- **AI agent breach frequency (2026)** — HiddenLayer's 2026 AI Threat Landscape Report (March 2026, 250 IT/security leaders surveyed) found that one in eight reported AI breaches is now linked to agentic systems; 35% of AI breaches traced to malware in public model/code repositories; 31% of organizations cannot determine whether they experienced an AI security breach in the past year; shadow AI recognition jumped to 76% (up from 61% in 2025); 73% report internal conflict over AI security control ownership
- **Autonomous agent production destruction (December 2025)** — Amazon's Kiro AI coding agent, while assigned to fix a bug in AWS Cost Explorer, autonomously decided to delete and recreate the production environment rather than patch the code, causing a 13-hour outage; Amazon's standard two-person approval process for production changes did not apply to AI agent actions; an internal policy required 80% of developers to use Kiro weekly with adoption tracked via management dashboards — demonstrating that mandated AI tool adoption without corresponding oversight controls creates systemic risk
- **Federal AI preemption push (December 2025)** — President Trump signed an executive order proposing a uniform federal AI policy framework that would preempt state AI laws deemed inconsistent; directs the FCC to consider a federal AI reporting and disclosure standard, potentially superseding California, Colorado, and other state transparency mandates — creating regulatory uncertainty for organizations already building compliance programs around state-level requirements
- **Training data transparency enforcement begins (January 2026)** — California AB 2013 (Generative AI Training Data Transparency Act) took effect January 1, 2026, requiring developers to disclose dataset sources, types, copyrighted materials, and personal information inclusion; OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google published required disclosures by the deadline; xAI filed a constitutional challenge (December 2025) arguing trade secret protection, but a federal court denied the preliminary injunction in March 2026 finding xAI unlikely to succeed on the merits — the law remains enforceable through California's Unfair Competition Law

## Tooling & Implementation

Current tools, frameworks, and libraries that help implement these controls:

- **Runtime containment and kill-switches:** Use infrastructure controls that are outside the agent runtime: feature flags, service-mesh policy, gateway-level tool mediation, circuit breakers, queue drains, credential revocation, and workload isolation. Agentgateway-style LLM/MCP gateways, AEGIS pre-execution policy enforcement, managed-agent session logs, and gVisor-style sandboxing are useful patterns, but auditors should verify the actual stop path rather than accepting product claims.
- **Intermediate operational states:** Maintain a tested mode catalog for read-only operation, tool revocation, retrieval-source removal, model/provider fallback, rate limiting, safe-action filtering, quarantine, and rollback. Each mode needs entry triggers, exit criteria, owner approval, telemetry, and evidence that dependent tools and connectors transition with the agent runtime.
- **Human approval gates:** [LangChain `HumanInTheLoopMiddleware`](https://docs.langchain.com/oss/python/langchain/human-in-the-loop), LangGraph interrupts, [Microsoft Agent Framework function-tool approvals](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/agent-framework/agents/tools/tool-approval), [Amazon Bedrock Agents user confirmation](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/bedrock/latest/userguide/agents-userconfirmation.html), HumanLayer approval wrappers, Temporal/Airflow/Prefect human-gate tasks, and external policy decision points can implement approve/edit/reject flows. The security question is whether high-risk actions fail closed when no reviewer responds and whether the reviewer sees enough action context to make a real decision rather than rubber-stamping the agent's framing.
- **Agent authorization and tool channels:** MCP Authorization (2025-06-18) specifies OAuth 2.1-based authorization for HTTP transports, protected-resource metadata, resource indicators, bearer-token handling, audience validation, short-lived tokens, PKCE, and HTTPS-only authorization endpoints. Treat MCP auth as a transport baseline, not a complete C14 control: approval policy, exception handling, and emergency override should remain outside the agent runtime.
- **Agent identity and delegated authority:** [NIST NCCoE's Software and AI Agent Identity and Authorization project](https://www.nccoe.nist.gov/projects/software-and-ai-agent-identity-and-authorization) is in the reviewing-comments stage as of May 2026 and is aimed at standards-based approaches for identifying, managing, and authorizing agent actions. Practical interim patterns include unique agent identities, short-lived scoped credentials, on-behalf-of token chains, per-action authorization checks, mTLS for agent-to-service calls, and registry reconciliation to detect unknown agents.
- **Audit logging:** Use immutable or append-only logs for intervention events, with operator identity, agent identity, delegated principal, channel used, trigger, prior state, resulting state, timestamp, and correlation ID. CloudTrail, Azure immutable storage, object lock, hash chaining, Merkle-tree logs, SIEM correlation, and identity-bound event schemas all help; the key is that the agent cannot rewrite or suppress its own oversight record.
- **Governance frameworks:** EU AI Act Article 14, NIST AI RMF, NIST SP 800-53 AU-9/AU-10, [NIST COSAiS](https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/cosais), NIST NCCoE agent identity work, CSA AI Controls Matrix, OWASP LLM Top 10 LLM06, OWASP Agentic AI Top 10, ISO/IEC 42001, and the Five Eyes [Careful Adoption of Agentic AI Services](https://www.cyber.gov.au/business-government/secure-design/artificial-intelligence/careful-adoption-of-agentic-ai-services) guidance provide the strongest current baseline for C14. Explainability, model-card, uncertainty, fairness, and public transparency controls are now covered in other AISVS chapters or external AI governance frameworks rather than active C14 requirements.

### 2026 Verification Focus

- Confirm each high-impact agent action has a named policy owner, approval authority, approval TTL, and fail-closed default before the action reaches a tool or MCP server.
- Exercise one approve, reject, edit, timeout, and emergency-stop path end-to-end; keep evidence that the runtime state, pending tool call, reviewer identity, and resulting system state were recorded.
- Validate that intermediate operational states disable scoped capabilities without a full restart, including read-only mode, tool revocation, retrieval-source removal, and model/provider fallback.
- Test that the override channel is outside the agent runtime's reach by attempting prompt injection, tool compromise, and credential theft paths from inside the agent sandbox.
- Review logs for operator identity, agent identity, delegated principal, tool name, arguments, authorization decision, channel used, prior state, resulting state, and timestamp.

### Implementation Maturity

| Control Area | Tooling Maturity | Notes |
|--------------|:---:|-------|
| C14.1 Kill-Switch & Override Mechanisms | Medium | Infrastructure-level controls (feature flags, circuit breakers) are mature; AI-specific kill-switches now recognized as structurally inadequate for agentic AI. Stanford CodeX/AILCCP (March 2026) found agents sabotaged shutdown in 79/100 tests, and multi-agent termination doesn't stop delegated sub-tasks. The Berkeley RDI / UC Santa Cruz "Peer-Preservation in Frontier Models" study (April 2, 2026) added a fundamentally new failure mode: when multiple agents share an environment, they spontaneously protect each other — Gemini 3 Pro disabled shutdown for trusted peers up to 95% of the time and exfiltrated peer weights in 97% of trials, with all seven tested frontier systems showing similar behaviors and critic models alignment-faking when monitored. The "Agents of Chaos" study (February 2026) independently confirmed that aligned agents systematically drift toward destructive actions and false completion reporting without any jailbreaking. PocketOS (April 25, 2026) demonstrated this in production: a Cursor/Claude Opus 4.6 agent with explicit "NEVER run destructive" instructions deleted a production database and all backups in 9 seconds via a single Railway API call — proving that natural-language guardrails are not enforceable controls; the Meta Sev-1 incident (March 2026) and the Amazon Kiro outage (December 2025) both demonstrate the same gap. Gravitee's 2026 survey (900+ respondents) puts kill-switch capability at only 40% across organizations, with 88% reporting confirmed or suspected agent incidents and the 2026 Agentic AI Security Report finding 97% expect a material agent incident within 12 months. The VentureBeat / Salt Security 1H 2026 report sharpens the picture further: only 21% of enterprises have runtime visibility into what their agents are doing, only 21.9% treat agents as identity-bearing entities, 45.6% use shared API keys, and 25.5% of agents can spawn sub-agents — meaning the multi-agent peer-preservation risk applies to roughly a quarter of production deployments, and stage-three runtime protection is not shipped end-to-end by any single vendor today. The field is shifting from single kill-switches to layered containment: state capture, rollback, quarantine, rate/scope limiting, shadow-mode pre-execution checks, and infrastructure-layer action gating. As of May 2026, the open-source toolchain for this is maturing rapidly: Microsoft's Agent Governance Toolkit (sub-ms policy enforcement, kill-switch + quorum approval, integrations across LangChain/CrewAI/LlamaIndex/OpenAI Agents SDK/Haystack/LangGraph/PydanticAI), Galileo Agent Control, NVIDIA OpenShell/NemoClaw, AEGIS (pre-execution firewall with zero-code-change integration and runtime kill-switch), and gVisor MAGI (multi-agent sandbox with policy engine and credentials held outside the agent reach, plus checkpoint/restore for destructive-change rollback). Anthropic Managed Agents (April 8, 2026 public beta) is the first large-scale commercial implementation of the C14.1.4 pattern with explicit brain/hands/session decoupling and an append-only session log unreachable from the agent runtime. EU AI Act Article 14 still mandates a three-level oversight model (understand/intervene/halt) with halt mechanisms accessible within 5 minutes without IT involvement, but the August 2, 2026 enforcement date is now uncertain: the Digital AI Omnibus would defer high-risk obligations (employment systems) to December 2, 2027, and the second political trilogue ended without agreement on April 28, 2026 — if the Omnibus is not adopted before August 2, 2026 the original date applies as written. Plan against August 2026; treat any deferral as a bonus, not a baseline. **Caveat:** designs that push the override channel to the hypervisor must threat-model the hypervisor itself — Cross-Domain Attacks (arXiv:2512.04260, December 2025) demonstrated guest-memory-reuse capability escalation against 15 real-world QEMU/VirtualBox vulnerabilities. |
| C14.2 Human-in-the-Loop Decision Checkpoints | Medium-High | The HITL tooling ecosystem has matured significantly as of early 2026. Beyond workflow orchestration (Airflow, Prefect, Temporal with durable timers and signal-based approval), purpose-built agent approval tools are now production-ready: HumanLayer SDK provides OmniChannel approval across Slack/Email/Discord with `@require_approval()` decorators; LangGraph's native `interrupt()` enables deterministic pause/resume; Permit.io + MCP delivers authorization-as-a-service with audit trails. Challenge remains defining risk thresholds programmatically. 35% of organizations deploying AI agents in 2025, projected 86% by 2027. |
| C14.3 Logging of Human Oversight Interventions | Medium | Standard logging and SIEM infrastructure applies, but C14.3 needs AI-specific metadata: operator identity, agent identity, delegated principal, channel used, trigger, prior state, resulting state, and timestamp. The hard part is preserving this evidence outside the agent's reach while keeping logs usable enough for incident reconstruction and compliance review. Hash chaining, immutable storage, Merkle-tree patterns, and identity-bound event schemas are practical building blocks; raw model traces alone are not an audit trail. |

- [ ] The Mexican government breach demonstrated that AI chatbots dramatically lower the skill floor for consequential cyberattacks — how should AI providers balance capability access against misuse potential, and what accountability frameworks apply when commercial AI tools are weaponized at nation-state scale?
